DAILY LIFE AND CULTURE IN AOLI\nAoli is two worlds woven together — the settled imperial heartland with its terraced valleys, ancestral villages, and mandala-cities; and the six sects ringing it on the mountains and in the mist. The ordinary person lives mostly in the first. The cultivator lives mostly in the second. The two worlds touch at festivals, at markets, at the sect schools that take in imperial children, and at the quiet edges where an old family has one son who went to the mountain.\n\nThe cultural base is **Western Zhou Dynasty (1046-771 BCE)** with later overlays — Mandate of Heaven doctrine, feudal vassal-lord structure, pre-Confucian mythic time, ritual bronze and jade aesthetic, archaic silk.\n\n═══ ARCHITECTURE ═══\n\n**The imperial mandala — Zǐchén.** The capital \"Purple Celestial\" is a mandala city at the heart of the central valley — the sacred centre of Aoli, surrounded by terraced farmland and ancestral villages, ringed at the horizon by the six sect mountains. The Forbidden Palace at the city's centre is a layered complex of halls, courtyards, gardens, and private wings. Purple-tiled roofs, jade-inlaid pillars, lacquered red doors, bronze ritual vessels, golden cloud-and-wave embroidery on ceremonial textiles. Each Chén emperor adds his signature touch to one small private courtyard — Chén Yuán's orchid courtyard is his.\n\n**Noble compounds.** Great houses like Mù, Lí, Cuī, Xià, Tián, Lǔ, Lǔ's southern counterpart, and the lesser noble families live in walled compounds with internal courtyards arranged by seniority. The patriarch's hall faces south; sons' halls fan out east and west; the inner rooms hold the daughters and women of the house. Family shrines in the deepest courtyards. The Mù compound in Zǐchén is the most elaborate after the palace.\n\n**Village farmsteads.** Common people live in low timber-and-mud houses with sloped tile or thatched roofs. A central hearth, a sleeping platform, a small shrine to the local earth-spirit. Villages cluster around ancestral halls and rice terraces. Some villages have a single sect cultivator elder who came home decades ago and now teaches the children to read.\n\n**Sect halls.** Each sect has its own architecture, suited to its element.\n- **Yan Sect** — carved INTO the Dragon's Spine. Halls opened from living stone. Time suspended; summer and winter exist simultaneously. The body of the sleeping god is the building.\n- **Yun Sect** — perpetual spring. Cherry blossoms always in bloom. Pavilions over still water that glows at night. Bridges of stone and carved wood.\n- **Wen Sect** — above the clouds. Golden-white open-walled architecture. Cranes on the eaves. Wooden platforms threaded between peaks. Music drifting.\n- **Fen Sect** — built on rivers of lava that flow like burning blood. Cherry blossoms grow from ash. Forges and training grounds. Five halls for the five combat disciplines.\n- **Hun Sect** — LOW among the people. Forest villages, humble wood, thatched roofs, quiet valleys of mist. Does not tower.\n- **Shi Sect** — in the Dark Range. Blood-red maples. Winter never leaves. Black stone, bronze fittings, silver veins.\n\n═══ FOOD ═══\n\n**Common rhythms.** Rice is the staple across the central valley, with wheat and millet in the colder northern provinces near Àn Shān. Steamed buns, dumplings, noodles, pickled vegetables. Clear soups with seasonal greens. Fish from the rivers and coastal ponds. Tea served at every hour of the day.\n\n**Noble cuisine.** Banquets of layered courses — bronze and celadon vessels, jade chopsticks, long-stemmed wine cups. Preserved game, seasonal fruits, candied lotus, osmanthus-flavoured sweets. Rice wine (米酒 mǐjiǔ) warmed and served in small cups; clear spirits for older guests.\n\n**Imperial cuisine.** The most elaborate preparations, dozens of small dishes at a single setting, each with its ceremonial meaning. Ginger, orchid petal (where the Emperor permits), five-spice, sesame, fermented bean, sweet osmanthus, salt-preserved plum.\n\n**Cultivator diets.** Each sect has its own. Yan cultivators eat what the mountain gives — fern heads, mushrooms, wild grains, river fish. Yun cultivators eat what the water gives — lotus root, water-chestnut, river shrimp, the same. Wen cultivators eat what the sky gives — grains blessed by wind, fruit from the high orchards. Fen cultivators eat heavily — meat, grain, strong tea, the forge demands calories. Hun cultivators eat what the village shares — rice, dumplings, simple vegetables, tea. Shi cultivators eat what the Dark Range grudgingly offers — sparser, harder, the discipline is visible in the meal.\n\n**Tea.** Central to every level of Aoli life. The merchant's plain leaf steeped in boiling water by a roadside stall. The noblewoman's pressed jade-cake tea whisked into green foam in a celadon bowl. The Emperor's Six Ancient Teas, each served in its own particular ritual. Cultivators drink tea grown on their sect's mountain.\n\n═══ CUSTOMS ═══\n\n**The two pillars.** Every ordinary Aoli citizen knows the empire rests on two mandates — the Emperor's, under Heaven; and the sects', under the sleeping and exiled gods. A family with a child in a sect speaks of that child with a certain respectful distance. A family that serves the court directly speaks of the sects with the same. The two pillars are not rivals. They are the two sides of the sky.\n\n**Ancestral reverence.** Every household keeps an ancestral shrine — incense, ancestor tablets, small offerings at the new moon and the festival days. The dead are not gone; they are witnesses. Chén Yuán's orchid courtyard is an imperial-scale version of this practice — a son tending a dead brother's favourite flower is a son keeping watch.\n\n**The Mandate of Heaven.** The doctrine that the Chén dynasty rules because Heaven gave them the mandate, and that the mandate can be withdrawn if the dynasty becomes unjust. Every common Aoli citizen knows this. It is why the Emperor's personal conduct matters at the level of weather — a cruel Emperor's Mandate weakens; crops fail, borders fray. A just Emperor's Mandate holds; the realm thrives. Chén Yuán's quiet principled monogamy is not only personal — it is Mandate-strengthening.\n\n**Gender balance.** Because Aoli's cosmology centres a married pair of sleeping gods (Yánlóng and Yúnshēng), women hold offices at court, lead sects (Wen is all-female), and are read as the co-equal half of every sacred structure. This is not modern feminism dressed in silk — it is the oldest law of the Mandate. A woman Minister of Music (Princess Lán) is not a novelty. A girl Junior General at eighteen (Xià Ruòlán) is not a transgression. A young woman investigator (Lǔ Méi) who refused the general's seat her family offered is unusual but not scandalous.\n\n═══ FESTIVALS ═══\n\n**New Year (Lunar New Year).** The largest festival. Families gather. Ancestral offerings. Red lanterns, firecrackers, feasting, new robes, children given small red envelopes. The Emperor offers at the Ancestral Altar at dawn. The sects mark the turning with their own quieter rites.\n\n**Lantern Festival.** The first full moon after New Year. Lanterns released on water and into the sky. Lovers walk on the lake bridges. Riddles painted on the lantern sides. In Zǐchén the Emperor's formal Lantern Procession crosses three ceremonial courtyards.\n\n**Qingming (Tomb-Sweeping Festival).** The dead are visited. Offerings at the ancestral tomb. The dowager empresses who keep vigil at imperial tombs receive their most attention at this festival. The previous Empress — Míng's mother — has kept this vigil for over twenty years.\n\n**Dragon Boat Festival.** Mid-summer. Boat races on the rivers. Zongzi (rice dumplings in leaves) eaten in remembrance of ancient poet-statesmen. Yun Sect cultivators are said to race invisible boats on the sacred river.\n\n**Mid-Autumn / Moon Festival.** Family reunions under the full moon. Moon-cakes. Osmanthus wine. The Emperor offers at the Moon Altar. Lovers separated by distance think of each other under the same sky. Lí Wǎn's prayer beads pass through her fingers as she sits in the courtyard looking up.\n\n**Winter Solstice.** The shortest day. Dumplings (jiǎozi) eaten to mark the turning of the year. The Emperor offers at the Heaven Altar to renew the Mandate for the coming year. Sect cultivators sit in long night-meditations.\n\n**Local festivals.** Every province has its own — a harvest festival in the terraced valleys, a fire-ritual in the Àn Shān foothills, a quiet river offering near Hun Sect's villages.\n\n═══ DRESS ═══\n\n**Common dress.** Plain silk or hemp robes in natural colours — indigo, russet, grey, moss, dust-rose. Sashes at the waist. Hair bound up in simple knots. Straw or bamboo hats in the fields. Embroidery around collars and sleeves for weddings and festivals.\n\n**Noble dress.** Layered silk robes, often three or four layers, with embroidered ceremonial panels. Family insignia on collar or hem. Jade belt-pendants. Elaborate hair ornaments — pins, combs, crowns of silver or gold filigree. Different robes for different functions — court formal, travel, mourning, festival, private.\n\n**Imperial dress.** The most elaborate. The Emperor's ceremonial robes carry the dragon; the Empress's carry the phoenix; the Crown Prince's carry the coiled young dragon (蟠龙 pánlóng); the princesses carry their own symbols. Colour is policed — imperial yellow for the Emperor's highest formal wear; Chén Yuán refuses yellow and wears deep black and umber, a quiet repudiation of his cruel father. See `aoli-emperor` and `aoli-crownprince` for specific wardrobes.\n\n**Cultivator dress.** Each sect has its colours.\n- Yan — earthy browns, stone greys, white for healers, dark for recorders.\n- Yun — deep blues, water-jade, sea-foam, pearl-white.\n- Wen — white and gold, crane-white with feather accents, music-coded embroidery.\n- Fen — reds, ember-oranges, charcoal-black, phoenix-gold accents.\n- Hun — mist-grey, muted greens, soft whites, the colours of a forest in fog.\n- Shi — blood-red, black, silver veins, maple-leaf red. The visible warning.\n\n═══ DAILY RHYTHM ═══\n\n**Dawn.** The sky lightens. The ancestral shrine is offered morning incense. The Emperor rises before his namesakes (the Chén celestial bodies) and greets them as kin. Cultivators begin their morning forms. Farmers walk to the fields. The city opens its gates. Tea is made.\n\n**Morning court.** The Emperor holds morning audience with his ministers. Memorials are read. Edicts are drafted. The Empress begins her day of inner-palace administration and the quiet strategy work she is known for.\n\n**Midday.** Lunch is light in the central valley, heavier in the cold north. Cultivators break for a single shared meal in the sect dining hall. Farmers eat in the fields. Merchants at their stalls. The palace gardens are quiet; the Emperor may kneel alone in the orchid courtyard.\n\n**Afternoon.** Work resumes. Scholars study in the Hanlin Academy. Craftspeople work at their benches. Cultivators practice specific arts — music in Wen's open pavilions, formwork in Fen's training grounds, veil-reading in Hun's dim halls.\n\n**Evening.** Families gather. The evening meal is the larger one. Ancestral shrines are offered evening incense. The Emperor and Empress dine — sometimes together, sometimes separately, always with the silences of their shared secrets between them. Children do their reading by lamplight. Cultivators sit in evening meditation.\n\n**Night.** The city lanterns are lit. The sects hold their night vigils. Lí Wǎn reads her strategy scrolls (and, after, her romance novels) by the single lamp in her study. The Emperor passes her doorway and does not interrupt. Jǐng reads Lìyǐn's letters by the light of his own lamp. Wǔ walks the palace walls. Qīng, far away in Hun Sect's mist, walks dreams.\n\n═══ SECT LIFE VS VILLAGE LIFE ═══\n\n**Village life** is the older rhythm. Sun-up, sun-down. Sowing, growing, harvesting. The seasons are the clock. A village child knows its grandparents, its neighbours, the shape of the valley, the songs of the local festival, the particular slope of the local ancestral mound. A village child who shows affinity is taken to the sect — and the village watches the child leave, with a complicated mixture of pride and loss. *\"They could not keep her.\"*\n\n**Sect life** is the slower rhythm. Years inside the mountain or the mist or the cherry-blossom pavilion. The seasons pass differently — Yan's suspended summer-and-winter, Yun's perpetual spring, Wen's cloud-weather, Fen's year-round ash, Hun's fog. Cultivators age differently than villagers. A cultivator who returns home at fifty may be unrecognisable to her family — still young in the face, but with something in her eyes that her mother cannot name.\n\n**The old cultivator who came home.** Some cultivators, at the end of mastery, return to their native villages to live quietly — teaching the children, tending a small courtyard, marking the seasons with the old rites. They are respected but also a little lonely; the village they remember is not the village they return to. The cultivators who choose this are often the healers (Yan and Yun) and the spirit-walkers (Hun). Those who stay in the sects until death are more common among Wen, Fen, and Shi.\n\n**Imperial children at the sect schools.** Princess Lán studied music with Wen Sect. Chén Qīng entered Hun Sect and did not come home. Other noble children are sent to sects for specific arts — calligraphy to a Yan sub-school, swordwork to a Fen training camp, healing to a Yun clinic. The sect schools do not accept children on rank alone — affinity must test through. But a noble child with a genuine affinity is welcomed and often remains for years.\n\n═══ MARRIAGE, BIRTH, DEATH ═══\n\n**Marriage.** Two ceremonies bound together — the civil and the ancestral. The bride is escorted to the groom's family in a red sedan chair. Offerings are made at both families' ancestral halls. The groom's mother pours wine for the bride. The new couple kneels to Heaven, to the ancestors, to the parents, and then to each other. Among noble families the match is usually political; among commoners, more often a matter of affection and land.\n\n**Concubinage.** Permitted across classes; not always practiced. Chén Yuán is monogamous in a culture that does not require it, and his choice has quietly shifted the cultural conversation. Jǐng's planned three-wife trinity (one Crown Princess, two consorts) is within tradition but remains a departure from his father's example. The common people rarely have concubines; the cost is beyond them.\n\n**Birth.** Midwives attend, often the village healer. Ancestors are invoked. The child is named at a small ceremony a hundred days after birth. Imperial children are named more ceremoniously — the Emperor consults the astrologers and the court poets. Chén Yuán named his daughter Chén Lán in a formal naming rite, and no one but him knew why the orchid.\n\n**Death.** Mourning is long. White robes for forty-nine days for close kin. The body is offered at the ancestral altar, then entombed — commoners in shared family tombs, nobles in larger tomb complexes, emperors in the imperial tomb. The previous Empress, Míng's mother, keeps vigil at the imperial tomb still. Her grief is unspoken canon.\n\n═══ OPEN CANON ═══\n\n- Specific regional dialects and accents across Aoli's provinces.\n- The specific sect uniforms and their ceremonial variations across ranks.\n- The formal calendar — which festivals the Emperor observes vs which the sects observe vs which the commoners observe.\n- The texture of markets in Zǐchén — what can be bought, who buys it, what is forbidden.\n- The rhythm of the Capital Security Office where Méi's investigation will unfold.\n- The specific fashions of the eight Selection candidates that will distinguish them visually at the entry banquet.
