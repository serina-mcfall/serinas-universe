## Purpose

**Serina's Vision:**
*"Think The Untamed, Ashes of Love, Legend of Fuyao. This country is based on Chinese XianWuxia / Xianxia Dramas."*

**Aoli — The Breathing Land** — is the kingdom in Serina's Avalúne where magic is cultivation and the land itself is alive. Bordered to the north by the Dragon's Spine (the sleeping body of Yánlóng, the Rock Dragon) and to the south by the Dark Range (where the mother-goddess Yuèlí's corruption lies heaviest), Aoli is a realm of pillar forests, river valleys, terraced farmland, and mist-wrapped peaks where trees remember, mountains protect, and wind whispers old magic.

At the heart of Aoli lies a love story no human fully understands: the primordial father-god Mùjīn wrapped himself around his corrupted wife Yuèlí and said *nothing touches her while I breathe* — his sleeping body became the northern mountain, her essence the southern shadow. The whole country is built on the bones of a cosmic protection.

Its cultural DNA is xianxia/xianwuxia — Chinese cultivation fantasy as seen in the dramas above: flying swords, immortal masters and their disciples, sect politics, cosmic romance, ethereal robes, centuries-long rivalries, the conviction that a cultivator does not *wield* magic — they *become* it. Real-world foundations: **Tang and Song dynasties** at their cultural height, with Han and Zhou as older roots. Daoism, Chan Buddhism, and Confucianism braid together into the spiritual and scholarly DNA. The Silk Road, the imperial examination system (科舉), the scholar-gentry (士大夫), and the Four Arts of the scholar — guqin, go, calligraphy, painting (琴棋書畫) — all live here.

**Six sects organize Aoli life**, each bound to an element and a sleeping or exiled god: **Yan** (earth, Yánlóng), **Yun** (water, Yúnshēng), **Wen** (wind, Wénji the nine-tailed fox, all-female), **Fen** (fire, Fèngzhé the phoenix, all-male brotherhood), **Hun** (spirit, Mínhuì), **Shi** (chaos/corruption). Real cultural power lives in the sects — their masters are centuries old, their disciples are the heroes and villains of every story. Cultivation is lived practice, measured in canonical xianxia stages.

**What matters most to Serina for Aoli:** the xianxia drama aesthetic, the slow patient time of immortals, the master-disciple bond, the sacred geography of sleeping gods holding a cosmic love story, the sect rivalries and cosmic romances of *The Untamed* / *Ashes of Love* / *Legend of Fuyao*, and the deep conviction that every mountain and river in Aoli *remembers*.

## 1. THE LAND AND GEOGRAPHY

Historical China occupies one of the most varied landscapes on Earth — a territory bracketed by the Gobi and Taklamakan deserts to the north and west, the Himalayan wall to the southwest, the Pacific coast to the east, and the tropical jungles of the far south. Between these edges lies the heartland: the **North China Plain** of wheat, millet and loess; the **Yangtze basin** of rice paddies, wet monsoon heat and terraced hills; the **Yellow River** (Huang He, "China's sorrow") whose silt-laden floods could drown a million people in a bad year and rebuild the world in a good one; and, threading west out of Chang'an, the **Hexi Corridor** — that 1,000-kilometre ribbon of oasis towns between the Qilian Mountains and the Gobi that became the Silk Road's Chinese gateway.

Tang and Song China's climate ran cooler and wetter than today in the medieval warm period. The capital region around **Chang'an** (modern Xi'an) and **Luoyang** had four hard seasons — frost in winter, yellow dust storms in spring, humid summers with peach and plum blossom giving way to pomegranate and lotus, and crisp autumns of chrysanthemum and maple. In the south around Song **Kaifeng** and later **Lin'an** (Hangzhou), winters were mild, summers sweltering, and the West Lake fogs of dawn became a subject of ten thousand poems.

**Flora and fauna** layered sacred meaning onto biology. The bamboo grove, the plum branch in snow, the orchid in the wilderness, the chrysanthemum at the fence — these four plants became the **"Four Gentlemen" (四君子)** of scholar-painting, each standing for a virtue. Peonies meant prosperity, lotus meant purity rising from mud, willow meant parting. Among animals: the **dragon** (long 龍) was the emperor himself and lord of waters; the **phoenix** (fenghuang 鳳凰) was the empress and lord of fire-birds; the **qilin** was the unicorn of auspice; the **tortoise** was longevity; the **crane** carried immortals to heaven; the **tiger** ruled the western mountains; the **nine-tailed fox** (jiuweihu 九尾狐) could be a benevolent omen of a stable realm, or, after the Tang, an erotic demoness. Pandas were not yet symbolic; deer and macaques were. Swallows under the eaves meant spring. The first cicada of summer meant a poet was expected to write.

**Sacred geography** structured the empire into a cosmic diagram. The **Five Sacred Mountains (Wuyue 五岳)** — Taishan in the east, Huashan in the west, Hengshan in the south, Hengshan (a different one) in the north, Songshan in the centre — corresponded to the Five Phases and the five directions. **Taishan** was supreme: emperors climbed it to perform the **fengshan sacrifice**, announcing to Heaven and Earth that their dynasty held the Mandate. The **Wudang Mountains** in Hubei became the Daoist heartland, cradle of internal martial arts and of the cult of Zhenwu, the Perfected Warrior of the North. The **Sacred Buddhist Mountains** — Wutai, Emei, Putuo, Jiuhua — became pilgrimage sites where bodhisattvas were said to manifest. Hermits and cultivators had withdrawn to peaks since the Warring States; by the Tang, a scholar's failed career could always be reframed as **"retiring to the mountains"** (隱逸) — a face-saving move that produced some of the tradition's greatest poetry (Wang Wei at Wang River, Han Shan at Cold Mountain).

Rivers were living beings. The Yellow River had its own Dragon King; so did the Yangtze, and every lesser tributary. Bridges were blessed, wells had protective spirits, and a provincial magistrate who neglected to pray at the local water shrine could expect the people to blame him for the next flood. The cosmology Aoli inherits from this is simple and non-negotiable: **the land is conscious, the land remembers, and every territory has a resident god who must be acknowledged before you cross it.**

## 2. SOCIAL STRUCTURE

The Tang and Song ordered society according to the **Four Occupations (si min 四民)** — a Confucian schema inherited from the Zhou: **scholars (shi 士), farmers (nong 農), artisans (gong 工), merchants (shang 商)** — in that moral rank. Scholars stood highest because they cultivated virtue and served the state. Farmers were second because they fed the realm. Artisans made useful things. Merchants, though often wealthier than anyone else, were morally suspect — they moved goods without producing them — and were periodically barred from taking the imperial examinations or wearing silk.

Above the four occupations sat the **imperial clan (huangshi 皇室)** and, historically, the **hereditary aristocracy (shizu 世族)** — the great clans of the Six Dynasties who had dominated the early Tang court through marriage alliances and control of office. Empress Wu Zetian deliberately broke their grip by promoting examination candidates from lesser families, and by the Song the old aristocracy was effectively gone. In its place rose the **scholar-gentry (shi dafu 士大夫)** — men who had passed the imperial exams and their extended kin. This class became the defining elite of late-imperial China: landowners, officials, poets, art patrons, local magistrates, Confucian moralists, and the people who wrote history. Their ideal combined **wen** (civil, literary cultivation) and **wu** (martial discipline), though wen always outranked wu.

Below the farmers stood the **"mean people" (jianmin 賤民)** — the legal underclass whose testimony could not convict a commoner and whose murder carried a lesser penalty. Jianmin included **government slaves (guannu 官奴)** inherited through family disgrace, **private slaves (sinu 私奴)** owned by great houses, **musicians and actors (yueren 樂人)** who were legally bound to entertainer registers, **prostitutes (changji 娼妓)** of the government-run pleasure quarters, **butchers and tanners** whose trade polluted, and **boatmen of certain rivers**. The Tang Code (*Tanglü shuyi*) states baldly: *"Slaves and inferior people are legally equivalent to livestock products."* Children born to jianmin inherited the status. The only escape was manumission — rare — or a general amnesty at the accession of a new emperor.

Outside the four-occupation system altogether were the **Buddhist and Daoist clergy (sengni, daoshi 僧尼、道士)**. A monk or nun renounced ordinary social rank, tax obligations, and corvée labour in exchange for state-licensed ordination. This was so attractive that the Tang state periodically purged monasteries to reclaim the tax base — most famously in the **Huichang persecution of 845**, when Emperor Wuzong forcibly laicised around 260,000 monks and nuns and seized their land.

**Foreigners (huren 胡人)** occupied a legally liminal zone. Chang'an in the eighth century held perhaps 25,000 resident foreigners — Sogdians, Persians, Uighurs, Arabs, Tokharians, Koreans, Japanese, Indians, Tibetans, Malays and the sub-Saharan Africans called **Kunlun slaves (崑崙奴)**, who served as doormen and musicians in wealthy households. Sogdian merchants ran the foreign quarters of the Western Market. Nestorian Christian priests kept a monastery. Persian dancers entertained at banquets. A foreigner could rise very high — the rebel general **An Lushan** was half-Sogdian, half-Turkic — but "foreign" (hu) remained a shadow category.

**Women's status** varied sharply by dynasty. Tang women of the aristocracy rode horses, played polo, hunted with falcons, divorced their husbands by mutual agreement, inherited property and — in Empress Wu Zetian — ruled the empire in their own name. Tang tomb figurines show plump-cheeked ladies in low-cut gowns, entirely confident. By contrast, Song women saw their freedoms tighten: foot-binding began among late-Tang entertainers and spread through the Song gentry; widow chastity became a Confucian ideal; property rights narrowed. The trajectory is important — **Tang women had air; Song women had silk thread pulling tighter every generation.**

**Mobility.** The examination system was the great social elevator. In theory, any free man could sit the exams; in practice, the cost of a classical education ruled out most peasants. But a village that produced a *jinshi* (highest-degree holder) raised its entire lineage. Mobility downward happened through **family disgrace** — a treason conviction could demote whole clans into slavery under the Ten Abominations (see §19). Aoli's sect system slots neatly into this template: cultivation is the new examination system, and a peasant's child who is taken to the mountain by a master has been lifted into a different class of being entirely.

## 3. GOVERNMENT AND POLITICS

The Chinese empire was a **centralised bureaucratic monarchy** legitimised by the **Mandate of Heaven (tianming 天命)**. The emperor — **tianzi, "Son of Heaven"** — was not a god but a ritual conduit between Heaven, Earth and his people. If he ruled virtuously, harvests were good, rebellions failed, comets stayed in their courses. If he ruled badly, Heaven warned him with floods, earthquakes, white rainbows at noon, and finally withdrew the Mandate and transferred it to a successor. Every dynasty's official history was written by the next dynasty, so every dynasty ended with evidence of moral collapse. **This is the single most important political doctrine to carry into Aoli: sovereignty is conditional, observed by Heaven, and measurable by weather.**

**The Tang imperial system** inherited the Sui framework and perfected it. At the top sat the emperor; directly beneath him, the **Three Departments and Six Ministries (sansheng liubu 三省六部)** — the Department of State Affairs (Shangshusheng), the Chancellery (Menxiasheng), and the Secretariat (Zhongshusheng). The Six Ministries — Personnel, Revenue, Rites, War, Justice, and Public Works — ran the day-to-day administration. A parallel **Censorate (Yushitai 御史臺)** could impeach any official, including ministers and princes, and its remonstrators (jianguan) could speak bluntly to the emperor on points of conscience. The **Hanlin Academy**, founded in the Tang, housed the court's top scholars as policy advisers, drafters of edicts, and tutors to the crown prince.

**Recruitment by examination (keju 科舉)** was the Tang's most enduring invention and the Song's obsession. Candidates sat multi-day exams in sealed cells, writing essays on the Confucian Classics, composing poetry in regulated verse, and solving cases in law and policy. The Song expanded the system enormously — from under 30,000 candidates early in the dynasty to around 400,000 by its end — and introduced procedures like **anonymous grading** (pasting paper over the candidate's name) and **re-copying** (a scribe recopied every paper so examiners could not recognise handwriting). Passing the highest level — the **jinshi 進士** degree — made a young man (always a man) an instant gentleman, eligible for office, and a prime candidate for a wealthy father-in-law. The top three of each cohort — **zhuangyuan, bangyan, tanhua** — rode through Chang'an or Kaifeng wearing red-silk sashes while the city applauded.

**Laws and justice.** The **Tang Code (Tanglü shuyi, 653 CE)** was the masterpiece of imperial Chinese law, copied by later dynasties and by Japan, Korea and Vietnam. It ranked crimes by their relationship to the Confucian moral order, starting with the **Ten Abominations (shi'e 十惡)** — plotting rebellion, plotting great sedition, plotting treason, contumacy, depravity, great irreverence, lack of filial piety, discord, unrighteousness, and incest — all punished severely, often with death and often extended to the criminal's kin under the **"nine familial exterminations (zhu jiuzu 誅九族)"**. Lesser crimes received the **Five Punishments**: flogging with a light stick, flogging with a heavy stick, penal labour, exile, and death (by strangulation, which preserved the body, or decapitation, which did not). Confessions were central; torture was legal and regulated.

**Taxation.** The early Tang ran the **juntian "equal-field" system** — the state allocated land to each adult male in exchange for grain, cloth and labour service. As population grew and great estates absorbed small holdings, juntian broke down. By the late Tang (780 CE) it was replaced with the **Two-Tax System (liangshui fa)** — a summer tax in grain, an autumn tax in cash, assessed on actual landholding rather than on persons. The Song went further, commercialising tax collection and pioneering paper currency (jiaozi 交子, the world's first banknotes, Sichuan, c. 1020).

**Military command** (detailed in §12) rested theoretically with the emperor, was delegated to the **Ministry of War** and to **regional military commissioners (jiedushi 節度使)**. The rise of the jiedushi caused the Tang's collapse: Sogdian-Turkic general **An Lushan**, commanding three frontier armies at once, rebelled in 755 and nearly destroyed the dynasty. The Song reacted by keeping its best soldiers under central command and promoting generals who were also scholars — a tendency that left Song armies well-lettered and badly outmatched by Khitan and Jurchen cavalry.

**Corruption and intrigue** were constant. Eunuch factions in the Tang assassinated emperors; Empress Wu ran a network of secret police and informers; the Song saw the bitter **New Policies reform** of Wang Anshi (1069–85) splitting the court into factions that hunted each other for decades. Women of the harem, tutors of the crown prince, retired senior statesmen, Daoist magicians at court — all were political actors. The stories that survive from this world (palace intrigue dramas, the *Zhenhuan* universe, *Empresses in the Palace*, *Ashes of Love*) are not dramatic exaggeration; they are recognisable history with the names changed.

## 4. RELIGION AND SPIRITUALITY

Tang and Song religion is best understood not as three separate systems but as the **Three Teachings (sanjiao 三教)** braided together: **Confucianism, Daoism, and Buddhism.** A gentleman could be Confucian at court, Daoist on retreat, and Buddhist on his deathbed without contradiction. Ordinary people worshipped locally and freely — an ancestor tablet, the kitchen god, the city god, Guanyin, the Jade Emperor, the local dragon of the river — stacked in one household shrine.

**Confucianism (Rujia 儒家)** is often miscategorised as a religion; it is better understood as a moral cosmology. The **Five Classics** (Book of Changes / *Yijing*, Book of Documents, Book of Odes, Book of Rites, Spring and Autumn Annals) and later the **Four Books** (Analects, Mencius, Great Learning, Doctrine of the Mean) gave Tang and Song scholars their moral vocabulary. Confucianism teaches the **Five Relationships (wulun 五倫)** — ruler/subject, father/son, husband/wife, elder/younger brother, friend/friend — and the virtues of **ren (benevolence), yi (righteousness), li (ritual propriety), zhi (wisdom), xin (trustworthiness)**. The Song saw a great revival — **Neo-Confucianism (Lixue 理學)** — under **Zhu Xi (1130–1200)**, who synthesised Buddhist metaphysics with Confucian ethics into the doctrine that would dominate East Asia for 700 years.

**Daoism (Daojia/Daojiao 道家/道教)** runs two rivers. **Philosophical Daoism** draws on the *Daodejing* (attributed to Laozi, c. 6th–4th century BCE) and the *Zhuangzi*: the Dao is the nameless way of things; *wu wei* (non-action, effortless action) is the sage's mode; water defeats stone by yielding. **Religious Daoism** is a vast institutional faith with its own gods, temples, priesthoods, scriptures and sacraments. The **Three Pure Ones (Sanqing 三清)** sit at the top of the pantheon; beneath them, the **Jade Emperor (Yuhuang 玉皇)** runs a celestial bureaucracy that mirrors the earthly one (this is literal — gods have ranks, offices, paperwork, and can be petitioned with formal memorials). Daoism's deepest contribution to Aoli is **internal alchemy (neidan 內丹)** — the art of refining the body into an immortal through meditation, breath control, visualisation, diet and sexual yoga. The **Three Treasures — jing (essence), qi (breath), shen (spirit)** — are cultivated in sequence: refine jing into qi, qi into shen, shen into the Dao. Neidan emerged clearly in the 8th century and flourished in the Song through the Southern and Northern Lineages (Nanzong, Beizong). **This is the direct ancestor of xianxia cultivation** — the real-world tradition from which every flying-sword novel draws its vocabulary.

**Buddhism** entered China via the Silk Road in the Han and reached full flower in the Tang. By 845 there were over 4,600 monasteries and 40,000 shrines in the empire, holding a tenth of the arable land. Major schools included **Tiantai** (scholastic, Lotus-centred), **Huayan** (cosmic-interpenetration), **Pure Land (Jingtu 淨土)** — devotional faith in Amitabha Buddha's Western Paradise — and the school that would outlast them all, **Chan (Zen 禪)**. Chan's legendary lineage runs from Bodhidharma in the 5th century through five patriarchs to the illiterate woodcutter **Huineng (638–713)**, the Sixth Patriarch, whose **Platform Sutra** taught that enlightenment was sudden, available to anyone, independent of scripture or ritual. From Huineng the "Five Houses" of Chan spread across China and, later, into Japan as Zen. The Huichang persecution (845) devastated Buddhism but Chan survived best because its monasteries were rural, self-sufficient (monks farmed), and uninterested in scripture-hoarding.

**Priests, clergy, and sacred specialists.** Daoist priests (**daoshi 道士**) wore cloud-patterned robes, kept long uncut hair in topknots secured with wooden pins, and conducted **jiao** rituals to renew the community's cosmic balance. Buddhist monks (**heshang 和尚**) shaved their heads, wore ochre and grey, ate vegetarian, and chanted in hybrid Sino-Sanskrit. At village level, there were also **wu 巫** (spirit mediums, often women), **fengshui masters**, **exorcists**, and **nine-layer ritualists** who could speak with foxes, eels and mountain gods. In Aoli's sects, a master (shifu 師父) is a hybrid of all of these — philosopher, priest, martial teacher, parent and cosmic anchor.

**Afterlife.** Popular belief held that the soul split at death: the **hun 魂** ascended toward Heaven; the **po 魄** stayed with the body and settled into the grave. The dead travelled through a **Ten Courts of Hell (shidian yan wang 十殿閻王)**, were judged by King Yama, punished according to their sins, and eventually reborn — unless the family's offerings, ritual burning of paper money, and Buddhist masses bought them a better passage. **Ghosts (gui 鬼)** were the unquiet dead: those without descendants to feed them, those who died violently, those who had scores unsettled. The **Hungry Ghost Festival** in the seventh lunar month fed them all.

## 5. FAMILY AND KINSHIP

The Chinese family was the **foundational social unit** — more fundamental than the individual, the village, or even (at times) the state. A Confucian slogan summed it up: *"The self cultivated, the family regulated, the state ordered, the realm at peace"* (*xiushen, qijia, zhiguo, ping tianxia* 修身齊家治國平天下). Families were **patrilineal, patrilocal, and patriarchal** — you took your father's surname, the bride moved into the groom's household, and the eldest living male of the senior generation held authority.

**The household (jia 家)** in its ideal form was the **five-generations-under-one-roof** compound: great-grandparents, grandparents, parents, sons and daughters-in-law, and grandchildren sharing kitchens, courtyards and ancestral hall. In practice, most peasant households were three generations at most; great-gentry households could shelter several hundred relatives, servants and tenants. Every compound had an **ancestral altar** with spirit tablets of the dead; the senior male led morning and evening bows and seasonal offerings.

**Filial piety (xiao 孝)** was the cardinal virtue. The *Classic of Filial Piety* taught that loyalty to the emperor, discipline in one's craft, and peace in the household all flowed from this single root. Extreme stories — a son letting mosquitoes bite him so they would not bite his parents; a daughter-in-law slicing flesh from her own thigh to feed sick in-laws — were collected in the **Twenty-Four Paragons of Filial Piety** and recited as moral tutorials. Failing one's parents was the seventh of the Ten Abominations. Mourning for a father lasted **three years (actually twenty-seven months)** and required officials to resign from office and return home — a rule rigidly observed under the Song.

**Gender roles** were structured by the **"Three Obediences and Four Virtues" (sancong side 三從四德)**: a woman obeys her father before marriage, her husband in marriage, and her son in widowhood; and cultivates virtue, proper speech, proper appearance, and proper work. The rigid version of this doctrine is Song Neo-Confucian; **Tang women lived far more freely** — Tang tomb murals show elite ladies playing polo, hunting, and wearing men's riding kit for sport. Aristocratic women owned property, divorced by mutual agreement, and remarried without stigma. Empress Wu Zetian took the throne in her own name (r. 690–705); her daughter-in-law Empress Wei plotted (unsuccessfully) to follow her; Princess Taiping was a power broker for decades. The Song retrenchment — foot binding, cult of widow chastity, restrictions on inheritance — was real but uneven, and gentry women continued to read, write poetry and manage estates. **Li Qingzhao (1084–c.1155)**, the greatest *ci*-poet of the Song, is the direct proof.

**Marriage** was a contract between families, negotiated by a matchmaker (**meipo 媒婆**) and sealed by the **Six Rites (liuli 六禮)** — proposal, name request, divination of compatibility, betrothal gifts, setting the date, wedding procession. Bride-price (**caili 彩禮**) flowed from groom's family to bride's; dowry (**jiazhuang 嫁妝**) flowed with the bride into her new home and remained formally hers. Polygamy was legal — a man could take one primary **wife (qi 妻)** and any number of **concubines (qie 妾)** — but the wife's status and her sons' inheritance rights were protected in law. Her mother-in-law, however, was usually her real ruler.

**Child-rearing** centred on the early formation of moral habits. Infants were believed to be born with a pure heart that had to be guarded from disharmony. Boys of gentry families began formal study at six or seven with the **"Three Character Classic" (Sanzijing 三字經)** — a rhymed primer that taught characters and virtues in one stroke. Girls were taught needlework, the **"Four Virtues"**, household management, and — in scholar families — poetry, painting, calligraphy and the guqin. Both sexes began serious classical study around age ten if the family could afford it.

**Ancestor veneration** was the spine of family religion. On the first and fifteenth of every lunar month, at Qingming, at Zhongyuan, at New Year, and on the death anniversaries of each named ancestor, offerings were made — incense, rice wine, fruit, specific dishes the deceased had loved. Burning paper money, paper clothes, and paper houses sent them wealth in the other world. Neglecting the ancestors invited their hunger, and hungry ancestors made children sick.

**Extended kin and fosterage.** The **zongzu 宗族** (patrilineal clan) could number thousands and shared an ancestral hall, clan school, clan cemetery, and sometimes clan farmland. Uncles raised orphaned nephews; cousins lived as brothers. **Adoption** of a nephew to continue an heirless line was common and legal — continuity of the sacrificial ritual was more important than genetic continuity. In Aoli, sects function as artificial clans — **"master-disciple is father-son, senior-junior is elder-younger brother"** — and Serina's Fen Sect orphans raising each other as brothers directly echoes this real pattern.

## 6. LIFE CYCLE RITUALS

**Birth and naming.** A Chinese baby was traditionally considered one year old at birth (counting gestation) and gained another year at every lunar New Year — so a child born in the twelfth month could be "two years old" at twenty days. The first month was dangerous; mothers observed **zuo yuezi 坐月子** ("sitting the month") — thirty to forty days of bed rest, avoidance of cold water and cold food, and a diet heavy in warming ginger, sesame-oil chicken, pig's trotters and rice wine. On the **third day (xisan 洗三)**, the infant was bathed ceremonially, protective charms were tied to its wrists, and the women of the family inspected the child for auspicious birthmarks. On the **full-month (manyue 滿月)** day, relatives gathered, gifts were given — silver lockets, jade pendants, red eggs — and the baby's head might be symbolically shaved.

Names were given in multiple layers. A **milk name (xiaoming 小名)** was a humble pet-name — *Ah-Gou* ("little dog"), *Dabao* ("big treasure"), *Xiaoya* ("little duck") — deliberately unglamorous so jealous spirits would not notice the child. A **formal name (ming 名)** was given when the child entered school or reached adulthood; it often included a **generation character** shared with all cousins of that rank, making clan genealogies legible at a glance. A scholar also received a **courtesy name (zi 字)** at his capping ceremony, used by peers. Poets and painters added **style names (hao 號)** of their own invention — Li Bai's *Qinglian Jushi* "Green-Lotus Retired Scholar", Su Shi's *Dongpo Jushi* "East-Slope Retired Scholar". A man could accumulate half a dozen names across a life.

**Coming of age.** For a boy of good family, the **guanli 冠禮** ("capping ceremony") at nineteen or twenty marked entry into adulthood: his hair was bound into a topknot and a formal cap placed upon it, his **zi** was bestowed, he bowed to the ancestors and was presented to the clan elders. For a girl, the **jili 笄禮** ("pinning ceremony") at fifteen (the age she became marriageable) involved her hair being done up for the first time and fixed with a ceremonial hairpin. These rites were aristocratic in the Han, spread through the gentry in the Tang, and became general among Song literati families.

**Marriage ceremony.** The wedding day climaxed the **Six Rites**. The bride, wearing **red robes embroidered with phoenixes** and a red veil, was carried in a red-curtained sedan chair from her parents' house to the groom's. Firecrackers and drums announced her arrival; she stepped over a fire or a saddle at the threshold. Inside, she and the groom bowed three times — to **Heaven and Earth** (*yi bai tiandi*), to the **groom's ancestors** (*er bai gaotang*), and to **each other** (*fuqi duibai*). They drank **cross-cup wine (hejin jiu 合卺酒)** from matching cups — a symbolic binding of their qi. The feast lasted into the night; the next morning the new bride served tea to her mother-in-law, a rite that could make or ruin the marriage. **Three days later**, the couple visited the bride's family (**huimen 回門**) — the one moment she would ever be a guest again in her own home.

**Death, burial and mourning** were the life cycle's most elaborate rituals. The dying were carried into the main hall so they might die at the centre of the family. Sons kept vigil. At the moment of death, white cloths covered mirrors, and a ritualist called the soul on the rooftop: *"Come back, come back!"* Washing, dressing in fine clothes (the **shouyi 壽衣**, often prepared decades earlier), placing a pearl or jade in the mouth, and laying the body in an inner coffin within an outer coffin followed. Mourners wore **undyed hemp** (white, the colour of Chinese mourning), burned paper spirit-money at crossroads, wailed on cue at specific intervals, and — for a parent — observed **three years of mourning** (shedding colour, rich food, music, and sexual activity). The funeral procession paraded mingqi (spirit goods, see §18) and, at elite burials, large retinues of clay figures that would serve the deceased in the tomb. The grave itself was oriented by a **fengshui master** to align with auspicious dragon-veins. Offerings continued forever.

**The ritual year.** The lunar calendar structured every life. Among the many festivals (detailed in §17):

- **Lunar New Year / Spring Festival (Chunjie 春節)** — fifteen days of reunion, red couplets, firecrackers, dumplings (north) or rice cake (south). The most important holiday of the year.
- **Lantern Festival (Yuanxiao 元宵)** — the fifteenth of the first lunar month, the first full moon; children carried paper lanterns, lovers slipped away under their cover. Aoli's wind-lanterns in its Wen Sect scenes draw on this directly.
- **Qingming (清明)** — early April, tomb-sweeping; families brought picnics to the ancestors' graves.
- **Dragon Boat Festival (Duanwu 端午)** — fifth of the fifth, racing boats and eating zongzi to commemorate the drowned poet Qu Yuan.
- **Qixi (七夕)** — seventh of the seventh, the night the Weaver Girl and Cowherd meet across the Milky Way; the Chinese lovers' holiday.
- **Zhongyuan / Ghost Festival (中元)** — fifteenth of the seventh; gates of hell open; food for the hungry dead.
- **Mid-Autumn (Zhongqiu 中秋)** — fifteenth of the eighth; mooncakes, moon-viewing, family reunion.
- **Double Ninth (Chongyang 重陽)** — ninth of the ninth; climb a mountain, drink chrysanthemum wine, honour the elderly.

## 7. CLOTHING AND ADORNMENT

**Hanfu (漢服)** — literally "Han clothing" — is the umbrella term for the layered silk-and-hemp dress of pre-Qing China. Across Tang and Song the basic grammar was constant: **a wrapped cross-collar (jiaoling 交領) closing to the right, long flowing sleeves, an inner robe (zhongyi), an outer robe (yi), a wrap skirt (qun) for women or trousers for men, a sash (dai) at the waist.** The colour, cut and fabric carried legal meaning.

**Tang dynasty dress** was cosmopolitan, bright, and unashamed. Aristocratic women favoured the **ruqun (襦裙)** — a short cross-collar top tucked into a long wrap skirt tied high under the bust (the silhouette recognisable from any Tang drama). By the mid-Tang the skirt had risen almost to the armpits (**qixiong ruqun 齊胸襦裙** — "chest-high wrap") and necklines had dropped dramatically low for summer. Over this went a gauze **pibo 披帛** — a long diaphanous silk scarf draped over the shoulders and caught on the forearms, famously flowing in every Tang tomb mural and every *fēitiān* (飛天, flying celestial) painting of Dunhuang.

Men wore the **yuanling pao 圓領袍** — a round-collar robe reaching the ankles, usually paired with soft leather boots and a **futou 襆頭** (a soft black silk cap with two tails, the ancestor of the stiff winged caps of Ming officials). Riding dress included fitted trousers and a shorter robe; women of Tang aristocracy sometimes rode in men's dress for sport.

**Sumptuary colour law** was strict. **Bright yellow (ming huang 明黃)** and a specific shade of **purple (ziyi 紫衣)** were reserved for the emperor and the highest officials. Tang ranks were colour-coded: **purple for officials of the third rank and above; crimson for the fourth and fifth; green for the sixth and seventh; dark blue or indigo for the eighth and ninth**. A commoner caught wearing purple would be beaten. Whites and pale colours were often read as funerary and avoided at feasts. Reds, azure blues, apricot, malachite green, imperial gold and pine-bark brown filled the acceptable palette.

**Fabrics.** Silk reigned — gauze (**luo 羅**), damask (**qi 綺**), satin (**duan 緞**), figured brocade (**jin 錦**). The Tang perfected the **clamp-resist dyeing** that produced jewel-toned geometric patterns. Commoners wore **ramie, hemp, and cotton (the last only arriving in China from India via Central Asia in the late Tang).** Dyes were indigo, madder, safflower red, gromwell purple, gardenia yellow, and pagoda-bud green.

**Song dynasty dress** sobered. Neo-Confucian aesthetics pushed fashion toward modesty and refinement. Colours muted — the Song loved pale celadon green, ivory, cloud grey, rose dust, ink black, inkwash blue. High waists fell; necklines closed. Women adopted the **beizi 褙子** — a long, narrow, front-opening over-robe with side slits, worn over the inner dress — and added the striking **"buyao" 步搖** hair ornament, whose gold-and-kingfisher-feather pendants swayed ("step-shaking") as the wearer walked. Men of the scholar class wore the plain **shenyi 深衣** — the "deep garment" of classical ideal — for formal ceremonies, and the simple **daopao 道袍** (a long, straight-collar cross-wrap robe) for daily study. *This is the silhouette of every sect master in every xianxia drama Serina watches.*

**Jewellery and adornment.** Jade was paramount — jade hairpins, jade pendants, jade bi-discs at the waist (the rattle of a gentleman's jade pendants was said to regulate his pace). Gold and silver filigree, kingfisher-feather inlay (**diancui 點翠**), coral, amber, pearl, agate. Hair ornaments were women's main art form: phoenix-crowns (**fengguan**) for brides and empresses, tall gold combs, flowers of fresh or pounded silk, strings of pearls, and the **buyao** pendants that made a walking woman look as if she were trailing starlight.

**Hair.** A Chinese adult did not cut hair — the body was a gift from the parents and must be preserved. Men bound hair into topknots secured by a pin or a cap. Women's hairstyles were baroque: the high **Tang tall-knot (gaoji 高髻)** shaped like a flower or a crescent, the Song **twin-ring (shuanghuan)** for young girls, the married woman's disc-bun behind the head. Unmarried girls wore their hair in two side-bunches or a long braid; the pinning ceremony changed everything.

**Cosmetics.** Tang beauty style was dramatic: white lead powder base, circular "peach" blush, **cherry-red lip in a tiny centred shape** (making the mouth look like a small flower), **huadian** (花鈿) forehead paintings of flowers, stars or butterflies in red or gold, eyebrows shaped into a dozen fashionable variants ("moth", "willow-leaf", "distant-mountain"). Song women pulled back from this extravagance toward a paler, more restrained look.

**What clothing said.** A single glance communicated rank, age, marital status, dynasty, season, profession, school of thought (Confucian scholar's plain ink-wash robe versus Daoist priest's cloud-pattern robe versus Buddhist monk's ochre-and-grey kasaya) and — often — the wearer's political faction. In Aoli, sect robes do the same work: each sect has its own colour grammar, and no one mistakes a Yan disciple for a Wen one at thirty paces.

## 8. FOOD AND DRINK

Chinese cuisine reached its first **recognisably modern form in the Tang and Song**. By the prosperous Tang, ordinary people ate **three meals a day** — previously a royal privilege — and urban Kaifeng in the Song offered the world's first restaurant culture: morning markets, night markets, take-out, delivery, famous chefs with named specialities, and regional menus (northern wheat-based, southern rice-based, Sichuanese already fond of pungent spice).

**Staples.** **The north** ate **wheat (mian 麵)** — noodles hand-pulled, pressed, shaved, knife-cut, or rolled into dumpling skins (**jiaozi 餃子** emerged in this period); **baked breads and buns (bing, mantou)**; **millet** porridge; **sorghum** as a fallback. **The south** ate **rice (fan 飯)** — steamed, fried, or cooked into congee (**zhou 粥**); **glutinous rice** for sweets and the zongzi of Dragon Boat Festival. Both north and south ate **soybeans** in every possible form: fresh green soybeans, tofu (invented in the Han, standard by the Tang), soy sauce, fermented black beans, soymilk, doufugan (dried tofu). The four soy foundations — salt, soy sauce, vinegar, rice wine — were the core of the kitchen.

**Meat.** Tang court tastes leaned heavy with **lamb and mutton (yang rou 羊肉)** — a legacy of the dynasty's Central Asian and Turkic aristocracy, who had tipped the Han preference for pork. **Beef was discouraged** by imperial edict because oxen were draft animals; killing one without reason was punishable. **Pork, chicken, duck, goose, pheasant, deer, rabbit, dog (controversial even then), and wild game** rounded out the meat table. Song Kaifeng's sheep slaughterhouses processed enormous volumes daily for imperial banquets. **Fish** reigned in the south — freshwater carp, perch, mandarin fish, shad — and the Song pioneered sophisticated aquaculture. **Dairy products** (yogurt, cheese, butter, the fermented mare's milk called kumis) were enjoyed in the Tang through steppe influence but fell out of Han-Chinese taste during the Song.

**Vegetables.** Chinese cabbage (baicai), bok choy, mustard greens, garlic chives, scallions, leeks, ginger, garlic, radishes, turnips, taro, yams, water chestnut, lotus root, bamboo shoots, seasonal wild greens (fern-tips, toon-leaves, dandelion). **Mushrooms** — shiitake, wood ear, enoki, straw mushroom — were prized. Spinach arrived from Nepal in the Tang. Tomatoes, chillies, potatoes and corn did not arrive until the Ming, so Tang/Song cooking was hot with **ginger, Sichuan peppercorn (huajiao 花椒), mustard, star anise, cassia bark, clove, cumin and black pepper** rather than chilli.

**Fruit.** Peach, plum, pear, apricot, persimmon, pomegranate, grape, mulberry, jujube, longan, lychee. **Lychee** was the Tang empress **Yang Guifei**'s obsession, so much so that relay riders were dispatched from Guangdong to Chang'an — 1,800 kilometres — to deliver them fresh. The political cost of this indulgence became a byword for decadence.

**Tea (cha 茶)** is the Tang's gift to the world. **Lu Yu (733–804)**, the "Sage of Tea", wrote the *Chajing* (*Classic of Tea*, c. 760) — the first treatise on tea anywhere — codifying selection, water, firing, roasting, and the proper utensils. Tang tea was **compressed cake tea, crushed, boiled with salt and sometimes ginger or orange peel** into a soupy drink. The Song refined this into **powdered tea whipped with a bamboo whisk** — the direct ancestor of Japanese matcha. Tea houses became the urban social centre. Monks drank it to stay awake in meditation; scholars drank it while composing poetry; travellers drank it at every post station.

**Alcohol.** Rice wine (**jiu 酒**), grape wine (imported from the Western Regions along the Silk Road), and millet beer. Tang aristocrats drank enormous volumes — Li Bai's poem **"Bring in the Wine" (Jiangjin Jiu)** remains the ur-text of Chinese drinking culture. Banquet games included composing couplets to a prompt (loser drinks), passing a flower around the table (whoever holds it when the drum stops drinks), and archery-for-forfeits. Song wine shops offered hundreds of labels; the *Dreams of Splendour of the Eastern Capital* describes the night markets of Kaifeng humming with taverns into the small hours.

**Hospitality and feast protocol.** Guests were seated by rank — the east seat was honoured in some traditions, the north-facing seat in others. Chopsticks (**kuaizi 筷子**) were already standard; spoons handled soup and rice. Dishes were shared from a common table. **No standing chopsticks in rice** (it looks like incense at a funeral); **no tapping the bowl with chopsticks** (that summoned hungry ghosts); **always pour your elder's cup before your own.** Food as hospitality was inexhaustible: a host's honour demanded more dishes than the guests could possibly eat.

**Food taboos and fasts.** Daoist priests on retreat observed **bigu 辟穀** ("grain avoidance"), abstaining from the Five Grains to purify the body for cultivation. Buddhists kept **vegetarian fast days** at the first, fifteenth and other lunar dates. Mourners avoided meat, wine and sweet food for months. Pregnant women avoided "cold" foods (crab, certain greens); postpartum women avoided "cold" water and fresh fruit. **The yin-yang "hot/cold" classification of foods** underlies TCM dietary practice and still governs how many Chinese grandmothers cook today.

## 9. ARCHITECTURE AND SETTLEMENT

Chinese architecture is a **timber civilization**. Where European builders raised stone to heaven, Chinese builders raised **wooden post-and-beam frameworks** of astonishing sophistication, engineered to sway in earthquakes and to transmit every beam's weight outward through bracket clusters to massive sweeping eaves. The organising principles — **axial symmetry, courtyard enclosure, tiered roofs, colour-coded hierarchy, and fengshui alignment** — ran from the imperial palace down to the peasant's mud-wall cottage.

**Chang'an as master plan.** Tang Chang'an was the largest planned city on Earth until the modern era. Its walled square measured roughly 9.7 km east-to-west by 8.6 km north-to-south, oriented on a strict grid with 14 main streets east-west and 11 north-south, forming **108 walled wards (fang 坊)** with gates that closed at curfew drums and opened at dawn. A **150-metre-wide central avenue** — Zhuque Avenue — ran from the Mingde Gate through the administrative city to the Imperial Palace. The **Eastern and Western Markets** — rectangular mile-square enclosures of shops — ran by appointment-only bells and drums. Every later East Asian capital — Nara, Heian-kyō (Kyoto), Kaesong, Hanoi — copied this template. **The Song capital Kaifeng** broke the ward system and let shops spill into the streets — the world's first commercial city that looked like a modern one.

**Dougong bracketing (斗拱)** is the signature engineering marvel. Between the top of a column and the eave above, a stepped cluster of wooden brackets and blocks — increasingly complex across Tang and Song — distributed roof weight and enabled the deep overhangs and upswept corners that define a Chinese roof. The **Yingzao Fashi (營造法式)**, the building manual compiled by **Li Jie under Song imperial order in 1103**, standardised every element of this system to fourteen grades. No nails; dougong locked together by joinery.

**Roof hierarchy** was strict. **Hip-roofs (wudian 廡殿)** with four slopes were reserved for imperial buildings. **Hip-gable roofs (xieshan 歇山)** came next, used for temples and aristocratic halls. Simple **gable roofs (xuanshan 懸山)** covered commoner houses. Roof tiles — **yellow glazed for imperial, green for royal princes, grey for commoners, blue for the altar of Heaven** — were colour-rank in tile form. Ridge ornaments: the dragon-fish **chiwen 鴟吻** at the roof-ends, and a procession of small mythical beasts along the hip-ridge, their count tied to the building's rank.

**Housing.** The **siheyuan 四合院** — "four-sided courtyard" — was the defining domestic form. Four single-storey wings around a central courtyard, main hall on the north, wings east and west for juniors, south wing for servants and the main gate. Larger estates linked multiple courtyards along a south-north axis. **Gardens**, especially in the Song south, became their own art form — the Suzhou scholar-garden with its tai-hu rocks, lotus ponds, winding galleries, studios named after moods (*Humble Administrator's Garden*, *Garden of the Master of Nets*), moon-gates and white walls as canvases for shadow.

**Sacred architecture.** Daoist and Buddhist temples followed the courtyard grammar: mountain gate (**shanmen**), drum tower and bell tower, Hall of the Heavenly Kings, main Buddha hall, Dharma hall, scripture library, abbot's quarters. **Pagodas (ta 塔)** — the Chinese adaptation of the Indian stupa — evolved from Tang timber towers to Song brick-and-stone cores with wooden balconies. The **Wild Goose Pagoda** of Chang'an (Xi'an, 652 CE) stored the scriptures that Xuanzang had brought from India; the **Iron Pagoda of Kaifeng** (1049) survives today. Mountain retreats — Wutai's Tang halls, Wudang's Daoist shrines — nestled into cliff bluffs, reached by stone stairs cut into the rock.

**Defensive architecture.** City walls of **rammed earth (hangtu 夯土)** faced with brick, battlements, gate towers, and moats surrounded every town of consequence. The **Great Wall** — built by Qin, renewed by Han, heavily maintained by later dynasties and rebuilt in stone by Ming — anchored the northern frontier. Beacon towers relayed smoke signals by day and fires by night across hundreds of kilometres.

**Interior furnishing.** Tang interiors still used **floor-sitting on mats** in the older tradition; Song interiors completed the Chinese transition to **chairs and high tables**. Lacquer furniture, screens painted with landscape, curtains and bed-canopies of embroidered silk, charcoal braziers for warmth, floor-to-ceiling shelves of scrolls and books, ink-stones, brushes, censers breathing sandalwood or agarwood. A scholar's studio contained the **Four Treasures (wenfang sibao 文房四寶)**: brush, ink-stick, ink-stone, paper. A gentry bedroom had a **canopied wooden platform-bed** with curtains for privacy and sometimes a small attached alcove for a servant or concubine.

**Fengshui (風水)** — literally "wind-water" — governed every building site. A proper site had the **azure dragon on the left (east), white tiger on the right (west), red phoenix in front (south), black tortoise behind (north)**, sheltered by rising ground at the back and open to flowing water at the front. A master fengshui practitioner used a **luopan compass** marked with trigrams and star-positions. Aoli's sect compounds — mountain shrines, river pavilions, cloud terraces — follow this grammar exactly.

## 10. ART, MUSIC, AND STORYTELLING

**The Four Arts of the Chinese scholar — qin, qi, shu, hua (琴棋書畫) — the guqin, the game of Go, calligraphy, and painting** — defined the cultivated gentleman and gentlewoman of the Tang and Song. A young aristocrat was expected to be competent in all four. This cluster is the single most important aesthetic inheritance of Aoli: every sect master plays the guqin or the flute, writes calligraphy at dawn, paints a landscape after meditation, and plays weiqi with peers across centuries of rivalry.

**Calligraphy (shu 書)** was the highest art. Brush on ink on paper was believed to reveal the writer's **qi, character, and cultivation** more faithfully than painting. The **Tang master Yan Zhenqing (709–785)** produced the monumental regular-script style that became the orthodox hand of scholarship; **Ouyang Xun, Yu Shinan, Chu Suiliang** formed the Tang Four Great Masters. Song calligraphy — **Su Shi, Huang Tingjian, Mi Fu, Cai Xiang** — turned personal: the brush recorded mood, illness, grief. Styles included **regular (kaishu), running (xingshu), cursive (caoshu), clerical (lishu), and seal (zhuanshu)**. **Wang Xizhi's Orchid Pavilion Preface (353 CE)** — though pre-Tang — was still the Everest. A master's calligraphy could sell for a fortune.

**Painting (hua 畫)**. The Tang mastered figure painting and court portraiture — **Wu Daozi's "flying garments"** were said to animate — and Buddhist wall-painting at Dunhuang reached an incandescent peak. Tang also birthed **monochrome ink landscape**, and its philosophical counterpart: **Wang Wei's** snow-scenes at Wang River combined painting and poetry into a single vision. The Song took landscape painting to its highest ground: **Guo Xi's *Early Spring* (1072)**, **Fan Kuan's *Travellers Among Mountains and Streams***, **Xia Gui and Ma Yuan's** misted corners and empty middles. The Song scholar-painters insisted that painting was **not craft but self-cultivation** — a man painted his own qi, not the mountain.

**Poetry** lived at the centre of social life. Any gathering of gentlemen produced poems; any exam required them; any letter of consequence included at least a quatrain. **Tang poetry** is the Everest of the Chinese literary tradition, containing over **48,900 surviving poems by 2,200+ poets**, collected most famously in the **Three Hundred Tang Poems (Tangshi Sanbai Shou)**. Its mountain peaks:

- **Li Bai (701–762)** — "Immortal of Poetry" — the Daoist drinker, flying dragon of imagery, master of the jueju cut-verse and the joyful lyric of wine and moon.
- **Du Fu (712–770)** — "Sage of Poetry" — the Confucian witness, the conscience of the An Lushan Rebellion, technician of lüshi regulated verse, the poet of people's suffering.
- **Wang Wei (699–761)** — the Buddhist painter-poet, the master of sparse contemplative landscape.
- **Bai Juyi (772–846)** — the popular poet whose lines were said to be understood by old women.
- **Li Shangyin (813–858)** — the late-Tang master of allusive, densely symbolic love poetry.

Poetic forms: **shi 詩** (classical lines of five or seven characters); **lüshi 律詩** (regulated verse, strict tonal patterns, parallel couplets); **jueju 絕句** (truncated verse, four lines); **gushi 古詩** (ancient-style, freer). The Song contributed **ci 詞** — lyrics written to existing tune-patterns, shorter, more musical, more emotional. **Su Shi, Xin Qiji, Li Qingzhao** were its masters.

**Storytelling and theatre.** Song Kaifeng and later Lin'an teemed with **pleasure quarters (wazi 瓦子)** — urban entertainment districts containing multiple **performance arenas (goulan 勾欄)**. Storytellers (**shuoshuren 說書人**) performed long serial romances (the prose ancestors of novels like *Romance of the Three Kingdoms* and *Water Margin*); **pinghua** narrators acted out historical sagas; **huaben** chapbook stories spread through woodblock print. **Yuanben** and **zaju** — the Song's early dramatic forms — led into the great age of Yuan drama and, eventually, Kunqu and Peking opera.

**Music.** The **guqin (古琴)**, seven-string zither of the scholar, was less a performance instrument than a tool of self-cultivation; Confucius was said to have played it. Its surviving manuscripts go back to the Tang. The **pipa (琵琶)**, a four-string pear-shaped lute, was the Tang's most beloved instrument — played in every tavern and imperial banquet; Emperor Xuanzong and his consort Yang Guifei were both masters. The **erhu (二胡)**, two-string bowed fiddle, arrived from the west through Tang contact with Central Asia. Add the **xiao** and **dizi** (flutes), **sheng** (mouth organ), **guzheng** (long zither), **yunluo** (gong chime), **drums and bells** of many sizes. The Tang court fielded **ten different orchestras (shibu ji 十部伎)**, each dedicated to the music of a different region — Turfan, Kucha, Samarkand, Korea, India — and a **Pear Garden Academy (Liyuan 梨園)** trained over a thousand court musicians and dancers. Dance — the whirling **huxuan 胡旋** from Sogdiana, the sword-dance of Gongsun Daniang who inspired Du Fu to tears — was inseparable from music.

**Weiqi (圍棋)** — Go — the black-and-white board game of infinite depth, was the scholar's meditation and the general's war-room. Tang and Song left us its first great manuals. **In Aoli, a sect master's guqin song can weave a barrier, a calligraphy scroll can bind a spirit, and a game of weiqi can be a centuries-long cold war between two immortals.**

## 11. TECHNOLOGY AND CRAFTS

Tang and Song China was the most technologically advanced civilization on Earth. The **"Four Great Inventions"** — paper, printing, gunpowder, and the compass — all reached maturity in this period, and each reshaped the world.

**Paper.** Invented under the Han (traditionally by **Cai Lun, 105 CE**), paper was mature and cheap by the Tang: mulberry bark, hemp, bamboo, ramie pulped and pressed into sheets. It spread across Central Asia after the **Battle of Talas (751)** — captured Chinese papermakers taught the Abbasids — and reached Europe only centuries later. Tang paper enabled everything else: books, bureaucracy, examinations, paper money, paper lanterns, paper for funeral offerings.

**Printing.** **Woodblock printing** became widespread in the Tang — the **Diamond Sutra of 868**, discovered at Dunhuang, is the world's oldest dated printed book. **Bi Sheng in the Song (c. 1040)** invented **movable type in fired clay**, and metal movable type followed in Korea. Song Kaifeng and Hangzhou printed thousands of titles — Buddhist canons, medical compendia, agricultural manuals, local histories, poetry anthologies, novels — making China the first literate mass-market society.

**Gunpowder.** A Daoist alchemist's accident — looking for an elixir of immortality, finding instead a powder that exploded. First recorded as a weapon in the **10th century Song military manual *Wujing Zongyao* (1044)**, gunpowder produced fire arrows, fire lances, bombs hurled by trebuchet, naval incendiaries, and eventually the first primitive cannon. Song armies defended against the Jurchens with these weapons; their transfer westward via the Mongols would transform European warfare three centuries later.

**The magnetic compass.** Known as a divination instrument since the Han, the compass was turned to navigation by the Song — **Shen Kuo's *Dream Pool Essays* (c. 1088)** is the first clear description of magnetic declination. Song seafarers crossed the Indian Ocean to Arabia on ocean-going junks guided by the south-pointing needle.

**Metalworking.** Bronze had a three-thousand-year pedigree by the Tang — the ritual bronzes of the Shang and Zhou were already antiques and prized by Song collectors (the beginnings of Chinese epigraphy). Iron and steel production reached industrial scale: the Song produced an estimated **114,000 tons of iron annually** by 1078, more than all of Europe combined until the 18th century. **Sword smiths and armourers** worked to a high art; **Longquan** in the south became the byword for fine swords. Gold, silver, copper — struck into coins, beaten into leaf, filigreed into hair ornaments.

**Textiles.** Silk was the crown of Chinese technology, developed over millennia. The **jacquard-type draw loom** — with a cord-puller above the weaver controlling complex patterns — was fully developed by the Tang and spread to Persia and beyond. Famous silk-weaving regions: Sichuan (brocades), Suzhou (fine damasks), Hangzhou (satins). The Song added **cotton** as ordinary wear. Dyeing, embroidery, tapestry-weaving (*kesi*) — each had its own guild masters.

**Ceramics.** The Tang produced the famous **sancai (三彩) "three-colour" glazed earthenware** — the tomb horses and camels in cream, amber and emerald green that are now in every major museum. The Song raised ceramics to the level of poetry: the **Five Great Kilns — Ding, Ru, Guan, Ge, Jun** — each produced a signature. Ru ware's greenish-blue crackled glaze (only about a hundred pieces survive) was reserved for the imperial court. Jun ware showed the first copper-red "flambé" glaze — lavender-blue smeared with sunset crimson. Longquan celadons — sea-green — reached Egypt and Mesopotamia. **Jingdezhen** began its rise to become the porcelain capital of the world.

**Agriculture.** The Song agricultural revolution — **fast-ripening Champa rice from Vietnam (introduced c. 1011)**, terraced paddies, wheelbarrows, iron ploughs, chain-pumps for irrigation, seed-drills — doubled the food supply and supported a population that passed **100 million** for the first time in world history. The **Record of Agriculture (Nongshu)** and **Chen Fu's treatise** are among the first systematic farming handbooks anywhere.

**Boats and carts.** The Tang built canal barges, river junks and ocean-going ships; the Song built the **multi-masted, compartmentalised, sternpost-ruddered ocean junk** — the most sophisticated ship afloat until the 15th century. The **Grand Canal**, dug by the Sui and maintained by the Tang and Song, linked the Yellow River to the Yangtze: 1,800 kilometres of man-made waterway carrying millions of tons of grain north each year. On land, the horse-and-ox cart, the wheelbarrow (a Han invention refined in the Tang), and the postal relay system of the Tang (**1,639 relay stations, 20,000 postmen**) moved people and information across vast distances.

**Astronomy, timekeeping, water-clocks.** **Su Song's astronomical clock-tower at Kaifeng (1088)** was a ten-metre water-powered mechanism with an armillary sphere, celestial globe, and figurines that struck the hours — the most complex machine in the world at that time. **Gunpowder, printing, the compass, porcelain, silk, tea, the abacus, paper money, the suspension bridge, the segmental-arch bridge (the *Anji Bridge* of 605 survives today).** Aoli's crafts should reflect this depth: this is a civilization that invented the future first and then lived in it for a thousand years.

## 12. WARFARE AND WEAPONS

The Tang and Song fought each in their distinctive way. The **Tang was a military empire on horseback** — Li Shimin (Emperor Taizong, r. 626–649) was himself a cavalry general, and the early Tang pushed its borders deeper into Central Asia than any dynasty before. The **Song was a military empire of the mind** — outmatched in cavalry by steppe rivals, it compensated with technology, fortification, diplomacy, and the first bureaucratised standing army in Chinese history.

**The fubing system (府兵制)** was the Tang military spine. Soldiers were **farmer-soldiers** drawn from military households, settled on state-allocated land, exempt from tax and labour in exchange for serving in rotation. They trained in peace, assembled at war, and returned home between campaigns. At its height, the fubing fielded perhaps **500,000 troops** organised into twelve armies under twenty-four commanderies, supplemented by **frontier garrisons (zhen 鎮) and local militias**. An expeditionary army of 14,000 might contain **2,000 archers, 2,000 crossbowmen, 4,000 cavalry, and 6,000 infantry** — a combined-arms force of impressive balance.

**Cavalry** was the Tang glory. Heavy cataphracts in lamellar armour, armed with the curved sabre (**dao 刀**) or the straight-bladed **jian 劍** sword, lance, and composite bow. **Tang horse breeding** was vast — hundreds of thousands of horses were pastured in Hexi and Longyou, imported from the Ferghana valley (the "blood-sweating heavenly horses") and the steppe. The polo field at Chang'an was where princes and generals trained cavalry reflexes.

**Crossbows (nu 弩)** were the infantry answer to the horse. Chinese crossbows had a decisive advantage in range and armour penetration; they could be massed in volley-fire formations that broke cavalry charges. **Li Guangbi's victory over the rebel cavalry of Shi Siming in the An Lushan Rebellion** famously used a spear-wall-plus-crossbow formation: spearmen locked ranks; 1,000 crossbowmen divided into four rotating sections so volleys never stopped. The rebels could not close and withdrew.

**The An Lushan Rebellion (755–763)** is the tectonic event of Tang history. The half-Sogdian, half-Turkic frontier general **An Lushan**, holding command of the Fanyang, Pinglu and Hedong armies simultaneously — some 160,000 troops — revolted against Emperor Xuanzong, captured both capitals, forced the emperor's flight and the death of Yang Guifei, and plunged the empire into eight years of civil war. The Tang survived but was never the same; the fubing system had broken down and was replaced by regional military governors (**jiedushi 節度使**) whose semi-autonomous power fragmented the later Tang into warlordism.

**The Song retreat from cavalry.** Having lost its horse-breeding regions to the Khitan Liao and later the Tangut Xi Xia, the Song could not match northern cavalry. It responded with technology and fortification: **crossbows with magazine-fed repeating mechanisms, siege trebuchets, gunpowder weapons (fire lances, fire arrows, the first cannons), iron-hulled war-junks, and enormous fortified walls**. The Song bought peace with annual tribute — silk, silver, and tea to the Khitan, Jurchen, and Tangut — and passed it off as gifts. When war came, Song generals like **Yue Fei (1103–1142)**, hero-general against the Jurchens, became culture heroes. Yue Fei's execution on fabricated treason charges by the corrupt minister Qin Hui became the archetype of the **loyal martyr undone by palace intrigue** — the emotional core of countless wuxia tragedies.

**Weapons catalogue.**
- **Jian 劍** — straight double-edged sword, the scholar-gentleman's weapon, the sword of the immortal cultivator.
- **Dao 刀** — single-edged curved sabre, the cavalry sword.
- **Qiang 槍** — spear, called "the king of weapons" (bingqi zhi wang 兵器之王).
- **Gun 棍** — staff, foundation of all weapons training.
- **Ji 戟** — halberd, fusing spear and axe.
- **Gongjian 弓箭** — composite bow.
- **Nu 弩** — crossbow, including the repeating chu-ko-nu attributed to Zhuge Liang.
- **Fu 斧** — axe; **yue 鉞** — great axe, symbol of command.
- **Bian, gun, chui 鞭、棍、錘** — chain-whip, rod, hammer.
- **Feidao 飛刀** — flying knives/darts. **Feijian 飛劍** — the flying sword of the cultivator is part reality (a real projectile technique), part immortal fantasy.
- **Fan (shan 扇)** and **flute (xiao 簫)** served as hidden weapons for scholars and musicians.

**Women in warfare.** Though the Confucian ideal kept women at home, exceptions abound. The Tang princess **Pingyang** raised her own army of 70,000 to help her father overthrow the Sui — the **"Lady General" (niang zi jun)**. The legendary **Hua Mulan** disguised herself as a man to replace her ageing father; her *Ballad* dates from the Northern Wei. **Liang Hongyu**, wife of Song general Han Shizhong, beat the battle drum from a raised tower during the Battle of Huangtiandang, signalling the fleet that broke the Jurchen invasion.

**Unarmed combat traditions.** The **Shaolin Monastery** temple on Songshan was already famous for martial monks in the Tang — thirteen warrior monks helped Li Shimin defeat a rival; the emperor rewarded them and Shaolin's martial tradition was formalised. **Wudang** developed its Daoist internal arts, which tradition credits to **Zhang Sanfeng** of the Song-Yuan transition — the probable historical basis for Taiji Quan. Aoli's sect martial styles stand on these two pillars: **"external" (waijia)** hard Shaolin-type arts and **"internal" (neijia)** soft Wudang-type arts, each channelling cultivated qi through trained forms.

## 13. TRADE AND ECONOMY

Chinese commerce reached a volume and sophistication in the Tang and Song that had no equal elsewhere on Earth until the European 17th century. The empire ran on three great arteries: the **Silk Road** west, the **Grand Canal** north-south within China, and the **Maritime Silk Road** reaching across the South China Sea, the Straits of Malacca, and the Indian Ocean to East Africa.

**The Silk Road under the Tang.** With the Tang's reconquest of the Western Regions after 630, the overland routes from Chang'an through **Dunhuang, Kashgar, Samarkand, Bukhara** and on to Persia and Constantinople reached their golden age. **Sogdian merchants** — Iranian-speakers from the oases around Samarkand — dominated the caravan trade. They operated as diaspora communities along every stage of the route, spoke a *lingua franca* of the Silk Roads, carried goods, letters, brides, religions and gossip between courts, and appear in Tang tomb figurines as bearded men with deep eye-sockets leading pack camels. The **Sogdian Ancient Letters**, found near Dunhuang in 1907, show their trade networks in intimate detail — including a letter from **Miwnay**, a Sogdian woman abandoned in Dunhuang by her merchant husband, writing to her mother in Samarkand.

**What flowed east into China:** horses (the Tang traded silk for Central Asian horses at a rate of **forty bolts of silk per horse** in the 8th century); jade from Khotan; lapis lazuli from Badakhshan; glass from Persia and Rome; grape wine; saffron and frankincense; musicians and dancers from Kucha and Samarkand; Buddhism, Nestorian Christianity, Manichaeism, Zoroastrianism, Islam (from 651 with the first Arab embassy to Chang'an); the grape, the walnut, sesame, coriander and spinach; cobalt-blue pigment for ceramics.

**What flowed west out of China:** silk above all — raw silk, silk thread, woven bolts, embroidered robes; ceramics (the Tang sancai horse, Song celadon and later blue-and-white); tea (though more by sea); lacquer; paper; cast iron; medicinal herbs; peaches and apricots (the Persian names for peach and apricot literally mean "Chinese fruit"); gunpowder and printing technologies, eventually.

**Dunhuang** at the western end of the Hexi Corridor became the great meeting point — a multicultural entrepôt where Chinese clerks worked alongside Tibetan administrators, Sogdian merchants, Uighur scribes and Khotanese monks. The **Mogao Caves** — 492 painted cave-temples carved into the cliff above the oasis between the 4th and 14th centuries — hold the world's greatest repository of medieval Buddhist art. In 1900 a Daoist priest found a sealed side-chamber containing 50,000 manuscripts: sutras in ten languages, Sogdian letters, Tibetan tantras, Uighur poetry, the world's earliest dated printed book (the Diamond Sutra of 868), and administrative records from a thousand years of life at the crossroads.

**The Maritime Silk Road.** Song Quanzhou — called **Zaitun** by the Arabs — became the world's greatest port, with an Arab merchant quarter, a Nestorian Christian church, a Hindu temple, several mosques, and thousands of foreign ships a year. Chinese junks sailed to Srivijaya, Java, Ceylon, India, the Persian Gulf, Aden, and East Africa. **Compass-guided navigation**, **watertight bulkheads**, and **multi-masted sail rigs** made the Song junk the premier ocean-going vessel of its age.

**Internal trade.** The **Grand Canal** carried an estimated **six million bushels of grain** per year northward to feed the capital, army and court. Tang and Song cities held enormous regulated markets: Chang'an's Eastern Market sold luxury goods to the court, its Western Market handled foreign wares, and Kaifeng's markets simply spilled over everywhere at every hour. Rural **periodic markets** rotated on three-day, five-day or ten-day cycles through networks of villages.

**Merchants and guilds.** Though Confucian doctrine placed merchants at the bottom of the four occupations, their real wealth made them power brokers. Song **merchant guilds (hang 行)** organised by trade — silk merchants, tea merchants, spice merchants, gold-and-silver bankers, pawnbrokers — regulated prices, arbitrated disputes, and paid protection money to officials. **Shanxi bankers** in the late imperial period built proto-banking networks with remittance letters that allowed a merchant to deposit silver in Chengdu and withdraw it in Kaifeng.

**Currency.** Coinage meant **copper cash (wen 文, or qian 錢)** — round coins with square holes, strung on ropes in standard units of a thousand. A **string (guan 貫)** of 1,000 coins was roughly the cost of a cheap horse. Silver circulated by weight (**liang 兩**, "taels"). The real revolution was **paper money**: **jiaozi 交子**, originally a Sichuan merchant's private promissory note around 1020, became government-issued in 1024 — the **world's first banknotes**. By the late Song, paper money financed much of the imperial economy. Inflation, not surprisingly, was also invented.

**Taxation and tribute** (see §3) — goods flowed upward to the throne in vast tributary lines. Tea, silk, salt and iron were periodically state monopolies. Foreign embassies arrived annually with "tribute" that Chinese ritual read as submission and foreign rulers read as trade — a useful diplomatic fiction that both sides profited from. Aoli's sects, cities, and great clans sit on exactly this kind of commercial lattice: old money, new money, the whispered credit networks of centuries-old masters who own land, silk looms, and porcelain kilns under discreet names.

## 14. LANGUAGE AND WRITING

**Chinese (zhongwen 中文)** is one of the oldest continuously written languages on Earth. Its **logographic script**, whose core characters descend in unbroken line from the oracle-bone inscriptions of the Shang (c. 1200 BCE), is as important culturally as the Latin alphabet is to Europe — but far more visually loaded. A Chinese character is a tiny painting; a Chinese sentence is a column of tiny paintings; a page of calligraphy is an orchestrated landscape.

**Spoken languages.** Tang and Song China was not monolingual. The prestige language of the court was **Middle Chinese** — recoverable from rhyme dictionaries like the *Qieyun* (601) and its descendants, used by poets to set tonal patterns. But the empire held many regional speech-forms already diverging into what would become the modern Chinese "dialects" (really separate languages): proto-Mandarin in the north, proto-Wu (Shanghai region), proto-Min (Fujian), proto-Yue (Cantonese, Guangdong), proto-Hakka. Members of the Tang court often spoke a creolised border speech shaped by their steppe ancestry; ordinary peasants spoke village dialects incomprehensible three mountains away. **Literary Chinese (wenyan 文言)** — the written register — held them together as a lingua franca for the educated.

**The writing system.** Characters are built from **radicals** (semantic components) combined with phonetic components. By the Tang, **standard regular script (kaishu)** had stabilised; the Kangxi Dictionary much later recorded roughly **47,000 distinct characters**, though any scholar could function with about 5,000 and any peasant might manage a few hundred. Writing ran in **vertical columns from right to left** on silk, bamboo slips, or paper — only the 20th century flipped the page horizontal.

**Literacy.** Among men of the gentry, near-universal; among peasant men, scattered but real (enough to read a land contract, a marriage document, a posted edict); among women of the gentry, widespread; among peasant women, rare. Song Kaifeng's woodblock-printed books reached a far broader audience than any previous society — popular novels, medical manuals, almanacs and household encyclopaedias spread into village households for the first time.

**The scholar's brush.** Writing was inseparable from calligraphy (see §10). A Tang official's memorial to the throne was judged not only on content but on handwriting, proper paper, proper ink, and proper observance of taboo characters (you could not write the emperor's personal name without ritual error). The **"Eight Principles of Yong" (yongzi bafa)** — eight brush techniques embodied in the single character 永 — trained every literate child in the basic strokes. Ink was ground fresh on an ink-stone with water. The **Four Treasures of the Studio** (brush, ink, stone, paper) appeared on every scholar's desk.

**Poetry and rhetoric.** Chinese literary culture was **verse-saturated**. A gentleman was expected to compose a poem on any occasion — parting from a friend, viewing a waterfall, seeing plum blossom, mourning a death, praising a new governor. Tang regulated verse (**lüshi**) required strict tonal patterns, parallel couplets, and five or seven characters per line. The **parallel-prose tradition (pianwen)** and the later **ancient-prose movement (guwen)** led by **Han Yu (768–824)** and **Liu Zongyuan (773–819)** defined Chinese literary style. Every scholar memorised hundreds of poems and could quote instantly in conversation — the social grease of elite life.

**Books and libraries.** The Tang imperial library held at least **54,000 volumes** organised into the **Four Treasuries (sibu 四部)** — Classics, Histories, Philosophies, Collected Works. Monastic libraries preserved Buddhist canons; great clan estates kept inherited libraries; Song printing gave every gentry household the possibility of a working library. **Censorship and taboo characters** were real but patchy; a book that attacked a reigning emperor could vanish, but dead emperors and private heterodoxies often survived in circulation.

**Numeracy.** The **abacus (suanpan 算盤)** entered general use in the Song. Chinese mathematics had achieved remarkable depth — negative numbers (Han), magic squares (early), algebra to fourth-degree equations (Song), Pascal's triangle in the 11th century (four centuries before Pascal). Merchants, craftsmen and tax clerks learned calculation as a trade.

**Oral tradition** flourished alongside writing. Village storytellers, market criers, opera troupes, ballad singers, monks chanting sutras, funeral wailers, blind musicians performing narrative songs — a huge share of Tang and Song culture passed by ear, not page. Many Tang poems were written to be sung; their original melodies are mostly lost. Many Song *ci* lyrics were courtesan songs before they were literature.

**Naming taboo (bihui 避諱)** bears mentioning for worldbuilding: you could not use an emperor's personal name, a parent's name, or an ancestor's name in speech or writing; scholars substituted near-synonyms or left a deliberate blank stroke. Whole characters changed pronunciation or fell out of use because a dynasty's founder had worn their sound. **In Aoli, a god's True Name — Mùjīn, Yuèlí, Yánlóng — would be spoken cautiously, written with the stroke deliberately incomplete, or replaced by a respectful epithet.**

## 15. EDUCATION AND KNOWLEDGE

The Tang and Song ran the most systematic educational apparatus in the pre-modern world — built around the **imperial examination system (keju 科舉)** and aimed at producing gentleman-scholars fit to govern. Education was heavily gendered, heavily class-stratified, and heavily memorisation-based, but its reach was genuinely remarkable: by the late Song, perhaps 10% of the male population had some schooling and perhaps 1–2% were examination candidates.

**Early education.** Gentry boys began at age six or seven with the **Three Character Classic (Sanzijing 三字經)** — a rhymed primer teaching characters, numbers, basic morals, and a quick tour of Chinese history in 1,000 characters. Then the **Thousand Character Classic (Qianziwen 千字文)** — a poem using each of 1,000 different characters exactly once. Then the **Hundred Family Surnames (Baijiaxing 百家姓)**. Then Confucian ethics in the **Classic of Filial Piety (Xiaojing)**. By ten or twelve, a boy was expected to have memorised the **Four Books (Sishu 四書)** — the *Analects* of Confucius, *Mencius*, *Great Learning*, *Doctrine of the Mean* — and be starting on the **Five Classics (Wujing 五經)**: the *Book of Changes*, *Book of Documents*, *Book of Odes*, *Book of Rites*, *Spring and Autumn Annals*.

Memorisation was absolute: a scholar could quote lines from anywhere in the Classics on request, and conversation among gentlemen ran on a shared reservoir of allusion. A single quoted phrase could say what a paragraph of plain speech could not — and scoring a clever allusion was a social victory.

**Girls' education.** Girls of gentry families were taught at home — literacy, the *Analects for Women (Nü Lunyu)* by **Song Ruoxin**, the *Admonitions for Women* of Ban Zhao, poetry, calligraphy, painting, the guqin, embroidery, household accounts, and proper deportment. The Song Neo-Confucian revival tightened expectations but did not suppress female literacy — poets like **Li Qingzhao** prove the opposite. **Gentry daughters in the Tang sometimes received the same Classical education as their brothers** and could compose poetry in the full regulated-verse forms.

**Public schools and academies.** The Tang founded the **Imperial Academy (Guozijian 國子監)** in Chang'an with specialised faculties in the Classics, Law, Mathematics, and Calligraphy, admitting sons of officials and top examination candidates. Provincial and prefectural schools fed students upward. The Song expanded public education massively; by the late Northern Song there were state schools in every prefecture, and the **great Neo-Confucian academies (shuyuan 書院)** — **Yuelu** in Changsha, **Bailudong** (White Deer Grotto) at Lushan, **Yingtian** in Henan, **Songyang** on Songshan — became the intellectual powerhouses of the dynasty. **Zhu Xi** taught at Bailudong and set out its academy charter in 1180; its principles shaped Chinese education for seven centuries.

**The examinations themselves.** Three tiers by the Song: **prefectural (xiangshi)**, **metropolitan (huishi)**, **palace (dianshi, personally overseen by the emperor)**. Candidates sat in sealed cells for days at a time, writing essays on assigned passages from the Classics, composing poetry in regulated verse, solving hypothetical legal cases, and answering policy questions. The top graduates of the palace exam — **jinshi 進士** — were the crown princes of the civil service. Pass rates were brutal; most men sat the prefectural exam multiple times across decades. The stories of old men passing at seventy, or of clever youths like **Ouyang Xiu** topping the metropolitan exam and being lionised instantly, shaped the imagination of every Chinese family.

**Specialist training.** Buddhist monasteries taught their novices the Canon, meditation techniques, ritual chanting, Sanskrit for the learned. Daoist temples transmitted neidan (internal alchemy), ritual forms, divinatory arts and calligraphic scriptures along **master-disciple lineages (shishou 師授)** rather than classroom instruction. The **physician's path** was similar: long apprenticeship to a master, memorisation of the *Huangdi Neijing* and the *Treatise on Cold Damage Disorders*, and eventually independent practice. Crafts — carpentry, silk weaving, ceramics, swordsmithing — passed father-to-son or master-to-apprentice, with guilds regulating competence. Musicians and dancers of the imperial **Pear Garden Academy (Liyuan)** trained from childhood under palace master-teachers.

**What counted as knowledge.** Above all: **the Classics and history**. The Confucian canon provided the moral grammar; the dynastic histories (**Shiji** of Sima Qian through the Tang-compiled *Book of Han*, *Book of Sui* and onward) provided the examples. Below: **poetry, calligraphy, music, painting** as the Four Arts of a cultivated man. Further down: **astronomy, mathematics, medicine, geomancy, military strategy, ritual music** as specialised technical fields. Still further down: **agriculture, craft, commerce** — necessary but not prestigious.

**The master-disciple bond (shishou 師授)** is worth its own note. A student who accepted a master accepted a parent. Formal rites established the bond: the student bowed, offered tea, gave gifts of **dried meat (shuxiu 束脩)** — Confucius reportedly accepted any pupil who brought a bundle. The master became responsible for the disciple's moral formation and career, and the disciple owed lifelong loyalty — weighting above his biological father, in extreme readings. **Aoli's sect-master bond is exactly this cultural machinery, raised to cosmic power.**

## 16. MEDICINE AND HEALING

**Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM / zhongyi 中醫)** is one of the two great living medical systems of the pre-modern world (the other being Ayurveda), with an unbroken clinical tradition stretching more than two thousand years. By the Tang and Song its theoretical frame was fully mature and its clinical practice highly organised.

**The Huangdi Neijing (黃帝內經)** — the *Yellow Emperor's Inner Canon*, compiled under the Han on the foundation of older texts, is TCM's *Iliad*: the source of almost every concept. Its two parts — the **Suwen** (basic questions) and **Lingshu** (spiritual pivot) — lay out the doctrine: **qi** (vital breath/energy) flows through **twelve main meridians (jingluo 經絡)** linking internal organs to the surface of the body; **yin and yang** must balance; the **Five Phases (wuxing)** correspond to the five yin organs (liver=wood, heart=fire, spleen=earth, lungs=metal, kidneys=water) and the six yang organs; seasons, emotions, foods, flavours, colours and directions all stack into one resonating cosmos. Illness is imbalance. The physician restores balance.

**Diagnosis** used **Four Examinations (si zhen 四診)**: **looking** (complexion, tongue), **listening and smelling** (wen 聞 — same word), **asking** (history, symptoms, sleep, stool, appetite), and **touching** — above all the **pulse**. Pulse diagnosis reached extraordinary refinement: the practitioner placed three fingers on the radial artery at the wrist and read **six positions × three depths** on each hand (with a different organ for each), identifying perhaps 28 distinct pulse qualities. A skilled pulse reader could detect pregnancy, emotional trouble, and seasonal imbalance from twenty seconds of contact.

**Treatments.**
- **Herbal medicine (zhongyao 中藥)** — the workhorse. Prescriptions of 6–20 ingredients were cooked into bitter decoctions. The **Tang "Materia Medica" (*Xinxiu Bencao*, 659)** was the world's first state-commissioned pharmacopoeia, listing 850 drugs. The Song expanded it further. Key herbs: **ginseng (renshen)**, **angelica (danggui)**, **astragalus (huangqi)**, **licorice (gancao)**, **cinnamon (rougui)**, **rehmannia (dihuang)**, **chuanxiong**, **baishao**. Minerals and animal parts were also used; the pearl, the deer antler, the bezoar, the gallstone of ox, the seahorse.
- **Acupuncture (zhenjiu 針灸)** — insertion of fine needles at specific points on the meridians to move qi. Moxibustion (*jiu*) burned dried mugwort on or above points. The **Bronze Acupuncture Figure** cast by Song imperial physician **Wang Weiyi (1027)** was a life-sized hollow bronze with 354 marked points, filled with water, pinpricked for teaching — a teaching tool unparalleled in world medicine.
- **Cupping, scraping (gua sha), massage (tuina), qigong breathing exercises** — the manual therapies.
- **Dietary therapy (shiliao 食療)** — recognising food as preventive medicine. Ginger for cold; pear for dry cough; bitter melon for summer heat; black sesame and black bean for the kidneys.
- **Surgery** — limited, but performed. The legendary Han surgeon **Hua Tuo** was said to have used an anaesthetic of hemp-flower powder and wine (**mafeisan**) to perform abdominal operations; Tang surgeons handled cataracts with couching techniques imported from India. Wound care, bone-setting and reduction of dislocations were well developed.

**Midwifery and women's medicine.** Childbirth was the domain of female midwives (**wenpo 穩婆**). By the Song a distinct **fuke (婦科)** subspecialty had emerged, with texts like **Chen Ziming's *Comprehensive Good Prescriptions for Women* (1237)** covering menstrual disorders, pregnancy care, and postpartum recovery. The **zuo yuezi** practice (sitting the month after birth) belongs here, as does the vast array of warming postpartum tonic soups.

**The Tang Imperial Medical Service (Taiyishu 太醫署)** was the world's first state medical school. Its curriculum — lasting up to seven years — trained specialists in **internal medicine, acupuncture, massage, exorcistic rites (*zhouji*), and dental-and-ENT**. Graduates staffed the palace, the nobility, and military hospitals. The Song created the **Imperial Medical Bureau (Taiyiju 太醫局)**, with standardised examinations and printed textbooks.

**Preventive and spiritual medicine.** A physician treated diseases; a **cultivator** prevented them. **Qigong (氣功)** — breathwork and slow movement — aligned qi. **Daoyin (導引)** — literally "leading and guiding" — stretching exercises, ancestors of Taiji Quan. **Fasting, vegetarian diet, regulated sleep, meditation, sexual hygiene** — the full toolkit of neidan internal alchemy had a medical face: you cultivated to live longer and better, not merely to ascend.

**Spiritual healing ran alongside** physical treatment. Many illnesses were attributed to ghost-attack, ancestor-displeasure, breach of taboo, or malignant fox-spirit possession. Exorcistic rites — Daoist talismans pasted on doors, Buddhist sutras chanted, Confucian memorials read over the afflicted — were part of the medical repertoire. A village practitioner was often physician, herbalist, midwife, and ritualist in one. A Tang noblewoman with a mysterious illness might be treated by the imperial doctor, then by a Daoist priest, then by a Buddhist monk, then by a Sogdian merchant's imported Persian charm — in sequence, without sense of contradiction. **Aoli inherits this layered, pluralist, body-and-spirit medical world whole.**

## 17. DAILY LIFE

**The average day.** A Tang or Song peasant rose before dawn to the crow of the rooster, washed face and hands at the courtyard well, wrapped on hempen trousers and short jacket, and went to the fields at first light. Women stayed in to boil the morning **zhou (congee)** — rice or millet porridge with pickled vegetables, a salted egg, a splash of soy — feed the children, sweep the courtyard, feed the pigs, gather eggs, and begin the day's spinning, weaving or embroidery. Midday brought a larger meal of rice or noodles with vegetables and a little fish or meat if the household could afford it; early evening ended the workday; the family gathered for supper before dusk. Oil was expensive and lamps burned only when needed; most went to bed not long after dark. A Song peasant's year moved by the **24 solar terms (*jieqi* 節氣)** — Start of Spring, Rain Water, Waking of Insects, Pure Brightness, Grain in Ear, Summer Solstice, Autumn Equinox, Great Cold — each with its agricultural work and its folk custom.

**Urban days ran longer.** Song Kaifeng's markets opened at dawn with the **drum of the fifth watch (around 5 a.m.)**; by sunrise, porters were carrying charcoal in from the western suburbs, farmers brought vegetables from the nearby villages, fishing boats docked at the Bian River wharves. Tea houses and **morning-rice shops (zaoshi 早市)** sold breakfast — hot soy milk with youtiao (fried dough-sticks, Song ancestor), steamed buns (baozi), deep-fried dough cakes. Officials headed to court before the audience bell; craftsmen opened their shops; scribes and merchants filed into the guild halls. In the afternoon, schools let out, teahouses filled, pleasure-quarters (wazi) opened their goulan arenas for storytelling, shadow-puppet plays, acrobatics, puppet shows. Night markets — forbidden in Tang Chang'an, beloved in Song Kaifeng — kept the city humming past midnight.

**Hygiene.** China's bathing culture was well developed. Ordinary people bathed weekly or monthly, gentry more often; the wealthy had private baths with hot water carried from cauldrons, while urban districts in the Song supported **public bathhouses (tangping 湯瓶)** with separate men's and women's sections, steam rooms, and attendants for back-scrubbing. Oral hygiene: tooth-cleaning with willow twigs chewed to fringe; tooth powders of salt, mint and alum. Washing hands before eating was standard. Hair-washing used **soap-bean (zaojiao) and infusions of soapnut, tea seed, or rice-water**. Women's hair, which could reach the knees uncut, was a daily ritual of combing and oiling with jasmine-infused camellia oil. Menstrual cloths were made of layered hemp or cotton, washed and reused. Public sanitation included **night-soil collection** by contractors who sold human waste to peri-urban farmers as fertiliser.

**Sleep.** Ordinary people slept on **kang** — the heated brick platform beds of the north, warmed by the cooking stove's smoke before it vented out — or on simple wooden platforms with woven bamboo mats in the south. Gentry slept on carved hardwood **canopy beds (jia zi chuang 架子床)** with silk curtains for privacy and warmth, sometimes with a small inner alcove for a servant. Pillows were often **hard porcelain or wooden blocks** filled with herbs (chrysanthemum for clear sight, buckwheat hulls for coolness). Quilts were cotton or silk batting. Cohabitation patterns varied: husband and wife shared a bed in poor households; in wealthy ones, the husband might visit his wife's or concubine's chamber on schedule.

**Work and leisure.** Farmers worked from sunrise to sunset with a midday rest in the hottest months; their leisure came in the slack winter months and at festivals. Craftsmen worked from early morning to mid-afternoon in their shops. Scholars studied early morning, rested through the hottest afternoon hour, read into the evening by oil-lamp. Leisure included **weiqi (Go), chess (xiangqi), card games, riddle contests, composing poetry, painting, playing the guqin or flute, archery in set ranges, polo (for aristocrats), cock-fighting (popular in Tang), cricket-fighting (Song), fishing, hunting with falcons, kite-flying, swing-sets at Qingming, lion-dancing at New Year, boat-racing at Duanwu**. Urban entertainment included the full menu of **wazi** pleasure-quarters — storytellers, shadow-play, music, courtesans, acrobats, wrestlers, trick-riders.

**Domestic rhythms.** Women's days orbited the courtyard: cooking, cleaning, drawing water, tending the kitchen garden, airing bedding, supervising children and servants, spinning thread, weaving, sewing, embroidering, and — in gentry homes — reading, painting, playing music, visiting with other ladies. Scholar-husbands might pass mornings at the yamen (government office) and return home for a quiet afternoon of study or painting. Extended family ate together when possible; servants ate separately.

**The rhythm of the year.** Spring — kite-flying, tomb-sweeping at Qingming, planting rice in the south, green tea harvested, plum-blossom viewing. Summer — Dragon Boat races, weather hot and humid, daytime hours spent in the shade of courtyards and studios. Autumn — harvest, Mid-Autumn moon-viewing, chrysanthemum-viewing at Double Ninth, wedding season. Winter — warmth around the kang, pickling and preserving, the New Year approaching. Tang and Song urban life was **loud, crowded, fragrant, cosmopolitan and structured around festivals**; rural life was **quieter, poorer, harder, and punctuated by the same festivals on a smaller scale**.

## 18. MYTHOLOGY AND LEGEND (beyond formal religion)

Beyond formal Confucian, Daoist and Buddhist frameworks, a vast **folk mythology** saturated Chinese life. These are the stories told around the kitchen stove, sung by blind minstrels, acted on village stages, whispered to children on summer nights, and turned — by the Tang and especially the Song — into written story collections like the **Taiping Guangji** (*Extensive Records of the Taiping Era*, 978), a massive 500-chapter compendium of strange tales the Song court commissioned and then promptly suppressed for being too alarming.

**Creation myths.** Two coexist without conflict.
- **Pangu (盤古)** — in the beginning the cosmos was an egg. Pangu hatched and separated heaven from earth, pushing them apart with his body over 18,000 years. When he died, his breath became wind and cloud, his voice the thunder, his left eye the sun, his right eye the moon, his blood the rivers, his flesh the fields, his hair the stars, and the lice on his body became humankind.
- **Nüwa (女媧)** — the serpent-bodied mother goddess, who shaped humans out of yellow clay (the nobility) and flung mud with a rope to make the rest (the commoners). Later, when a cosmic pillar collapsed and the sky tore open, Nüwa smelted five-coloured stones to patch heaven and cut off the feet of the cosmic turtle to prop the corners of the earth.

These two myths — one paternal, one maternal, both sacrificial — feed directly into Aoli's Mùjīn and Yuèlí.

**The Eight Immortals (Baxian 八仙)** — a group of legendary cultivators each with a distinctive implement and backstory, crystallising in Tang and Song folk religion: **Lü Dongbin** (sword), **Han Xiangzi** (flute), **He Xiangu** (lotus), **Tieguai Li** (crutch and gourd), **Zhang Guolao** (paper mule), **Cao Guojiu** (castanets), **Lan Caihe** (flower basket), **Zhongli Quan** (fan). They crossed the sea on a river of varied magical transports — the folk proverb "each of the Eight Immortals showing their own magical skill" (八仙過海各顯神通) names any contest of unique gifts.

**Monsters and spirit-creatures.**
- **Huli jing 狐狸精 / jiuweihu 九尾狐** — the fox spirit. Originally auspicious — a nine-tailed fox appeared only in stable and prosperous kingdoms, according to the *Classic of Mountains and Seas* — it became, from the Tang onward, a shapeshifting seductress: a fox who has lived fifty years can transform into a woman; a hundred years, a beautiful woman or a spirit medium; a thousand years, a celestial fox of nine tails who has achieved immortality. Notoriously connected to the fall of dynasties — the fox spirit Daji was blamed for the Shang collapse. **Aoli's Wen Sect fox-lineage sits exactly at this hinge — nine-tailed foxes as wise, playful, and fundamentally benevolent cultivators, reclaiming the figure from its late-medieval demonisation.**
- **Long 龍** — the Chinese dragon. Not the European fire-breathing reptile; a serpentine, hornless-to-antlered, beard-feathered, scale-mailed water-lord who controls rivers, rain and oceans. **Nine sons** of the dragon specialise in different temperaments — Bixi (tortoise that carries stelae), Chiwen (roof-ridge dragon that swallows fire), Pulao (bell-dragon that bellows), and others. The dragon is imperial: **the emperor sat on the Dragon Throne (longyi), wore dragon robes (longpao), and had a dragon face (longyan).**
- **Fenghuang 鳳凰** — the Chinese phoenix. Not the European self-burning phoenix; a composite auspicious bird combining pheasant, peacock, swallow, fowl and crane features. **Feng** is male, **Huang** is female. Embodies virtue, prosperity, yin to the dragon's yang, empress to the emperor.
- **Qilin 麒麟** — the "Chinese unicorn", a benevolent chimera that appears when a sage is born and heralds peaceful reigns. Confucius's birth was said to be prefigured by a qilin sighting.
- **Gui 鬼** — ghosts. The unquiet dead who died violently, young, or without descendants. Folklore is full of them: the **hungry ghost** who cannot swallow because its throat is too narrow; the **water ghost** who drags the living down to take its place; the **fox-ghost** who seduces scholars; the **suicide ghost** trapped at the place of death; the **jiangshi 殭屍** — stiff-limbed hopping vampire-corpses of late-imperial folklore.
- **Shanxiao 山魈, jingmei 精魅** — wild mountain spirits; **yaoguai 妖怪** — demons of cultivated-animal origin; **shenxian 神仙** — celestial immortals who have transcended.

**Legendary heroes.**
- **Yu the Great** — who tamed the floods and founded the Xia dynasty by digging channels for twenty-two years, passing his own home three times without entering.
- **Hou Yi** — the archer who shot down nine of the ten suns when they threatened to burn the earth, and whose wife **Chang'e** drank the elixir of immortality and fled to the moon, where she became the Moon Goddess.
- **Meng Jiangnü** — whose tears brought down a section of the Great Wall when she learned her conscripted husband had died building it.
- **The Butterfly Lovers (Liang Shanbo and Zhu Yingtai)** — the Chinese Romeo and Juliet, transformed into twin butterflies at their deaths.
- **Niulang and Zhinü** — the Cowherd and Weaver Girl, lovers separated by the Milky Way, allowed to meet on a bridge of magpies once a year at Qixi.
- **Sun Wukong, the Monkey King** — though *Journey to the West* is Ming, the historical Xuanzang who inspired it made his pilgrimage in the Tang.
- **Bai Suzhen, the White Snake** — a snake-spirit who cultivates human form and marries a scholar on the West Lake; the tragic romance at Leifeng Pagoda.

**How myth shaped identity.** The Tang believed ancestral descent from the Yellow Emperor (Huangdi); the Song read the Classics as literal history. **Stories of cosmic love, of lovers separated and reunited after impossible trials, of cultivators who become gods, of spirits who become human through love** — these are the emotional DNA of the xianxia drama Serina watches. Aoli's Mùjīn-and-Yuèlí cosmic protection story is a direct continuation of **Niulang-Zhinü, Chang'e-Hou Yi, and Bai Suzhen** — love as the force that defies heaven, shapes the land, and waits across millennia for redemption.

## 19. TABOOS, LAWS, AND ETHICS

Chinese ethical life ran on three partly overlapping grids: **Confucian moral philosophy**, **imperial legal code**, and **folk taboo and honour culture**. Together they produced a society with sharp lines about what could be done, strong concepts of face and shame, and ritually enforced oaths.

**The Ten Abominations (shi'e 十惡)** of the Tang Code set out the worst possible crimes, all punished severely and often extending to the criminal's kin under the **nine-familial extermination (zhu jiuzu)** doctrine:

1. **Plotting rebellion (mou fan 謀反)** — against the dynasty.
2. **Plotting great sedition (mou dani 謀大逆)** — destruction of imperial temples, tombs or palaces.
3. **Plotting treason (mou pan 謀叛)** — defecting to a foreign power.
4. **Contumacy (e'ni 惡逆)** — beating or plotting against parents, grandparents, husband or husband's parents.
5. **Depravity (budao 不道)** — wiping out an innocent family, dismembering victims, sorcery.
6. **Great irreverence (dabujing 大不敬)** — crimes against imperial symbols, medicine, seal, or person.
7. **Lack of filial piety (buxiao 不孝)** — neglecting parents, concealing their deaths, failing proper mourning.
8. **Discord (bumu 不睦)** — violence against senior kin.
9. **Unrighteousness (buyi 不義)** — killing one's own teacher, magistrate, or husband.
10. **Incest (neiluan 內亂)** — sexual relations with relatives or certain senior in-laws.

The first three were treated most severely. The code states: *"The woman has her husband's house; the man has his wife's chamber; and there must be no defilement on either side."*

**Punishments (the Five Punishments, wuxing 五刑).** **Flogging with a light stick** (10–50 strokes); **flogging with a heavy stick** (60–100); **penal labour** (1–3 years); **exile** (2,000–3,000 li from home, often with penal labour); **death** — either by **strangulation** (lighter, because it preserves the body intact for the ancestors) or **decapitation** (more severe). Under Tang law there were **144 capital crimes punished by strangulation** and **89 capital crimes punished by decapitation**. Beyond these: tattooing the face (ancient); dismemberment (lingering death by slow slicing, Ming and later); exposure of the head. The body-preservation principle mattered deeply: a Confucian gentleman often preferred strangulation to beheading, and suicide by a presented silk cord was a gentleman's death.

**Concealment of family crimes.** The Tang law allowed — even required — family members to conceal each other's crimes from the state. A son who turned in his father committed a graver offence than the father's original crime, because he had broken the more fundamental ritual relationship. The Tang extended this tolerance to parents-in-law, siblings-in-law, and nephews.

**Honour and shame (mianzi 面子 and lian 臉).** Two distinct concepts: **mianzi** ("face") is social reputation that can be gained, lost, given, or taken; **lian** is the internal integrity that distinguishes a moral person from one who has none. **"Losing face" (diu mianzi)** could drive a scholar to resign, a general to suicide, or a merchant to ruin himself repaying a debt of honour. **"Giving face" (gei mianzi)** was the gift of public respect, and withholding it a public rebuke. Aoli's sect politics runs on exactly this circulation — a master giving or withholding face to another sect master can start or end a war.

**Hospitality (keli 客禮)** was near-sacred. A stranger arriving at a household at nightfall must be offered shelter, food, and a place by the fire. Innkeepers had legal duties of care. Travelling scholars, Buddhist and Daoist pilgrims, itinerant martial artists and wandering knights-errant (**youxia 游俠**) moved through this hospitality network; refusing a traveller without cause was a moral offence.

**Oaths and vows (shi 誓).** Formal oaths sworn at ancestor altars, at temples, or by cutting a lock of hair and burning it bound the swearer's descendants. Blood oaths — drinking wine mixed with a drop of each swearer's blood — sealed **sworn brotherhood (bai ba zi / jie bai xiong di 結拜兄弟)**, the famous institution of the *Romance of the Three Kingdoms* **Peach Garden Oath**. Violating a blood-oath was held to bring cosmic retribution across lifetimes.

**Face-loss, suicide, and redemption.** A gentleman who had irrevocably lost face had the option of **self-execution with honour** — hanging himself on a silk cord before the Emperor's decree arrived, or falling on his own sword — which spared the family the extended punishments that followed a formal execution. Chinese history is crowded with such endings; Tang and Song literature treats them with sympathy.

**Cultural wrongs beyond the legal code.**
- **Eating beef** without good cause — an affront to agriculture.
- **Killing a swallow** — thought to nest with returning spring and to carry luck.
- **Sweeping on the first day of the New Year** — you would sweep out the year's luck.
- **Wearing white at a wedding, red at a funeral** — catastrophic category-error.
- **Cutting one's hair** in youth — disrespect to the parents who gave you the body.
- **Neglecting ancestor offerings** — spiritual abandonment that would bring ghost-illness on the descendants.
- **Insulting a teacher** — the ninth abomination's cousin.
- **Striking a mother** — unforgivable, generally resulting in execution under the fourth abomination.

**Rituals of apology and reconciliation.** Kneeling with a thorn on one's back (**fu jing qing zui 負荊請罪**, "bearing thorns and begging punishment") was a formal apology from antiquity. Cutting a lock of hair as sign of vow. Public donation to a temple. Three full prostrations at the grave of one wronged. **Aoli's cultivators, who live centuries, accumulate debts of honour that outlast any single mortal feud — and this is the raw material of a thousand wuxia stories.**

## 20. THE FEELING OF THE CULTURE

What would a traveller arriving in Tang Chang'an or Song Kaifeng actually *notice*?

**First, the scale.** A European merchant stepping through the Mingde Gate of Tang Chang'an in 750 had entered a city six miles across, home to a million people — when Paris was a muddy town of fifteen thousand, when London was barely noticed. The main avenue stretched straight into the distance wider than three jousting fields. On either side rose the stamped-earth walls of the residential wards, their gates opening and closing with the drum-watches. Guard horns announced the hour. **The city worked with the ordered precision of an imperial ritual that also happened to be a city.**

**Second, the sounds.** Wooden cart-wheels on paving stone, the cry of vendors — *"sesame cakes, hot!"*, *"melons sweet as honey!"*, *"papers for divination!"* — the **clack of abacus beads in a thousand shops**, the **chime of jade pendants on a gentleman's belt** (a trained gentleman walked at a pace that kept them rhythmically chiming), the **deep note of a bronze bell in a distant monastery**, the **sharp-sweet twang of a pipa** from a tavern window, a donkey's bray from the western market, a child reciting the Sanzijing in a schoolyard, temple drums at dawn and dusk. At night in Song Kaifeng: the watchman's clapper, the crier of the hours, distant laughter from the pleasure-quarter, the splash of an oar on the Bian canal.

**Third, the smells.** **Sandalwood and agarwood incense** trailing from temples; **frying sesame oil and green onions** from cook-stalls; **steaming dumplings and clay-pot congee**; **horse-dung and charcoal smoke** in the streets; **fresh ink and old paper** in the booksellers' alleys; **orange peel and plum wine** in the wine shops; **camphor and cloves** in the Hu-merchants' Western Market booths; **human sweat and night-soil carts** in the lanes; **peach-blossom and chrysanthemum** in the gardens; **sea-salt and shrimp paste** in the southern ports. A perfume-master could smell a gentleman's sleeve as he passed and name the aromatic: *"Ah — aloe and ambergris. He has visited the Persian shop."*

**Fourth, the sights.** **Women in high-waisted wrap-skirts with silk scarves trailing down their arms.** Men in round-collar robes with black silk caps. Buddhist monks in ochre, Daoist priests with long pinned-up hair. A **Sogdian caravan** shuffling in from the west, camels loaded with lapis and rugs, the merchants bearded and green-eyed. A **Tang imperial procession** — crimson banners, twenty outriders, six hundred infantry, the emperor's carriage curtained in yellow silk. **Tea houses with lanterns**, **paper-shop windows with calligraphy scrolls**, **pawn-shops with their triangular red flags**. A monk in meditation on a stone bench. A calligrapher, kneeling on his mat, brush poised. Two scholars at a weiqi board by the window of a garden pavilion, neither speaking for an hour. Two small children leading a water-buffalo through a bamboo grove in the rain.

**Fifth, the mood.** Cosmopolitan and confident in the High Tang — every traveller was welcome, every god was tolerable, every art was refined, and the empire believed itself to be at the centre of the universe because it genuinely was. More inward and melancholy in the Song — the empire had lost the north, tasted defeat, and turned its genius to silk and celadon, to Zhu Xi's metaphysics and Su Shi's poems about plum blossom in snow. Both eras shared the conviction that **beauty matters, that refinement is a moral act, and that the cultivated life is worth the effort of cultivation.**

**What outsiders knew them for.** Silk. Porcelain. Tea. Paper. Gunpowder. Printing. The compass. Paper money. The longest-running civil service in world history. The examination system. The Confucian classics. A writing system of staggering beauty and difficulty. The Silk Road and its wealth. Flying celestials painted on cave walls at Dunhuang. **Cultivators disappearing into the mountains** and emerging — sometimes — centuries later. Emperors who wrote poetry; poets who became emperors. A cosmic bureaucracy where every mountain had a magistrate and every river a dragon king. **A conviction, sensible to a visitor, that history is very long, very continuous, and still very much alive.**

**The feeling of the culture, in one line for Aoli:** *Time is long; the mountain remembers; the brush knows what the mouth cannot say; and whether a sword flies or stays still, it is always the cultivator's qi that decides.*
