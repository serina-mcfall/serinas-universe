## Purpose

**Serina's Vision:**
*"The Farmlands. Peasants and slaves. A powder keg. Where druid-blooded people were enslaved after the purge. They make flowers grow and fields produce abundance without knowing it's magic in the blood. Like the French Revolution building slowly. The dam will break."*

**Ardenmere — The Farmlands** — is the province of Caerndrath where the soil is rich and the peasants are not free. Its cultural DNA draws from **Medieval Anglo-French feudal society, c. 900–1300 CE** — the Norman Conquest through the High Middle Ages, before the Black Death and the peasant revolts. The land is the England and France of Domesday and *chevauchée*: manor estates, open-field farming, three-field rotation, the manorial court, the tithe barn, the harvest feast.

Underneath the feudal order, Ardenmere carries a secret: **the peasants bonded to the land are the enslaved descendants of druid-blooded refugees from Braedun** — they can make crops grow and fields flourish without knowing it is magic in their blood. The nobility still feeds on their hidden magic without naming it. It is the greatest secret of the province, hidden in plain sight.

The peasant-noble tension is a powder keg. The druid blood hides in crafts that look like farming: the hedge-witch, the shepherd whose flock never sickens, the ploughman whose furrows always yield. The Great Hawthorn — Ardenmere's pillar tree — is a peasant threshold tree, old enough to have watched the serfs become serfs.

**What matters most to Serina for Ardenmere:** the ache of bonded labour, the dignity of hidden magic, the deep green of a fertile land that doesn't belong to those who work it, the slow burn toward revolution, the hedge-witch and the ploughman who don't know they descend from druids, and the ancient Hawthorn at the crossroads waiting for the dam to break.

## 1. THE LAND AND GEOGRAPHY

### The patchwork country

Medieval England and northern France between roughly 900 and 1300 CE share a single landscape imagination: a rolling, temperate country of oak woods, open fields, slow rivers, and church spires rising above thatched roofs. This is the England of the Weald, the Cotswolds, the Severn valley, the Thames basin; the France of Île-de-France, Picardy, Normandy, Champagne, the Loire. The climate is the benign "Medieval Warm Period" (roughly 950–1250 CE), when vineyards grew as far north as Yorkshire, harvests were generous, and the European population roughly tripled.

The characteristic view from a manor window: a church tower, a cluster of thatched cruck cottages, the lord's stone hall, strips of ploughland striped like corduroy across three great open fields, common pasture beyond them, a wood of oak and beech and ash, and hedgerows lacing the whole together. The landscape is deeply humanised. Unlike the Celtic wilderness of Braedun, nearly every inch of Ardenmere-country is worked, named, fenced, tithed, taxed, and walked by someone. No piece of land is unknown to someone.

### Flora, fauna, and sacred plants

Staple crops: wheat, rye, barley, oats, peas, beans, vetches. Garden plants: onions, leeks, cabbages, garlic, parsnips, cherries, apples. Trees: **oak** (timber and pannage — pigs were fattened on acorns), **ash** (tool handles), **hazel** (coppiced for wattle), **elm**, **beech**, **willow** (hurdle-making), and above all **hawthorn** — the hedging tree that separated field from field and world from Otherworld. A hedgerow was often called a "quickset" when planted from hawthorn cuttings. Old hawthorns at crossroads and field corners were left uncut even in Christian centuries, and country people hung rags and ribbons on them — half-remembered pagan gestures the priest did not ask about.

Hawthorn in Ardenmere is not decorative — it is **memory**. The druid-blood peasants cannot say why the old thorn at the crossroads matters, only that it does. The Great Hawthorn is the survival of the druidic sacred tree in the clothes of a medieval hedgerow.

Sacred and common animals: **horses** for knights and ploughing (oxen more commonly for ploughing — a peasant plough-team was six or eight oxen), **sheep** for wool (England's fundamental wealth), **cattle** for milk and meat, **pigs** for winter meat, **chickens** and **geese** at every cottage. Wild fauna: deer (reserved to the king and nobility — poaching was a hanging offence), boar, wolves still extant in forests, foxes, hares (a peasant food, but poached), salmon in the rivers, eels in every ditch.

### Sacred geography

Where Celtic Britain held the land itself as sacred, the medieval Christian landscape was sacred through **human overlay** — every parish had a church, every crossroads a cross, every significant tree a story. But beneath the Christian grid, the older thresholds still held:

- **The parish church** — built, almost invariably, on the site of an older sacred place. Yew trees in churchyards predate the churches.
- **The holy well** — most English and French villages have or had a "holy well" dedicated to a local saint. Nine out of ten were pagan sacred springs absorbed into Christianity. Pilgrims still left pins, coins, rags.
- **The crossroads** — liminal space where the ordinary law thinned. Suicides, excommunicates, and unbaptised children were buried at crossroads. Criminals were hung at them. The fae were thought to gather there at midnight.
- **The hawthorn and the stone** — field boundaries, parish boundaries, manor boundaries were "beaten" each year by a walking procession (Rogationtide), and the boys of the parish had their heads knocked against boundary stones so they would remember the line. This is a folk-memory of older ritual.
- **The great hill, the fording place, the ancient mound** — every region had them. Many barrows and stone circles from the Neolithic still stood, ploughed around but not destroyed, because the ploughmen would not touch them.

### What this means for Ardenmere

Ardenmere is the green, cultivated, patchwork province. It is beautiful and bountiful — **and none of it belongs to the people who work it.** The hawthorn hedges that seem purely agricultural are also the last living druidic boundary-markers. The "holy wells" are pagan wells in Christian clothing. The Great Hawthorn at the crossroads is the living memory of a sacred grove — and the peasants who gather there on feast days are the descendants of its tenders, speaking the old ritual without knowing what it is.

## 2. SOCIAL STRUCTURE

### The Three Orders — the medieval story of itself

The high medieval world told one story about itself, and it told it obsessively. The story was the **Three Orders**, first articulated in the eleventh century by Gerard of Florennes, bishop of Cambrai, and Adalbero of Laon. God had ordained that humankind be divided into three estates:

- **Oratores** — "those who pray." The clergy. The Church prayed for the souls of the other two.
- **Bellatores** — "those who fight." The nobility, the knights. They defended the other two.
- **Laboratores** — "those who labour." The peasantry, the vast majority. They fed the other two.

Georges Duby's great study *The Three Orders: Feudal Society Imagined* (1978) argued that this tripartite scheme was not a neutral description but an **ideology** — a story the oratores and bellatores told about themselves to justify the arrangement that fed them. The peasantry carried the weight of the other two estates on its back, and the story said this was God's will. For a thousand years, Western Christian society largely believed it.

### The fine gradations below

Inside each order, enormous variation:

**The clergy (oratores):** Archbishops and bishops were great lords; cathedral canons lived well; abbots of the richest monasteries rivalled earls. Below them: parish priests (often the younger sons of peasants, barely literate), monks in enclosed orders, nuns, lay brothers (*conversi*, the working monks of Cistercian granges), friars (from the 13th century onwards), anchoresses walled up beside churches, hermits, and the "lollards" and wandering preachers the institutional Church could not quite control.

**The nobility (bellatores):** King → dukes and earls (counts in France) → barons → knights → esquires (landed but unknighted) → gentry. Below knighthood the lines blurred into the free yeomanry. Above the king stood, in theory, the emperor and the pope.

**The peasantry (laboratores):** This is where Ardenmere's drama lives. The peasantry was NOT a uniform mass. From most to least privileged:

- **Freeholders / free tenants (sokemen, franklins)** — free men who held land of the lord for fixed money rent. Could leave, sue, marry, and bequeath as they chose. The upper edge of peasant society. Some were nearly as wealthy as minor gentry.
- **Villeins** — the largest single class. Legally unfree (bound to the manor), but held substantial plots (a "virgate" — about 30 acres — was a standard villein holding). Owed heavy labour services on the demesne, plus rent in kind and cash. Could not leave the manor without the lord's permission. Their children inherited their unfree status. But villeins had rights — they could not be evicted so long as they performed their services, and they used the manorial court for their own disputes.
- **Bordars and cottars (cottagers)** — held a cottage and a small garden, perhaps 1–5 acres. Worked for wages on the lord's demesne or for wealthier peasants. Could be free or unfree. The precarious rural poor.
- **Landless labourers** — day labourers without any holding at all. The bottom.
- **Slaves (*servi*)** — true chattel slavery, inherited from late Roman and Anglo-Saxon practice, lingered in pockets into the twelfth century (the Domesday Book of 1086 counts about 10% of the English population as slaves). By 1200 the category had largely been absorbed into villeinage, but the idea of the "bonded" labourer persisted.

### The problem of the unfree

A villein was not quite a slave and not quite a free man. He could not be bought and sold as an individual, but he and his family were transferred with the manor if the land changed hands. He owed his lord labour services (*week-work* — usually three days per week on the demesne) plus *boon-works* (extra labour at critical seasons — ploughing, harvest) plus a slaughter of other dues: **merchet** (a fine to marry outside the manor), **heriot** (the best beast of his estate owed to the lord at his death), **leyrwite** (a fine on an unmarried daughter for fornication), **chevage** (a fee to live off the manor), **tallage** (an arbitrary tax the lord could levy almost at will). The villein ground his grain only at the lord's mill, baked only in the lord's oven, pressed his grapes only in the lord's wine press — and paid for each privilege (see *banalités*, section 3).

The line between free and unfree was the great political question of rural life. Peasant litigation repeatedly tried to prove individual families were free by birth — and lords repeatedly tried to prove the opposite. Small moves (fleeing to a chartered town for a year and a day, for instance, could confer freedom) could change a family's fate.

### What this means for Ardenmere

**The druid-blooded peasants are the bondmen of Ardenmere.** They are not the cottars; they are the **villeins** — the great mass who hold land but cannot leave, who feed the realm but cannot claim its fruits. Their hidden magic is extracted through the manorial system itself: the lord's mill grinds grain blessed by hands that do not know they bless it, the wool from their sheep is impossibly fine, the harvests on druid-blood strips come in early and full. The nobles do not know why their estates prosper. The peasants do not know why they are more alive than their masters. The hedge-witch, the ploughman, the alewife — each a villein, each a priest of an older order, each owing the lord labour they can never name.

## 3. GOVERNMENT AND POLITICS

### The feudal pyramid

Feudalism in its classic form, as Marc Bloch described it in *Feudal Society* (1939), is a system of **personal bonds of dependency** running up a steep pyramid. The king held all land in theory, and granted great estates (*fiefs*) to his greatest men — dukes, earls, counts. Each of these *tenants-in-chief* swore **homage** and **fealty** to the king. They in turn sub-infeudated portions of their land to lesser men (barons, knights), who swore homage to them. Those knights might further sub-infeudate to household knights or to free tenants.

The bond was reciprocal. The lord owed his vassal **protection** — in war, in court, in material need. The vassal owed his lord **service** — chiefly forty days of military service per year, "aid" (financial contributions for the lord's ransom, eldest son's knighting, eldest daughter's marriage, and crusade), "counsel" (attendance at the lord's court when summoned), and the great ceremonial act of **homage**: kneeling, placing his hands between the lord's hands, and declaring himself the lord's man.

Beneath this, parallel and interlocked with feudalism, ran **manorialism** — the economic system by which the peasantry was bound to the soil.

### The Norman conquest and the Domesday Book

In 1066 William the Conqueror defeated Harold Godwinson at Hastings. Within twenty years he had rebuilt English society on Norman feudal lines. The Anglo-Saxon thegn class was effectively erased; by 1086 only two Englishmen out of the two hundred greatest landowners still held their estates. William then commissioned the **Domesday Book** — completed in 1086, a county-by-county survey of nearly every manor in England, recording who held what, how many ploughs, how many villeins, how many mills, how much woodland, how many pigs. "So narrowly did he cause the survey to be made," the *Anglo-Saxon Chronicle* complained, "that there was not one single hide nor one virgate of land, nor even (shameful to tell) one ox, one cow, or one swine, that was not set down in his writ."

Domesday is the defining image of medieval administration: the conqueror's clerks writing down everything the conquered own, so nothing can be hidden. For Ardenmere, the equivalent is a great catalogue-book — perhaps kept in the royal capital or the Temple — listing every manor, every villein, every head of cattle, and therefore every hidden druid in the realm without anyone knowing it.

### Manorial court, royal court, Church court

Three parallel justice systems ran through every medieval village:

- **The manor court (hallmote / curia)** — met every three weeks or so. The lord's steward presided. The jury was a panel of the wealthier villeins. This court handled the daily business of manorial life: disputes over boundary ditches, cases of fornication, widows' dower, tenants' services, the fining of brewers whose ale was substandard, the election of reeve and hayward. This was where the peasants' lives were governed.
- **The King's court** — Henry II of England (r. 1154–1189) revolutionised royal justice with the **Assizes** (Clarendon 1166, Northampton 1176), the writ system, and the circuit judges (*justices in eyre*) who rode the country hearing pleas in the king's name. The jury as a fact-finding body evolved here. Trial by ordeal (hot iron, cold water) was effectively abolished after the Fourth Lateran Council (1215) forbade clergy to participate, leaving no sacred authority to legitimise it. In France, Philip Augustus (r. 1180–1223) and Louis IX (r. 1226–1270) built a parallel royal justice — the *Parlement of Paris* — and the *droit coutumier* (customary law compilations) began to be written down (most famously Beaumanoir's *Coutumes de Beauvaisis*, 1283).
- **Church court** — any matter touching sin, marriage, legitimacy, wills, or clergy was tried in ecclesiastical court under canon law. Church courts could fine, excommunicate, or refer to the civil arm for burning.

### Taxation and the bleeding of the peasantry

A villein family paid, in various forms, perhaps half its annual output to someone other than itself:

- **Rent** — in cash, kind, or labour.
- **Week-work** — two or three days a week on the lord's demesne, unpaid.
- **Boon-works** — extra "voluntary" labour at critical times (ploughing-boon, hay-boon, harvest-boon).
- **Banalities** (*banalités*) — fees for using the lord's **mill** (where the peasant was forbidden to grind his own flour), **oven** (where the peasant was forbidden to bake his own bread), **wine press** and sometimes **bull or boar** (*banalité de tor et ver* — only the lord could own a breeding male). The miller and baker took a cut for themselves on top of the lord's share. Peasants HATED the miller.
- **Tithe** — one tenth of all produce to the Church (see section 4).
- **Heriot** — the best beast (or best chattel) to the lord at the tenant's death.
- **Merchet, leyrwite, chevage, tallage** — as above.
- **Scutage** — the knight's alternative to military service: a cash payment in lieu of the forty days. By the thirteenth century, scutage had largely replaced actual service from knights and was a form of royal taxation in its own right.
- **Royal taxation** — aids, tenths, fifteenths, poll taxes (the poll tax of 1380 detonated the Peasants' Revolt of 1381).

### Corruption, intrigue, and the slow failure of the system

The system was riddled with rot. Stewards skimmed. Bailiffs extorted. Reeves (who were themselves peasants) played favourites. The royal justices in eyre could be bribed. Tax farmers squeezed the peasantry beyond endurance. Lords waged **private war** (officially forbidden in England, openly practised in France) over disputed inheritances. Great magnates built **affinities** — networks of retainers bound by indenture rather than feudal homage — that made late medieval France into a patchwork of private armies (see the Hundred Years' War, the *grandes compagnies*, the Armagnac-Burgundian civil war).

### What this means for Ardenmere

Ardenmere is ruled by a king (or a count, or a high lord) who sits above a pyramid of barons and knights. The land is catalogued in a great domesday-book held in the capital. Each manor has a lord, a steward, a reeve, a hayward, a bailiff. The manorial court meets under an oak or in the tithe barn. Royal justice rides through twice a year. The Church court sits above them all for matters of the soul. The peasants pay tithe, rent, week-work, boon-work, merchet, heriot, tallage, and banalities. **They pay in the magic they do not know they have.** Every sack of their grain is heavier than it should be. Every fleece weighs more. The realm is richer than its accountants can explain.

## 4. RELIGION AND SPIRITUALITY

### Christianity as the universal frame

Medieval Ardenmere-country is monolithically, fanatically, daily Christian. Western Christendom from 900 to 1300 had no competing religion at home (Jews existed in small ghettos in the towns; Muslims were the enemy overseas). The Church was not a department of life — it was the frame of life. Time was measured by the Church calendar (the day began and ended at the monastic bells; the year began with Advent; the workweek was punctuated by saints' feasts). Birth, marriage, and death were ecclesiastical sacraments. The village priest baptised, married, buried, heard confession, and absolved the dying. One in every fifty adults was in some form of religious order.

### The parish church

The parish church was the **civic unit** as much as the spiritual one. It was usually the only stone building in the village, the largest covered space, the meeting-place, the storehouse for tithe grain (in the tithe barn, often larger than the church itself). Church-ales were brewed in the church-house to raise funds for repairs. Banns were read at the church door; weddings took place there before the party moved inside; bodies lay in state in the nave; the parish feast spilled out into the churchyard.

The parish priest was often a local man — sometimes a peasant's son who had been taught enough Latin to mumble the Mass. His Latin was frequently bad. His theology might be shaky. He held **glebe land** (typically a virgate of his own to farm) and collected **tithes** (one tenth of all produce; "greater tithes" — grain, wood, hay — went to the rector, "lesser tithes" — young stock, eggs, dairy — stayed with the parish priest). He was legally required to be celibate after the Gregorian reforms of the eleventh century but in many country parishes the priest's "housekeeper" and their children were an open secret.

### The monastic alternative

Parallel to the secular church ran the monastic orders — men and women who had fled the world to save their souls by prayer and labour:

- **Benedictines (Black Monks)** — followed the Rule of St Benedict (c. 540 CE): *ora et labora*, prayer and work, with a triple vow of poverty, chastity, and obedience. The day was divided into the **eight offices** (Matins, Lauds, Prime, Terce, Sext, None, Vespers, Compline). The abbey ran its own estates, ate in silence to the reading of scripture, and slept in a common dormitory. The ideal.
- **Cluniacs** — the great reform of 910 CE at Cluny in Burgundy. Emphasised liturgy over manual labour; the Cluniac day was so crowded with elaborate sung offices that the monks had no time for farming. Cluniac houses were richly decorated, politically connected, and (to their critics) too worldly.
- **Cistercians (White Monks)** — the counter-reform. Founded at Cîteaux (1098) by those who thought Cluny had betrayed the Rule. The Cistercians fled to remote valleys — Fountains, Rievaulx, Clairvaux — and built severe, unadorned, pointed-arched churches. They revived manual labour, using **lay brothers** (*conversi*) to work the vast granges of land they brought under cultivation. The Cistercians became Europe's great sheep-farmers, hydraulic engineers, and agricultural pioneers. Bernard of Clairvaux (1090–1153) was their great voice.
- **Friars** — from the 13th century: Franciscans (the Grey Friars, founded 1209 by Francis of Assisi) and Dominicans (the Black Friars, founded 1216 by Dominic de Guzmán). Unlike monks, friars lived *in* the world — preaching, begging, hearing confessions in the towns. They were the urban preaching arm of the Church.

Monasteries were hospitals, inns, libraries, schools, banks, landlords, and courts of refuge. They copied books. They kept the accounts. They buried the kings. They were the Church's economic engine and its cultural archive.

### Pilgrimage

Medieval Christians believed the physical body mattered — and so did the physical places where holiness had been. A pilgrimage was a physical journey to a relic or shrine, undertaken to expiate sin, fulfil a vow, seek a cure, or deepen faith. The great pilgrimage routes:

- **Santiago de Compostela** (Galicia, Spain) — the shrine of St James the Great. Stations across France and Spain. Pilgrim's scallop shell badge.
- **Rome** — the shrines of Peter and Paul. Jubilee years.
- **Jerusalem** — the ultimate, increasingly impossible after 1187.
- **Canterbury** — after 1170, the shrine of Thomas Becket, martyred in his own cathedral by knights of Henry II. Chaucer's *Canterbury Tales* (1387) sets itself on this pilgrimage.
- **Walsingham** (Norfolk) — the English Loreto, a shrine of the Virgin.
- **Mont-Saint-Michel** (Normandy) — the great tidal monastery.

Pilgrims wore distinct clothing, carried staves, and came home with **pilgrim badges** (lead tokens) pinned to their hats and cloaks.

### The thin older substrate

Beneath the Christian monopoly, older things persisted. People blessed wells. They hung rags on hawthorn trees. They beat boundaries. They feared the **fae** — the fairy folk, the *gens de petite foi* — at crossroads and on Midsummer's Eve. They made May garlands and danced round fires on St John's Eve. The Church absorbed what it could (St Brigid's Day = Imbolc; All Saints = Samhain) and rebuked what it could not. Cunning-folk and hedge-witches practised healing charms that were half paternoster and half older mumble.

### The afterlife

The medieval soul went, after death, to:
- **Heaven** — for saints and the fully shriven
- **Hell** — for the damned, the unrepentant, heretics, suicides
- **Purgatory** — for almost everyone else, a place of purifying fire where souls worked off their unrequited sins through torment. The length of Purgatory could be shortened by masses said for the dead — hence the Catholic Church's vast industry in **chantries**, **obits**, and **prayers for the dead**. The wealthy endowed chapels where priests would pray for their souls in perpetuity.

### What this means for Ardenmere

Ardenmere is monolithically Solenthéran (or whatever the Tyramare-exported state church is called in the world), with **parish churches** at the heart of every village, tithe barns beside them, and a pilgrimage route that runs through the province to some great shrine. The **monastic orders** own much of the best land — Cistercian-like granges where silent lay brothers farm wool and the sheep yield impossibly fine fleeces (druid blood in the shepherds again). **Beneath the orthodox faith, the older rites survive in peasant hands** — the blessing of fields, the May garland, the rag on the hawthorn. The priest does not ask. The peasants do not remember they once knew. **This is the great secret: the state faith lives off the suppressed druidic remnant, which it has criminalised at the top and preserved at the bottom.**

## 5. FAMILY AND KINSHIP

### The nuclear family — Hanawalt's argument

Barbara Hanawalt's *The Ties That Bound* (1986), using coroners' inquests, manorial court rolls, and poll tax records, demonstrated that the medieval English peasant family was **overwhelmingly nuclear, not extended**. A household typically consisted of husband, wife, unmarried children, and perhaps one elderly parent or an unmarried sibling. Extended kin lived nearby — often in neighbouring cottages — but not under the same roof. The average completed family had four to six surviving children; infant mortality was crushing (perhaps a third of children died before age five, another sixth before fifteen).

This is different from the Celtic extended kin-group and different from the Mediterranean "great house" family. The Anglo-French peasant household was small, bounded, and fierce in its mutual obligations.

### Gender roles — division of labour

The medieval rural economy was built on a **sharp sexual division of labour** but also on **deep mutual dependency**. A peasant man without a wife could not run a farm; a peasant woman without a husband could not, either. Widowhood was a routine crisis requiring rapid remarriage (or the widow's formal taking-up of the "free bench" — her right to keep a portion of her late husband's holding as long as she remained chaste and didn't remarry).

**Men's work:** ploughing (nearly always), sowing, mowing, threshing, heavy carting, timber work, the ox-team, the heavy forge, warfare, hunting.

**Women's work:** dairying (milking, butter, cheese), poultry, kitchen garden, pig-raising, brewing ale (this was overwhelmingly female labour until the late Middle Ages), spinning (nearly every free hour a woman was not cooking, she was spinning — this is why "spinster" came to mean unmarried woman), weaving (until the mechanical loom turned weaving into male guild labour in towns), baking bread (at the lord's oven), preserving, salting, candle-making, washing, child-rearing.

**Shared work:** harvest. At harvest every able-bodied person in the parish, male and female, adult and child, worked in the fields. Gender roles dissolved at harvest and at threshing. Gleaning — the right of the poor to glean what was left in the fields after the harvest — was largely women's and children's work.

### Women's legal position

Under English common law, a married woman was *feme covert* — her legal identity subsumed into her husband's. She could not sue, sign contracts, or own property independently of him. An **unmarried or widowed** woman (*feme sole*) had much more legal freedom — she could hold property, sue and be sued, run a business, and in towns even join certain guilds.

Despite coverture, peasant women's economic contribution was openly recognised. The **alewife / brewster** was in most villages a woman; medieval English court rolls are full of women fined for brewing substandard ale (this is how we know so many women were brewers). The **midwife** was always a woman. Female weavers, fullers, spinsters, and dairywomen kept the rural economy running.

At the aristocratic level, noblewomen could be enormously powerful — particularly as **regents** for absent husbands or young sons. Empress Matilda (1102–1167) fought a civil war for the English crown. Eleanor of Aquitaine (1122–1204) was queen of France and then of England, mother of Richard the Lionheart and John, and held her own court at Poitiers. Blanche of Castile (1188–1252) ruled France twice as regent for her son Louis IX. The medieval aristocratic wife was not a decorative object — she was an administrator, a diplomat, an estate-runner, and in her husband's absence an effective sovereign.

### Marriage — the economics

Peasant marriage was a **partnership in a working enterprise**. A woman brought her dowry (bedding, kitchen goods, perhaps a cow, perhaps a strip of land), a man brought his holding or his trade. The couple consented at the church door (the wedding was exchanged outside the church, the mass was inside afterwards), and cohabitation made the marriage real in the eyes of the Church. Canon law required only free mutual consent; parental permission was theoretically unnecessary, though disobedience to parents over marriage was a social disaster.

A villein could not marry outside the manor without paying **merchet** to the lord. A peasant family's marriages were the largest investment of their lifetime. Delayed marriage was common — the average age at first marriage in late medieval England was around 24 for women and 26 for men, which is a much older "European Marriage Pattern" than is often assumed.

### Inheritance — primogeniture and its discontents

Under the dominant Anglo-French feudal regime, land descended by **primogeniture**: the entire estate to the eldest legitimate son. If there was no son, it was divided between daughters as co-heiresses; if there were no children, it passed to the nearest male kinsman.

Primogeniture solved one problem and created others. It kept great estates intact — crucial to the feudal order. But it produced an entire class of **landless younger sons** who had to make their way as knights-errant, crusaders, clerics, tournament fighters, lawyers, or mercenaries. Much of the romance-literature mood of twelfth-century France is the longing and rage of younger sons.

Among peasants, custom varied. Some manors practised primogeniture; others **ultimogeniture** ("borough English," the youngest son inherits, on the logic that elder sons have already been set up in life); a few practised partible inheritance. Daughters typically inherited only through dowry.

### Fosterage, apprenticeship, and servant-hood

Children left home young. Noble children were fostered out to other noble households from about age seven, to learn courtesy and arms at another lord's court — a system Anglo-Norman nobility had inherited partly from Celtic fosterage practice. Peasant children were sent into **service** in other households from age ten or twelve — the "life-cycle servant" typical of northwest Europe. Sons were apprenticed to craftsmen for seven years (see section 11). Daughters went into dairy, alehouse, or gentry household service. This produced a large, mobile, adolescent-young-adult population passing between households before marriage.

### Elders and the retired

Older peasants who gave up their holdings to an heir often signed a **maintenance agreement** — a formal contract recorded in the manorial court. The heir undertook to provide the retired parent with food, fuel, clothing, and a place by the fire for the rest of their life. These contracts were minutely specific ("a bushel of wheat and half a bushel of rye every month, one new pair of shoes a year, free access to the hearth") because elders who were cheated had nowhere else to go.

### What this means for Ardenmere

Ardenmere's peasant household is **nuclear, hard-working, gender-differentiated, and tightly bound to the manorial economy**. The wife brews, spins, keeps the dairy; the husband ploughs and goes to the boon-works. At harvest they stand shoulder to shoulder in the fields. The eldest son will inherit — the younger may go for a soldier, a priest, or a vagabond. A grandmother in her corner of the hearth has signed a maintenance-agreement in the manor court, and her words to the children are stories no one has written down. **The druid-blood tends to run through the women** — alewife, midwife, hedge-witch, dairywoman — because these are the trades that have always been female and have always dealt with the hidden forces of fermentation, birth, milk, and herb.

## 6. LIFE CYCLE RITUALS

### Birth and baptism

Childbirth took place at home with the **midwife** and a circle of female kin and neighbours — the "gossips" (*god-sibs*, spiritual kin). Men were excluded. The midwife was often a cunning-woman as well: she said prayers, burned certain herbs, handled the delivery, and tied the umbilical cord. Dangerous births carried brutal odds. If the mother was dying with the baby alive inside her, the midwife was licensed to perform emergency caesarean to save the child's soul — and to baptise it herself if no priest could be fetched in time.

**Baptism** was the urgent sacrament. Medieval theology held that an unbaptised child went to **Limbo** at best or Hell at worst. So infants were carried to the church within a day or two of birth. The baptismal font stood near the church door — the symbolism was pointed: the child was *entering* the church. **Godparents** were named (two godfathers and a godmother for a boy, two godmothers and a godfather for a girl) and a saint's name was chosen. Godparenthood created a spiritual kinship that was a real social bond for life.

**Churching of women** — after childbirth a woman was ritually "unclean" for forty days. Her return to church for the **Churching** rite re-integrated her into the community. This was a women's ceremony, attended by her gossips; the priest met her at the church door with a candle and prayers.

### Naming

Names in medieval Anglo-French society were a narrow pool. By 1300 in England, about half of all men were called William, John, or Robert; among women, Alice, Matilda, and Joan dominated. Norman names (William, Henry, Richard, Robert, Hugh, Geoffrey, Walter, Ralph) supplanted Anglo-Saxon names (Edward, Edgar, Alfred, Ethelred — survived mostly in saint cults) in the generations after 1066. French influence was everywhere. Peasant naming borrowed from the nobility with a lag.

### Coming of age

There was no single rite of passage for peasants equivalent to aristocratic knighting. The important transitions were economic:
- **Age seven** — the "age of reason"; children began serious work and could be criminally responsible.
- **Age twelve for girls, fourteen for boys** — canonical age of marriage; girls considered nubile; boys could be knighted at the same age, though effective knighting was usually closer to eighteen or twenty-one.
- **First communion / confirmation** — the sacramental entry into adult church life.
- **Apprenticeship or service** — leaving home, around ten to twelve, into a seven-year apprenticeship or life-cycle service.

For the aristocracy, the great rite was **knighting** — the *accolade* or dubbing. A noble boy began as a **page** at another lord's court from age seven, learning courtesy, serving at table, being taught letters and arms. At fourteen he became a **squire** — attending a particular knight, caring for his armour and horses, and following him into battle as a shield-bearer. At twenty-one, if he had proved himself, he could be **dubbed**. The ceremony developed over the twelfth and thirteenth centuries into an elaborate vigil (bath, white robe, night in the chapel), followed by the accolade (a blow from the flat of the lord's sword on the shoulder) and the receipt of spurs and sword. Only a knight could dub another knight.

### Marriage

Marriage happened at the church door — *in facie ecclesiae*, "in the face of the church." The priest met the couple outside, the vows were exchanged, dowry and dower publicly declared, and the wedding party then moved inside for the **Nuptial Mass**. Banns (announcements of intention to marry) were read for three successive Sundays in the parish church of each party to allow anyone who knew an impediment to speak up.

The wedding feast was the great peasant celebration. A villein wedding ran for a day and a night; a noble wedding for days. Gifts were exchanged. The bride wore her best clothes (white was not yet standard; she wore whatever fine garment she had). Fertility rituals (throwing of grain, blessings of the marriage bed) punctuated the day.

For nobles, marriages were dynastic arrangements made by kin councils. Child betrothals were common among the aristocracy — Matilda was betrothed at eight and married the Holy Roman Emperor at twelve. For peasants, real consent mattered more, and the delayed marriage pattern (mid-twenties) meant most peasant brides and grooms were adults choosing their own partners within the narrow circle of their parish.

### Death, burial, and mourning

Medieval Europeans did not run from death — death was constant, familial, and ritualised.

When a person was dying, family and neighbours gathered. The priest was sent for to administer the **Last Rites** — Confession, the **Eucharist** (Viaticum — "for the road"), and **Extreme Unction** (anointing with holy oil on eyes, ears, lips, hands, feet). Prayers for a good death were recited. A crucifix was held in the dying person's hands. The passing bell tolled — three times for a man, two for a woman — so the parish could pray for the departing soul.

After death, the body was washed by women of the family and wrapped in a **shroud** of plain linen. The corpse lay in the home for a day or two — visited by neighbours, watched over all night, surrounded by candles. Peasants were **not coffined**; they were carried to the churchyard wrapped only in the shroud and laid in a shallow grave. Only the wealthy were buried in coffins or stone tombs. The grave was dug in the consecrated ground of the **churchyard** — the central communal space of village life, where the dead lay literally among the living.

A **Requiem Mass** was said. The deceased's best beast went to the lord (heriot), and the second-best to the priest (mortuary fee). **Month's mind** masses (a month after death) and **year's mind** anniversaries were commemorated. The wealthy endowed **chantries** — chapels with priests saying daily masses in perpetuity for the repose of their souls. Everyone believed in Purgatory; everyone believed masses shortened its duration.

Grief was supposed to be contained. Ostentatious wailing and lament were forbidden by councils. Mourning clothes were dark. Widows wore full mourning veils and black gowns for a year.

### Festivals of the year

The medieval year ran by church festivals that mapped almost exactly onto the agricultural year:

- **Christmas** (25 Dec, twelve days to Epiphany) — midwinter, Yule-log, feast, lord's hospitality. One of four quarter days.
- **Plough Monday** (first Monday after Epiphany) — the return to ploughing. Ploughs blessed at the church.
- **Candlemas** (2 Feb) — Purification of the Virgin. Candles blessed. Ewes lambing.
- **Lent** (forty days of fast before Easter) — no meat, no dairy, no eggs for the pious.
- **Shrove Tuesday** — last feast before Lent. Pancakes.
- **Easter** — the great feast. Resurrection. Eggs blessed.
- **Lady Day** (25 March, Feast of the Annunciation) — quarter day. Old New Year.
- **Rogationtide** — three days before Ascension. The **beating of the bounds** procession, walking the parish boundaries, blessing crops.
- **May Day / Beltane** (1 May) — maypole, garlands, greenery. Much older than its Christian overlay.
- **Midsummer / St John's Eve** (23–24 June) — bonfires, vigils. Quarter day.
- **Lammas** (1 Aug) — "loaf-mass." The first loaf of the new wheat harvest blessed in church. Beginning of harvest.
- **Michaelmas** (29 Sept) — the great **harvest feast**. End of harvest. Geese eaten. Rents paid, tenancies renewed, the hayward's and reeve's year ended and new officers elected. Quarter day.
- **All Saints / All Souls / Samhain** (31 Oct – 2 Nov) — the dead remembered.
- **Martinmas** (11 Nov) — the **slaughter**. Livestock that could not be overwintered were slaughtered, salted, and hung in the rafters to smoke. Fresh blood pudding, offal. Winter began.

### What this means for Ardenmere

Ardenmere's year is the church year, overlaid on the fields. Babies are carried to the font at the church door; marriages happen at that door; bodies are carried out to the graveyard beside it. **Every great festival is both a church feast and an older peasant feast** — Lammas is a druidic harvest in Christian clothes, Beltane survives in the May garlands, the boundary-beating of Rogationtide is a pagan circuit-ritual with a crucifix at its head. The Great Hawthorn, in Ardenmere, is the natural gathering-point for every one of these old-substrate festivals. The priest does not go there. The peasants do.

## 7. CLOTHING AND ADORNMENT

### The visible language of class

Medieval clothing was the most efficient **class-marker** in daily life. A glance at someone's gown, shoes, and head-covering told you everything: their rank, trade, marital status, parish, whether they were widowed or maiden, whether they were travelling, whether they were in mourning. The sumptuary laws of the fourteenth century (in England, from 1337 onwards) tried to legislate what cloth and colour each class could wear. Farmers, the 1327 ordinance said, were forbidden to wear dyed garb; their fabrics had to remain undyed wool and linen.

### Peasant dress

**Men.** The foundation was the **linen shirt** or *chemise* — the undergarment, next to skin, washable. Over this a wool **tunic** (called *cotte*) to the knee or mid-calf, belted at the waist. On the legs, **braies** (loose drawers) and **hose** (wool leggings tied to the waistband of the braies). On the feet, **turnshoes** — leather shoes made inside-out and turned right-way-round, with simple leather soles that wore out quickly and were patched endlessly. Many peasants went barefoot in summer.

For working in fields: a **hooded cape** or *cotehardie* against rain, a straw hat in summer, a **wool gown** (*surcote*) in winter. In cold: a sheepskin or goat-skin over the shoulders. Leather gloves for specific work.

Colours were muted: **undyed wool** in every shade from cream to dark brown to near-black (this was what was available cheaply — dyed cloth was a luxury). Dyes that peasants could obtain: **woad** (blue), **madder** (red-orange), **weld** (yellow), walnut husks (brown), oak galls (dark). Bright colours and multi-colour patterns were restricted by law and cost to gentry and above.

**Women.** The **chemise** underneath, as for men. Over it, a wool **kirtle** (long-sleeved, ankle-length, laced or pinned to fit), and over the kirtle in colder weather a **surcote** or **overdress** (often sleeveless, sometimes hooded). On the head, the **wimple** — a linen cloth wrapped around the throat and over the head, worn by all married women from about 1100 onwards; a **veil** or **coif** for maidens. Hair was always covered in public — uncovered hair was associated with unmarried virgins, brides on their wedding day, and shamed women. On the feet, turnshoes or pattens (wooden overshoes for mud).

A woman's working apron (a linen cloth tied at the waist) was ubiquitous. Her distaff (for spinning with flax or wool) hung at her side so she could spin while walking or standing — "the tongue and the distaff are a woman's constant companions," the saying ran.

Pregnancy was accommodated by **adjusting the kirtle's lacing** rather than by special maternity dress.

### Knightly and noble dress

Nobles wore the same basic garments — **chemise, cotte, surcote, hose** — but in dyed wool, silk, fine linen, with fur trim, embroidery, and elaborate cut.

**Furs** were a crucial rank-marker: **ermine** (winter weasel) was for royalty and the greatest nobles; **miniver** (grey squirrel back) for lesser nobles; **budge** (lambskin) for gentry; **rabbit** and **cat fur** for the middling sort. Sumptuary laws specified exactly who could wear what fur.

**Dyes** for the nobility: **scarlet** (the most prized red, from *kermes* insects — a peasant could not afford it in a year's wages), **purple** (Tyrian, nearly royal); bright **blues** and **greens** from imported dyes. Silk came from Mediterranean trade — Byzantine and Chinese silks made it up the river routes. Cloth of gold for ceremonial garments.

**The cote-hardie** — a close-fitting, buttoned long tunic of the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries — and the **houppelande** — a flowing long robe with huge sleeves — were peak aristocratic fashion by the fourteenth century. Shoes became increasingly pointed: the **poulaine** (pointed-toe shoe) grew ridiculous in the fourteenth century and had to be tied up with chains. Sumptuary laws tried (and failed) to regulate toe length.

### Women's fine dress

Aristocratic women wore the **bliaut** (twelfth century — a tight-laced gown with a long swept skirt), then the **cote-hardie** (fitted, buttoned), then elaborate **houppelandes** and over-gowns of silk and embroidered velvet by the fourteenth century. Headdresses escalated from the wimple and barbette (early period) to the **horned hennin**, **crespine** (netted cage for hair), **steeple hennin**, and elaborate constructed headdresses of the later Middle Ages.

Girdles (belts) with silver-gilt buckles; rings; brooches (crucial for fastening a cloak at the throat); necklaces; **paternosters** (prayer-bead chains, the ancestor of the rosary) at the waist.

### Clerical dress

Parish priests wore a **cassock** (long black robe) in daily life, a **surplice** (short white over-robe) and **stole** when performing sacraments. Monks wore the full habit of their order — black for Benedictines and Cluniacs, undyed natural wool (appearing white) for Cistercians, brown for Franciscans, white and black for Dominicans. Nuns' habits were similar. The **tonsure** — the shaved crown of the head — was mandatory for all male religious.

### What clothing communicated

- **Married vs unmarried (women)** — wimple and covered hair vs veil or loose hair.
- **Class** — fabric (linen/wool vs silk/velvet), colour (undyed vs dyed), fur trim, length of gown, ornament.
- **Trade** — the miller's white hat; the baker's; the smith's leather apron; the mason's tool belt; the pilgrim's scallop shell and staff.
- **Mourning** — dark, undyed cloth, widow's barbette.
- **Religious vocation** — habit, tonsure.
- **Guild** — livery colours.

### What this means for Ardenmere

In Ardenmere, the peasants wear **undyed wool and linen** — cream, brown, grey, dim green — the colours of the fields themselves. A hedge-witch is indistinguishable from any other peasant widow at a glance: black wimple, grey kirtle, undyed apron, turnshoes. The nobles flash **scarlet, cloth-of-gold, miniver and ermine** against the green country — their clothing a display of how much they extract. Sumptuary laws keep the druid-blood peasants in undyed cloth, forbidding them the colours their ancestors wove. **The old ritual garments — the green linen, the rag on the thorn, the wildflower garland — are half-remembered in May Day and in the peasants' Sunday best.**

## 8. FOOD AND DRINK

### The peasant diet — bread, pottage, ale

Bread, pottage, ale. This is the medieval peasant trinity. Christopher Dyer's studies of medieval English living standards (*Everyday Life in Medieval England*, *Making a Living in the Middle Ages*) have rebuilt in detail what a villein actually ate.

**Bread** was the centre of the diet. A peasant consumed perhaps two to three pounds of bread per day — easily half of caloric intake. But the bread was rarely **white wheaten bread** (that was a luxury for the lord's table). Peasant bread was:

- **Maslin** — a mixed loaf of wheat and rye together. The commonest peasant bread in central England and northern France. Dark, dense, hearty, slightly sour.
- **Rye bread** — heavier, darker, the staple of poorer households and of the north.
- **Barley bread** — coarse, greyish, the bread of the poorest.
- **Oat bread / oatcake** — in the north and west, where oats grew best.
- **Bean or pea bread** — in famine years, flour was eked out with ground pulses.

The lord's table ate **simnel** (fine white wheaten) or **wastel** (next-best white). The parish priest might eat maslin most days and wastel at Easter.

**Pottage** was the everyday stewed meal — vegetables, grain, herbs, sometimes a bone, simmered endlessly in a cauldron over the hearth and never fully emptied. A pot might bubble for weeks, replenished daily. The base was often **oats or barley** cooked to mush with whatever was available: onions, leeks, cabbage, peas, beans, turnips, parsnips, nettles, sorrel, dandelion greens, wild herbs. A **pottage** was modest; a **frumenty** (cracked wheat with milk, almonds, honey, spice) was festive.

**Ale** was the universal drink. Water was dangerous (especially near towns); milk was mostly processed into cheese or butter for sale; ale — **brewed unhopped from malted barley, weakly alcoholic, drunk within days of brewing before it soured** — was safe, nourishing, and cheap. Every household with a woman of brewing age brewed ale. When a household brewed more than it needed, the householder hung a bush on a pole outside the door — the origin of the "alestake" — and neighbours could come and buy. The **alewife** (*brewster*) was one of the great female trades. Beer (hopped) did not replace ale in England until the late fifteenth century.

### Meat, fish, and dairy

Meat was rarer than modern readers assume. The poorest villeins ate meat only a few times a year — at the slaughter (Martinmas), at the harvest feast, at a wedding. Better-off villeins and free tenants ate **salt pork** and **bacon** through the winter (the pig in the rafters was the peasant's protein bank). **Chicken and eggs** — from the household fowl — were commoner than red meat. **Mutton** was eaten as sheep aged out of wool production. **Beef** was expensive; **venison** was forbidden to anyone but the lord (poaching was capital).

**Fish** became huge during **Lent** — no meat, no dairy, no eggs for forty days. **Salt cod** and **salt herring** from the North Sea and the Hanseatic trade stocked the winter; **freshwater fish** (pike, perch, carp, trout, eel) were farmed in manorial fishponds. Every great monastery had fishponds; many manors did too. An eel pie was a luxury. A pickled herring was ordinary Lent food.

**Dairy** — cheese, butter, buttermilk — was called **white meat** and was peasant food. The rich ate cheese only as a dessert course or as a preserved luxury (aged hard cheese travelled well). Butter was salted for keeping. Milk was mostly processed into cheese for storage; fresh milk was a children's drink when available.

### Herbs, seasoning, sweet

Pepper, cinnamon, cloves, ginger, nutmeg, saffron — these **spices** were imported from the East through Venice and Genoa, astronomically expensive, used extravagantly at noble tables to signal wealth. A noble feast dish might be spiced until barely recognisable as meat; this was the point. Peasants could not afford imported spice.

Peasants used **herbs** from the garden and hedgerow: parsley, sage, rosemary, thyme, chives, mint, wild garlic, fennel, dill, savory, hyssop, tansy. **Salt** — essential for preserving — was cheaper than spice but still a real expenditure.

**Sweetness** came from **honey** (there were bees in nearly every village — the monasteries kept huge apiaries for beeswax candles; the honey was a by-product) and from **fruit** — cherries, plums, apples, pears, wild strawberries, blackberries. **Sugar** was a medicinal rarity from the crusader East. **Mead** (honey fermented) was a rural alternative to ale.

### Feasting and hospitality

A **great feast** (noble wedding, royal visit, holy day) was political theatre. Lords showed their power by the number of guests fed. A feast ran to many **courses** and might include:

- **Pottages and soups** to begin.
- **Boiled and roasted meats** in variety — capons, partridges, a whole sucking pig, perhaps a swan (serjeanty of swan-keepers existed), peacock redressed in its feathers.
- **Sotelties** — elaborate sculpted sugar or pastry pieces, often allegorical.
- **Pies** — fish pies in Lent, venison pies at Christmas, a pie of songbirds.
- **Bread and cheese** to close, with sweet wines or spiced **hippocras**.

**Hospitality** was a sacred duty. A lord was honour-bound to feed any arriving guest, any wandering monk, any pilgrim — and the poor at the gate. On feast days the lord often held an **open-gate feast** for his peasants, who came and ate salt pork and pease pottage in the great hall. This was part of the reciprocal myth of feudalism: we serve you, you feed us at Christmas.

### Fasting

Roughly **one day in three of the medieval year was a fast day**. All Fridays, Saturdays (sometimes), the whole of Advent, the whole of Lent (forty days), the vigils of great feasts. A fast meant no meat, no dairy, no eggs — fish only. This produced a huge medieval fishing industry and strange culinary compromises (beaver's tail was declared "fish" because it lived in water).

### What this means for Ardenmere

Ardenmere's peasants eat **maslin bread, pease pottage, and household ale**. A pot of pottage bubbles on every hearth. **The alewife brews.** At Martinmas the pig is slaughtered and hung from the rafters to smoke. The lord's table has **wheaten wastel, roast meats, fish in Lent, and spice from beyond the sea** — the spice trade running through Tyramare. At the Michaelmas harvest feast the lord's gate stands open for his peasants to eat salt pork and drink his ale.

**The peasant food is the true food of Ardenmere.** It is what the druid blood still eats. And the subtle magic in it — the ale that ferments just a little too reliably, the bread that keeps a little too well, the pottage that fills a little too fully — is why the peasant children grow tall and strong in a province where the lord's children sicken. The nobility do not understand. The peasant women do not understand. Only the reader understands: the druid-blood is in the grain and the barm and the hearth-pot.

## 9. ARCHITECTURE AND SETTLEMENT

### The English village — shape on the ground

The archetypal English medieval village was a **nucleated settlement** — twenty to fifty houses clustered along a road or around a green, with the parish church at one end and the lord's manor house at the other. Radiating out from the village were the **open fields**, divided into strips, then the **meadow** (hay), the **common** (grazing), and the **waste** or **woodland** (pannage for pigs, firewood, timber). A village's fields ran in three great blocks, each rotated yearly: one sown with winter wheat or rye, one with spring barley/oats/peas, one lying **fallow** (see section 11 on three-field rotation).

The **village green** was a communal space: the site of the May Pole, the parish stocks and pillory, the wells, the public hearth, games of football (a brutal, almost anything-goes village game). The blacksmith's forge was on or near the green. The alewives hung their alestakes on the front of their cottages.

The **church** — invariably of stone — dominated. Its tower was the village's landmark, visible across the fields. Beside it stood the **tithe barn** (enormous — the Great Coxwell tithe barn in Oxfordshire is 152 feet long and still standing) where the parish's tithe grain was stored. The **churchyard** wrapped around the church, the living tread where the dead lay.

The **manor house** — timber-framed in the early period, stone by the twelfth century — stood separately, often with a moat, a gatehouse, a great **hall** as its heart, a **solar** (private chamber) for the lord's family, a **buttery** and **pantry**, **kitchens** (separated from the hall for fire safety), **stables**, **granaries**, a **dovecote** (the right to keep pigeons was restricted to lords), a **fishpond**, and a **garden**.

### Peasant houses

The medieval peasant house was most often a **cruck-framed** or **aisled-timber** structure. Two pairs of **cruck blades** — massive curved oak timbers, each pair cut from the same naturally curved tree and joined at the apex — formed the roof skeleton. Between them, the walls were built of **wattle-and-daub** (hazel rods woven horizontally through vertical staves, then plastered with a mix of clay, straw, dung, and hair) on low stone footings, and the roof was **thatched** with straw or reed.

The commonest peasant dwelling was the **longhouse**: a rectangular building with two rooms at one end for the family (an inner "bower" for sleeping and an outer "hall" with the hearth) and a **byre** at the other end for the animals, separated from the human living space by a low cross-passage. The humans were warmed by the heat of the livestock. The roof had no chimney; smoke from the central hearth rose to a gap at the ridge or wound out through the thatch. Everything — clothes, food, lungs — carried the smell of wood smoke.

Better-off villein houses had more rooms: a **hall** with the hearth (where the family ate and where guests were received), a **chamber** (solar) for the householder, a **buttery** for drink, a **pantry** for bread. Wealthier yeoman houses had **upper chambers** reached by ladders.

Poorer cottagers had a single-room **cott** — sixteen feet by twelve, one door, a smoke hole, a beaten-earth floor.

### Furnishings

A peasant house held very little. The **hearth** was the centre — an open stone ring or flat hearthstone with a cauldron hung over it on an iron trammel. A **trestle table** (boards on trestles, dismantled when not in use), **benches** along the walls, a **chest** for clothing and valuables, a **spinning wheel** or **spindle and distaff**, the cooking pot, wooden bowls and spoons (wooden, rarely horn; no pottery for daily use — an earthenware pot broke too easily), a **salt-cellar**, perhaps a **candlestick** (rushlights more commonly), straw-filled mattresses on the floor at night and rolled into a corner by day. Windows were small, shuttered, unglazed. The door bar dropped at night.

### The manor house and the hall

The **hall** — the great central room of the manor house — was the heart of aristocratic domestic life. A single high-roofed space, sometimes with a central **open hearth** (smoke rising to the louvred lantern in the roof), later with a **wall fireplace** and **chimney**, it was where the lord ate with his household, heard petitions, held manorial court, and received guests. The **high table** stood at the dais end on a raised platform. The **screens passage** at the service end separated the hall from buttery, pantry, and kitchens.

Above the high table, often, ran a **solar** — the lord's private chamber, reached by a stair, lit by larger windows, furnished with the family bed (curtained, canopied), chests of linen, perhaps a prie-dieu for private prayer. By the twelfth century aristocratic families were increasingly withdrawing from the hall into the solar for privacy. This shift — from the open hall to the private chamber — runs through medieval domestic architecture like a slow tide.

### Castle and cathedral

**Castles.** The Normans came to England with the **motte-and-bailey** — an earthen mound (motte) topped with a wooden keep, beside a fortified courtyard (bailey). By the twelfth century these were being rebuilt in stone — the great **square stone keeps** of the Norman period (Rochester, Dover, the Tower of London's White Tower). By the thirteenth century, concentric castle design (Edward I's Welsh castles, notably Caerphilly, Conwy, Beaumaris) had produced some of the most advanced fortifications in the world.

**Cathedrals.** In the twelfth century, French architecture underwent a revolution. **Abbot Suger** (1081–1151), rebuilding the abbey church of **Saint-Denis** near Paris between 1140 and 1144, combined **pointed arches, ribbed vaults, and flying buttresses** to allow higher, thinner walls pierced by vast windows of stained glass. The Gothic was born. In the next two centuries it swept Europe: **Chartres** (1194 onwards), **Reims** (1211), **Amiens** (1220), **Notre-Dame de Paris** (1163), **Canterbury's Trinity Chapel** (1175), **Salisbury** (1220), **Westminster** (1245). Gothic cathedrals were expressions of theology in stone and glass — their light was meant to be the light of heaven. Suger wrote of being "transported from the material world to the immaterial" in their light.

### Settlement patterns and village layout

Not every village was the nucleated-church-and-green type. Other patterns:
- **Nucleated villages** — the classic English Midland type.
- **Dispersed hamlets** — common in Wales, southwest England, and much of Normandy and Brittany; scattered farmsteads, no central village.
- **Bastides** — planned grid-pattern towns founded in southwest France in the thirteenth century (mainly along the disputed English-French border) for defensive and economic reasons.
- **Monastic villages** — the Cistercian **grange**, a working farm run by lay brothers, might have no lay population at all.

### Building materials and techniques

Most medieval building was **timber and thatch**. Stone was reserved for church, castle, manor house, tithe barn, bridge. Local stone defined local architecture: the Cotswold oolitic limestone (honey-gold), the Kentish ragstone, the flint of East Anglia, the red sandstone of the West Midlands, the chalk of Wessex. In France, Caen limestone (which the Normans also shipped to England for castle-building), the white stone of Paris, the granite of Brittany.

Techniques: **wattle-and-daub** for peasant walls; **half-timbering** (exposed oak frame, plastered infill) for better houses; **ashlar** (dressed stone) for cathedrals and keeps; **rubble-and-mortar** for castle walls.

### What this means for Ardenmere

Ardenmere is the land of **thatched villages clustered around grey stone churches**, of **manor houses at the village's far end**, of **open fields striped with strips to the horizon**, of the **Great Hawthorn at the crossroads** — older than anything else in sight. At the province's heart stands a Gothic cathedral built in the imperial style, its glass burning with colour. The peasant cottages are cruck-framed, wattle-and-daub, thatched, smoke-hazed. The lord's manor has a hall, a solar, a chapel, a gatehouse, and a dovecote — the dovecote especially, because doves are the lord's right alone. **The landscape of Ardenmere is the great green English Midlands stretched over a French feudal grid — and every peasant cottage has a bundle of ash and rowan pinned above the lintel, the way hedge-witches still do in villages nobody has quite Christianised.**

## 10. ART, MUSIC, AND STORYTELLING

### Sculpture, wall-painting, and illumination

Medieval Anglo-French visual art was overwhelmingly **religious** and **public**. Cathedrals were decorated from floor to ceiling. The **tympanum** over the west door of a cathedral (Chartres, Autun, Vézelay) carried a great Last Judgement or Christ in Majesty, carved in stone. Capitals of columns crawled with Biblical scenes, grotesques, fantastic animals, the Labours of the Months. Stained-glass windows narrated saints' lives in brilliant blues and reds that turned the church interior into a jewelled light-box.

Inside smaller parish churches, the walls were usually **whitewashed and painted** with scenes from scripture — Christ in Majesty above the chancel arch, a Last Judgement (Doom) above the nave, Virgin and Child on the north wall, devil-tormented sinners on the south. These are the "biblia pauperum" — Bibles for the poor, who could not read. Survivals (Pickering, Winchester) still show how bright these interiors were.

**Manuscript illumination** reached a peak in the Gothic period. The great productions:
- **Bibles moralisées** — moralised picture Bibles made for royal patrons (Blanche of Castile).
- **Psalters** — the psalms with illustrated calendar and hours of the Virgin.
- **Books of Hours** — the lay devotional masterpiece. A Book of Hours was a small, personal prayer book structured around the liturgy, typically with an illuminated calendar of the **Labours of the Months** — January feasting, February warming at the fire, March pruning, April courting, May hawking and riding, June mowing, July reaping, August threshing, September treading grapes, October sowing, November slaughtering pigs, December feasting again. These calendars are our best visual evidence of peasant labour. The **Très Riches Heures du Duc de Berry** by the Limbourg brothers (c. 1412–1416) is the supreme example, but the genre goes back to the twelfth century.
- **The Bayeux Tapestry** (c. 1070, embroidered in England for a Norman patron) — 230 feet long, 20 inches tall, telling the Norman Conquest. Linen with wool embroidery. Our best visual document of eleventh-century warfare, armour, building, dress.

### Music

**Liturgical music.** The dominant music of the Church was **Gregorian chant** (plainsong) — monophonic, unaccompanied, sung in Latin by the clergy, following the liturgical year. By the twelfth century, **polyphony** was being developed at Notre-Dame de Paris under **Léonin** and **Pérotin** — the **Ars Antiqua** — with two, three, or four voices over a held chant melody. This is the beginning of Western polyphonic music.

**Secular music.** In noble halls, **minstrels** (skilled performers) sang and played for their keep. Instruments: **harp** (the most prestigious, associated with King David), **lute**, **gittern**, **psaltery**, **hurdy-gurdy** (the **vielle à roue** — a cranked drone instrument), **recorder**, **shawm** (a loud double-reed precursor to oboe), **sackbut** (early trombone), **tabor** (small drum), **bagpipe**. Outside the hall, at village feasts, pipe-and-tabor duets, **bagpipes**, and **shawms** played for dancing. The **carol** (from *carole*, a round-dance) was the universal village dance.

### Courtly love and the troubadours

The most distinctive literary invention of the twelfth century was **courtly love** — a code and lyric poetry of refined, suffering, usually adulterous or unconsummated love of a knight for a married noblewoman. It arose in southern France (Occitania) in the late eleventh century, in the songs of the **troubadours** (Occitan-language poet-musicians). The first named troubadour was **William IX of Aquitaine** (1071–1126) — Eleanor of Aquitaine's grandfather.

The troubadour tradition travelled north with Eleanor and her daughter **Marie de Champagne**. Eleanor's court at **Poitiers** (1168–1173) and Marie's at **Troyes** became centres of courtly poetry. Marie was the patron of **Chrétien de Troyes**, the Arthurian romancer. Andreas Capellanus's half-satirical *De Amore* (c. 1180) codified the "rules" of courtly love.

### Chansons de geste and Arthurian romance

Two great narrative traditions ran side-by-side:

- **Chansons de geste** — heroic epics of warfare and vassalage. The archetype is the **Song of Roland** (*Chanson de Roland*, earliest surviving manuscript c. 1100, Anglo-Norman), recounting the death of Charlemagne's paladin Roland at Roncesvalles in 778. Roland's horn **Olifant**, his sword **Durendal**, his death on the field — the poem is the European *Iliad*. The chansons celebrated the **bellatores' code**: loyalty to one's lord, courage unto death, hatred of the infidel. Charlemagne and his paladins, William of Orange, Raoul of Cambrai — these were the heroes of the early feudal imagination.

- **Romances** — the later, courtly tradition. **Chrétien de Troyes** (active c. 1160–1190) wrote five Arthurian romances: **Erec et Enide**, **Cligès**, **Lancelot ou Le Chevalier de la Charrette** (introducing Lancelot and his adulterous love for Guinevere), **Yvain ou Le Chevalier au Lion**, and the unfinished **Perceval ou Le Conte du Graal** (introducing the Holy Grail). Chrétien's romances invented the whole literary machinery of the questing knight, the enchanted forest, the mysterious lady, the quest for the sacred object. They swept Europe — translated into German (*Parzival* by Wolfgang von Eschenbach), Norse, Welsh (the *Mabinogion*), and English (the alliterative *Sir Gawain and the Green Knight*). The **Arthurian cycle** was compiled in French prose in the thirteenth century (the **Vulgate Cycle** / **Lancelot-Grail**) and reached its English fulfilment in Malory's *Le Morte d'Arthur* (1485).

### Oral tradition and the storyteller

Below the written chansons and romances ran a deep oral tradition — **beast fables** (the *Roman de Renart*, the fox-trickster cycle), ballads (by the late Middle Ages, the Robin Hood cycle), saint-legends, and the endless talk at the hearth on winter nights. The village **rhymer** or **story-teller** was often a travelling figure — a half-jester, half-beggar who sang for his supper. Pilgrims told tales on the road (which is of course the frame of Chaucer).

### Dance, drama, theatre

**Carole** (round-dance) was the peasant dance; **estampie** an instrumental dance form; **basse-danse** a refined courtly dance. On high feast days the **mystery plays** and **miracle plays** were performed — dramatisations of scripture and saints' lives, staged on wagons moving through the town (York, Chester, Coventry cycles). By the later Middle Ages the **morality play** (Everyman) had added allegorical figures — Death, Good Deeds, Knowledge — to the repertoire. Village **mummers** performed rough comic plays at Christmas.

### What this means for Ardenmere

Ardenmere's art is **public, religious, and patterned on the Anglo-French model**. Parish churches have Doom paintings. The great cathedral has stained glass telling the history of the kingdom. In noble halls, **minstrels** sing **chansons de geste** in the hero-age and **romances** in the later period. A troubadour tradition has bloomed at a princess's court in the last generation. The peasants, meanwhile, sing the **old songs** — harvest ballads, May songs, love-laments older than any clerk's writing. These oral peasant songs carry the **druid memory** in their imagery: the green man in the leaves, the salmon of wisdom in the well, the king who must die for the land. None of the singers knows what the songs once were. They only know they are beautiful and sad and old.

## 11. TECHNOLOGY AND CRAFTS

### Agricultural technology — the quiet revolution

The period 900–1300 was a **quiet technological revolution** in European agriculture. The basic inventions that fed the population boom of the High Middle Ages were not flashy but transformative:

- **The heavy mouldboard plough** — a wheeled plough with a coulter (vertical cutting blade), share (horizontal cutting blade), and mouldboard (curved wooden or iron plate that turned the sliced sod over). It could open the heavy, clay-rich soils of northern Europe that the old Roman scratch-plough could not handle. It required a team of six or eight oxen (or, by the thirteenth century where available, a team of horses), which few peasants could afford alone — hence the **cooperative plough team**, with multiple households sharing draft animals and plough.

- **The three-field system.** Earlier medieval farming used two fields (one sown, one fallow); the three-field rotation split the arable into three great fields — one **winter-sown** (wheat or rye, planted in October–November), one **spring-sown** (barley, oats, peas, or beans), one **fallow** — each rotating yearly. This increased productive land from half to two-thirds, and the introduction of legumes (peas, beans, vetches) on the spring field **fixed nitrogen** and fed the soil. The three-field system matured in France and England between c. 850 and 1150 CE.

- **The horse-collar** (padded, resting on the shoulders, enabling horses to pull without choking — in contrast to the throat-and-girth harness of antiquity) and the **horseshoe** made the horse a practical plough animal by the twelfth century, though oxen remained more common for ploughing because they were cheaper to keep.

- **Watermills and windmills.** The watermill was inherited from Rome but multiplied enormously in the medieval centuries — Domesday counts over 5,600 watermills in England in 1086. The **windmill** (post-mill type, where the whole body of the mill rotated on a central post to face the wind) appeared in northwest Europe around 1180 and spread rapidly. Mills ground grain (monopoly of the lord — see banalités), fulled cloth, sawed timber, forged iron, mashed malt. Jean Gimpel's *The Medieval Machine* argued that medieval Europe was the first civilisation to systematically substitute non-human power (water, wind) for human muscle.

### The peasant's tool-kit

The peasant's year of work was performed with hand tools:
- **Ploughshare** and **coulter** (iron).
- **Sickle** for reaping — small, serrated, one-handed.
- **Scythe** for mowing — long, two-handed, straight-bladed in this period (the modern curved scythe is later).
- **Flail** — two wooden sticks joined by a leather thong, for threshing grain on the barn floor.
- **Winnowing fan** — for separating chaff from grain.
- **Hoe**, **spade**, **mattock**, **pitchfork**, **rake**.
- **Axe**, **billhook** (curved hedging knife), **froe** (for splitting wood).
- **Shears** — for sheep.

Most of these were iron-headed on wooden handles, produced by the village blacksmith.

### Textile production

**Wool** was the defining industry of medieval England. By 1290 there were perhaps five million sheep in England producing around 30,000 sacks of wool a year. Raw wool was exported to **Flanders** (and increasingly to **Florence** in the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries) for weaving into fine cloth. The English fleece — especially from the Welsh Marches, the Cotswolds, and Lincolnshire — had the **longest, strongest staple** in Europe. The wool trade made England rich; it made Flanders a powerhouse; it propelled the Italian banking houses (the Bardi, the Peruzzi); and the **Great Wool Subsidy** was the financial base of Edward III's wars.

The **stages of cloth-making:** shearing (May–June, the great sheep-shearing) → sorting → scouring (washing) → beating → carding or combing → **spinning** (women's work, continuous, everywhere) → warping → **weaving** (women in rural production; increasingly male in urban guilds after the introduction of the horizontal loom in the eleventh century) → **fulling** (beating cloth with fuller's earth to thicken it — originally trodden by foot, later mechanised in water-powered **fulling mills**) → dyeing → shearing the nap → tentering (stretching to dry on **tenter-hooks**).

**Linen** — from flax — was grown for undergarments and sheets. Every cottage had a small **flax-patch** in the garden.

### Metalwork

The village **smithy** forged horseshoes, plough-irons, knives, axes, sickles, scythes, door hinges, nails. The great ironworking regions were the **Weald** of Sussex and Kent and parts of the **Forest of Dean** and **Yorkshire**; in France, Normandy, Lorraine, and the Massif Central. **Charcoal** from coppiced oak and hazel fuelled the **bloomery furnaces** (small, clay-walled, producing wrought-iron blooms that were then hammered out).

Master smiths forged **swords** and **armour** — see section 12. The swordsmith was a nearly magical figure in peasant imagination, cousin to the druid: he commanded fire and iron.

### Pottery, glass, woodwork

Medieval **pottery** was mostly lead-glazed earthenware — cooking pots, jugs, bowls. Fine wares were imported from France (Saintonge) or the Rhineland. **Glass** was rare and expensive. Window-glass appeared in cathedrals (stained, in small pieces set in lead calmes); domestic windows were normally open or shuttered or closed with oiled linen. The great glassworks were in the Rhineland and in Venetian Murano.

**Woodwork** was pervasive — every plough, every cart, every bucket, every bowl, every roof-beam, every bedstead. The medieval forest was a managed resource: **coppiced** woodland (cut on a rotation of seven to fifteen years) produced poles for hurdles and charcoal; **pollarded** trees (cut above grazing height) produced fodder and fuel; **standards** (full-grown oaks left to mature) were reserved for major timber. England in 1300 was probably more heavily forested than any time since — but every acre of it was known and worked.

### Transport

**Land travel** was slow. Roads — inherited from Rome or built since — were poor; medieval roads were often just rights of way across unfenced country. A laden cart did perhaps fifteen miles a day; a mounted messenger forty; a post-relay (royal messenger system) could do a hundred in summer.

**Rivers** were the great arteries. The Thames, the Severn, the Trent, the Seine, the Loire, the Rhône carried far more freight than the roads. A bulky cargo like wool, stone, or wine almost always moved by water.

**Coastal shipping** — **cogs** (high-sided single-masted square-rigged ships) dominated the North Sea trade by the thirteenth century. A cog could carry two hundred tons. The cog linked the English wool ports (Boston, King's Lynn, Hull, Southampton) to Flanders (Bruges), to Bordeaux (wine), and to the Baltic (Hanseatic timber, furs, grain).

### What this means for Ardenmere

Ardenmere's technology is the **quiet triumph of the open field** — the heavy plough, the ox-team, the three-field rotation, the village water-mill turning the grain the lord demands it turn. Every village has a **smithy**, a **mill** (the lord's), a **bakehouse** (the lord's), a **tithe barn**, a **sheep-fold**. At sheep-shearing the whole village turns out. The druid-blood in the peasants makes the fleeces the heaviest in the kingdom — the Flanders buyers come to Ardenmere first because the wool is famous for its weight and softness, and the lords are rich because of it, and the peasants who shear the sheep have no idea why the sheep are so blessed.

## 12. WARFARE AND WEAPONS

### The knight — central figure of medieval warfare

The defining warrior of Anglo-French feudal society was the **mounted knight** — a heavily armoured nobleman on a trained warhorse, trained from boyhood in the use of lance, sword, and shield. The knight was not merely a soldier but the incarnation of an entire social order: the *bellator* whose military service justified his land-holding.

**Equipment of the eleventh-century knight** (from the Bayeux Tapestry):
- **Hauberk** — a knee-length mail shirt (**mail** = "chainmail" made of interlinking iron rings, each riveted), with long sleeves, often with an integral hood (**coif**).
- **Gambeson** / **aketon** — a quilted padded tunic worn under the mail to absorb the shock of blows.
- **Conical helmet** with a **nasal bar** (a vertical strip of iron covering the nose).
- **Kite-shaped shield** — tall, rounded at the top and tapering to a point at the bottom, protecting the left side of the rider from shoulder to knee.
- **Lance** — a wooden shaft, ten to twelve feet long, with a sharp iron point.
- **Sword** — double-edged, straight, around thirty-two to thirty-six inches of blade, worn at the left hip on a broad belt. The sword was the knight's **identity-weapon**, often named and passed down (Roland's *Durendal*, Charlemagne's *Joyeuse*).

**Evolution through the twelfth and thirteenth centuries:**
- Mail extends to cover the legs (**chausses** — mail hose).
- Helmets evolve: from conical with nasal, to the **flat-topped enclosed great helm** (late twelfth / thirteenth century) that covered the whole head and face, pierced with eye-slits and breathing holes.
- **Heraldry** emerges — identifying coat-of-arms painted on shield, worn as a **surcoat** over the mail, embroidered on horse-caparison and banner. With faces hidden by the great helm, heraldic identification became essential.
- **Plate armour** begins to emerge in the late thirteenth century — reinforcing plates over vulnerable joints (**poleyn** at the knee, **couter** at the elbow) — and will flower into full plate harness in the fourteenth century.

### The warhorse — the knight's other half

The **destrier** was the great warhorse, trained for battle — bred for size, strength, and temperament. A destrier cost as much as a peasant's entire farm. The knight would also own a **palfrey** (smooth-riding travel horse), **rouncey** (general riding horse), and **sumpter** (pack horse). The **courser** was a faster, lighter warhorse popular by the thirteenth century. The cavalry **charge** — a tight formation of knights with couched lances driving at a gallop into an enemy line — was the shock weapon of the age.

### Tactics and the shape of medieval battle

Medieval battles were relatively rare events compared to **sieges** and **chevauchées** (raids). When they did happen, they followed rough patterns:
- **Cavalry charge** by the knightly corps.
- **Infantry** (spearmen, men-at-arms) holding ground.
- **Archers** — Anglo-French warfare increasingly dominated in the later period by the **longbow** (Welsh and English, two hundred yards effective range, armour-piercing) and the **crossbow** (used by Genoese mercenaries, French royal army — slower rate of fire but a trained crossbowman could be found fast). The English longbow victories of Crécy (1346) and Poitiers (1356) broke French chivalry — but those are late. In the core 900–1300 window, the archer is still largely a supporting arm.

**Siege warfare** — the **castle** defined strategy. An attacker could:
- **Assault** the walls (escalade, scaling ladders).
- **Mine** — dig tunnels beneath towers to collapse them.
- **Siege-engine**: trebuchet (counterweight siege engine, able to throw three-hundred-pound stones over walls), mangonel (torsion-powered), ballista (giant crossbow).
- **Starve** — by far the commonest method. A siege might last a year. Garrison and civilians inside starved until surrender or until relief.

### The tournament

The **tournament** was the central institution of aristocratic military culture. Originating in the twelfth century in northern France as an informal free-for-all (**mêlée**) between mounted teams, it evolved into a codified sport with individual jousts. A tournament:
- Trained knights for real battle.
- Made reputations (the young William Marshal — later regent of England — built his fortune on the tournament circuit of the 1170s).
- Built political affinities.
- Was also **extremely dangerous** — real deaths occurred regularly.
The Church repeatedly condemned tournaments; kings repeatedly permitted them, because they were the theatre of chivalric identity. The **pas d'armes** (a defender holding a bridge or pass against all comers), the **joust of peace** (with blunted lances), and the **joust of war** (with sharp lances) were later refinements.

### The chivalric code — ideology as armour

**Chivalry** was, as scholars like Maurice Keen have shown, an ideological construction — a narrative the military aristocracy told about itself to dignify what was often simple violence. The code prescribed:
- **Prowess** — personal courage and skill at arms.
- **Loyalty** — to one's lord above all else.
- **Largesse** — generous giving; the knight who hoarded was no knight.
- **Courtesy** — especially to women and to fellow knights (less so to peasants or infidels).
- **Franchise** — the freedom and openness of manner proper to a gentleman.
- **Piety** — Christian faith; service of the Church.
- **Defence of the weak, widows, orphans, churches.**
- **Mercy** to a defeated honourable opponent.

The chivalric code was **breached constantly** in practice, but its existence as an ideal shaped behaviour — especially the treatment of captured **noble** enemies (who could expect courteous imprisonment and ransom) as against peasants and footsoldiers (who were killed out of hand).

### Peasant levy and the common soldier

Below the knights, the bulk of any medieval army was **infantry** — drawn from the peasantry by levy or hire. Every free man was theoretically obliged to military service in his kingdom's defence. **Arrayed foot-soldiers** carried spears, bills (long-handled chopping blades), bows, or crossbows. Armour for these men was minimal: a quilted **jack** or **gambeson**, a **kettle helmet** (wide-brimmed steel cap), perhaps a small shield. Peasants died by the thousand in battles the chronicles record as noble victories or defeats.

### Women in warfare

While the battle-line was a male space, **noblewomen defended castles** in the absence of their husbands. **Nicola de la Haye** held Lincoln Castle for King John and Henry III. **Jeanne de Montfort** commanded a defence of Hennebont in the Breton succession war (1342). **Eleanor of Aquitaine** led armies on the Second Crusade. Queens in regency — Blanche of Castile, Matilda — directed armies through their commanders. The late medieval exemplar is of course **Joan of Arc** (1412–1431), but she is beyond the core window.

### How war was declared and ended

A medieval war was declared by **defiance** — a formal renunciation of homage, a throwing-down of the glove, a letter of challenge. It was ended by **treaty**, **marriage alliance**, **ransom payments**, or **truce**. Captured nobles were **ransomed** (the ransom of the French king John II after Poitiers in 1356 was set at 4 million gold écus — a sum France could never fully pay). Peasant prisoners were slaughtered, enslaved, or simply left.

### What this means for Ardenmere

Ardenmere is defended by its **knightly aristocracy** — armoured, horsed, lance-and-sword — whose tournaments shape their identity and whose castles dot the countryside. Below them, **the peasant levy** — billhooks and longbows — fights the wars its lords begin. **The wool of Ardenmere funds the war-machines of the realm.** And beneath all this, the druid-blood peasants understand war the way their druid ancestors did: that it is mostly a waste, and that the lord in his bright colours is always the one who chooses it, and that the blood soaking the fields after a battle is not chivalric — it is **the land being fed again.**

## 13. TRADE AND ECONOMY

### The local economy — mostly self-sufficient

The medieval peasant economy was **not purely subsistence** — it was not even mostly subsistence by 1200. Christopher Dyer's *Making a Living in the Middle Ages* and the work of Bruce Campbell have shown that peasant households were deeply enmeshed in market activity, selling surplus grain, cheese, wool, eggs, and ale for cash, and buying iron tools, salt, pepper (occasional splurge), fish (for Lent), cloth (beyond household spinning), shoes, and ecclesiastical dues.

But **most of what a peasant needed was made within a few miles of where they lived.** The village supplied food; the local market town (a few hours' walk) supplied tools, cloth, salt, and news; the regional fair supplied luxuries; the great international trade route reached only the richest.

### Markets, fairs, and the gild merchant

Every market town had a **weekly market** — a day on which peasants from surrounding villages came in to sell eggs, cheese, chickens, surplus grain, and buy what they needed. Market rights were granted by royal charter; the lord of the town took tolls.

**Fairs** were larger, annual or semi-annual events, often lasting weeks. The great **Champagne fairs** (Troyes, Provins, Lagny, Bar-sur-Aube — running in a cycle from the late twelfth to the early fourteenth century) were the supreme international market of northern Europe: Flemish cloth, Italian silk and spice, English wool, German metalwork, Scandinavian furs all changed hands there. **Italian merchants** and bankers (the Bardi, the Peruzzi, the Medici later) ran the exchange. In England, the great fairs were **Stourbridge** (outside Cambridge), **St Ives** (Huntingdonshire), **Winchester**, **Boston**.

### The wool trade

Medieval England had an extensive wool supply unrivalled anywhere in Europe. By 1290, around five million sheep produced about 30,000 sacks of wool a year, and at peak around 40,000–45,000 sacks were exported. This trade was the **engine of the English economy** and the **financial base of the English crown**. The king taxed wool exports heavily (the "custom" or "subsidy"), and the trade funded both domestic government and overseas war.

The **wool staple** — the designated port or town through which all export wool had to be funnelled so the crown could collect duty — was established at various times at Dordrecht, Antwerp, Bruges, or Calais. Merchants of the **Company of the Staple** became the great wool exporters of England.

Raw wool went primarily to **Flanders**, where cities like **Bruges**, **Ghent**, and **Ypres** turned it into fine broadcloth. Flemish cities became wealthy and powerful — and their political struggles (the Matins of Bruges 1302, the Battle of the Golden Spurs) are part of the story of medieval urban autonomy.

By the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries, English cloth-making began to displace raw wool export: **Yorkshire**, **East Anglia**, **the West Country**, and **Wales** developed their own weaving, fulling, and dyeing industries. The fulling mill was a key technological driver of this shift.

### Currency

The dominant Western currencies of the period were silver-based:
- **Penny** (*denarius*) — the standard silver coin, weighed a pennyweight (1/240 of a pound).
- **Shilling** (*solidus*) — twelve pence; rarely an actual coin, more an accounting unit.
- **Pound** (*libra*) — 240 pence or 20 shillings; a money-of-account. The pound sterling was notionally a pound's weight of silver pence.

Halfpennies and farthings (quarter-pennies) were produced by **cutting** pennies into halves and quarters, which were legal tender. Gold coins (the **florin** from Florence, the **ducat** from Venice, the **noble** from English mints after 1344) entered the picture in the late thirteenth century as international trade grew.

Most peasant transactions were in **pence** and **halfpennies**; a shilling was real money; a pound was very serious wealth. A villein's cash rent might be a few shillings a year.

### Long-distance trade routes

Beyond the wool trade, the main trade arteries of 900–1300 CE:
- **Bordeaux wine** to England — after Aquitaine passed to England through Eleanor's marriage to Henry II in 1152, English consumption of Gascon claret exploded. Wine was one of England's largest imports.
- **Baltic trade** — the **Hanseatic League** (German merchants, confederation from the twelfth century onwards) carried **furs, amber, wax, pitch, flax, timber, grain** from the Baltic through their depot (the **Steelyard**) in London and through Bruges.
- **Mediterranean luxuries** — pepper, cinnamon, cloves, nutmeg (Indonesia, India), silk (China through Silk Road, Byzantium, and Italy), cotton (Levant), alum (Asia Minor), dyestuffs, paper (first reached northern Europe from Muslim Spain). Venice and Genoa controlled most of this trade.
- **Slave trade** — late Anglo-Saxon England exported slaves; by the twelfth century the domestic English slave trade had largely died out, but the Mediterranean slave trade continued (Venetians and Genoese trafficking enslaved people from the Black Sea and sub-Saharan Africa).

### Banking and credit

Medieval Christendom theoretically forbade **usury** (lending at interest) — so banking developed through clever workarounds. The **Lombards** (northern Italian bankers), the **Cahorsins** (merchants from Cahors in southern France), and for two centuries the **Jews** (before the expulsions — from England 1290, from France repeatedly) ran the great credit business of Europe. By the thirteenth century, Italian houses (Bardi, Peruzzi, later Medici) had become international banks lending to kings. The collapse of the Bardi and Peruzzi in the 1340s — defaulted on by Edward III of England — shook European finance.

The **fair letter of payment** developed at Champagne, and the **bill of exchange** — a merchant's promise to pay in another city in another currency on a fixed date — allowed trade to move without physical coin.

### Taxation

See section 3 for seigniorial dues. Royal taxation (tallage, fifteenths and tenths, poll taxes, custom on wool) increasingly dominated in the later Middle Ages. The **poll tax of 1380** — a flat per-head tax of a shilling on every adult regardless of wealth — was the immediate trigger for the English Peasants' Revolt of 1381.

### What this means for Ardenmere

Ardenmere is **rich in wool** — and the wool trade is the great engine of both provincial and realm-level wealth. The fleeces of Ardenmere's sheep are famous: the Flemings and the Italians come buying; the Ardenmere staple-town is a Flanders-facing port or border-town. The great **silver staple fair** of the province runs for two weeks at Michaelmas — Italian bankers, Flemish weavers, English merchants, and the king's customs officers all in the same dusty streets. **The peasants who sheared the sheep watch the merchants ride off with the wool and see, year after year, that the wealth of Ardenmere flows out of their hands and into everyone else's.** That watching is slow kindling.

## 14. LANGUAGE AND WRITING

### Three languages, three worlds

After the Norman Conquest of 1066, England became a **trilingual society**, and France was **multilingual** across its many dialects. In England:

- **Latin** — the language of the Church, of learning, of royal administration, of the law. Every educated person had at least passive Latin. The liturgy was entirely Latin.
- **Anglo-Norman French** — the language of the Norman nobility and their court, increasingly the language of polite culture and high literature. English kings spoke Anglo-Norman (and later Parisian French) as their first language until at least the fourteenth century. Edward I (r. 1272–1307) was the first English king whose first language was probably English.
- **Middle English** — the language of the English peasantry and lesser gentry, absorbing thousands of French loan-words over the centuries to become the language of Chaucer by 1390. The English spoken at the village level remained robustly Germanic in grammar but increasingly French in vocabulary for anything touching law (*justice, judge, jury, parliament, noble, servant, peasant*), cookery (*beef, pork, mutton* — from French, versus the Germanic animal names *cow, pig, sheep*), warfare, and art.

In France, a great split ran between:
- **Langue d'oïl** — northern French dialects, the language of Paris, Normandy, Champagne, Picardy. The ancestor of modern French.
- **Langue d'oc / Occitan** — the language of the south, of the troubadours, of Aquitaine and Provence. A distinct Romance language, closer to Catalan than to Parisian French. The Albigensian Crusade (1209–1229) against the Cathars of Languedoc broke the political autonomy of the south and began the long linguistic dominance of northern French.
- **Breton** (Celtic), **Basque**, **Flemish**, and regional dialects everywhere.

### Writing systems and literacy

Writing was **Latin alphabet** throughout, but scribal hands evolved rapidly:
- **Caroline minuscule** (from the court of Charlemagne, ninth century onwards) was the clear, round, beautifully legible hand of early medieval manuscripts.
- **Gothic script** (twelfth century onwards) — the compressed, angular, black-letter hand of the High and Late Middle Ages. What we now call "medieval script" is mostly this Gothic.
- **Court hand / chancery hand** — looser, cursive versions for administrative documents.

**Materials:** parchment (scraped and prepared sheepskin or calfskin — calfskin for the very finest manuscripts, called **vellum**); **ink** made from oak-galls, iron sulphate, and gum; **quills** from goose-wings; **knife** to scrape out errors; **ruler** and **plummet** (lead) for ruling lines; **gold leaf** for illumination; **pigments** of lapis lazuli (ultramarine — expensive), malachite (green), vermilion (red), white lead, saffron yellow, oak-gall black. Paper began to reach northern Europe from Muslim Spain and Italy from the thirteenth century but did not dominate until the fifteenth.

**Literacy** is a complicated question. Among the peasantry, literacy was near zero — perhaps a few percent of adult males in the thirteenth century could sign their names, a proportion that rose through the fourteenth and fifteenth. Among the clergy, full literacy was normal. Among the nobility, a growing proportion through the period — by 1300 most noblemen could at least read (write was a separate skill, often left to clerks). Among town burgesses and merchants, **commercial literacy** — enough to read a bill, write an account — was widespread by 1300.

"Benefit of clergy" — the right to be tried by an ecclesiastical court rather than a civil court, which carried far lighter punishments — could be claimed by anyone who could read a **Latin Bible verse** ("the neck-verse," usually Psalm 51). This produced a rough and ready literacy test that saved many a literate layman from the gallows.

### Poetry and rhetoric

Latin poetry was a scholarly art — classical metres, the twelfth-century **renaissance of the classics** at Chartres and elsewhere, Latin lyric of the **Goliards** (wandering students who wrote drinking songs and parodies — *Carmina Burana*, famously). Vernacular poetry bloomed alongside: troubadour lyric in Occitan, trouvère lyric in northern French, Minnesang in German, romance in French, alliterative verse in English (surviving robustly in the west and north of England, as in *Sir Gawain and the Green Knight*).

The medieval **rhetorical tradition** — taught from ancient authorities like Cicero, Priscian, Donatus — structured both Latin prose and vernacular composition. Letter-writing manuals (**ars dictaminis**) shaped the elegant prose of royal chanceries. Preaching manuals (**ars praedicandi**) shaped the Latin sermon and, by the thirteenth century, the vernacular sermon of the friars.

### Oral culture persisting

Despite the growing reach of writing, much of medieval culture remained oral. Peasants spoke stories, songs, charms, and genealogies that were never written down. The village rhymer and the winter fireside tale transmitted a body of folk narrative that survived parallel to the written tradition — and, in many cases, outlived it. Jack tales, green-man legends, ballads, charms for curing cattle, love-lyrics in the kitchen — this was the peasants' literature, and every word of it was spoken.

### What this means for Ardenmere

Ardenmere is probably **trilingual** or **bilingual**: the peasants speak the local vernacular (call it an Ardenmere tongue with deep Braedunic substratum — the druid-blood families hold older words in their grandmother-speech); the nobility speak the prestige-language of the realm (borrowed from the capital, Tyramare-inflected); the clergy speak the Solenthéran liturgical tongue (the realm's Latin-equivalent). **The peasant vernacular of Ardenmere preserves Braedunic words that no one knows are Braedunic** — words for herbs, words for stars, words for the old festivals, words for the old gods. Songs in the peasant tongue rhyme on sounds that do not match the prestige language. A hedge-witch whispering a charm over a sick child is speaking, without knowing it, in her druid great-grandmother's tongue.

## 15. EDUCATION AND KNOWLEDGE

### The peasant child's education

Peasant education was almost entirely **practical and oral**. From about age seven a child was expected to work: tending geese, gleaning, scaring birds from the sown field, helping in the dairy or at the loom. A boy learned ploughing from his father; a girl learned spinning, baking, and dairy work from her mother. Children absorbed the entire rural year — which plant to pick when, which sign in the sky meant rain, how to read the face of the field — by watching, copying, and being corrected.

Religious instruction came from the parish priest. Children were expected to learn the **Pater Noster, the Ave Maria, the Credo**, and the **Ten Commandments** by rote, in Latin or vernacular. The vernacular **Lay Folks' Catechism** was ordered in English dioceses in 1357. Confession before Easter was compulsory by the Fourth Lateran Council of 1215, and the priest could quiz parishioners on their basic faith.

### Song schools and grammar schools

Where there was enough of a town or cathedral nearby, **song schools** taught boys the liturgy; **grammar schools** (attached to cathedrals or abbeys, occasionally supported by guilds) taught Latin grammar through the standard textbooks — Donatus, Priscian. A bright peasant boy with a patron (a sympathetic priest, a monastic connection) might go this route and enter the clergy.

By the thirteenth century, specialist schools had begun to emerge:
- **Cathedral schools** (Chartres, Paris, Laon, Reims, Canterbury, Lincoln) — centres of advanced Latin learning, theology, the "seven liberal arts."
- **Monastic schools** — still important but declining as cathedral schools rose.
- **Universities** — the great thirteenth-century institution. **Bologna** (studium of law, founded late eleventh century), **Paris** (theology, from Abelard and the cathedral school, organised as a university by 1200), **Oxford** (late twelfth century), **Cambridge** (1209), **Salamanca** (1218), **Montpellier** (medicine). The university was a **studium generale** — a guild of masters and students with royal or papal privileges, self-governing, with its own courts and eventually its own colleges. Students wore clerical garb and were subject to ecclesiastical law.

### The liberal arts and university curriculum

The classical **seven liberal arts** organised learning:
- The **Trivium** — Grammar, Rhetoric, Logic. Studied first.
- The **Quadrivium** — Arithmetic, Geometry, Astronomy, Music. Studied later.

After the Arts, a student could proceed to the higher faculties: **Theology** (the queen of sciences, longest course, Paris the supreme faculty), **Law** (canon law at Bologna, civil law at Orleans), **Medicine** (Salerno and Montpellier). Study took years — a Paris doctorate in theology could require fifteen to twenty years of work.

The **scholastic method** — dialectical reasoning using carefully stated questions, objections, and responses — dominated university thought. The great figures: Peter Abelard (*Sic et Non*, twelfth century), Peter Lombard (*Sentences*, the standard theology textbook, c. 1150), Thomas Aquinas (*Summa Theologiae*, 1260s–1270s — Aristotelian theology), Bonaventure, Duns Scotus, William of Ockham.

### The knowledge-world of a noble household

A noble boy was taught at home and in fosterage — reading (increasingly in vernacular, sometimes Latin), writing (often later and less essential), arithmetic enough to keep estate accounts, riding, hunting, hawking, chess, music, dancing. Military skills from the squire's training. A noble girl received similar early education — often more literary, including devotional reading (the Psalter, Books of Hours). Noblewomen were often more literate in the vernacular than their husbands.

The great **household book** of a noblewoman often included medical recipes, household accounts, poetry collected over a lifetime, family records. Noblewomen were expected to run the estate in the lord's absence, which required literacy and numeracy.

### Libraries

The great medieval libraries were **monastic**. Cluny had several hundred manuscripts by 1100; major abbeys held a few hundred volumes each — vast by medieval standards. Cathedral libraries (Canterbury, Durham, Chartres) were comparable. Universities built up libraries rapidly; the Sorbonne of Paris was founded with a library in mind (1257). The royal and great-noble household collections were smaller but growing in the later period.

A book was a precious object. A single medieval bible might take a scribe a year or more to produce; it cost the price of a vineyard or a small house. Books were **chained** to lecterns in monastic libraries to prevent theft.

### Folk knowledge and the unwritten curriculum

Beside and beneath the Latin schoolroom ran an entirely different body of knowledge, all oral, all practical, nearly all anonymous:
- **Agricultural knowledge** — the farming year in deep detail, weather-reading, soil-reading, which fields to plough when.
- **Herbal knowledge** — which plants heal, which harm, which mark paths, which feed the bees.
- **Weather lore** — centuries of observation distilled into sayings ("red sky at night, shepherd's delight").
- **Charm and ritual** — prayers and charms for every crisis (a cow not yielding milk, a child with fever, a lost key, a cross traveller).
- **Genealogy** — who was kin to whom in the parish, which fields belonged with which house, who owed whom.
- **Story** — the endless cycle of stories shared at the hearth.

None of this was taught in schools. All of it was transmitted by watching, listening, and doing — grandmother to granddaughter, father to son.

### What this means for Ardenmere

Ardenmere's formal education is **Latin, clerical, and narrowly noble**. The cathedral school and the university (if it exists within the realm) teach the sons of the gentry and the cleverer peasant boys who have found a clerical path. Most peasants never see the inside of a schoolroom.

But Ardenmere is saturated with **unwritten peasant knowledge**. The hedge-witch knows seven hundred uses of the plants within a mile of her cottage. The ploughman reads the sky. The dairywoman knows her cheese-making by smell and thumb. **The druid-blood's inheritance survives as peasant craft** — not called magic, not taught as doctrine, simply done as skill. An Ardenmere peasant's grandmother holds, in her kitchen and her head, fragments of what the Braedunic druids once held in their sacred groves — broken, half-remembered, but alive.

## 16. MEDICINE AND HEALING

### The medical landscape

Medieval medicine existed in a **layered system** that ran from the university-trained **physician** at the top to the village **cunning-woman** at the bottom, with many specialists in between. Nearly everyone had access to healing of some kind; few had access to the most learned kind.

### The humoral model

The dominant theory of medicine across the Latin West was **Galenic** and **humoral** — inherited from the Greek physician Galen (second century CE) via Arab translators and commentators (Avicenna / **Ibn Sina**, whose *Canon of Medicine* became the standard textbook of university medical faculties; Rhazes / **al-Razi**; Haly Abbas). The theory:

Four **humours** flowed through the body: **blood** (hot and moist, sanguine), **yellow bile** (hot and dry, choleric), **black bile** (cold and dry, melancholic), and **phlegm** (cold and moist, phlegmatic). Health was **balance**; illness was **imbalance**. Treatment restored balance by purging excess — **bleeding, purging, vomiting, sweating, clysters**. Food and drink had humoral qualities (lettuce cold, wine hot, beef moist, etc.), and diet was prescribed to correct imbalances. Each person had a natural **complexion** — predominant humour — that shaped temperament (sanguine = cheerful, choleric = angry, melancholic = sad, phlegmatic = calm).

Astrology interacted with medicine: the moon in a particular sign meant certain parts of the body should not be operated on; the zodiac was mapped onto the human body (the **Zodiac Man** diagram in surgical manuscripts).

### Physicians, surgeons, apothecaries

- **Physicians** (*medicus / physicus*) — university-trained in humoral theory; diagnosed by examining urine (the **uroscopy flask**), pulse, and humoral signs; prescribed dietary regimens and drugs; rarely touched the patient themselves. Were reserved for the wealthy. The great medical schools: **Salerno** (southern Italy — its women medical writers like **Trotula** produced respected gynaecological texts in the twelfth century), **Montpellier**, **Paris**, **Bologna**.
- **Surgeons** (*chirurgus*) — practical operators who did setting bones, removing arrows, amputation, cautery, draining abscesses, suturing wounds, cataract surgery. Trained by apprenticeship, not university. Lower status than physicians, higher than barbers. **Hugh of Lucca** (early 13th century) and his son **Theodoric** promoted wound-healing by cleanliness and primary closure rather than the then-standard suppuration-forcing treatments. **Henri de Mondeville** and **Guy de Chauliac** (fourteenth century) wrote foundational surgical manuals. Battlefield surgery — arrow-extraction, amputation with a red-hot cauterising iron, leg-splinting — was a brutal but skilled trade.
- **Barber-surgeons** — also did bleeding, tooth-pulling, minor cutting. The red and white barber-pole is a remnant of bleeding-days (red for blood, white for bandages).
- **Apothecaries** — compounded drugs from plant, mineral, and animal materials. The pharmacopeia included poppy (opium, the "blessed painkiller"), mandrake, henbane, belladonna (all dangerous nightshades), willow-bark (salicylate — for fever), dragon's blood (plant resin), theriac (a legendary polypharmacy cure-all with dozens of ingredients including viper-flesh, wine-aged for years).

### Folk medicine and the cunning-woman

Most peasants had no access to a physician. Their medical care came from:
- **Family members** — mothers, grandmothers, aunts.
- **The village cunning-woman (wise woman, hedge-witch, wortcunner)** — a peasant woman learned in herb-lore and charms. She treated fevers, agues, cuts, burns, colic, bites, broken bones set crudely, babies' ills, cattle sickness. Her knowledge was oral, inherited from her own mother or mother-in-law, and often thousands of years old in its origins. She was paid in kind — a loaf of bread, a cheese, a chicken — not in cash.
- **The monastery** — most great monasteries ran an **infirmary** and often a lay hospital for pilgrims and the poor. Monastic medicine was literate (they had Galen's texts, Dioscorides's herbal) but hands-on, with infirmarians cultivating herb gardens whose plans survive (St Gall's plan, ninth century, is the model).
- **The shrine** — pilgrimage to a healing saint. Becket's shrine at Canterbury, St Thomas's water (a few drops of his blood diluted in water, sold to pilgrims), the shrine of Saint Foi at Conques, Walsingham, Compostela — all claimed healing miracles. A desperate patient walked, crawled, or was carried to the shrine.
- **The wise-man or magician** — for things that were clearly bewitched (a cow that suddenly stopped giving milk, a child who stopped thriving). The lines between cunning-folk (respected) and witches (feared) were real but slippery. Most of the period we're covering (900–1300) predates the witch-craze (which exploded in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries — the *Malleus Maleficarum* is 1487). In the thirteenth century, cunning-folk were largely tolerated; some were even licensed by bishops.

### Hildegard of Bingen and female medical authority

One extraordinary figure: **Hildegard of Bingen** (1098–1179), German Benedictine abbess, visionary, composer, poet, and medical writer. Her *Physica* is an extensive compendium of the healing properties of plants, stones, animals, and elements; her *Causae et Curae* is a medical textbook on causes and cures. She wrote at a moment when formal medical education was overwhelmingly closed to women, yet her authority as an abbess and visionary let her compose, and be read, across Europe.

Similarly, **Trotula of Salerno** (twelfth century) — or the *Trotula* texts attributed to her — wrote on women's medicine: menstrual regulation, childbirth, contraception (within strict theological limits), cosmetics. The *Trotula* circulated widely and was a standard reference for centuries.

### Midwifery

Midwifery was **entirely female**. Male physicians almost never attended a birth unless the mother was dying and heroic intervention was demanded. Midwives were often informally trained (apprenticed to a senior midwife) and were licensed by the bishop from the later Middle Ages onwards — the bishop's licence was needed because the midwife was empowered to perform **emergency baptism** of infants at risk of dying, and so had to be doctrinally reliable. Midwives handled delivery, post-partum care, and the first few weeks of the infant's life.

### Physical vs spiritual healing

The medieval distinction between **body-healing** and **soul-healing** was thin. Plague was understood as divine punishment; physical illness was often accompanied by confession; the sacrament of **Extreme Unction** was both a spiritual preparation for death and, theologians argued, a possible physical healing. Saints' relics were used for both. **Charms** woven into medical treatment — an Ave Maria recited over the poultice, a cross drawn in saliva on the forehead — are pervasive in medieval English medical manuscripts (see the **Leechbook** tradition, though earlier).

### What this means for Ardenmere

Ardenmere's medicine runs on every layer of this system. The lord's household has a physician from Tyramare or the cathedral city, with his uroscopy flask and his Latin texts. The monastery has its infirmary and herb garden. The village has its **wise-woman** — a peasant widow who knows every plant in the hedgerow, who has midwifed every baby born in the parish for thirty years, who boils up willow-bark for fevers and comfrey for bruises and lady's-bedstraw for childbed. **Her knowledge is druidic — and she does not know it.** Her herbs work a little too well. Her broken bones knit a little too fast. The priest does not ask why; the lord does not ask why; the nobility send for her when the physician has failed. She is a fulcrum of the hidden order.

## 17. DAILY LIFE

### The peasant's day

A villein's day was **shaped by the sun and the church bell**. In summer, work began at first light (around 4 a.m.) and ended at last light (around 8 or 9 p.m.) — a working day of fourteen or fifteen hours, with meal-breaks. In winter, the working day was much shorter — perhaps eight hours — and much of it was spent on indoor tasks (threshing, repairing tools, spinning, tending the stock in the byre).

A typical summer day in harvest season:
- **Before dawn** — rising. Stirring the hearth-fire's embers into flame. The wife putting on the pottage pot. A piece of yesterday's bread and a mug of ale to break the night fast.
- **Dawn to mid-morning** — out to the field. Mowing, reaping, binding sheaves. The whole family working — the father and eldest sons reaping with sickles, the wife and daughters binding the sheaves behind them, the children gleaning and running errands.
- **Mid-morning** — a break for bread and cheese and ale, eaten in the field.
- **Mid-morning to noon** — back to work.
- **Noon** — dinner (the main meal) — pottage, bread, cheese, perhaps a piece of salt bacon. Eaten sitting on the ground under a tree.
- **Afternoon** — back to the field. The heat of the day. Work continues.
- **Evening** — back to the cottage. Supper — more pottage, more bread, more ale. A piece of cheese. Perhaps an egg.
- **Dusk** — the animals fed and bedded down in the byre; the children put to bed; the fire banked for the night; the family to bed on straw mattresses on the floor or on wooden frames for the better-off.

The **church bell** punctuated the day — Matins (before dawn), Prime (dawn), Terce (mid-morning), Sext (noon), None (mid-afternoon), Vespers (evening), Compline (nightfall). The peasant didn't recite the offices, but he heard the bell and knew the time by it.

### The year

The peasant year ran in a closed cycle that every medieval child grew up inside:
- **January** — ploughing the winter field; threshing; mending tools; feasting on salt meat (Christmas still fresh).
- **February** — hedging and ditching; pruning vines; lambing.
- **March** — spring sowing: oats, barley, peas, beans. Ploughing continues.
- **April** — finish sowing. Gardening. First stirring of growth.
- **May** — the glorious month — fields are up. May Day. Hedging. Sheep washing.
- **June** — haymaking. All hands out in the meadow.
- **July** — weeding the growing grain. Shearing the sheep.
- **August** — **harvest**. The biggest labour of the year. Reaping begins with Lammas (1 August).
- **September** — harvest continues; wheat and rye brought in; Michaelmas (29 September) marks the end.
- **October** — ploughing for winter wheat. Autumn sowing.
- **November** — Martinmas (11 November) — the slaughter. Salting pork. Autumn ploughing finishes. Pannage (pigs in the oak woods for acorns).
- **December** — firewood. Threshing. Christmas. Twelve days of midwinter feasting.

### Hygiene

Medieval Europeans were **less filthy than Victorian stereotype claims**. Towns had public **bathhouses** (**stews** in English — which also developed secondary meanings as brothels, because the two trades often shared premises). Peasants rinsed hands and face daily. Full bathing was less frequent — once a week was considered clean — but the lord's household had baths, and bathing was a hospitable rite for honoured guests (being bathed by the host's daughters was a courtesy in Arthurian romance).

Teeth were cleaned with cloth, rags, chewed sticks, powdered herbs. Hair was washed, combed, often held against lice with fine-toothed combs (bone combs survive in vast numbers archaeologically). Lice, fleas, and bedbugs were nonetheless universal and endemic.

Latrines — the **garderobe** in a castle (a projecting privy over a pit or river), a **cesspit** in a town or manor, a **midden** (dung-heap) in a peasant cottage — disposed of waste. London had **public latrines** (Whittington's Longhouse seated 128 at once, 64 men and 64 women in separate compartments, over the Thames tide).

**Laundry** was done in the river or at the communal washing-place; washing was women's work, strenuous, and (the records of court cases show) a place of fierce gossip and quarrels.

### Leisure

The medieval peasant worked hard but did not work relentlessly. The Christian calendar gave perhaps fifty Sundays and forty to sixty saints' feast days a year on which work was formally forbidden — around **a hundred days of enforced non-work annually**. That's more holiday than a modern worker, in theory.

How peasants spent leisure:
- **Church services** (Sunday Mass was compulsory, with attendance fines for non-attendance; the faithful were expected to confess once a year at Easter).
- **Games and sports** — football (brutal village vs. village), wrestling, quoits, nine-men's-morris, tables (backgammon), chess (for the richer), dice, hurling, stoolball (a cricket ancestor), archery practice (compulsory for English males under Edward III, to keep the longbow tradition alive).
- **Drinking** at the alehouse.
- **Visiting** family and neighbours.
- **Storytelling** and singing at the hearth.
- **Dancing** at festivals.
- **Watching mystery plays** when the guild wagons came through.
- **Hunting** (forbidden — but still done — for peasants poaching; legal for the lord).

### The home at night

A peasant cottage after dark was warm, smoky, dark, and crowded. A single **rushlight** (a peeled rush dipped in animal fat) gave a tiny point of light; a **tallow candle** a little more. A big log in the hearth banked overnight. The family slept on straw mattresses, often all in one room, the parents perhaps in a curtained space, children together. Outside, the **curfew bell** (French *couvre-feu*, "cover fire") rang to tell everyone to cover their hearth-fires for safety against the night winds — the origin of the word *curfew*.

### What this means for Ardenmere

Ardenmere's daily life is the **shape of English Midland peasant life with a French polish**. Men in the field from dawn; women at the hearth, the spinning wheel, the dairy, the oven; children everywhere underfoot; the pig in the rafters smoking; the alewife brewing in the back room; the church bell marking the hours. **The rhythm is ancient** — cow in the byre, fire in the hearth, grain in the pot, wool on the distaff, song in the winter evening. Every day a small liturgy of labour. Every year a great liturgy of seasons. **The druidic year survives whole** in the peasant calendar of Ardenmere — not recognised as druidic, but lived as seasonal, lived as agricultural, lived as Christian. The old gods, if they still watch, watch through the work and the bread.

## 18. MYTHOLOGY AND LEGEND

### Arthur, Camelot, and the Matter of Britain

By the High Middle Ages, the **Matter of Britain** — the Arthurian cycle — had become one of the three great literary cycles of Europe (along with the Matter of France, Charlemagne and his paladins; and the Matter of Rome, the classical legends). Arthur had existed in Celtic tradition for centuries, but in 1138 **Geoffrey of Monmouth** published his *Historia Regum Britanniae* — a faux-history that turned Arthur into an emperor who conquered half of Europe. Geoffrey invented Merlin, the sword in the stone, the fight against the Saxons, and much of the rest. **Wace** translated Geoffrey into Anglo-Norman French (the *Roman de Brut*, 1155) and introduced the **Round Table**. Chrétien de Troyes added Lancelot, the Grail, and the mood of courtly love. The Vulgate Cycle (1210s–1230s) gave the full prose romance. By 1300, Arthur was the imaginative king of the medieval Anglo-French mind.

**What Arthur meant:** the **once and future king** — the ideal ruler who served the land rather than dominating it. **Camelot** as the perfect court where justice lived. The **Round Table** as egalitarian fellowship among equals. The **Grail** as the sacred object sought by knights at the uttermost edge of meaning. **Morgan le Fay** — originally a healer and keeper of Avalon, gradually darkened into a sorceress-antagonist. **The Lady of the Lake** — sovereignty bestowed and reclaimed by the sacred feminine. Arthur's death not as an ending but a **sleeping** in Avalon, to wake again when Britain has need.

In Ardenmere, this cycle exists as **the kingdom's own legendary past** — stories of the old king who slept before the Purge, stories of the Round Table of druid-druids-and-nobles who worked together, stories of the Hawthorn-keeper who holds the king's sword until Britain needs it again. The peasants tell these stories at the hearth. The nobility dismiss them as peasant fancy. Both parties are wrong about why they matter.

### The Holy Grail and the quest

The **Grail** emerged, in Chrétien's unfinished *Perceval*, as a mysterious object carried in a procession in a magical castle — Perceval's failure to ask "whom does the Grail serve?" led to the Waste Land. Robert de Boron's *Joseph d'Arimathie* (c. 1200) Christianised the Grail as the cup of the Last Supper, brought to Britain by Joseph of Arimathea. The Vulgate Cycle expanded the Grail quest into the great spiritual quest of the Round Table, culminating in Galahad's achievement of the Grail and his ascension into heaven.

The Grail is the archetype of **the sacred object that heals the broken land** — which makes it a particularly powerful myth for a culture aware of its own fracture. For Ardenmere, the "Grail" might be the forgotten knowledge of the Braedunic druids — or a specific object that carries that memory, hidden in a peasant cottage or an overgrown standing stone.

### Robin Hood and the wood-outlaw

Alongside the knightly legend runs the **peasant legend** — and its hero is **Robin Hood**. The earliest surviving Robin Hood ballads are late (*A Gest of Robyn Hode*, c. 1450), but the figure appears in fourteenth-century references and may well go back earlier. Robin:
- Lives in the **greenwood** — Sherwood Forest, the king's deer, the outlaw's refuge.
- Robs the rich (the abbot, the sheriff of Nottingham, corrupt prelates) and gives to the poor.
- Is loyal to the true king but in rebellion against the unjust local power.
- Is joined by a company — Little John, Will Scarlet, Maid Marian, Friar Tuck.
- Shoots the longbow. Steals the king's deer. Fights with quarterstaff.
- Keeps the old rough justice of the people.

Robin Hood is the **peasant's fantasy of justice** — a fantasy of the green wood as a zone outside the manorial grid, where the law of the lord does not reach. He is the imaginative cousin of the druid fugitive: a figure of a people who remember there is another way to live.

### The Wild Hunt, the Green Man, the fae

Beneath and beside the literary mythology ran the **folkloric mythology** of the countryside:

- **The Green Man** — the leaf-faced figure carved into church roof-bosses, misericords, and capitals across England and France. Pre-Christian in origin, absorbed into the Christian iconography of the parish church, he is the spirit of vegetation, the forest's consciousness. For Ardenmere, the Green Man is a **druid survival in stone** — carved by peasant masons who half-remembered who he was.

- **The Wild Hunt** — a ghostly cavalcade of spectral hunters led sometimes by Herne the Hunter, sometimes by King Herla, sometimes by the devil. Heard on stormy nights. The living who saw it were sometimes swept up and carried away. Pan-European folk belief with Celtic, Germanic, and older roots.

- **The Fair Folk** — the fairies, the elves, the *gens de petite foi*. Small, strange, beautiful, dangerous. They lived in hills, in mounds, in hawthorn thickets, under lakes. Stealing children and leaving **changelings**. Luring mortals into the Otherworld from which they returned a hundred years later not knowing a night had passed. The fairies were appeased with offerings of milk in saucers at the door, small loaves of bread at the threshold, ribbons on the hawthorn. Nobody admitted to believing in them in front of the priest. Everybody put the milk out.

- **Saints who behave like demigods** — St George, St Michael, the Virgin Mary especially — took on some of the roles of the older gods. Marian apparitions, wells that healed, relics that performed miracles. The Virgin in particular absorbed the love once given to the great goddesses.

- **Werewolves** (*loup-garou* in French, widely feared in rural France) — men who changed shape at the full moon. **Witches** — feared but not yet at fever-pitch. **Dragons** — in local legend, defeated by saints, whose hill or well was shown to pilgrims.

### Monsters at the edge of the map

Medieval bestiaries and maps populated the world's edges with **Plinian races** — Cynocephali (dog-heads), Blemmyae (headless men with faces in their chests), Sciapods (one-footed men who used their foot as a sunshade), griffins, basilisks, manticores, sea-monsters. The known world was a small disc of civilisation surrounded by a vast, terrifying unknown. This framed both the **pilgrimage** (a venture into the half-known) and the **crusade** (a plunge into the outer world).

### How myth shaped cultural identity

Medieval Anglo-French identity was built on **triple allegiance**:
- Christian — the shared universal faith and the saints' lives.
- Arthurian / chivalric — the knightly code, the quest, the Round Table.
- Local — the parish saint, the village story, the fae, the Green Man, the old stone.

A peasant might not think of himself as "Ardenmeri" in any state-national sense. He would think of himself as "of St Margaret's parish, under Sir Hugh's manor, in the lands of the Count of West Marches, in the realm of the King." Identity was **laminated from local to universal**, with the **local stories** the deepest layer — the layer where the druidic memory survives.

### What this means for Ardenmere

Ardenmere is **densely mythologised**. At the parish level, every village knows its ghost, its fairy-hill, its holy well, its ancient stone with a story. At the provincial level, there is a legend-cycle of the once-and-future king who slept before the Purge — sometimes called by a name that would embarrass the priests. At the realm level, the orthodox chivalric romance is told in the manor halls. **These three layers know about each other and lie about each other.** The peasant says yes-Father to the priest's catechism and goes home and puts milk on the doorstep. **The Green Man in the parish church roof-boss is the druidic god's face, carved there by a mason whose great-grandfather was a druid, who could not have said so if asked.**
