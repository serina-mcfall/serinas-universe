# Cultural Deep Research for a Fantasy World-Building Project

I am building a fantasy fiction world called Avalúne. Each country in my world is inspired by a specific real-world ancient civilization (or fusion of civilizations). I need a comprehensive cultural research dossier on each of the real-world cultures below so I can build my fictional countries on a foundation of authentic, researched cultural DNA.

**Research time period:** Focus on the ancient, classical, and early medieval eras of each culture — roughly the period when each was at its cultural height before significant outside influence (e.g. pre-Roman Celtic Britain, Tang/Song dynasty China, Viking Age Scandinavia, etc.). For each culture, make clear which specific historical period you are drawing from.

**Output format:** A separate dossier per culture, each organized under the same consistent category headings (listed below). Use clear section breaks. Include specific historical examples, not just generalities. Where cultures influenced each other, note the connections.

## The Cultures to Research

1. **Ancient China** (primary focus: Tang and Song dynasties, but include earlier periods like Han and Zhou as relevant) — includes cultivation practices, Daoism, imperial courts, the scholar-gentry, the Silk Road era
2. **Viking Age Scandinavia + Medieval Mongolian Steppe Culture** (as a fusion) — Norse warrior society, sagas, beast-bonding traditions in both cultures, shamanism, the ger/longhouse, horseback warfare, clan structures
3. **Pre-Roman Celtic Britain + Celtic Ireland** — druids, the Iron Age, Stonehenge-era megalithic cultures as precursors, Arthurian legend's Celtic roots, the Otherworld, fire festivals, oral tradition
4. **Byzantine Empire + Medieval Italian City-States (Venice, Florence, Genoa)** — as a fusion — guild politics, complex court intrigue, the Orthodox Church structure, mercantile republics, councils, eastern Mediterranean aesthetics
5. **Medieval Anglo-French** (roughly 10th-13th century) — feudal agriculture, serfdom, manor estates, the peasant-noble relationship, the tension between free and bonded labor
6. **Ancient and Medieval Middle Eastern desert cultures** — specifically a fusion of **Bedouin Arabian, Ancient Egyptian, and Berber Saharan** cultures — caravan trade, desert survival, tribal structures, relationship with water, the sacred oasis
7. **Byzantine Empire + Tang Dynasty China** (as a fusion crossroads culture) — the historical Silk Road cities where East met West: pleasure quarters, courtesan culture, spice trade, multicultural cosmopolitan centers, the hybrid art and architecture of places like Dunhuang or Constantinople
8. **Legendary advanced civilizations** — specifically **Atlantis myths + ancient Egyptian priestly caste + imagined high-magic aerial civilizations** — for a culture that presents itself as refined, wealthy, and superior while hiding a dark secret underneath

## Categories to Cover (for EACH culture above)

For every culture, please provide research on ALL of the following categories. Go deep on each — this is for worldbuilding, so specific details, sensory information, and real examples matter more than high-level summaries.

### 1. THE LAND AND GEOGRAPHY
- Physical landscape, climate, weather patterns
- Flora and fauna — what grew there, what animals were sacred/common/dangerous
- How the people related to their environment
- Sacred geography — specific places they considered holy

### 2. SOCIAL STRUCTURE
- The full class/caste system from top to bottom
- How mobility between classes worked (if at all)
- The role of slaves, servants, or bonded labor
- The relationship between nobility and commoners
- Outsiders, outcasts, and the "untouchables"

### 3. GOVERNMENT AND POLITICS
- Who ruled and how they came to power
- Laws and justice systems
- Taxation, tribute, economic obligations
- Military organization and command
- Diplomacy with neighbors
- Corruption, intrigue, and power struggles

### 4. RELIGION AND SPIRITUALITY
- Gods, spirits, or sacred forces
- Creation myths and cosmology
- Priests, shamans, druids, monks — training and roles
- Sacred texts or oral traditions
- Rituals, sacrifices, offerings
- The afterlife and the soul
- Religion in daily life
- Magic and the supernatural as part of everyday belief

### 5. FAMILY AND KINSHIP
- Family structure
- Gender roles and rights
- Marriage customs and taboos
- Child-rearing and education of the young
- Elders and ancestor veneration
- Fosterage, adoption, extended kin networks

### 6. LIFE CYCLE RITUALS
- Birth and naming customs
- Coming of age and initiation
- Marriage ceremonies
- Death, burial, and mourning
- Festivals that marked the year

### 7. CLOTHING AND ADORNMENT
- Everyday clothing for different classes and genders
- Ceremonial and festival dress
- Colors, fabrics, dyes available
- Jewelry, tattoos, body paint, hair styling
- What clothing communicated about status, marriage, profession

### 8. FOOD AND DRINK
- Staple crops and main foods
- Meat, fish, dairy availability
- Spices, herbs, seasonings
- Feasting customs and food as hospitality
- Alcohol and ritual drinks
- Food taboos and fasts
- Mundane daily meal vs. special occasion food

### 9. ARCHITECTURE AND SETTLEMENT
- Housing for different classes
- Public buildings, temples, halls
- Defensive structures — walls, forts, hillforts
- How cities or villages were laid out
- Building materials and construction techniques
- Interior furnishings and domestic life

### 10. ART, MUSIC, AND STORYTELLING
- Visual art — painting, carving, weaving
- Music — instruments, purposes, performers
- Oral tradition — poets, bards, storytellers
- Written tradition (if any)
- Dance and performance

### 11. TECHNOLOGY AND CRAFTS
- Metalworking and metals worked
- Textile production
- Pottery, glass, woodworking
- Agricultural techniques and tools
- Boats, carts, transportation
- Cutting-edge technology of the era

### 12. WARFARE AND WEAPONS
- Fighting styles and military tactics
- Weapons — materials, wielding
- Armor and protection
- Warrior class and warrior culture
- Women in warfare (if applicable)
- How war was declared, fought, and ended

### 13. TRADE AND ECONOMY
- Exports and imports
- Currency and barter systems
- Markets and merchants
- Long-distance trade routes

### 14. LANGUAGE AND WRITING
- Languages spoken
- Writing systems and literacy
- The spoken word in cultures without writing
- Poetry, rhetoric, and verbal arts

### 15. EDUCATION AND KNOWLEDGE
- How children were taught
- Specialized training (priesthood, scholarship, crafts)
- Libraries, schools, universities (if any)
- What kinds of knowledge were valued

### 16. MEDICINE AND HEALING
- Illnesses and treatments
- Herbalism and folk medicine
- Surgery and midwifery
- Physical healing vs. spiritual healing

### 17. DAILY LIFE
- Average person's day
- Work, leisure, rest
- Hygiene and personal care
- Sleep, homes, domestic rhythms

### 18. MYTHOLOGY AND LEGEND (beyond formal religion)
- Stories told around fires
- Legendary heroes
- Monsters, creatures, supernatural
- How myth shaped cultural identity

### 19. TABOOS, LAWS, AND ETHICS
- Deep cultural wrongs
- Honor and shame
- Hospitality laws
- Oaths, vows, sacred promises

### 20. THE FEELING OF THE CULTURE
- What a traveler would notice first
- Smells, sounds, sights
- Ambient "mood" of the culture
- What outsiders knew them for

## Additional Requests

- **Use specific historical examples.** Name real rulers, battles, artifacts, sites, practices.
- **Include sensory detail.** Smells, sounds, textures, tastes.
- **Note connections between cultures.** Where they traded, overlapped, influenced each other.
- **Include minority or often-overlooked groups** within each culture — women, slaves, outsiders, religious minorities — not just the ruling class.
- **Cite sources at the end** for further reading.
- **Length:** Do not compress. Give depth. A 20,000-word dossier is preferred over a 2,000-word summary.
