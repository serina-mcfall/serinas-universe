## 1. THE LAND AND GEOGRAPHY

### Constantinople — the city on two continents

Constantinople sat on a triangular peninsula where the **Golden Horn**, the **Bosphorus Strait**, and the **Sea of Marmara** met. Europe on one side, Asia on the other, water on three. The city *was* the crossroads — you could not travel overland from Eastern Europe to Western Asia without passing through the Bosphorus, and you could not ship goods from the Black Sea to the Mediterranean without passing beneath its walls. Geography made Constantinople rich before any emperor ever did. The **Golden Horn** was a natural deep-water harbour where Venetian, Genoese, Arab, Rus, and Bulgar ships anchored alongside Byzantine galleys; a great iron chain could be drawn across its mouth to seal the city in wartime. Around the peninsula rose the **Theodosian Walls** — triple fortifications with a moat that held off besiegers for a thousand years until gunpowder finally broke them.

The land around Constantinople was fertile but narrow — orchards and vineyards behind the walls, grain fleets sailing in from Egypt and later the Black Sea. The climate was temperate Mediterranean, with wet mild winters and hot dry summers. Fog rolled off the Bosphorus at dawn. Seabirds followed every fishing boat. The scent of the city was **salt water, lamp oil, cypress, incense, and horses**.

### Chang'an — the city of the four cardinal gates

Tang Chang'an (modern Xi'an) was the largest city on Earth in the 8th century, with nearly one million inhabitants inside its walls. It lay on the **Wei River plain** in the Guanzhong basin, sheltered by the Qinling Mountains to the south and the loess plateau to the north. The surrounding land was fertile yellow-earth farmland — wheat, millet, hemp, mulberry. The city was **rectangular, precisely gridded**, 9.7 km east–west by 8.6 km north–south, with 108 walled residential wards and two great markets (Eastern and Western). The **Western Market** was the Silk Road's terminus — Sogdians, Persians, Arabs, Uighurs, Tibetans, and Koreans all traded there. The **Eastern Market** served the Chinese aristocracy.

Chang'an's climate was continental — freezing winters with dust storms blown off the steppe, blazing summers, brief flower-heavy springs. Beyond the walls stretched apricot and peach orchards, mulberry groves for silkworms, and the imperial hunting parks. Further west the land broke into loess cliffs, then the **Hexi Corridor** running toward Dunhuang and the Taklamakan. The **sacred Mount Hua** rose within three days' ride — Daoist hermits lived on its terraces.

### Solis — a city on the bridge between worlds

For Solis, fuse the two geographies: a great **bridge-city** spanning a strait where a warm southern sea meets a cooler northern one, backed by fertile river plains and mulberry groves, with the **Silk Road caravan route** ending at its eastern gate and **the sea-lanes** beginning at its western harbour. Solis should feel like a peninsula between waters — the landmass is narrow, so that to move east–west you always cross a bridge, a ferry, a canal. Walls on the landward side, a chained harbour on the seaward, and beyond the walls the mixed farmland of olive, vine, mulberry, rice paddy, and apricot orchard. The air smells of **brine, spice smoke, sandalwood, and horse sweat** — the Silk Road's perfume.

### Sacred geography

Byzantine Constantinople was studded with **holy springs** (hagiasmata), miraculous icons in specific churches, and columns where saints had stood. The **Column of Constantine** and the **Column of Justinian** in the Augustaion were axis-mundi points. Tang Chinese cities had their own sacred sites — **Temple of the Peach Blossom**, Daoist and Buddhist monasteries, the **shuilao** (water-old-ones) shrines at river bends. Dunhuang's Mogao Caves were a *pilgrimage landscape*, 492 cave-temples carved into a single cliff-face. For Solis, imagine both: holy-water fountains at street corners, cave-shrines dug into the harbour cliffs, a great central basilica *and* a great central pagoda sharing a plaza, and somewhere in a courtyard nobody maps — **the Mulberry pillar tree**, which is the actual axis of the world.

## 2. SOCIAL STRUCTURE

### Byzantine Constantinople — rank, not blood

Byzantine society had a rigid hierarchy but — unlike Western feudalism — it was based on **office and title**, not hereditary lineage. In theory, any free-born citizen could rise. In practice, the path ran through the imperial bureaucracy, the church, or the army. The hierarchy from top to bottom:

- **The Emperor (Basileus) and Empress (Augusta)** — the living icons of Christ on earth, absolute in theory. Theodora's case is the famous proof of Byzantine mobility: a bear-keeper's daughter, courtesan, and actress became Empress and ruled beside Justinian as near-equal. Justinian had a law literally rewritten so he could marry her.
- **The Senate and high aristocracy** — the **dynatoi** (powerful ones). Great land-owning families (Doukas, Komnenos, Angelos, Phokas). Their power came from estates, court titles, and marriage alliances.
- **The civil bureaucracy** — the *logothetes*, eunuch chamberlains, scribes, notaries. Eunuchs had a special, quite elevated role in Byzantium: castrated men could not found dynasties, so emperors trusted them with high office. Some of the greatest Byzantine generals (Narses) and patriarchs were eunuchs.
- **The clergy** — from the Patriarch of Constantinople down through bishops, priests, monks, nuns. Monasteries held huge estates. The Patriarch crowned and, occasionally, deposed emperors.
- **The military** — *strategoi* (generals) commanding the themes, tagmata (guard regiments), and the Varangian Guard (Rus and Norse mercenaries personally loyal to the Emperor).
- **The merchants and guilds** — silk-weavers, goldsmiths, spice sellers, money-changers. Tightly regulated by the **Book of the Eparch**. Respectable but never truly noble.
- **Free commoners** — farmers, artisans, sailors, soldiers' wives, midwives, hippodrome attendants.
- **Slaves** — war captives, debtors, bought on the markets. Could buy freedom. Could rise (a slave could become a general's steward and accumulate wealth).
- **The demi-world** — actors, acrobats, chariot-drivers, mime performers, courtesans. Legally infamous, socially indispensable. Theodora came from this layer.

### Tang China — examination, not blood (eventually)

Tang society preserved the great aristocratic clans but — uniquely — introduced the **imperial examination system** (*keju*) as a parallel path. By the 9th century, roughly 85% of grand chancellors had entered via examination. Hierarchy:

- **The Son of Heaven** — the Emperor, with the Empress and consorts.
- **The imperial clan and great aristocratic families** — the "Seven Great Surnames" who had dominated since the Northern Dynasties.
- **The scholar-officials (shi)** — the literati who passed the examinations. Below nobility, above merchants, but in many ways the real operators of the empire.
- **Farmers (nong)** — most of the population. The Confucian "root of the realm."
- **Artisans (gong)** — silk-weavers, potters, metalworkers. Skilled and respected but below farmers in Confucian theory.
- **Merchants (shang)** — wealthy, often wealthier than officials, but Confucianism ranked them LAST of the four respectable classes because they "produced nothing."
- **Entertainers and courtesans** — registered in the *jiaofang* (music training quarter). The highest courtesans of Pingkangfang were artists, poets, calligraphers — ranked alongside the literati in talent, below them in status.
- **Slaves and bound servants** — a small, restricted category. Tang law distinguished *guanhu* (official slaves) from free commoners carefully.

### The Solis synthesis

Solis should combine **Byzantine title-hierarchy** (ranks, medallions, colours of belt that you wear) with **Tang examination-aristocracy** (you can rise through tests, through talent in the arts, through the guilds). The aristocracy is a fusion — some families are ancient and land-owning, others are new money from the caravan trade, others are **examination-families** whose grandfather was a scribe and whose grandson might be chancellor. **The merchants outearn the nobles** — but they cannot wear the imperial purple, and so they buy titles and marry into land.

**The pleasure quarter is its own class.** In both Pingkangfang and Constantinople's theatre districts, registered courtesans occupied a strange liminal rank: legally infamous, officially segregated, but *culturally central*. Emperors took wives from here. Scholars wrote poetry to them. Lady Pearl's house in Solis inherits this exactly — a place where the lowest legal class holds the highest cultural power. The **Smiling Blade** runs the public face — every visiting dignitary goes through him before they meet anyone who matters.

## 3. GOVERNMENT AND POLITICS

### Byzantine imperial government

The Byzantine Emperor was **isapostolos** — "equal to the apostles" — Christ's deputy on earth, head of both state and church (caesaropapism, though the Patriarch pushed back). Imperial succession was a mess: *no clear law of succession.* Emperors were made by army acclamation, senate vote, popular approval in the Hippodrome, or murder. More than a third of Byzantine emperors were deposed, blinded, castrated, or killed. **Porphyrogenitus** ("born in the purple") — children born to a reigning emperor in the purple birthing chamber of the palace — had the strongest legitimacy, but even this was often overridden by a strong general's coup.

The bureaucracy was enormous and literate. The **Logothete of the Dromos** ran the post and foreign affairs. The **Sakellarios** ran the treasury. The **Eparch of the City** (*prefect*) ran Constantinople — regulating guilds, markets, prostitution, and public order. The **Book of the Eparch** (c. 900 CE) lays out in extraordinary detail which trades could do what, where, and for how much. This was a regulation-obsessed state.

**Justinian's Corpus Juris Civilis (528–534 CE)** is the legal foundation: the *Code* (imperial law), the *Digest* (juristic opinion), the *Institutes* (law textbook), the *Novels* (later legislation). It systematized a thousand years of Roman law and is still the ancestor of every civil-law legal system in the world. Byzantine law was **written, written, written**. Crimes were codified. Punishments were graded. Disputes went to *judges* who cited precedent.

### Tang imperial government

The Tang adopted the **Three Departments and Six Ministries** system (*sansheng liubu*). Three coordinating departments at the top — Secretariat (drafts edicts), Chancellery (reviews them), Department of State Affairs (executes them) — and six ministries beneath: Personnel, Revenue, Rites, War, Justice, Works. It was the most sophisticated bureaucracy on earth in its time.

The examination system (*keju*) recruited officials from across the empire. Candidates studied the Confucian Classics for years, memorising thousands of lines. The *jinshi* degree ("presented scholar") was the prestige prize. Successful candidates were feted — and traditionally they spent their celebration nights in **Pingkangfang**, the courtesan quarter, drinking and composing poetry with registered courtesans. The connection between examination success and the pleasure quarter was so fixed that it became a poetic topos: Meng Jiao's "I gallop through Chang'an streets, viewing all its flowers in one day" is about a new *jinshi* drinking his way through Pingkangfang.

**Law in Tang China** was codified in the **Tang Code (Tanglü shuyi, 653 CE)** — hugely influential across East Asia, copied by Japan and Korea. Five standard punishments (caning-light, caning-heavy, penal servitude, exile, death), ten abominations (the gravest crimes — treason, impiety, unfilial conduct). The magistrate was judge, jury, and often interrogator (confession was legally required for conviction, so torture was permitted within limits).

### Corruption, intrigue, and the shadow-state

Both empires lived on intrigue. Byzantine court politics were a chess game of eunuchs, patriarchs, generals, and imperial widows. The **blinding** (*typhlosis*) was the preferred way to disqualify a rival from the throne without technically killing him. Tang intrigue ran through consort politics — which imperial concubine controlled the emperor's ear, which minister she favoured, which prince she backed for succession. **Empress Wu Zetian**, the only woman to officially rule China in her own name, rose from concubine through sheer political will and executed her rivals.

### Solis government

Solis should be a **council republic topped by a hereditary-but-contested monarch** — imagine Byzantine emperor + Venetian doge + Tang examination system layered together. A Doge/Basileus figure elected from the great houses by a council of guilds and nobles, serving for life or until deposed; a **Council of Seven** (guilds of silk, spice, finance, glass, iron, salt, and the pleasure quarter — yes, the pleasure quarter has a seat); an examination-recruited civil service; and beneath all of that, **the real power** — Lady Pearl's information network, which the monarch depends on without knowing how completely. The formal law is the **Luminous Code** (the Solis answer to the Codex Justinianus). The informal law is **whatever Lady Pearl writes on silk and sends to your door**.

## 4. RELIGION AND SPIRITUALITY

### Byzantine Orthodoxy — the public religion

Eastern Orthodox Christianity was the official religion, pervading every moment of Byzantine life. **Icons** were at the centre: painted panels of Christ, the Theotokos ("God-bearer" — Mary), and saints, believed to be windows onto the holy persons depicted. The famous **Iconoclast Controversy** (726–843 CE) saw emperors attempt to ban icons as idolatry; it ended with icons triumphantly restored and an annual feast of their return (the "Triumph of Orthodoxy"). The liturgical year began on **September 1** and was built around twelve Great Feasts, with Pascha (Easter) above all. The daily offices (Matins, Vespers, the Hours) ran in every monastery and cathedral. Incense, chanted psalms in Greek, candles before icons — the sensory signature of the Byzantine sacred.

**Monasticism** was central — tens of thousands of monks and nuns across the empire, the most sacred communities on **Mount Athos** (founded 963 CE). Monks practiced **hesychia** ("stillness") and the Jesus Prayer, believing that constant inner prayer could lead to theosis — deification, partaking of divine nature.

### Tang pluralism — the religious supermarket

Tang China was uniquely tolerant of foreign religions. Within Chang'an's walls one could find:

- **Buddhism** — the dominant foreign religion, enormously powerful, with hundreds of monasteries. Pilgrims like **Xuanzang** (who journeyed 16 years to India, 629–645 CE, and returned with 657 Sanskrit sutras) made Chang'an a global Buddhist capital.
- **Daoism** — the native religion, favoured by the Tang imperial family (who claimed descent from Laozi). Alchemy, immortality-seeking, mountain hermitage.
- **Confucianism** — the state orthodoxy, defining examination, ritual, and family ethics.
- **Zoroastrianism** — the Sogdian and Persian merchant religion. Fire temples (*xianci*) stood in Chang'an. The Tang permitted them to worship but forbade native Chinese from joining.
- **Nestorian Christianity** (called *Jingjiao* — "Luminous Teaching") — Church of the East missionaries arrived 635 CE. The **Xi'an Stele** (781 CE) records 150 years of Chinese Christian history, listing monasteries in over 100 cities.
- **Manichaeism** — Persian dualist religion, especially favoured among the Uighurs who helped the Tang against the An Lushan Rebellion (755–763).
- **Islam** — Arab and Persian merchants brought Islam to Guangzhou and Chang'an from the mid-7th century; the first mosque in China is traditionally dated to 627.
- **Judaism** — communities in Kaifeng and along the Silk Road.

In 845 CE, Emperor Wuzong launched the **Great Anti-Buddhist Persecution**, which also suppressed Zoroastrians, Manichaeans, and Christians. The religious pluralism of the early Tang never fully recovered.

### The Silk Road as religious highway

Religion moved along the Silk Road as much as silk did. Buddhism entered China through Sogdian and Kushan intermediaries; the **Mogao Caves at Dunhuang** are the physical record of this transmission, with cave-paintings blending Gandharan-Greek sculptural style, Iranian costume, and Chinese brushwork. **Manichaeism** reached China via Persia and Central Asia. **Nestorian Christianity** came via Syria and Persia. Pilgrimage was an engine of transmission: Xuanzang's westward journey and the later **Faxian**, **Yijing**, and **Wukong** expeditions brought back texts, images, and customs.

### Solis religion — the melted pantheon

Solis should host the sacred *everything*. In the great square at the city's heart, a **Domed Basilica** to the Sun-Father (an Orthodox-styled temple full of icons and incense) stands opposite a **Five-Tiered Pagoda** for the Pearl-Mother (Buddhist-styled with bells at every corner), with a **Fire Altar** to the Zoroastrian god of flame between them, and a small **Nestorian chapel** set off to one side carrying a stele in two scripts. The city lets everyone pray.

But Solis is **jeweled rot** — beneath the glitter, religion has collapsed into idol worship, money worship, pleasure worship. The great festivals are pageants. The icons are gilded over cracked wood. The priests take bribes. **The only living sacred thing in Solis is the Mulberry pillar tree in Lady Pearl's courtyard** — and nobody knows it's there, because nobody worships what they don't see. The Water Twin keeps a temple that is not a temple, a goddess who is not a goddess, and the city mistakes her pleasure house for the edge of the sacred when it is actually the centre. This is the theological scandal of Solis: **the city has built cathedrals everywhere the gods are not, and one brothel where a god is.**

## 5. FAMILY AND KINSHIP

### Byzantine family — the Christian household

Byzantine families were nominally patriarchal but, in practice, mothers and grandmothers wielded enormous influence. Marriages were arranged, usually by family elders, often in the girl's early teens. **Dowries** were contractual — the bride's family provided the dowry, the groom a counter-gift, and all was recorded in writing. Betrothal was legally binding from age 7, marriage consummated typically at 12–14 for girls. Divorce was restricted but possible on specific grounds (adultery, treason, attempting murder of spouse); second marriages were permitted but frowned upon, third marriages strongly discouraged, fourth marriages canonically forbidden.

The **gynaikeion** (women's quarters) in noble homes was a *private* space where women could weave, spin, and converse — not a prison. Women shopped in the markets, attended church (seated separately from men), visited bathhouses, and owned property. Widows controlled their dowries. Empresses could rule in their own name (Irene, 797–802 CE, Zoë and Theodora, 11th century).

Children were sacred; the Orthodox baptism was performed around day 40, and the child received a saint's name. **Godparenthood** (*kumbaros*) created a quasi-kinship bond between families as strong as blood. Elders were venerated; grandmothers told the **grandmothers' tales** (*paramythia*) that kept folk memory alive alongside the formal Scripture.

### Tang family — the Confucian lineage

Tang families organized around **Confucian filial piety (xiao)**. The father was head of household; obedience to parents was the first virtue; the eldest son inherited the lineage role. The **Five Relationships** structured life: ruler–subject, father–son, husband–wife, elder–younger brother, friend–friend. Marriage was parent-arranged, typically between families of equal status. Cousins on the mother's side could marry; cousins on the father's side could not (same-surname marriage was forbidden).

Tang women had more freedom than in later dynasties. They rode horses, played polo, wore Hu (foreign) dress, kept property through marriage, and sometimes divorced. **Empress Wu Zetian** shattered the ceiling altogether by becoming the only female emperor in Chinese history (r. 690–705). The Tang practiced polygamy for those who could afford it: one principal wife, multiple concubines, children of concubines recognised as legitimate but ranked below the principal wife's children.

Ancestors were central. Every house had an **altar** to the lineage ancestors, where incense was burned at the new moon, full moon, and on death anniversaries. The eldest son led these rituals. To die without a son was a catastrophe — the ancestors starved in the next world. **Adoption** was therefore common; a brother's son could be adopted to continue the line.

### Fosterage and chosen kin

Byzantine society practiced **godparenthood** as an extra-blood tie. Tang China practiced **sworn brotherhood** (*jiebai xiongdi*) — men of no blood relation binding themselves by oath to be brothers, sharing fortunes and fates. Both cultures understood that the family could be made, not only born.

### Solis kinship — blood, oath, and purchased bond

Solis inherits all of this. Marriages are arranged at the upper levels, love-made at the lower. Dowries are contractual and written. Women of the pleasure quarter constitute their own kinship network — **sisters by the sash**, courtesans who have shared a house swear lifelong loyalty to one another regardless of later patrons. This is Lady Pearl's actual blood-family: the women of her house are her true kin, bound by sworn-sisterhood, godparenthood, and contract. **A girl taken in by Lady Pearl is more truly her daughter than any blood-child could be** — because the bond was chosen, witnessed, and sealed in ink.

The **Mulberry pillar tree** in Lady Pearl's courtyard has a kinship meaning too: mulberry is the tree whose leaves feed silkworms, and silk binds East to West. Every woman of the house touches the bark on entering. It is their family altar, though they do not know it is an altar.

## 6. LIFE CYCLE RITUALS

### Birth and naming

**Byzantine:** An infant was baptised around 40 days after birth, at which point it received its saint's name. The **forty-day churching** — the mother's return to the sanctuary after postpartum seclusion — was a formal rite. A silver cross was often placed around the infant's neck. The godfather stood sponsor, and the child was anointed with chrism (myron) on the forehead, eyes, ears, nostrils, mouth, chest, hands, and feet.

**Tang:** An infant was given a *rǔmíng* (milk-name, affectionate nickname) at birth and a formal *míng* (personal name) at or after the **hundred days' celebration** (*bai ri*). A feast was held; relatives gave red envelopes. A boy received his formal name from his father; a girl often from a grandmother. The child might also receive a **zì** (courtesy name) at the coming-of-age ceremony decades later.

### Coming of age

**Byzantine:** Boys were considered adults at around 14 (the age of majority in Roman law varied). First beard, first shave, first time entering adult society. For girls, betrothal and marriage served as the coming-of-age.

**Tang:** Boys celebrated the **guan li** (capping ceremony) at 20, receiving their *zì* (courtesy name) and putting up their hair under a cap. Girls celebrated the **ji li** (hairpin ceremony) at 15, putting up their hair with a ceremonial hairpin — signifying readiness for marriage.

### Marriage

**Byzantine wedding** — a complex, multi-stage ritual. The **engagement (mnesteia)** was celebrated with exchange of rings; the **betrothal (arrhabon)** with a dowry contract; the **wedding (gamos)** with crowning. The priest placed matching crowns (*stephana*) on the couple's heads, joined by a ribbon, and led them in a threefold circuit around the altar — the "Dance of Isaiah." Feasting followed for days. Music, dancing, poetry, and bawdy songs.

**Tang wedding** — the Six Rites (*liu li*): (1) proposal through matchmaker, (2) exchange of birth dates, (3) fortune-telling for compatibility, (4) exchange of betrothal gifts, (5) setting the date, (6) the wedding itself — the bride carried in a red palanquin, red veil over her face, bowing three times (to heaven and earth, to ancestors, to husband), then drinking the **cross-cup wine** (*hejin jiu*) with her groom. Nine days of feasting for the wealthy. The wedding night traditionally featured guests teasing the bride outside the bridal chamber in the custom of *naofang* ("stirring up the room").

### Death and funerals

**Byzantine:** The body was washed, clothed in the deceased's best garments, anointed with oil, and laid out with an icon on the chest and a cross in the hands. A **wake (parastasis)** was held. Funeral procession to the church, then to burial in the cemetery or, for the wealthy, in a church crypt. **Memorial services** (mnemosyna) were held on the 3rd, 9th, and 40th day after death, and then on every anniversary — with kollyva (boiled wheat with sugar and pomegranate seeds) eaten in memory.

**Tang:** Death rituals were lavish for the wealthy and Confucian in form. The body was dressed in multiple layers of silk, placed in a carved coffin, and kept in the house for extended mourning (49 days, 100 days). **White mourning clothes** — unhemmed hemp for the closest kin. The funeral procession could stretch a mile with paid mourners, musicians, Daoist priests, and Buddhist monks chanting. Burial goods included spirit-money, miniature servants (mingqi), and model houses — a whole household sent into the next world. Tang people believed strongly in the ancestral afterlife; some Buddhist adherents chose cremation instead.

### Annual festivals

**Byzantine:** The liturgical year structured the calendar — **Christmas (Dec 25)**, **Theophany (Jan 6)**, **Pascha (Easter)**, **Pentecost**, **Dormition of the Theotokos (Aug 15)**. The **Hippodrome races** were civic festivals tied to religious dates. January 1 was the Kalends — New Year's feasting.

**Tang:** **Spring Festival / Lunar New Year** (the great renewal), **Lantern Festival** (15th day of the first month — all Chang'an lit with lanterns, the curfew lifted, courtesans dancing on the avenues), **Qingming** (tomb-sweeping in spring), **Dragon Boat Festival** (5th day, 5th month), **Qixi** (the lovers' festival, 7th day, 7th month), **Mid-Autumn** (moon-viewing, 15th day, 8th month).

### Solis festivals — the twinned calendar

Solis runs *two calendars braided together*. New Year is celebrated twice — once in autumn (Byzantine September), once in late winter (Lunar). The Lantern Festival and the Theophany procession share a week. Weddings in Solis take the **crowning** from Byzantium and the **cross-cup wine** from the Tang, the **red palanquin** and the **stephana ribbon** both. This is the city's genius and its rot: it ritualises *everything*, twice.

## 7. CLOTHING AND ADORNMENT

### Byzantine dress — the coded body

Byzantine clothing was **semiotic** — every colour, cut, and pattern told the viewer your rank, office, and status. Men wore the **tunica** (*chiton*) belted at the waist and layered with a **dalmatica** (long wide-sleeved over-tunic) or a **sagion** (cloak fastened at the shoulder with a jewelled fibula). The **paragauda** — embroidered decorative panels applied to the tunic — signalled rank: silk, gold thread, specific patterns.

Women wore the **chiton** (linen or silk undertunic) with a **stola** over it, pinned at the shoulder, and a **maphorion** (veil) covering hair for modesty in public. Married women kept hair covered; maidens could go uncovered. **Pearls** were everywhere — sewn onto borders, dripping from earrings, draped around necks.

**Imperial purple (Tyrian purple)** was a state monopoly. Extracted from murex shellfish (11,000 shells for one gram of dye), purple silk was restricted by law to the imperial family. The **Book of the Eparch** set exact rules — a private citizen found weaving purple could have the cloth confiscated and face heavy fines. Purple boots were the emperor's alone. To be "born in the purple" (*porphyrogenitus*) meant born in the imperial birthing chamber, literally walled in porphyry.

Other Byzantine dress notes: **gold brocade**, **silk damask**, **embroidered hems**, **jewelled belts**, heavy earrings of pearl-and-emerald, cloisonné enamel pendants. Men wore **beards** (neatly trimmed); women wore their hair in elaborate **plaited crowns** under veils. The eunuchs of the court wore distinctive long robes and were beardless by nature.

### Tang dress — the cosmopolitan silhouette

Tang fashion was the most cosmopolitan in Chinese history. Women wore the **qixiong ruqun** — a high-waisted skirt tied above the bust, a short upper garment (*ru*), and a long translucent silk shawl (*pibo*) draped across the shoulders. Necklines in the High Tang were famously low — women could show a "sea of jade" of exposed skin. Hair was arranged in **elaborate buns** (sometimes a dozen varieties in a single court) adorned with golden combs, jade hairpins, kingfisher-feather ornaments, and silk flowers.

Persian influence ran through Tang women's clothing: the **round-collared robe** (*yuanlingpao*) with tight sleeves, originally a steppe-warrior garment, was adopted by aristocratic women who rode horses, played polo, and hunted. This **hufu** ("barbarian dress") was fashionable in the early and mid Tang. Sassanid Persian **pearl-circle motifs** appeared on silk brocades.

Tang makeup was elaborate: **white lead face powder**, **red rouge on cheeks**, **red or yellow forehead ornaments** (*huadian*) painted or cut from gold foil and pasted on, **vermilion lip stain**, **black eyebrow dye** (*dai*) in fashionable shapes (the "moth-antenna" and "willow-leaf" brows). The **dimple ornaments** (*miandian*) — small dots of colour beside the mouth — mimicked the palace beauty-marks.

Men wore round-collared robes over inner garments, black boots, and futou headscarves. Officials wore colour-coded robes by rank: purple for officials of rank 3 and above, crimson for 4–5, green for 6–7, blue for 8–9. **The purple-wearing official** was a mark of the highest bureaucratic caste.

### Silk Road fusion dress

At Dunhuang, cave murals show figures wearing impossible combinations: Chinese scholar in a Sogdian caftan over a Greek-draped chiton, women in qixiong ruqun with Persian pearl-circle brocade patterns and Byzantine-style crowns. This is the actual look of Solis.

### Solis fashion — jeweled rot made cloth

Solis dress is **excessive**. The aristocracy wears silk brocade of Sogdian-Sassanid pearl circles with Byzantine gold thread; women layer qixiong ruqun beneath Orthodox-style stola; men wear round-collared robes belted with Byzantine jeweled cords. **Everyone who can afford it wears pearls** — the city's signature. Hair worn up in Tang styles but veiled for public religion.

**The pleasure quarter dresses most gloriously of all.** Lady Pearl's courtesans wear silks dyed in shades no aristocrat is legally permitted — real imperial purple, saffron, vermilion, kingfisher-blue. Gold-threaded hems. Shoulders bared like a Tang court lady, veil dropped like a Byzantine empress. Jade bangles stack up each arm; the wrist hangs heavy with tiny bells.

**Lady Pearl herself** — imagine a woman in deep-sea pearl-grey silk, sleeves trailing floor-long, hair arranged in the Tang court fashion with a single white-jade hairpin carved as a wave. She does not need gold. She is the water-dragon's sister. The pearls sew themselves to her.

## 8. FOOD AND DRINK

### Byzantine table

The Byzantine diet was Mediterranean at its core. **Bread** was the staple — wheat for the wealthy, barley and millet for the poor. Olive oil in everything. **Fish** (especially from the Black Sea and Bosphorus — mackerel, tuna, red mullet, anchovy) was more common than meat. The famous **garos** (garum — fermented fish sauce) remained in use from Roman times, though increasingly the Byzantines used it more sparingly. **Cheese** (feta-like brine cheeses), **yogurt**, **honey** (the standard sweetener), and **wine** (mixed with water except at feasts).

Spices arrived from the Silk Road: **pepper**, **cumin**, **cinnamon**, **cloves**, **saffron**, **mastic** (the resin chewed and used to flavour drinks and sweets), **sumac**. Byzantine cooks made **sweet-and-sour sauces** (*savors*) with honey and vinegar, **pickled vegetables**, and **stuffed vine leaves**. **Bread and olive oil and wine** — the Christian trinity of table, a holy meal.

Fasting was religious — Lent (40 days before Easter) meant no meat, dairy, wine, or oil; only bread, vegetables, and water. Every Wednesday and Friday were also fast days. This created an elaborate **Lenten cuisine** of bean stews, vegetable pies, and olive-based dishes.

At the imperial table, guests reclined Roman-style on couches in the early empire, but later Byzantine dining shifted to seated tables. Multi-course feasts could last hours. Entertainment — musicians, dancers, mime performers — throughout.

### Tang table

Tang cuisine was at the height of its cosmopolitan expansion. The northern staple was **wheat** (noodles, flatbreads, dumplings), the southern staple **rice**. Persian and Sogdian merchants brought new ingredients: **grapes** (for wine), **pomegranates**, **pistachios**, **walnuts**, **carrots**, **cumin**, **coriander**, **sesame**, **pepper**, **saffron**. Central Asian bakers set up shop in Chang'an selling **huobing** (flat sesame cakes) — a Silk Road snack still familiar today as *shaobing*.

**Tea** came into its own in the Tang — **Lu Yu's Classic of Tea** (*Chajing*, 760 CE) codified the practice. Tea was brewed compressed into bricks, broken off, powdered, and whisked or boiled with salt. Tang tea was not the steeped green tea of today; it was closer to Japanese matcha.

**Meat** — pork, mutton (especially Central Asian-style roasted mutton), goat, chicken, duck, goose, fish from the Yangtze. **Fermented soybean products** (jiang, the ancestor of soy sauce, miso, and doubanjiang). **Sichuan pepper** for tingle. **Wine** — grape wine (*putaojiu*) from Persian-introduced varietals, millet wine, rice wine.

Banquets at the Tang court involved dozens of courses, dancing girls, musicians from Kucha and Samarkand, Persian acrobats, and wine-pouring attendants. Cups circulated in elaborate drinking games with poetry forfeits. Li Bai — the Tang's greatest poet — was so famous for his drunken poetry that the emperor reportedly kept him at court as a "drunken immortal."

### Silk Road food as cultural glue

The Silk Road moved food as heavily as silk. Carrots (Persia to China), grapes (Persia/Central Asia to everyone), silk culture (China to Persia and later Byzantium), saffron, clove, nutmeg, frankincense, myrrh — the scent of the world's kitchens travelled along the caravan routes.

### Solis table — the fusion feast

A Solis dinner begins with **flatbread and small dishes** (mezze-style): olives, cheese, pickled vegetables, yogurt sauces, saffron rice, sesame cakes, *xiaolongbao* steamed dumplings, dolmades (stuffed vine leaves), spiced lamb skewers. **Tea AND wine** are both served — a fundamental Solis thing. The main courses might be **roast mutton with cumin, spring-onion, and pomegranate syrup**, **fish baked whole in salt crust**, **duck glazed with honey-soy**, **pilaf of saffron rice with pistachio and currant**, **noodles in broth**, **sweet-and-sour carp** done in the Tang style but plated Byzantine. Desserts: **honey-soaked phyllo pastry** rolled with pistachios (baklava), **mooncakes with sweet bean paste**, **candied fruits**, **sesame balls**.

**Drinks:** tea in every form, grape wine, millet wine, rice wine, rose water, citrus sherbet, yogurt drinks (ayran/lassi), fortified wines mulled with spice.

**At Lady Pearl's house**, the food is an orchestrated seduction — eight small savoury courses, eight small sweet courses, each paired with a wine or tea or perfumed water. The **Smiling Blade** oversees the menu personally. Every dish is beautiful. Every dish might be poisoned. Most are not. None of the guests are sure.

**Food taboos:** the city is so religiously plural that *everyone's* taboo is honoured *somewhere* — pork is served but also avoided, beef is served but also avoided, meat is sometimes refused on Buddhist vegetarian days, shellfish avoided by the Orthodox on fast days. A Solis cook has to know, for each table, which god is being fed.

## 9. ARCHITECTURE AND SETTLEMENT

### Byzantine architecture — the dome and the icon

Byzantine architecture's defining achievement is **the dome on pendentives** — a spherical triangular transition that allows a circular dome to sit on a square base. The masterwork is **Hagia Sophia** (537 CE), designed by Isidore of Miletus and Anthemius of Tralles for Justinian. Its central dome is 31 metres across and appears, as contemporaries wrote, to be "suspended from heaven by a golden chain." Forty windows at the dome's base flood the interior with light that shimmers off gold mosaic.

Byzantine churches typically had: a **narthex** (entry vestibule), a **naos** (main worship space, crowned by the dome), a **bema** (raised sanctuary), and an **iconostasis** (screen of icons separating sanctuary from nave). Interiors were covered in **gold mosaic**, **marble revetments**, **painted icons**, and **coloured glass**. The effect was meant to be a foretaste of heaven.

Secular architecture included the **Great Palace** (a sprawling complex beside the Hippodrome), **forums** along the Mese with statues on columns, **hippodromes** for chariot racing, **aqueducts** (Valens' aqueduct), and **underground cisterns** — the **Basilica Cistern** is a 138×65-metre subterranean chamber supported by 336 marble columns, holding 80,000 cubic metres of water beneath Constantinople's streets.

Housing ranged from five-storey insulae for the urban poor to sprawling **domus** estates for the rich, with courtyard gardens, mosaic floors, and private baths. The **Theodosian Walls** — triple walls with a moat — protected the landward side of the city.

### Tang architecture — the timber frame and the curved roof

Chinese architecture was **timber post-and-beam** construction on raised stone platforms, with tiled curved roofs whose eaves swept upward at the corners. Walls were **non-load-bearing** — the roof sat on a grid of wooden columns, freeing the walls for screens, lattice windows, and sliding partitions. Large halls used elaborate **dougong** bracket systems to carry the weight of the great tiled roofs.

Tang Chang'an was a **gridded imperial city** — a rectangular wall enclosed 108 residential wards, each itself walled and gated. The wards were shut at night by drum signal. The **Imperial City** in the north centered on the **Daming Palace** complex with its massive audience halls. Buddhist architecture introduced the **pagoda** — a multi-storey tower that adapted the Indian stupa into Chinese timber form. The **Wild Goose Pagoda** (Dayan Ta, 652 CE) still stands in Xi'an — built to house the sutras Xuanzang brought back.

**Markets** had their own architecture — two great walled marketplaces (Eastern and Western), each with rows of shops grouped by trade (the silk street, the saddle street, the fur street, the spice street).

### Dunhuang — the cave-temple

At Dunhuang the Mogao Caves are a UNESCO site: 492 cave-temples carved into a single cliff-face over a kilometre long, from 4th century through the 14th. Each cave is a self-contained Buddhist universe — painted walls, painted ceilings, central pillars, sculpted Buddhas, Apsaras (flying celestial musicians) drifting across the ceilings. The art fuses Gandharan-Greek drapery, Indian-Persian figures, Central Asian costume, and Chinese brushwork.

### Solis architecture — the city of thresholds

Solis architecture is *layered*. The skyline is a collision: Byzantine domes rising from heavy stone drum, and beside them Chinese pagodas rising in timber tiers, with the occasional Sogdian fire-temple cube and Nestorian cross-spire between. Houses of the great houses combine a **stone ground floor** (Byzantine structure) with an **upper timber storey** (Tang construction), a **courtyard garden** in the Chinese style at the centre, and a **domed reception hall** at the front.

Streets are grid-planned in the old quarters (Tang-derived), but chaotic in the harbour quarters (Byzantine-Mediterranean organic growth). The **Pleasure Quarter** is its own walled ward — gates closed at night, lanterns at every door. Inside, the architecture is its most beautiful: interior courtyards, latticed galleries overhead, water channels running through the floors, small private pavilions off every main hall.

**Lady Pearl's House** — officially the **Pavilion of Drifting Pearls** — occupies an entire courtyard block. To the street it shows a modest painted facade. Inside, galleries open onto a vast central courtyard, and at the courtyard's heart — shaded by nothing else — stands **the Mulberry pillar tree**. The tree is impossibly large. The building was constructed *around* it. The roof has a hole for the upper branches. When it rains, water falls on the tree first, then into the courtyard fountain, and the courtesans know: Yúnzhū is home; the tree is listening.

Beneath the house runs a **cistern and tunnel network** — Basilica-Cistern-style — connecting to the harbour, to seven major houses in the city, to the palace, and to a forgotten sea-cave beyond the walls. Lady Pearl's intelligence network runs through the water.

## 10. ART, MUSIC, AND STORYTELLING

### Byzantine art — the icon and the mosaic

Byzantine art served the sacred. **Icon painting** used strict iconographic conventions: Christ with a specific face, Mary with a specific veil, saints with specific attributes — so consistent that a viewer could identify any figure. The style was flat, hieratic, frontal; perspective was reversed (lines diverged toward the viewer, making the icon seem to look out at you).

**Mosaics** covered the walls of every great church and palace: millions of tiny tesserae of gold, stone, and coloured glass, set at slight angles so the light bounced unevenly and the whole surface shimmered. The Ravenna mosaics of Justinian and Theodora (San Vitale, 547 CE) are the archetype — the emperor and empress and their courts, all frontal, all gilded, all eternal.

**Music** was vocal and liturgical — chant in eight modes (the **Octoechos** system). No instruments in church (a theological choice). At the court, though, there were pipe organs, cymbals, lyres, and imperial trumpets. Theatre was Greek-derived: **mime** (*mimos*) troupes, acrobats, dancers, and the bawdy stage performances at which Theodora had made her name before marrying Justinian.

**Literature** — Byzantine literature was Greek-language, erudite, and heavily classical-referencing. Histories (Procopius, Anna Komnene's *Alexiad*), hagiographies (saints' lives), hymns (Romanos the Melodist), epic poetry (*Digenes Akritas*, the Byzantine border-hero epic).

### Tang art — the painted hand-scroll

Tang painting reached a golden age. **Wu Daozi** (680–759 CE) was the master — his brushwork said to move like the wind. Landscape painting (*shanshui*) emerged as a distinct genre; hand-scrolls unrolled slowly from right to left, revealing journeys through mountain, river, and mist. **Horse painting** (Han Gan) and **figure painting** (Zhou Fang's Tang court ladies — plump, heavy-cheeked, robed in translucent silks) are signature images.

**Calligraphy** was considered the highest of the arts. Emperor Taizong personally collected **Wang Xizhi's** "Preface to the Orchid Pavilion" (4th c.) and ordered it buried with him. Every scholar-official practiced calligraphy — your hand was a moral signature.

**Poetry** was the cultural core of the Tang. The **Quan Tang Shi** ("Complete Tang Poems") collects 48,900 poems by over 2,200 poets. **Li Bai** (the wine-drunk immortal), **Du Fu** (the sage-of-poetry), **Wang Wei** (the painter-poet of Mount Zhongnan), **Bai Juyi** (the plain-speaking populist), and the **four great courtesan-poets** — **Xue Tao**, **Yu Xuanji**, **Li Ye (Jilan)**, **Liu Caichun** — were Tang celebrities.

**Xue Tao** (c. 770–832) was registered as a courtesan in Chengdu, but her poetry attracted the military governor Wei Gao and she became one of the most famous literary voices of her time. She designed her own small-format paper (**Xue Tao jian**) for writing poems on, tinted pink. **Yu Xuanji** (c. 844–869) wrote "I regret my silk robes hide my talent, while men's names fill the exam lists" — a direct cry against gender barriers. **Li Jilan** was a Daoist priestess-poet who corresponded with the great male poets of her age.

**Music** in Tang China was enormously cosmopolitan. The **pipa** (four-stringed lute, Persian-derived) was the favourite court instrument. The **Ten Music Bureaus** at the imperial court included ensembles from Samarkand, Bukhara, Kashgar, Kucha, Turfan, India, and Korea. The **daqu** — grand suites of music and dance — were the Tang's answer to opera. Women musicians and dancers (*jinü*) were registered in the **jiaofang** (music training palace) and supplied to the court.

### Silk Road fusion art

Dunhuang cave art is the visual proof of fusion: Apsaras (Buddhist flying goddesses) in diaphanous Sogdian sleeves, Buddha in a Greek-style toga but with Chinese eye-shape, jatakas (stories of Buddha's past lives) painted with Persian pearl-circle borders.

### Solis arts — twin tongues, twin instruments

Solis is a city of **two scripts, two musics, two theatres**. A tavern might have a pipa player at the front and a Byzantine lyre player at the back, both trading off through the evening. Poetry is written **in two columns** — a Chinese-derived character column on one side, an alphabet on the other, the same poem in two languages.

**Painting in Solis** combines icon-gilding with ink-wash landscape. A Lady Pearl portrait would have a gold-leaf Byzantine background, but the figure drawn with Tang brush — round cheeks, translucent silk, half-glance.

**At Lady Pearl's house**, every courtesan must master **the Four Arts** (music, poetry, calligraphy, painting) *AND* the Byzantine arts (dance, rhetoric, voice, chant). A girl spends ten years training before she takes her first client, if she ever does. Many never do — they become scribes, spies, poisoners, diplomats. The house is a conservatory that happens to be a brothel. Or a brothel that happens to be a conservatory. Nobody remembers which came first.

## 11. TECHNOLOGY AND CRAFTS

### Byzantine technology — the hidden edge

Byzantium preserved more Roman technology than the Latin West and developed its own specialties. **Silk-weaving** — smuggled from China in the 6th century by Nestorian monks who allegedly brought silkworm eggs hidden in hollow canes — became an imperial monopoly, with state factories (*gynaikeia*) producing the purple-and-gold brocades of court. **Glassmaking** continued Roman techniques; Byzantine glass was prized across the Mediterranean and Silk Road. **Cloisonné enamel** (coloured glass paste set in gold wire cells, fired to make jewellery and icons) was a Byzantine signature — the **Pala d'Oro** in Venice is Byzantine cloisonné at its height.

**Greek fire** — the most famous Byzantine invention. A petroleum-based incendiary liquid that burned on water and could not be extinguished by normal means. Delivered through bronze siphons on warships or hand-thrown grenades. The formula was a state secret passed from emperor to emperor, and when Constantinople finally fell in 1453, the recipe was already lost. Its composition — thought to include naphtha, quicklime, sulphur, resin, and potassium nitrate — has never been reconstructed.

**Byzantine coinage** — the **solidus** (gold coin) was the dollar of the medieval world, holding its purity and weight for 700 years. Byzantine mechanical engineering included **automata** at the imperial throne (the Emperor Theophilus had mechanical gold lions that roared and mechanical birds that sang — to awe foreign ambassadors into submission).

**Medicine and optics** were advanced. **Aqueducts**, **cisterns**, **water-mills**, and **dockyards** were substantial infrastructure.

### Tang technology — the inventive golden age

Tang China was a technological powerhouse. **Woodblock printing** was invented and refined in the Tang — the **Diamond Sutra** (868 CE, now in the British Library) is the world's oldest dated printed book. This would revolutionize knowledge circulation across East Asia.

**Gunpowder** — the Tang alchemists discovered it while searching for immortality elixirs. First weaponized uses came in the late Tang and Song periods. **Paper** — already invented under the Han, it became cheap and widespread under the Tang; paper money experiments began under the late Tang.

**Porcelain** — Tang **tri-colour ware** (sancai) glazed pottery in amber, green, and cream is famous, but the transition toward true porcelain (kaolin-based, high-fired) began here, to flower fully under the Song. **Silk production** reached peaks of sophistication — the **jin** brocades, **luo** gauze, and **sha** crepe silks were all Tang specialties.

**Metallurgy** — Tang China produced the **Damascus-style pattern-welded steel** known from the Silk Road. **Lacquerware**, **gold filigree**, **inlaid bronze** — all Tang crafts.

### Silk Road crafts

The Silk Road moved techniques as much as goods. Chinese silk reached Byzantium; Byzantine glass reached Chang'an; Persian carpet-weaving, Sogdian metalwork, Indian cotton-dyeing, and Tibetan tanning all flowed along the caravan route.

### Solis technology — the borrowed toolbox

Solis workshops combine techniques. A **Solis silk loom** uses the Chinese pattern-weaving draw-loom with the Byzantine jacquard-like figure-harness. **Solis glass** uses the Roman-Byzantine recipe but with Tang sancai glazes applied after. **Solis paper** is Chinese-tradition (cheap, smooth, practical) but printed with Byzantine-style iconographic borders. **Solis porcelain** is fired in Tang kilns but painted in Byzantine iconography.

**Solis alchemy** braids **Tang Daoist immortality-searching** with **Byzantine alchemy** (which itself came from Alexandrian Greek tradition). The perfumers in the Pleasure Quarter are alchemists in all but name — fragrances layered with narcotic, aphrodisiac, and sometimes lethal infusions.

**Lady Pearl's house** is technologically astonishing without being showy: the water-cistern network beneath doubles as a **listening system** — specific chambers have acoustic channels that carry sound from specific rooms upstairs into specific listening-chambers below. A girl of the house who knows the system can listen to any conversation in any parlour without being seen. The Byzantine Basilica Cistern beneath Constantinople is the real-world referent — but Lady Pearl's is a *listening* cistern.

**Greek fire** has its Solis analogue — a controlled **naphtha-sulphur-resin fire-weapon** kept under the Palace's and Lady Pearl's most secret vaults, for use if the city is ever truly besieged.

## 12. WARFARE AND WEAPONS

### Byzantine warfare — the strategic mind

Byzantine warfare was not the gleaming cavalry-charge of legend. It was **strategic, layered, patient, and manipulative**. The Byzantines preferred to avoid battle altogether — using diplomacy, bribery, marriage alliances, and playing enemies against each other. When they fought, they fought with **science**.

The army was organized into **themes** — military provinces whose soldiers were settled farmers liable for service. Elite forces included the **tagmata** (central guard regiments) and the **Varangian Guard** — Norse, Rus, and Anglo-Saxon mercenaries bound by personal oath to the Emperor, famous for their two-handed Dane axes.

Byzantine weapons: the **spatha** and the later **paramerion** (single-edged cavalry sabre), the **kontos** (long cavalry lance), the composite recurve bow (adopted from steppe neighbours), the **spathion** sword, and the **plumbatae** (lead-weighted darts). Armour included **klibanion** (lamellar scale or plate), mail hauberks, **spangenhelms** (segmented helmets), and cavalry face-masks.

The **cataphracts** were fully-armoured heavy cavalry — horse and rider both in mail and lamellar, charging with lance and mace. **Strategikon** manuals (Maurice's *Strategikon*, Nikephoros Phokas' *De Velitatione*, Leo VI's *Taktika*) gave detailed tactical doctrine: when to engage, when to retreat, how to exploit terrain, how to handle steppe horse-archers, how to use Greek fire.

At sea, **dromons** — long galleys — carried Greek-fire siphons as their main weapon.

### Tang warfare — the cosmopolitan army

The Tang military was heavily **cavalry-based**, reflecting its inheritance from the steppe-adjacent Northern Dynasties. The **fubing** (garrison militia) system settled soldiers on land, who served rotating terms. Elite troops included the **Flying Cavalry** and the personal guard regiments.

Tang weapons: the **dao** (single-edged sabre), the **jian** (straight double-edged sword, the "gentleman's weapon"), the **qiang** spear, the composite recurve bow (the Tang was a master archery culture), the **halberd** (*ji*), and the **crossbow** (*nu*) — Chinese crossbows were more powerful than the contemporary Western ones. Armour ranged from **lamellar** and **mountain-pattern** scale armour to cloth-covered brigandine.

The **Tang General** (e.g., Guo Ziyi, the saviour against An Lushan) was a scholar as much as a warrior. Military theory drew on **Sun Tzu's Art of War** (5th c. BCE) — the canonical text — and the **Seven Military Classics**.

Women in warfare: **Princess Pingyang**, Li Yuan's daughter, led an "Army of the Lady" to help her father overthrow the Sui and found the Tang dynasty. She commanded men in the field. Tang aristocratic women played polo — a martial sport. **Mulan** is a pre-Tang folk ballad but circulated widely in the Tang — the woman who takes her father's place in the army.

### Silk Road warfare

Silk Road cities lived between powers. Most had walled citadels and paid tribute to whoever was strongest at the moment. Steppe cavalry dominated the overland routes. **Sogdian kings** were famous for balancing between the Turks, Tang, Persians, and Arabs.

### Solis warfare — the city that would rather not fight

Solis does not want to fight. Solis wants to **pay you to fight someone else**. The city maintains a small professional standing army (a Varangian-style elite guard, recruited from Isenholt warriors and Aoli cultivators), a navy of Silk Road galleys with fire-siphon weapons, and a huge intelligence budget. Its preferred tools are **gold, secrets, assassins, and disinformation** — all provided via the Pleasure Quarter and Lady Pearl's network.

Solis weapons in open battle: composite bow, sabre, lance, fire-siphon ships. Solis weapons in the dark: **poison**, **blackmail**, **a courtesan with a small jade hairpin**, **a letter that was never actually written**, **a rumour seeded in the Western Market**.

**The Smiling Blade** — a Shi Sect fragment-carrier — is Solis's most honoured swordsman, the duelling-master of the Pleasure Quarter. He is beautiful and laughing, and nobody he has ever duelled has lived to say what his style looked like. The Byzantine duelling tradition (mime-fighters, entertainment combat) and the Chinese jianghu martial-arts world merge in him.

**The courtesans of Lady Pearl's house** train in weapons. Hairpin, fan, ribbon, and small concealed blade. Each of them could kill. Most choose, most nights, not to. This is the deepest scandal of Solis: **the most lethal fighting force in the city wears silk and pours wine and seems to serve.**

## 13. TRADE AND ECONOMY

### The Silk Road — the spine

The Silk Road was not a single road. It was a **network** of overland caravan routes stretching 7,500 miles from Chang'an across the Taklamakan desert, through the Pamir mountains, past Samarkand, through Persia, into Anatolia and the Mediterranean. The southern **Maritime Silk Road** ran sea-lanes from Guangzhou through Malacca, India, Oman, and the Red Sea to Alexandria.

Along these routes moved:
- **Silk** (west)
- **Gold, silver, amber, glass, wool, linen, papyrus** (east)
- **Spices** — pepper, cinnamon, clove, nutmeg, cardamom, saffron (mostly west from South/Southeast Asia)
- **Incense** — frankincense from Oman/Yemen, myrrh from Somalia
- **Horses** — the Ferghana "blood-sweating" horses, prized in China
- **Gems** — lapis lazuli from Afghanistan, jade from Khotan
- **Porcelain, paper, tea, lacquer** (west, especially after Tang)
- **Slaves, mercenaries, and brides** in both directions
- **Religion, language, technology, art, music** — the invisible cargo

### Merchants and middlemen

The Silk Road's indispensable middlemen were the **Sogdians** — Iranian-speaking merchants from the Samarkand-Bukhara region. Sogdian was the *lingua franca* of the Silk Road from the 4th to 9th centuries. Sogdians ran caravan companies, banked, and had permanent communities in every major Silk Road city. Tang records list them in Luoyang and Chang'an as the dominant foreign merchant group.

**Sogdian Letters** (found at the "Ruined Wall" in Dunhuang, c. 313 CE) are merchant correspondence in Sogdian script — a real-time snapshot of Silk Road business, complete with anxieties about unpaid debts and news of a sacked city.

Other merchant communities:
- **Persians** — Sassanian-era and post-Islamic Persian traders
- **Arabs** — especially on the maritime route; a large Arab community at Guangzhou
- **Jews** — the **Radhanite** Jewish traders linked Francia through Byzantium to China
- **Armenians** — moved between Byzantium and the East
- **Byzantines (Rhomaioi)** — Greek-speaking Christians trading into Persia and occasionally beyond

### Currency and money

Byzantine **solidus** (gold) and **miliaresion** (silver) circulated as far as China. Tang **kaiyuan tongbao** copper cash circulated in millions as the everyday currency. Tang merchants used **flying cash** (*feiqian*) — early paper certificates of deposit redeemable in other cities — an ancestor of paper money. **Bars of silver** and **bolts of silk** functioned as high-value currency substitutes along the road.

### Markets

**Chang'an's Western Market** had over 200 specialized trades. Byzantine Constantinople had the **Forum of Constantine**, the **Forum of Theodosius**, and countless specialised markets for silk, fish, spices, and slaves. Tight state regulation — Byzantine guilds under the Book of the Eparch, Tang markets opened and closed at fixed hours by drum signal.

### Solis trade and economy

Solis is the terminus of both the overland Silk Road and the maritime routes. **Its economy is the economy of the crossroads** — it produces little of its own, but **everything passes through it, pays tax to it, and loses a little of itself into its pockets.**

The **Seven Guilds** — silk, spice, finance, glass, iron, salt, and the Pleasure Quarter — each control a sector. The **silk guild** runs the city's weaving (pattern-woven brocades that fuse Chinese motifs with Byzantine borders). The **finance guild** handles the money-changers and letters of credit that make long-distance trade possible — Solis bankers extend credit from Aoli to Ardenmere and back. **The Pleasure Quarter Guild** — which formally exists to regulate the courtesan houses — is actually Lady Pearl's front for her intelligence network. She sells information like silk; she charges like a spice merchant.

**Solis is rich beyond reason**. The aristocracy wears the wealth of twenty countries. The common people work in ten languages. The Pleasure Quarter produces, alone, more annual revenue than some small kingdoms. And yet the city has **no reserves of its own** — everything is pass-through, everything is loaned, everything is leveraged. A year-long caravan disruption could starve it. **Jeweled rot**: a city built on a bridge that could burn beneath it.

## 14. LANGUAGE AND WRITING

### The languages of Byzantium

Byzantium was **Greek-speaking** from the 7th century forward (Latin persisted in law and army terminology but fell out of daily use). The **koine** Greek of the New Testament and Atticizing high-register Greek of literary prose existed side by side. Educated Byzantines composed in the classical Attic register; common people spoke a Greek already evolving toward modern Greek. Byzantine churches also held liturgies in **Syriac, Coptic, Armenian, Georgian, and Slavonic** for their various linguistic communities.

### The languages of Tang China

Tang Chang'an spoke **Middle Chinese** — reconstructable from Tang rhyme dictionaries like the *Qieyun* (601 CE) and *Guangyun*. It was a tonal language with four tones and consonant codas lost in modern Mandarin but preserved in modern southern Chinese languages (Cantonese, Hakka, Min). Japanese, Korean, and Vietnamese borrowed huge swathes of Middle Chinese vocabulary — modern Japanese "kan-on" readings of kanji preserve Tang pronunciations.

Alongside Chinese, Chang'an's streets rang with **Sogdian** (the Silk Road lingua franca), **Turkic** (various dialects of the steppe peoples), **Tibetan**, **Persian**, **Sanskrit** (among Buddhist monks), **Korean**, **Japanese**, and **Arabic**.

### Writing systems

**Byzantine Greek** used the Greek alphabet. A **miniscule** (cursive) Greek script developed in the 9th century, replacing the earlier uncial, and became the dominant bookhand. Parchment and papyrus (replaced by paper from the 10th c. onward).

**Chinese characters** were the dominant script, plus a growing number of Sanskrit-derived Buddhist texts transliterated into Chinese. The Tang saw major standardization of character forms under the imperial academies. **Sogdian script** — a cursive Aramaic-derived alphabet — was the Silk Road's main administrative hand. **Old Uyghur, Tibetan, Turkic runes, Arabic, and Pahlavi** all appeared on Silk Road inscriptions.

The **Xi'an Stele** (781 CE) carries its Nestorian Christian text in both **Chinese characters** and **Syriac script** — literally bilingual across two continents.

### Poetry and the arts of the tongue

**Byzantine rhetoric** was a major pursuit — the *progymnasmata* exercises trained schoolboys in declamation, ekphrasis (vivid description), and encomium. High-register letters were composed as literary performances.

**Tang poetry** was perhaps the greatest Chinese poetic era. Regulated verse (*lüshi*) with strict tonal and rhyme patterns; quatrains (*jueju*); the old-style freer forms (*gushi*). The examination system required poetry composition — you could *literally not* become an official without being able to compose in tonal-regulated verse on set topics. This means the entire civil service was a community of poets.

**Courtesan poetry** — Xue Tao, Yu Xuanji, Li Jilan — stands with the best of the Tang male poets. Poetry between courtesan and client was an established mode; a night at Pingkangfang might produce a handful of poems that would later be anthologized.

### Solis languages — the bazaar babel

Solis is **tri-lingual at minimum**. The administrative language is a Greek-derived **Solisan Classical**, used in legal documents and on coins. The common trade language is **Silk-Tongue** (a pidgin of Chinese and Sogdian with a heavy Greek loan-load) — spoken in the Western Market and on ship decks. The courtly poetic language is **Aoli-derived Classical Chinese**, used for the literate arts. Every educated Solisian moves between all three.

**Writing** uses dual scripts. Official documents are written in Greek letters on the right half of the page and Chinese characters on the left half. Poetry is often composed in both languages simultaneously — a Solisian poet is bilingual by definition.

**At Lady Pearl's house**, a courtesan is expected to read and write both scripts, compose poetry in both traditions, speak at minimum four languages, and sing in six. The **Smiling Blade** is reputedly fluent in eleven — he reads all the diplomatic correspondence that flows through the quarter.

**Lady Pearl's actual voice** is reportedly accented in a way nobody has ever placed. Some say it is older than any known language. This is because it is.

## 15. EDUCATION AND KNOWLEDGE

### Byzantine education — the paideia

Byzantine education inherited the classical Greek **paideia** — a curriculum based on grammar, rhetoric, philosophy, and the Greek classics (Homer above all). Boys of the middle and upper classes attended **grammatical schools** where they memorized Homer, Hesiod, and the dramatists; then **rhetorical schools** where they learned composition and declamation. Girls of high rank were educated privately at home — many Byzantine aristocratic women were literate, and some, like **Anna Komnene** (daughter of Emperor Alexios, who wrote the *Alexiad*), were among the most learned historians of their age.

Higher learning was concentrated at the **Imperial University** (*Pandidakterion*), founded by Theodosius II in 425 and refounded in various forms over the centuries — chairs in grammar, rhetoric, philosophy, law, and medicine. The **Patriarchal School** under the cathedral of Hagia Sophia was a parallel institution for theology. Manuscripts were copied and preserved in monastic scriptoria — much of classical Greek literature survives only because Byzantine monks copied it.

### Tang education — the Confucian ladder

Tang education was overwhelmingly organized around the **imperial examinations (keju)**. Boys of ambitious families began memorizing the Confucian Classics around age 6: the **Four Books** and **Five Classics** — the *Analects*, *Mencius*, *Great Learning*, *Doctrine of the Mean*, and the five (*Book of Changes*, *Book of Documents*, *Book of Odes*, *Book of Rites*, *Spring and Autumn Annals*). A committed student would memorize tens of thousands of characters by adolescence.

The **Imperial Academy** (*Guozijian*) in Chang'an was the apex school. Local prefectural schools fed into regional exams, which fed into metropolitan exams, which fed into the **palace exam** chaired by the emperor. Passing was a social earthquake — a peasant-born boy could become a minister.

**Private tutors** served those who could afford them. Buddhist and Daoist monasteries ran their own libraries and schools. Women of the upper class were commonly literate; some, especially Daoist priestesses (like Li Jilan), were educated to the highest level.

### Specialized training

Both cultures trained specialists separately:
- **Physicians** — apprenticed under masters, with textbook memorization (Byzantine Galen, Chinese Huangdi Neijing and Shanghan Lun).
- **Priests and monks** — sacred-text memorization, ritual training.
- **Artisans** — apprenticeship in family workshops, guild-regulated.
- **Diviners and astrologers** — esoteric traditions passed master-to-student.
- **Courtesans** — yes, courtesans. The **jiaofang** (music training palace) in Tang China trained girls in music, poetry, dance, calligraphy, and etiquette from childhood. Pingkangfang courtesans were the product of years of intensive arts-training. In Byzantium, the theatrical troupes trained their performers similarly.

### Libraries

The **Imperial Library of Constantinople** held hundreds of thousands of manuscripts. The **Monasteries of Mount Athos** and the **Stoudios Monastery** in Constantinople preserved texts through the Dark Ages. In China, the **Hanlin Academy** was the Tang's great scholarly centre. The **Dunhuang library cave** (Cave 17 at Mogao), sealed around 1000 CE and rediscovered in 1900, yielded over 40,000 documents in more than 20 languages — the richest single cache of Silk Road literature known.

### Solis education — twin curricula

Solis maintains **both curricula**. A noble boy learns Homer *and* the Analects. A noble girl learns Anna Komnene *and* Xue Tao. The **Imperial Academy of Solis** has two wings: the Greek wing teaches rhetoric, philosophy, and law; the Chinese wing teaches the Classics, poetry, and calligraphy. Students take exams in both.

**At Lady Pearl's house**, the training is the most rigorous in the city. A girl of seven arriving at the gate enters a ten-year curriculum:
- Calligraphy (both scripts)
- Poetry composition (both traditions)
- Music (pipa, qin, lyre, voice)
- Dance (Tang court dance *and* Byzantine ceremonial)
- Languages (minimum four)
- Etiquette and protocol (for courts she may never enter but may need to deceive)
- **Poisons and antidotes**
- **Weapons** (hairpin, fan, ribbon, concealed blade)
- **Negotiation and seduction as separate skills**
- Memory training (she must be able to recall conversations, faces, letters)
- Orthodox and Buddhist scripture enough to recognize any devout client's references

By 17 she is as educated as any bureaucrat of the Academy. By 25 she could run a small kingdom.

## 16. MEDICINE AND HEALING

### Byzantine medicine — Galen's long shadow

Byzantine medicine preserved and extended the Greek medical tradition, centered on **Galen of Pergamon (2nd c. CE)** and the **Hippocratic corpus**. The theory of the **four humours** (blood, phlegm, yellow bile, black bile), each with its elemental quality, governed diagnosis — illness was an imbalance to be rectified through **bleeding, purging, dietary regulation, herbal remedies**, and occasional surgery. The **pulse** was read according to elaborate Galenic categories (size, strength, speed, frequency, hardness).

Byzantium invented the **hospital** as we know it — **xenones** (houses for strangers) and **nosokomeia** (houses of the sick) run by the church as institutions of Christian charity. By the 11th–12th centuries, Constantinople's great hospitals had specialized wards, resident physicians, outpatient clinics (*iatreia*), and even surgical theatres. The **Pantokrator Hospital** (12th c.) had a charter specifying staffing and treatment protocols.

Byzantine surgery was advanced for its age — cataract couching, hernia repair, bladder-stone removal. **Paul of Aegina** (7th c.) wrote a seven-book medical encyclopedia that was standard in the Islamic world.

Pharmaceutical texts — the **iatrosophion** — collected recipes from Galen, Dioscorides, and later Byzantine doctors. Monastic herb-gardens supplied raw materials.

### Tang medicine — the balance and the needle

Tang Chinese medicine was organized around the **theory of qi** (vital energy), **yin and yang**, and the **Five Phases** (wood, fire, earth, metal, water). Illness was seen as qi disharmony, addressed through:

- **Herbal medicine** — enormous pharmacopoeias. Sun Simiao's **Qianjin Yaofang** ("Prescriptions Worth a Thousand Pieces of Gold," 652 CE) remains a foundational text.
- **Acupuncture** — needles inserted at meridian points to regulate qi flow.
- **Moxibustion** — burning mugwort on specific points.
- **Dietary therapy** — food as medicine, balanced by the yin/yang and five-flavours system.
- **Massage and manual therapies** (*tuina*).
- **Daoist breath cultivation** (*qigong*, though not named that yet) — self-healing through breath and movement.

The Tang government ran the **Imperial Medical Bureau** — examining physicians, certifying them, and dispatching medical relief during plagues. The **Bencao** (materia medica) tradition produced the great **Xinxiu Bencao** (659 CE), the world's first officially commissioned pharmacopoeia, describing over 850 medicinal substances.

Tang physicians also engaged with **Indian Ayurveda** (translated Buddhist medical texts), **Persian medicine** (the *Bencao* includes Persian drugs), and **Central Asian healing practices**.

### Midwifery and women's medicine

Both cultures had **midwives** (Byzantine: *maia*; Chinese: *wenpo*) — a female profession handling birth, early infant care, and gynaecological problems. Male physicians rarely touched female patients directly; diagnosis by **pulse via a silk thread** was a Chinese workaround for the upper-class female patient who could not be directly examined.

### Silk Road medicine

The Silk Road moved pharmaceutical substances as avidly as silk. **Persian saffron**, **Arab musk**, **Indian sandalwood**, **Sogdian resins**, **Chinese ginseng**, **Byzantine theriac** (a compound cure-all containing dozens of ingredients including opium) — all traded along the route.

### Solis medicine — four fluids, five phases, one hospital

Solis runs **dual-tradition medicine**. Every major hospital has a Galenic physician *and* a Chinese-tradition physician on duty; serious cases are treated by both in consultation. Acupuncture needles sit beside bleeding-bowls. A Solis prescription might read: *"Three nights of moxa on the Lower Sea of Qi, with saffron-theriac by mouth at waking, and the phlegmatic humour purged on the fourth day by the Galenic method."* This collaboration would have horrified either Galen or Sun Simiao, but Solis doctors have been doing it for generations.

The **Hospital of the Two Masters** near the harbour is the city's great medical centre — Orthodox nursing sisters in white, Buddhist-trained herbalists in grey, Persian surgeons in blue, all moving between wards.

**At Lady Pearl's house** the medical arts are more private and more deadly. The house has its own resident physician (a woman trained in both traditions) and its own **poisoner** — a courtesan whose entire purpose is the opposite art of the physician. The poisoner can prepare twenty toxins that act at different speeds, with different symptoms, leaving different or no traces. She can also brew twelve antidotes. She is Lady Pearl's **Blind Poisoner** equivalent — or perhaps reports to her directly.

## 17. DAILY LIFE

### A Byzantine day

In Constantinople the day began before dawn with **church bells** and the calls of the **watchmen closing their shift**. The household rose — a noble household with servants, a common household with mother and elder children. Water drawn from the cistern or neighbourhood well. A simple **breakfast** of bread dipped in wine or watered wine, with olives and cheese.

The market opened at sunrise. Men went to their work — workshops, the forum, the port, the civil offices. Women did household work — spinning, weaving, cooking — but also shopped, visited neighbours, attended church. **Public baths** (thermae) were a daily social centre; Byzantines of all classes bathed often, and the baths were places for gossip, business, and flirtation (segregated by sex, or by hour).

Midday meal was light. Afternoon rest in summer. The main meal was in the evening — wine, bread, a vegetable stew, fish or meat for the affluent. In wealthy houses, this might be a social dinner with guests, wine, and music.

Evening: church again for vespers, then home. **Oil lamps** burned in every house — olive oil lamps made of bronze, clay, or glass. Bedtime was early by modern standards — the working poor rose with the sun, the rich could stay up at torch-lit dinners.

**Hippodrome days** — chariot races — turned the whole city out. Tens of thousands packed the stands, chanting for Blues or Greens. Races alternated with public proclamations, executions, and triumphal processions.

### A Tang day

Chang'an ran on the **drum-and-bell system**. At dawn, the great drums at each ward gate sounded and the walled residential wards opened; the Western and Eastern Markets opened at midday by drum; at dusk the drums sounded again and the wards closed for the night. A citizen caught on the street after curfew risked punishment. Only the **Pingkangfang** and the vicinity of the Daming Palace had some relaxation of the curfew.

Morning: water from the household well, a simple breakfast of congee (rice porridge), pickled vegetables, steamed buns, tea. Officials rode to court — the great morning audience with the Emperor began at dawn. Merchants opened shops. Women of the household managed cooks, laundry, and weaving; aristocratic women might ride in the park or play **polo** (a Tang pastime for women as well as men).

Midday: a larger meal. Afternoon work or rest. Officials returned from court; the examination candidates studied; the scholars wrote letters.

Evening was the city's most vibrant time. The wards might close, but **within** each ward, markets, teahouses, restaurants, and wine-shops thrived. Pingkangfang was a twenty-four-hour city within the city — courtesans entertained, poets composed, scholars drank, the lantern-lights glowed until dawn.

### Hygiene and bathing

Byzantines bathed in **public baths** — hot, warm, cold rooms in sequence. Tang Chinese bathed in **private bathhouses** or at home; the emperor and consort had elaborate **hot spring** retreats (Huaqing Palace near Chang'an, where Emperor Xuanzong and Yang Guifei bathed together in the poem "Song of Everlasting Sorrow").

### Sleep and home life

Byzantine beds were wooden frames with mattresses stuffed with wool or feathers, covered in linen. Tang beds were low wooden platforms (*kang* in the north, heated from beneath by hot air from the stove) with silk coverlets. Both cultures used **oil lamps** and **candles** for evening light.

### Solis daily life — the bridge that never sleeps

Solis runs on a **hybrid schedule**. The drum-and-bell system operates for the city walls and the great markets; but the pleasure quarter, the harbour, and the foreign merchant wards operate on their own hours. **Solis never fully sleeps** — someone is always trading, always praying, always pouring wine.

A Solis day for a middling merchant: tea and sesame-bread at dawn, a morning walk through the Western Market for news, meetings in a teahouse with Sogdian partners at midmorning, a Byzantine-style midday meal at a tavern, afternoon in the counting-house, evening at the hippodrome-stadium for the chariot races (which Solis inherited wholesale from Byzantium), then a late dinner in the Pleasure Quarter — where the courtesans recite poetry in two languages and the Smiling Blade strolls past with his smile.

**At Lady Pearl's house**, the schedule inverts. The house wakes at midday. Afternoon: bathing, dressing, training, rehearsal. Evening through dawn: guests, performance, the real work. Dawn: accounts settled, letters dispatched, reports sent to the underwater room where Lady Pearl hears the day's gathered news before retiring to her chamber at full sunrise. The house runs on moonlight as other houses run on sun.

## 18. MYTHOLOGY AND LEGEND

### Byzantine folklore — saints, empresses, and monsters

Byzantine popular imagination was densely populated with **saints** whose lives blurred into folklore — **St George** slaying the dragon, **St Demetrios** appearing on a white horse to save Thessaloniki, **St Nicholas** rescuing children (ancestor of Santa Claus). Miracle stories (*thaumata*) circulated around specific icons and relics — each city had its own patron-saint and its own miracle story.

Byzantine popular stories also featured:
- **Digenes Akritas** — the border-lord hero whose epic fuses Greek, Arab, and Armenian elements. A warrior of mixed heritage (his name means "the two-blooded"), he defends the eastern frontier and fights both men and supernatural creatures.
- **Alexander the Great Romance** — Alexander reborn in legend, flying by gryphon, descending the sea in a glass sphere, questing for the water of life.
- **Empress Theodora** — whose rise from courtesan to empress became a folk narrative, told and retold with embellishments.
- **Monsters**: the **drakon** (dragon/serpent), the **strigla** (witch/vampire), the **gorgona** (mermaid), and the **nereides** (water spirits who could charm men to drowning).

### Tang folklore — immortals, foxes, and ghosts

Tang popular tales were collected in **chuanqi** (transmission-of-the-strange) stories — a new literary form combining high literary style with fantastic content. Common themes:

- **Fox spirits** (*huli jing*) — supernatural women (usually) who could shapeshift from fox to beautiful human. Often lovers to mortal men, sometimes helpful, sometimes fatal. The nine-tailed fox (*jiuwei hu*) is the greatest of them — Wen's ancestry in Serina's framing. Tales like "The Fox Girl of Renshi" (6th c., classic Tang retellings) establish the type.
- **Ghost lovers** — the woman who comes to a scholar's window at night and is revealed at dawn to be dead. Love across the boundary of death is a perennial Chinese theme.
- **Immortals** (*xian*) — Daoist sages who have transcended mortality through cultivation, pills, or sudden enlightenment. The **Eight Immortals** (Ba Xian), though fully codified only later, have Tang antecedents.
- **Dragon kings** (*long wang*) of the four seas, living in underwater palaces.
- **Monkey-demon** tales, forerunners of the later Journey to the West.
- **Lost-land tales** — a man wanders into a Peach Blossom Spring valley, finds a paradise community, returns home to find decades passed.

Tang literature loved the **ghost story, the immortal-encounter, the fox-wife**. Scholars compiled them for amusement, but they were also believed — at the edge, at least. The thin membrane between the ordinary and the strange was a living cultural reality.

### Silk Road legends

The Silk Road produced its own legendarium. Sogdian merchants told tales of the **Fire Mountain** and the **Kingdom of Women**. Buddhist travelogues embedded miracle stories at every stage of Xuanzang's journey. The **Kingdom of Wakhan**, **Khotan's jade rivers**, **the singing sands of Dunhuang** — every oasis had its myths.

### Solis mythology — where East meets West, the stories marry

Solis folklore is full of **love stories across cultural lines**: the Byzantine princess and the Sogdian merchant, the Chinese courtesan and the Varangian guard, the god-in-disguise and the mortal girl. These are the city's signature narrative form, because the city itself is a marriage of opposites.

**Fox spirits** are a common belief in Solis, brought east-to-west by the Aoli cultural influence. But they braid with **Byzantine mermaid-lore** — the water-woman who draws men into the deep. **The most dangerous rumour in Solis is that the Lady of the Pearl House is neither human nor a fox nor a mermaid, but something older than all three. That she has been in Solis longer than the city has stood. That the tree in her courtyard is her sister.**

Some truths circulate as folktale, because a truth told as a story cannot be punished:
- *"The pearl in the oyster is a drop of the mother's blood."*
- *"The first courtesan of the house was a dragon's daughter who forgot the way home."*
- *"Lady Pearl pays for every child of Solis to be baptised in sweet water — because she remembers her sister's milk."*

These are exactly true. Nobody believes them.

**The Smiling Blade**, for his part, has a growing folklore of his own: the laughing swordsman who never kills the same way twice, who has been seen in Chang'an and Constantinople in the same week, who is perhaps a ghost, perhaps an immortal, perhaps a man. (He is something stranger. He is a fragment-carrier.)

## 19. TABOOS, LAWS, AND ETHICS

### Byzantine honour and shame

Byzantine ethical life was shaped by **Orthodox Christianity**, **Roman law**, and the **honour codes of the classical Mediterranean**. Core wrongs included:

- **Treason (perduellio)** against the Emperor — punishable by death, blinding, exile, confiscation of property.
- **Apostasy** from Christianity — by late Byzantine period, conversion *away* from Orthodoxy was a capital crime.
- **Adultery** — particularly for women; adulterous wives could be mutilated (noses cut, a famously Byzantine punishment) and sent to monasteries.
- **Oathbreaking** — swearing on the Gospels or on a relic was binding beyond ordinary contract. Perjury was believed to damn the soul.
- **Hospitality violation** — not as sacred as in some other cultures, but still powerful. Hosting and protecting a guest was a Christian duty.
- **Iconoclasm (during the controversy)** — smashing or failing to honour icons was heresy, then orthodoxy, then heresy again, depending on the decade.

Byzantine shame culture included the **public humiliation** of defeated rivals — paraded in the Hippodrome backwards on a donkey, heads shaved, before being blinded or exiled. **Face** mattered — the imperial family's dignity was a state concern.

### Tang honour and shame

Tang ethics were shaped by **Confucian filial piety**, **Buddhist compassion**, and the **Tang Code's Ten Abominations** (*shi'e*) — the gravest crimes in the legal system:

1. Plotting rebellion against the emperor
2. Plotting great sedition (destroying imperial temples or tombs)
3. Plotting treason (defecting to an enemy)
4. Contumacy (killing or severely beating parents/grandparents)
5. Depravity (killing three or more unrelated people, or dismembering)
6. Great irreverence (stealing imperial ritual objects)
7. Lack of filial piety (cursing parents, neglecting mourning)
8. Discord (killing or injuring senior kin)
9. Unrighteousness (killing one's official superior)
10. Incest (sexual relations with senior kin or a parent's concubine)

Five of the ten concern **family-against-family** crimes — the Confucian core. The lawful state was an extension of the lawful family. **Filial piety** was the master-virtue; to fail at it was to fail at being human.

**Oaths** in China were sworn by **cutting a chicken's throat** (ritual sacrifice) or **drinking blood-wine** (mixing wine with a drop of each swearer's blood). Sworn brotherhood was a lifelong bond punishable in the supernatural afterlife if broken.

**Face** (*mianzi*) — honour as social standing — was a core Tang concept. To humiliate someone was to cause them to "lose face," a graver wound than physical injury.

### Silk Road ethics

Silk Road trade ran on **reputation and contract**. A merchant whose word could not be trusted was quickly frozen out of caravan networks. Sogdian letters show merchants anxiously tracking debts and reputations. **Hospitality** was sacred across the entire route — to betray a guest was to lose the right to travel.

### Solis ethics — the beautiful rot

Solis's official ethics draw on **Byzantine Orthodox virtue, Tang Confucian propriety, and Silk Road hospitality**. Its laws codify them.

Its **actual ethics** are what Serina calls **jeweled rot**. In Solis:
- **Greed is celebrated**, provided it is tasteful.
- **Deception is admired**, provided it is clever.
- **Pleasure is sacred**, provided you pay.
- **Truth is optional**, provided the lie is beautiful.
- **Loyalty is contractual**, measured in coin or favour.

But beneath the rot, the **Pleasure Quarter operates its own ethics** — the **Code of the Sash**. In Lady Pearl's house, the sworn sisters observe rules older and stricter than the city's laws:

1. **No sister's body belongs to anyone but herself.** A client who violates consent faces death — delivered quietly, without trace.
2. **No sister is betrayed to the city.** The house's enemies never learn anything from its own.
3. **A debt sworn in the house is honoured to the grave.** A rescued girl owes the house nothing but her oath; her oath is everything.
4. **Information is the house's property, not the sister's.** She gathers it; Lady Pearl distributes it.
5. **The Mulberry Tree is never spoken of.** Its existence is the deepest oath of the house.

To break any of these is exile from the sisterhood — which in Solis is exile from life itself. **The house enforces more faithfully than the state. The brothel is more honourable than the palace.** This is the deepest irony — and deepest beauty — of Solis.

## 20. THE FEELING OF THE CULTURE

### What a traveller notices first

A caravan cresting the ridge sees Solis laid out across the strait. The first thing that strikes them is **light** — the city is so full of gold, of silver, of painted tile, of polished marble, of glazed porcelain roof-ornaments, that it catches the sun and scatters it. The domes of the great basilica shine like fish scales. The pagoda roofs glow green and bronze. Ships gild the harbour with their lanterns.

Then comes **sound**. Bells — Byzantine bronze bells from the basilica, small jingling bells from the pagoda, the ceremonial drums at the market gates, the clack of wooden blocks from street-sellers, the call of Uighur merchants in Silk-Tongue, a thousand conversations in twelve languages running together, and — over it all — the distant lute and lyre playing somewhere in a courtyard.

Then **smell**. Unforgettable, specific, signature Solis: **saffron and salt water, sandalwood and horse sweat, frankincense and grilled mutton, rose water and wine, wet stone, steamed rice, and the sharp clean smell of mulberry leaves and the mulberry silkworm floss that drifts, sometimes, on the breeze from nowhere anyone can place.**

Then **faces**. Every colour of skin. Every fold of eye. Byzantine Greek scholars in black cassocks walking beside Sogdian bankers in caftans beside Aoli cultivators in flowing robes beside Varangian guards in fur beside Arab sailors in white. Every beard shape. Every jewellery tradition. The traveller has walked into the only city in the world where everybody is somebody's foreigner.

### The ambient mood

The traveller walking the Mese — Solis's great central avenue, a fusion of the Byzantine Mese and the Tang Scarlet Bird Avenue — feels **alive** in a way few other cities manage. There is a sense that **everything is for sale** but also that **anything is possible**. A peasant arriving from the farm might, within a week, be a wine-server; within a year, an entertainer; within a decade, a wealthy merchant; within a lifetime, a senator. The city lies, but it also delivers.

Under this is a lower note, unignorable once you hear it: **the city is tired**. It has been the centre of the world too long. Something in its bones is rotting. The beggars outside the basilica are more desperate than beggars elsewhere, the drunks louder, the fevers more persistent, the priests more cynical. The Pleasure Quarter's lanterns glow more brightly than any temple's candles, and everyone knows it.

### What outsiders know Solis for

- **Silk and spices** — every bolt, bale, and peppercorn passes through Solis markets.
- **Intrigue** — Solis ambassadors are feared in every court. Solis-trained diplomats are borrowed by other kingdoms.
- **The Pleasure Quarter** — mentioned in a thousand poems, envied by a thousand kings, entered reverently by ambassadors from every corner of Avalúne.
- **Religious tolerance** — every god is welcome here, for a price.
- **Poetry written in two scripts** — the city's literary signature.
- **The greatest bridge ever built** — across its central strait, a miracle of joint Byzantine-Tang engineering, hung with lanterns by night, crossed by caravans and courtesans alike.

### What Solis knows about itself (and doesn't say)

The city knows it is **jeweled rot**. The old men in the teahouses know the glittering coins in their pockets rest on empty vaults. The priests know the icons are gilded over cracked wood. The senators know the laws are for sale. The courtesans know the nobles are not quite as noble as they pretend.

And **Lady Pearl knows**, because she sees it all, that the Mulberry tree in her courtyard is holding up more than the sky above her house. It is holding up a city that has forgotten how to pray, a people who have forgotten what they love, a country that has forgotten how to be one thing. She runs her pleasure house. She runs her spy network. She takes in the girls and the children and the broken diplomats. She waits.

When the Fellowship arrives in Solis — which they will — they will feel, first, **enchantment**. Then, as they walk deeper, **unease**. Then, when they find the Pavilion of Drifting Pearls and step into the courtyard, they will feel — all six of them — something they cannot name. The tree does not speak. But the tree is awake, and it has been waiting for them.

**This is Solis. The Bridge. The jeweled rot. The courtesan who is a goddess. The brothel that is a fortress. The pillar tree in a city that sold its gods. The last, most beautiful, most hidden mercy in a land of excess.**
