## 1. THE LAND AND GEOGRAPHY

### Plato's Atlantis — The Island Described

Plato's *Timaeus* and *Critias* (composed c. 360 BCE) are the only ancient sources for Atlantis. In them, the Egyptian priests of Sais tell the Athenian statesman Solon that nine thousand years before Solon's time, there existed an island "larger than Libya and Asia put together," positioned "in front of the Pillars of Heracles" (the Strait of Gibraltar). This island commanded an empire stretching as far east as Egypt and Tyrrhenia (western Italy). At the centre of the island, on a small hill near the sea, stood a temple to Poseidon, surrounded by **concentric rings of land and water — three of water, two of land** — all perfectly circular, carved by Poseidon himself at the birth of the city. Beyond these rings stretched a vast rectangular plain, said to be "of sublime excellence and fertile," surrounded by mountains reaching the sky. Atlantis was divided among **ten kings**, the twin-born sons of Poseidon and the mortal woman Cleito, with the eldest, Atlas, holding the greatest and most central portion. The metal **orichalcum** — now unknown, "only a name" in Plato's time — was mined everywhere on the island and valued second only to gold; it lined the walls and pillars of Poseidon's temple, whose roof was ivory adorned with gold, silver, and orichalcum. Canals, harbours, and bridges connected the concentric rings. Elephants roamed the wild uplands. The island was self-sufficient, its soil producing "all the good things of earth."

### Egyptian Geography — The Real Anchor

The Egyptian priestly caste — Aurum's primary historical foundation — lived in a landscape utterly different from any sky city but equally organised around a single axis: the **Nile**. Egypt was "the gift of the Nile" (Herodotus). The annual inundation from July to October deposited black silt on the floodplain, making Egypt the richest grain-producing civilisation in the ancient Mediterranean. The land divided sharply into **Kemet ("the Black Land" — the fertile strip)** and **Deshret ("the Red Land" — the deserts flanking it).** Beyond the black strip, almost nothing grew. Beyond it, the **Western Desert** (Libyan) and the **Eastern Desert** (Arabian) stretched in cliffs and dunes. The great temple cities — **Thebes (Waset, modern Luxor), Heliopolis (Iunu), Memphis, Abydos** — all hugged the river.

### Sacred Geography in Egypt

The Egyptians did not see the land as neutral space. Every nome (province) had its temple; every temple had a **naos (sanctuary)** containing the god's cult statue. The temple was built as a model of the cosmos: its pylon gateway the horizon, its hypostyle hall the primordial marsh, its sanctuary the **mound of first creation** (the *benben*) rising from the chaos waters of Nun. Specific sites carried unique sanctity: **Heliopolis** was where Atum self-generated on the first mound; **Abydos** was the burial place of Osiris's head; the **Siwa Oasis** held the Oracle of Amun that Alexander consulted. The **west bank of Thebes** was the realm of the dead (where the sun sets); the east bank was the realm of the living.

### Applying This to Aurum — Geography of the Sky Cities

Aurum is the fusion of these two landscapes: the concentric, ringed, island-city of Atlantis lifted into the air. The historical dimension that is **entirely fictional** is the floating itself — there is no real-world analogue for a city hovering in the sky. The closest fictional analogues are Swift's Laputa (a disk "about four and a half miles in diameter, with an adamantine base" levitated by a magnetic central stone), Miyazaki's flying island (propelled by volucite/aetherium crystal, with a gigantic central tree rooted through the disk), and BioShock's Columbia (a constellation of Victorian-era districts held aloft by fictional "quantum levitation").

For Aurum the likely geography is: **the Capital** — a central disk-city built in Atlantean concentric rings, its heart the Archival, its outer ring the palaces of the Ten Houses (ten kings echo); **satellite cities** drifting at respectful distances like moons around a planet; **connecting skyways** of glass and brass that bend between them; **the gardens** — roof terraces, glass domes, hanging conservatories, clockwork-irrigated orchards. The cities cast shadows on the country below. Beneath them lies the hinterland — the quarries that provide stone, the harvest villages that provide food, and (hidden by the fog) the **harvesting grounds** where magic-bearing children are taken. **Sacred geography in Aurum:** the innermost ring of the Capital (the Archival, the paulownia tree, the sealed sanctum) stands in the exact position Plato gave Poseidon's temple — highest, most central, most forbidden. Citizens can see the outer walls. Only priests walk beyond them. Only the Veiled Advisor walks in.

## 2. SOCIAL STRUCTURE

### Egyptian Class System — The Primary Template

New Kingdom Egypt (c. 1550–1070 BCE) was organised as a **rigid but not absolutely closed pyramid**. At the apex stood the **Pharaoh**, a living god whose person was a bridge between the human and divine orders. Below the Pharaoh sat the **royal family, the Vizier** (tjaty — the prime minister, split in the New Kingdom into Viziers of Upper and Lower Egypt), then **nobles** who governed the nomes. Next came the **priesthood** — a hereditary caste by the first millennium BCE — and the **scribes**, the bureaucratic class on whom the entire administration ran. Below them sat **soldiers, artisans (goldsmiths, sculptors, stonemasons), and merchants**; beneath these, the **peasant farmers** who made up some 80% of the population and who did not own the land they worked (the land belonged to the king, nobles, or temples). At the bottom sat **slaves** — mostly foreign war captives, debtors, or condemned criminals — who could in many cases buy freedom, own property, marry, and testify in court, and who were often treated as members of the household rather than chattel.

### Priestly Caste as Hereditary Elite

This is the spine of Aurum. By the New Kingdom, and especially in the Third Intermediate Period (c. 1070–664 BCE), the Egyptian priesthood had **hardened into a hereditary caste**, with priestly offices passed from father to son. The High Priest of Amun at Karnak owned two-thirds of all temple land in Egypt and controlled 90% of its ships — meaning the priestly class functionally out-wealthed the pharaoh. Within the priesthood itself, sharp internal hierarchies existed: **Hem-Netjer-Tepi ("First Servant of God," High Priest)** at the top; **Hery-Heb ("He Who Is Upon the Ritual Scroll," Lector Priests)** as the intellectual elite, handling ritual texts and heka (magical) utterances; **Sem Priests** for funerary rites; **Wab ("pure") Priests** as the mid-tier general officiants; and below them, temple craftsmen, singers, musicians, and labourers. To become a priest one had to be born to a priestly family, trained in the House of Life, and initiated through increasingly restrictive purification rites.

### Outsiders and the Underclass

Egyptian "untouchables" were those ritually impure: those who handled corpses (embalmers, who were revered AND shunned), butchers (blood), dung collectors, and certain fishers. Foreigners occupied a fluid position — Nubians, Libyans, and Levantines could rise to high office but were always marked as *other*. Slaves existed in various conditions: household slaves often treated well, agricultural slaves on temple estates the most numerous, and state slaves in the quarries and mines with the harshest lives.

### Applying This to Aurum — The Covered Secret

Aurum inherits the Egyptian pyramid but exaggerates its extremes. At the top sits a **Sovereign** (the Pharaoh-echo) who is both divine and ornamental, whose power is real but whose every word is shaped by the priestly caste that has chosen him. Below, the **Ten Houses** (Atlantean ten kings) — noble bloodlines that govern districts of the sky cities and have controlled the Capital for generations. Next, the **Priestly Caste** — the real power, hereditary, trained in the Archival — led by figures like the **Veiled Advisor**, the **Chief Archivist**, and the **Hem-Netjer of the Paulownia** (whose existence is itself a secret — the public is told the tree is a decorative heirloom). Below them, **the scribes** — tens of thousands of them, copying, cataloguing, controlling what is written, what is forgotten. Then the **artisans** (goldsmiths, clockmakers, glassblowers — Aurum's famous exports), the **merchants**, the **guards**, and the **labouring citizens** who keep the cities running.

Beneath them: **the servants** — an invisible army of maids, porters, nursemaids, cooks. Beneath them: **the country below** — farmers who grow Aurum's food and who live under the fog that drifts down. Beneath THEM: **the harvest class** — a category not officially acknowledged. These are the ones the ruling caste identifies as magic-bearing. They disappear. Their families are told they have been "elevated" — chosen for a great honour in the Capital. They never return. The fog helps the family forget. **The social pyramid in Aurum is a layered seal — each level protected from the level below by a bubble of managed ignorance.**

## 3. GOVERNMENT AND POLITICS

### Atlantis — The Ten Kings and Divine Mandate

Plato's Atlantis was governed by **ten kings** (the ten firstborn twins of Poseidon and Cleito). Each ruled his own allotted portion of the island, but all ten met periodically at the central temple of Poseidon to renew their covenant. There they hunted a sacred bull with staves and nooses, slit its throat over an inscribed pillar of orichalcum that bore the laws of Poseidon, and drank wine mingled with its blood. After this rite the kings rendered judgment on each other's disputes, wearing dark blue robes, sitting through the night by the dying fires of the sacrifice. Their law was divine, their authority absolute within their territories. Over generations, however, their divine portion "became diluted" — power and avarice replaced restraint, until **Zeus resolved to punish them** and the island sank beneath the sea.

### Egyptian Pharaoh and Governance

In Egypt, the **Pharaoh** was the living Horus and the son of Ra, ruling by divine right and embodying *ma'at* (cosmic order, truth, balance). He owned, in theory, all Egypt. In practice he ruled through the **Vizier (tjaty)** — the highest non-royal official, who supervised taxation, the law, and the construction of royal tombs; the **nomarchs** who governed the 42 provinces; and an army of scribes and inspectors who reported to the Vizier. Law was codified in *ma'at* — no formal single legal code survived but extensive court records show a system of precedent, contract law, inheritance law, property law. Punishments ranged from fines and beatings to mutilation and, for the gravest crimes, impalement or burning. Justice in theory reached even to the poorest — a peasant farmer named Khun-Anup whose donkeys were stolen by a landowner's agent famously took his grievance up the chain until it reached the High Steward Rensi and the king himself ("The Eloquent Peasant," c. 1850 BCE).

### Priestly Political Power — The Critical Lever

By the reign of **Ramesses III (c. 1186–1155 BCE)**, the Amun priesthood had grown so wealthy that roughly 15% of Egypt's entire population lived on temple land. By 1080 BCE the **High Priest Herihor** seized effective control of Upper Egypt, founding a priest-kingdom at Thebes while Pharaoh ruled only Lower Egypt from Tanis. This is the **central structural model for Aurum's politics**: the priestly caste, ostensibly servants of the crown, becoming the real rulers. Governance was conducted through **oracles** — during festival processions, citizens would submit written questions to the bark (ceremonial boat) carrying the god's statue; the statue would "nod" (forward for yes, backward for no) to answer. The priests carrying the bark controlled the nodding. Civil cases, criminal cases, matters of state, military campaigns, even promotions of officials — all could be decided by the god, which is to say, by the priests.

### Applying This to Aurum — Throne Behind a Veil

Aurum's politics fuse these two structures. **Public face:** the **Sovereign**, descended from the Ten Founding Houses, enthroned in the Capital, visible at festivals, adorned with the gold of the sun and the blue of the sky, pronouncing law. **Actual face:** the **Veiled Advisor** (the Shi Sect fragment-carrier), a figure whose face is never seen — always behind a veil of silver gauze, always in shadow or beside the Sovereign's ear. Citizens are told the veiled one speaks the will of the gods. The Advisor does not deny this; he simply lets the myth do the work. Below the Sovereign sit the **Council of Ten** (the heads of the Ten Houses, Atlantean echo) who meet monthly in the Archival's Chamber of the Pillar to renew their covenant over wine mixed with a drop of blood from a tiny ceremonial cut (a vestigial echo of the bull sacrifice — the populace is told it is symbolic; only the Advisor knows it is how the tree still recognises them).

**Laws:** harsh against magic. Any citizen demonstrating magical ability is to be reported to the priesthood for "elevation." The wording is always gentle. The enforcement is absolute. **Taxation:** light on citizens, heavy on the hinterland below, paid in grain and in the occasional "elevated" child. **The military:** a standing air-legion, airships and clockwork crossbowmen, whose loyalty is to the Sovereign but whose officers are all priestly-caste younger sons. **Diplomacy with neighbours:** Aurum treats the other nations of Avalúne with the polite condescension of a civilisation convinced of its own superiority. It does not wage war — why would a god wage war on a worm? It trades, it watches, it keeps its fog thick.

## 4. RELIGION AND SPIRITUALITY

### Egyptian Religion — The Working Model

Egyptian religion was **temple-centred, ritual-dense, and image-based.** The gods were not worshipped through belief but through *service.* Each morning, before dawn, the High Priest of the temple (or a wab priest acting for him) entered the **naos (sanctuary)** where the god's cult statue stood. The ritual was always the same: **break the seal on the shrine; burn incense; prostrate; recite the morning hymn ("Awaken in peace, great god, awaken in peace"); wash the statue with water from the sacred lake; anoint it with seven sacred oils; apply green malachite paint to the left eye and black galena paint to the right; dress it in fresh linen of four colours (white, red, blue, green); present it with a meal of bread, beer, meat, fruit, milk; leave it to absorb the divine essence of the food; withdraw; re-seal the shrine**. The ritual was performed again at noon and evening. The priesthood existed to *feed, clothe, and wake the gods.* The statue was not a symbol — it WAS the god, in that the god could and did inhabit it.

### The Ennead and the Cosmogony

The Heliopolitan creation myth, taught to every scribe: before anything, there was **Nun** — the boundless, dark primordial waters. From Nun rose a mound (the *benben* — the first solid thing), and on it **Atum** self-generated, "the complete one." Atum produced **Shu (air) and Tefnut (moisture)** through a divine act (spitting, or masturbation — different texts tell it differently). Shu and Tefnut produced **Geb (earth) and Nut (sky),** who produced **Osiris, Isis, Set, and Nephthys**. These nine deities formed the **Ennead of Heliopolis**. The Egyptian cosmos was conceived as a bubble: Nut arching over, Geb lying beneath, Shu holding them apart, with Nun still surrounding everything and threatening to return if ma'at ever truly failed. This is an important shape for Aurum: a civilisation perched on a thin crust of light, always aware of the chaos beneath.

### Sacred Knowledge Behind Veils

Sacred knowledge in Egypt was **layered by access.** Only purified priests entered the outer courts; only initiated lector priests entered the hypostyle hall; only the High Priest entered the naos; only the Pharaoh or his deputy performed the actual contact with the statue. This **geography of revelation** — concentric circles of who-knows-what — is directly transferable to Aurum. Egyptian "mystery schools" are a later Hellenistic concept largely inflated by Renaissance esotericists, but the core truth holds: the deeper into the temple you walked, the more you were permitted to see, and the oaths of silence were severe.

### Applying This to Aurum — The Official Religion and the Buried Truth

Aurum's **public religion** is the cult of the **Radiant Ones** — a pantheon of twelve gods of the sky, sun, clockwork, and craft. The Sovereign is their earthly voice; the priestly caste tends their statues in the great golden temples that crown each sky city. Daily ritual follows the Egyptian pattern exactly: the dawn awakening, the meal, the seven oils, the four-colour linen, the evening sealing. Citizens leave offerings at outer shrines; priests carry the bark of the Radiant One through the streets on festival days and the god nods yes or no to petitioned questions.

**But the buried truth:** the Radiant Ones are a fiction. The religion was invented sixteen generations ago to cover the real reason the cities fly — the **paulownia pillar tree** in the Archival, which is fed blood. The priestly caste knows this at the highest levels. The scribes know fragments. The wab priests know nothing — they carry a story they believe is true.

The **afterlife** doctrine teaches that souls of the worthy ascend to the Silver Gardens above the Capital (mirroring Egyptian Field of Reeds, and the Atlantean-Platonic notion of a celestial realm for the virtuous). Souls of the unworthy sink to the world below, to "become peasants." This doctrine is, of course, useful — it sanctifies the hierarchy. **Magic** in Aurum is officially a **sickness, a curse from the gods, a punishment for ancestral crimes.** The magical child is pitied, not feared — and "elevated." Hidden behind all of this is Mínhuì, the silent goddess in the Archival, tending a corrupted tree and watching her brother's legacy be mis-used. Her presence is the faintest counter-weight to everything the official religion is built on. **The real god of Aurum is a quiet noblewoman cataloguing scrolls, and nobody knows her name.**

## 5. FAMILY AND KINSHIP

### Egyptian Family — The Real Model

Family in Egypt was **nuclear** with **strong extended kin networks.** Households typically consisted of a husband, wife, children, and often elderly parents or unmarried siblings. Marriage was a **civil arrangement, not a religious sacrament** — no ceremony, no priest, no vow. A couple was considered married when they moved in together and set up a household; the legal reality was "a woman who is in a man's house." Couples often wrote marriage contracts specifying property, inheritance, and terms of divorce. A first marriage typically occurred in the late teens for men, mid-teens for women.

### Women's Rights — Remarkable for the Ancient World

Egyptian women held **legal equality with men** in property, inheritance, and court. They could:
- Own property independently (land, slaves, houses, jewellery, tools)
- Sign contracts in their own name
- Initiate divorce for fault (adultery, infertility, abuse) or no-fault (incompatibility)
- Leave a marriage with what they had brought in PLUS one-third to two-thirds of acquired joint property
- Inherit equally with male siblings (each parent's property divided equally among children regardless of gender)
- Testify in court and bring suit
- Work as scribes, physicians, priestesses, musicians, weavers, traders, landowners

The title **"God's Wife of Amun"** — held by a royal princess who remained theoretically celibate and ruled as High Priestess of Amun at Karnak — was one of the most powerful positions in Egypt during the Twenty-fifth and Twenty-sixth Dynasties, controlling vast estates and functioning as a political ruler over Upper Egypt.

### Children, Elders, and Ancestors

Children were cherished — infant mortality was high, and loved children were expected, not hidden. Mothers typically breastfed for three years. Children of the elite were educated at home or sent to temple schools by age five. Children of commoners learned their parents' trade by imitation and help. **Elders** were respected and cared for by children (especially eldest sons, who also conducted their funeral rites). **Ancestor veneration** was universal — the dead were believed to remain present, and offerings of food, flowers, and water were brought to tombs regularly; "letters to the dead" written on pottery bowls asked deceased relatives for intercession in family disputes or illness.

### Atlantis and the Ten Houses

Plato's Atlantis offers a different family model: the ten kings are **twin-born**, descended from the mortal woman Cleito and the god Poseidon, bound by a covenant passed down through their lineages. Family there is **dynastic** — blood is destiny, and the family name carries rule. Over generations this hardened into the oligarchic corruption that destroyed them.

### Applying This to Aurum — The Ten Houses and the Caste-Lines

Aurum braids both traditions. At the **elite level**, the Ten Founding Houses function like Plato's ten kings — dynasties whose marriages are arranged for political alliance, whose children are educated in the Archival from age four, whose surnames carry centuries of accumulated privilege. Marriages within the Ten Houses are negotiated by specialists called **Match-Scribes**; astrologers and priests verify the compatibility of birth charts. Inheritance among the Ten Houses is primogeniture through the male line (an Atlantean inheritance), but the real wealth is titles within the priesthood, which pass father-to-son and mother-to-daughter.

Among the **priestly caste**, the Egyptian hereditary model rules: priest-families, scribe-families, Archivist-families marry within their caste. Each family has its "workshop" (its area of knowledge, its scroll-lineage). Intermarriage outside the caste is not forbidden but quietly discouraged — the wrong blood dilutes the work.

Among **ordinary citizens**, something closer to the Egyptian civic model applies — free men and women marry by moving in together, write marriage contracts, own property jointly, divorce by mutual agreement, inherit equally. Women hold strong legal rights (Aurum is, on the surface, one of the more egalitarian societies of Avalúne). The fog rarely reaches into homes — people love each other, raise children, grow old, remember their parents. They simply do not remember the child next door who "was elevated."

**Mínhuì's position within this:** she is a quiet noblewoman of the Archival — exactly the kind of woman Aurum expects: well-born, scholarly, unmarried or widowed, cataloguing scrolls. The cover is perfect because it is *so* culturally normal. **Her hidden family is the whole bloodline of the tree.** Her real kin is Fèngzhé, dead a thousand years, whose fragment she still protects.

## 6. LIFE CYCLE RITUALS

### Egyptian Life Cycle — The Template

Egyptian life moved through a tight calendar of rites. **Birth** took place at home; the mother squatted on **birthing bricks** (decorated with images of Bes, the dwarf-god protector of childbirth, and Tawaret, the hippo-goddess of pregnancy). Midwives and female relatives attended. The child was presented to household gods, named within seven days (often by the mother, often after a deceased relative, often carrying a divine element — "Beloved of Amun," "Ra is Merciful"). Names had power; a true name could be a vulnerability, so many children had public and private names.

**Coming of age** was marked for boys around **age 14** by circumcision (documented in tomb scenes at Saqqara, c. 2400 BCE) and, for those entering the priesthood or scribal class, by the first cutting of the **sidelock of youth** — a shaved head with a single braided lock over one temple, worn until initiation. Girls came of age with menarche and a change of hairstyle. Elite children entered their adult social role around this age.

**Marriage** required no ceremony — the couple moved into a house together and the household gods were informed. Wealthy families held feasts; poorer families simply shared a meal with the neighbours. Bride-price and dowry were negotiated. The husband signed a contract acknowledging the wife's property.

**Death** — the most elaborate life rite in any culture. The body was taken to the **per nefer (beautiful house)**, the embalmer's workshop. The brain was extracted through the nose with a hook, the viscera through a left-flank incision and stored in four **canopic jars** bearing the heads of the four Sons of Horus. The body was packed with natron salt for 40 days to dry, then wrapped in linen bandages (sometimes a mile of linen per body), amulets placed between the wrappings (the Eye of Horus, the Ankh, the Djed pillar, the Scarab over the heart). This took 70 days.

On burial day, the mummy was placed in a coffin and carried in procession to the tomb by family, mourners, and priests. At the tomb, the **sem priest** in a leopard-skin robe performed the **Opening of the Mouth** ceremony: using an adze, a peseshkef (a forked flint blade), and other sacred tools, he touched the mummy's mouth, eyes, ears, and nose, **restoring the senses** for use in the afterlife. The deceased was then sealed into the tomb with food, beer, furniture, figurines (**shabti** servants), jewellery, and a copy of the **Book of the Dead** — spells to navigate the underworld, pass the forty-two judges, and be weighed against the feather of ma'at in the Hall of Two Truths.

### Festivals Marking the Year

The Egyptian year had 12 months of 30 days plus 5 epagomenal days. Major festivals included:
- **Wepet Renpet (New Year, mid-July)** — tied to the heliacal rising of Sirius, announcing the Nile flood; the death and rebirth of Osiris.
- **Opet Festival** — 11 to 20 days of procession carrying Amun, Mut, and Khonsu from Karnak to Luxor and back, renewing the pharaoh's divine authority.
- **Beautiful Feast of the Valley** — the god Amun crossed the Nile to visit the tombs of the west bank; families camped among the graves of their ancestors and feasted with them.
- **Festival of Drunkenness** — honouring Hathor/Sekhmet, with mass public intoxication, music, and erotic celebration to appease the goddess who once nearly destroyed humanity.

### Applying This to Aurum — The Ritual Year

Aurum's life rites layer Egyptian form over Atlantean-imperial grandeur. **Birth** in elite houses is attended by a **Natal Scribe** who records the hour, the exact position of stars, the weather — all cross-referenced against the Archival's birth-rolls. The infant is brought before the household shrine of the Radiant Ones and named in a seven-day ceremony. For common families, birth is domestic; the midwife's role is honoured but not ritualised. **First Threads (coming of age)** at fourteen involves the child receiving their first pleated linen robe in the colour of their family's House — a public declaration of lineage. For priestly-caste children, First Threads is the day their hair is shaved and they enter the Archival's lower library as a novice scribe.

**Marriage** in Aurum involves the signing of the **Marriage Codex** — an elaborate illuminated document cataloguing the couple's property, terms, expectations. The priesthood witnesses but does not officiate. A feast follows; an astrologer reads the stars; the Radiant Ones are offered incense.

**Death** follows an Egyptian-inflected ritual with Atlantean finery. Bodies are embalmed (a profession of ritual experts, revered and half-shunned). The **Opening of the Air** ceremony — Aurum's version of Opening of the Mouth — uses a silver adze and declares the deceased's soul free to rise to the Silver Gardens. The tomb is a niche in the Capital's lower ring, sealed with a carved portrait and a scroll. **The secret of Aurum death:** magical children who are "elevated" do not die — their bodies are drained by the Archival's innermost caste and left nowhere. Their families are told a simple elegy, attend a symbolic funeral with an empty coffin, and — helped by the fog — forget.

- **The Long Night** — the darkest night of the year, when priests descend into the Archival's innermost chamber for rites the public never witnesses. This is when the tree is fed.

## 7. CLOTHING AND ADORNMENT

### Egyptian Clothing — White Linen, Careful Gradations

The fabric of ancient Egypt was **linen** — from flax grown along the Nile, spun and woven by women in temple and household workshops. Wool existed but was considered **ritually impure** (it came from an animal) and was forbidden to priests inside temples. Linen came in grades: coarse *sḫt* for peasants, finer *pȝqt* for middle classes, and the famously translucent **"royal linen" (sšn)** — "woven air," light enough to read through, reserved for royalty and the gods. Most cloth was white or off-white; dyed textiles existed but were rare (dyeing in the pre-industrial Mediterranean was expensive).

**Men** typically wore a **kilt (schenti)** — a length of linen wrapped around the waist, pleated or plain, knee-length for workers and ankle-length for nobility. Nobles often layered a transparent outer garment over a simpler inner kilt. Upper bodies were often bare for labourers; nobles wore short-sleeved tunics (*kalasiris*) or draped shawls.

**Women** wore a **sheath dress (kalasiris)** — a close-fitting garment from under the breasts to the ankles, held up by one or two shoulder straps. Noblewomen layered pleated, semi-transparent robes over this. Women of the lowest classes sometimes worked in loincloths only. The famous pleated linen of the New Kingdom was produced by pressing wet linen between grooved wooden boards and allowing it to dry into permanent folds.

### Priestly Dress — Purity Made Visible

Priests wore **only white linen** — no wool, no leather, no animal-derived fibre at all. Their heads and bodies were **entirely shaved** — Herodotus noted "they shave the whole body every third day so that no vermin or filth may be found upon them while serving the gods." They bathed twice daily in the sacred lake. The lector priest often wore a **sash crossing the chest diagonally**; the sem priest wore the **leopard skin** over one shoulder during funerary rites (the one exception to the wool-rule, and it had its own rationale — the leopard represented the power the priest had subdued). The High Priest of Ptah at Memphis wore the **sidelock of youth** — a single braided lock of hair remaining on a shaved scalp — as a sign of his ritual purity.

### Cosmetics, Wigs, Jewellery

**Kohl** — made from ground galena (lead sulphide) for black, malachite (copper) for green — was applied heavily around the eyes by both men and women of all classes. It was **aesthetic, medicinal (protecting the eyes from sun-glare and insects), and magical (reinforcing the protection of the Eye of Horus)**. Cheeks were stained with red ochre; lips tinted with the same. **Wigs** were worn by all adults of status, elaborately braided, curled, and perfumed with **incense cones** on top of the head that melted through the day, releasing scent.

**Jewellery** was worn in extraordinary quantity by both sexes: **collars (wesekh)** of inlaid gold, lapis lazuli, carnelian, turquoise, and faience; **armbands, anklets, rings, pectorals, earrings, diadems**. Lapis lazuli (blue, imported from Afghanistan, symbolising the heavens) and gold (the flesh of the gods) were the most prestigious combinations. Amulets carrying protective hieroglyphs (the Ankh, Djed, Tyet, Scarab, Eye of Horus) were worn on cords around neck, wrist, and waist.

### Applying This to Aurum — Refinement as Armour

Aurum's clothing is **Egyptian in foundation, Victorian-steampunk in silhouette, and Tang-Chinese in ornament** (Serina's "Asian twist"). The basis is still **fine linen in white and the pale pastels of the dawn sky** — ivory, silver-grey, champagne, muted gold, dove-blue. Cloth of **silk and spider-silk** is available to the elite (traded from Aoli or grown in the sky-city silkworm gardens). **Fitted bodices** for women, long flowing skirts, layered pleated over-robes with trailing sleeves. **Men** wear long coats with high collars, fitted at the waist, often with a cross-sash showing caste; underneath, pleated linen trousers and soft leather shoes.

**The priestly caste** wears **white linen only**, heads shaved, scalps sometimes covered with close-fitted caps of silver silk. Their robes are impeccable. **The Veiled Advisor** wears silver-grey silk that flows to the floor and a veil of hammered silver links so fine it moves like water. Nobody has seen his face. The veil is never lifted in public. **Scribes** wear ink-blue robes with embroidered hieroglyph-like sigils on the cuffs — one sigil added for each discipline mastered.

**Cosmetics**: both men and women of the elite line their eyes with **kohl**, paint their lips the colour of pomegranate, and wear their hair in elaborate braided wigs threaded with strings of pearls or gilded wire. Young initiates wear the **sidelock of youth** on shaved heads. **Jewellery** is extraordinary: **wide gold collars**, pectorals of gold filigree and lapis, rings on every finger, tiny clockwork pendants that tick softly against the breastbone. Anklets with bells for the women of the Ten Houses. Every piece carries a sigil. Most are simply decorative; a few, in the priestly caste, carry actual minor spells etched inside the metal.

**What clothing communicates:** the colour of your collar, the number of pleats in your robe, the metal of your clasps, the sigil on your sleeve — every element is legible to those who know. An Aurum aristocrat can read a stranger's lineage and rank in three seconds. The fog helps — in the street, a priest passes and the citizen's eyes slide past, not noticing, not questioning.

## 8. FOOD AND DRINK

### Egyptian Staple Diet

The Egyptian diet centred on **bread and beer**. Bread was made primarily from **emmer wheat** (a harder, more labour-intensive grain than modern wheat) and barley, ground between stone querns by women in every household. It was baked daily, often on the outside walls of bell-shaped clay ovens. Dozens of varieties existed — flat loaves, conical "sun bread," sweet honey cakes, fig-stuffed breads, long pointed loaves for offering tables. The grit of stone-ground grain was a major cause of tooth wear in Egyptian mummies — nearly every adult's teeth were worn flat by thirty.

**Beer (henqet)** was a staple, not a luxury. Everyone drank it — men, women, children, labourers and pharaohs. It was thick, low-alcohol, sour, nutritious, served in wide-mouthed jars with a straw because of the sediment. Workers on state projects were paid in bread-and-beer rations (the builders of Giza received roughly 10 loaves and 2 jugs of beer per day). **Wine** — made from Delta grapes, pomegranates, figs, dates — was an elite drink, stored in amphorae labelled with vineyard, year, and vintner.

**Vegetables:** onions, leeks, garlic, lettuce, cucumbers, lotus roots, papyrus shoots, chickpeas, lentils, fava beans. Onions in particular were associated with strength and healing (workers at Giza were famously fed them). **Fruits:** figs, dates, grapes, pomegranates, melons, dom-palm fruit, sycamore figs. **Meat:** beef (expensive, elite/festival), mutton, goat, pork (lower-status, sometimes taboo), waterfowl (duck, goose, pigeon), fish from the Nile. **Honey** was the primary sweetener. **Milk and cheese** were consumed fresh (keeping was hard in the heat).

### Food Taboos

**Priests** observed strict dietary restrictions during temple service: **no fish** (identified with Set and the drowning of Osiris); **no onions** (Plutarch: "the priests abstain from onions... the plant was considered unclean and generates flatulence and passion"); **no pork** in many temples; and during service periods, **no sexual activity** and **no wine**. Ordinary Egyptians had fewer taboos; certain nomes had local taboos (one nome avoided fish, another pork, another crocodile meat, etc.).

### Hospitality and Feasting

Feasting at elite level involved long tables heaped with offerings — whole roasted geese, beef ribs, piles of bread, pomegranates stacked in golden bowls, jars of wine cooled in water, conical cakes. Guests were anointed with perfumed oil, given garlands of lotus and cornflower, and seated on cushions. Music (harp, lute, lyre, double-flute) played; dancers performed; servants fanned the room with ostrich feathers. Hospitality was a **moral obligation** — to turn a guest away, to feed them poorly, to refuse bread and water was a cultural sin.

### Applying This to Aurum — Refinement on a Plate

Aurum's cuisine fuses Egyptian staples with an **imperial, ceremonial refinement** (think Tang-China banquet culture, Byzantine table manners, Victorian formalism). The staple grains are **wheat and rice** (sky-city hydroponic rice paddies are a famous innovation). Bread comes in dozens of forms — **golden pleated loaves** (named for the priestly robes), **silver crescents**, **star-cut shortbread**. **Wine** — not beer — is the drink of the elite, served in gilded clockwork goblets that hum faintly when filled. **Tea**, imported from Aoli, is ceremonial — served in tiny porcelain cups with silver strainers, the brewing itself a performative art.

**The dining table of an Aurum aristocrat** at dinner: poached river-fish brought up from the country (symbolic — fish here is a delicacy, not a taboo), roasted pheasant glazed with honey and saffron, asparagus in clarified butter, pomegranate seeds scattered over saffron rice, candied ginger from Aoli, lotus-root soup, apricot tarts, iced-cream shaped like blossoms (cooled by clockwork icehouses powered — unnamed — by a drop of the tree's magic). Each course is announced by a small silver bell.

**The priestly caste observes dietary restrictions** during service: no meat, no strong spirits, no aphrodisiac foods (onions, leeks, garlic). They eat plain bread, steamed vegetables, tea, and a ritual wine infused with lotus petals. The **Chief Archivist** and the **Veiled Advisor** are rumoured to eat almost nothing — a single meal a day, taken alone, of unsweetened porridge and water. **Mínhuì eats what quiet noblewomen eat** — tea, plain rice, fruit. Nobody notices that she does not age. Nobody notices that she rarely drinks wine.

**Ordinary citizens** eat well — Aurum is wealthy and famous for its tables. Bakery windows full of glass-cased pastries, street vendors selling spiced chickpeas in paper cones, ice-vendors hawking flavoured ices in summer. **The country below** eats simply — bread, vegetables, a little meat on festival days. The fog keeps them content.

**Food taboos:** no blood in Aurum cuisine. Meat is bled thoroughly before cooking. Black pudding, rare beef, any "unclean" blood-containing dish is forbidden to the priestly caste and scorned by the aristocracy. The reason given is refinement. The real reason is older — the tree drinks blood, and no mortal should share that drink. **Hospitality** in Aurum is performative — a host shows refinement through the variety and presentation of the table, the quality of conversation, the etiquette of the tea service. To be a bad host is social ruin. To be a celebrated host is to be invited to the Sovereign's salon.

## 9. ARCHITECTURE AND SETTLEMENT

### The Egyptian Temple — The Master Template

Egyptian temples were built as **models of the cosmos.** A priest entering the temple was walking through the world-creation:

1. **The pylon gateway** — two massive trapezoidal towers of stone with a doorway between, representing the twin mountains of the eastern horizon. Above them flew great banners on flagpoles.
2. **The open courtyard** — a large sunlit space where commoners gathered on festival days. Columns lined the walls; statues of the pharaoh stood at intervals.
3. **The hypostyle hall** — a dim, columned hall with the sensation of a stone forest. At Karnak the Great Hypostyle Hall measures 54,000 square feet and contains 134 sandstone columns, the central twelve rising to 21 metres (69 feet). Columns carved as papyrus and lotus bundles represented the primeval marsh. Light entered only through clerestory stone-grille windows in the centre.
4. **The inner vestibules** — progressively smaller, darker rooms, the floor rising, the ceiling lowering, the light dimming.
5. **The sanctuary (naos)** — a small, utterly dark chamber at the temple's deepest point, containing a gilded wooden shrine in which the cult statue of the god stood. Only the High Priest entered. The floor here was the highest point in the temple — the **primordial mound of first creation**, the *benben.*

The architectural progression **mimicked the creation myth** — from outer chaos into inner order, from sunlight into sacred darkness, from public space into the singular point of god-contact.

### Domestic and Civic Architecture

Egyptian houses were built of **sun-dried mud brick**, plastered and whitewashed. **Peasant houses** were one or two rooms with a flat roof used as a sleeping terrace in hot weather. **Nobles' houses** were compound estates with multiple courtyards, reception halls, family quarters, servants' quarters, granaries, gardens with pools and fig trees. Interiors featured **plastered walls painted with wall scenes** (harvest scenes, family portraits, servants at work); floors of packed earth or flagstone; furniture of wood, leather, and woven rush (stools, folding chairs, beds with headrests). Villa estates had **walled gardens** with rectangular lily pools and shade-giving sycamore figs — a central feature of Egyptian domestic pleasure.

### Cities

Cities like **Thebes, Memphis, Akhetaten (Amarna)** were organised around temple complexes. Akhetaten — built from scratch by Akhenaten c. 1346 BCE — shows the ideal: a central axis with the royal palace and the Great Temple of Aten, surrounded by residential districts for priests, scribes, craftsmen, and workers, with storage granaries and workshops on the edges. Streets were unpaved, often narrow and winding (except for processional ways). Markets were open-air.

### Atlantis — The Concentric City

Plato's Atlantean capital was built as **concentric rings of land and water**: two land-rings, three water-rings, all precisely circular, carved by Poseidon. Bridges connected them; the rings were walled, the outermost walls covered in **brass**, the middle in **tin**, and the innermost — around Poseidon's temple — in **orichalcum** that "flashed with the red light of fire." At the centre stood the temple of Poseidon: **200 metres long, 100 metres wide**, its exterior covered in silver, its roof-pinnacles in gold, its interior roof of ivory adorned with gold, silver, and orichalcum. Statues of gold of the ten kings and their wives stood outside. A great canal connected the rings to the sea; the city's harbour was full of ships.

### Applying This to Aurum — Architecture of the Sky

Aurum fuses all three traditions — Egyptian temple progression, Atlantean concentric rings, and Victorian-steampunk aesthetic — into a civilisation whose architecture is its proudest achievement.

**The Capital** is built on Atlantean principles: a central disk-city in concentric rings. The **outermost ring** is industrial and mercantile — airship docks, warehouses, banks, trade halls, barracks. The **second ring** is residential — the elegant townhouses of the middle class, the craftsmen, the scribes. The **third ring** is aristocratic — the palaces of the Ten Houses, clockwork gardens, glass conservatories, theatres. The **innermost ring** is the Capital Temple and the Sovereign's Palace, and at its exact centre stands **The Archival** — a massive library complex, golden-domed, hundreds of feet tall, surrounded by a moat of still silver water.

**The Archival itself** follows the Egyptian temple progression. Outer courtyards where scholars of every citizen-class may read under supervision. A **Great Hall of Columns** (the hypostyle hall translated) where rows of brass columns shaped like vertical scrolls support a ceiling painted with constellations; light enters only through a high clerestory of stained glass. Inner vestibules — the Chamber of the Forgotten Tongues, the Chamber of the Sealed Scrolls, the Chamber of the Ten (where the council meets). And at the very heart, reached by a corridor so narrow two can barely pass: **the Sanctum of the Pillar.** Inside stands the **paulownia tree (Dìwǔ)** — growing through the floor, its roots anchoring the entire city below, its canopy pressing against a glass dome above. Its leaves shed gold in autumn. Its blossoms open silver-purple in spring. Its trunk is scarred with tiny cuts where blood has been drawn. **Only the Veiled Advisor, the Chief Archivist, and Mínhuì herself are allowed into this chamber.**

**Civic architecture:** brass, glass, polished wood, marble. Pneumatic tubes deliver messages between offices. Clockwork elevators rise through towers. Glass-enclosed promenades connect the rings. **The skyways between cities** are brass-trussed bridges suspended on cables of spider-silk, tinted with protective gold leaf. Ordinary citizen homes are comfortable, well-made, with brass fittings and stained-glass panels. The country below, under the fog, has stone farmhouses, thatched barns, dirt roads. The worlds never see each other except at festival processions and at the arrival of the harvest — and then only through a haze.

## 10. ART, MUSIC, AND STORYTELLING

### Egyptian Visual Art — Rules and Meaning

Egyptian art is one of the most codified visual traditions in human history. **For three thousand years** its rules barely changed. Figures were drawn in **composite view** — head in profile, eye from the front, torso turned frontally, legs in profile — because each body part was shown in its most recognisable aspect. Size indicated importance: the pharaoh towered over enemies and subjects regardless of actual height. A strict **canon of proportions** — originally 18 squares from hairline to sole, refined to 21 in the Saite period — governed every human figure.

**Colour was symbolic:**
- **Gold** = the flesh of the gods, immortality, the sun (statues of gods were gold-skinned; the pharaoh's face in tomb portraits was often gilded).
- **Blue (lapis lazuli)** = the sky, the Nile flood, divine power (the gods' hair was lapis-blue; the ceilings of tombs were blue with yellow stars).
- **Red (ochre)** = life, victory, male skin tone; also danger, chaos, the god Set.
- **Green (malachite)** = rebirth, growth, the flesh of Osiris as lord of the underworld.
- **White (gypsum)** = purity, sacred linen, truth.
- **Black (soot, galena)** = fertility, the Nile silt, death in its rebirth aspect.

Art was **functional, not decorative.** Tomb paintings ensured the deceased's afterlife. Temple reliefs renewed the daily ritual. A carved ankh held the power of life. Hieroglyphs themselves were both writing and art — each sign both read and looked at.

### Music and Dance

Music in Egypt was **everywhere** — temple ritual, festivals, funerals, banquets, work. Instruments included the **harp** (both large standing harps and small portable ones), **lute** (introduced in the New Kingdom from Asia), **lyre**, **double-flute (*memet*)**, **sistrum** (a sacred rattle associated with Hathor), **drums** of various sizes, **clappers** (pairs of carved wood or ivory pieces for percussion). **Female musicians (*shemayet*)** were temple priestesses; their music was believed to please the gods and create the spiritual atmosphere for effective ritual.

Dance was professional — temple dancers accompanied religious processions; banquet dancers (often female, often Nubian, sometimes nearly nude) performed acrobatic routines for the elite; funerary dancers performed mourning rites at tombs. Singing in groups, clapping, and rhythmic chanting accompanied agricultural labour.

### Storytelling and the Written Tradition

Egyptian literature is one of humanity's oldest continuous written traditions. **The Story of Sinuhe** (c. 1900 BCE) — a courtier's exile and return — was memorised by every scribal student for two millennia. **The Tale of the Shipwrecked Sailor**, **The Tale of Two Brothers**, **The Eloquent Peasant**, **The Book of Thoth**. Wisdom literature (**The Instructions of Ptahhotep**, **The Maxims of Any**) taught ethics. Love poetry flourished in the New Kingdom: "My sister's arms are wreaths of flowers; my sister's hair is a net of lapis lazuli." Hymns to the gods, especially the **Great Hymn to the Aten** of Akhenaten, are among the earliest monotheistic prayers in world literature.

### Applying This to Aurum — The Performing Empire

Aurum is a civilisation that **performs itself** — its art, music, and literature are the polished surface under which the horror sleeps.

**Visual art** in Aurum fuses the Egyptian symbolic canon with Tang-China porcelain aesthetics and Victorian Pre-Raphaelite realism. Official portraiture follows a **codified canon**: the Sovereign is always painted largest, face gilded, eyes outlined in lapis-blue kohl. The colour symbolism inherited from Egypt holds: **gold = divine; silver = moon, women, the Archival; blue = sky, sovereignty; green = growth, the gardens; white = purity; red = forbidden (the colour of blood, avoided in public art).**

Priestly-caste painters work in illuminated manuscripts — they illustrate the **official Codex of the Radiant Ones**, the **Book of Ascensions**, the **Annals of the Ten Houses**. Public art — murals on civic buildings, reliefs on theatre facades — tells the approved history. **Hidden art exists:** heretical painters in the lower ring paint things the censors would burn — tiny paulownia leaves in corners, a veiled figure with a cut on his palm, a child's face disappearing into fog. Nobody admits seeing these. Mínhuì owns several.

**Music** is Aurum's most celebrated art. Elaborate orchestras play at every major function — violins (adapted from Isenholt), harps, flutes, the clockwork "silver harpsichord" that plays itself when wound, and a huge vaulted pipe organ in the Archival that was designed to mask the sound of the tree's occasional groan. **Women of the priestly caste** are trained as singers (the *shemayet* echo) — their voices carry the hymns of the Radiant Ones through festival processions.

**Literature:** illuminated scrolls, leather-bound codices, the mass-produced press-scrolls for ordinary citizens. Novels are popular — serial romances about noble love, adventure stories about sky-pirates, moral tales in which magic is discovered and the afflicted child is nobly "elevated." A **wisdom literature** tradition flourishes: the **Hundred Sayings**, the **Codex of the Sovereign's Mirror**, the **Aphorisms of the Third Archivist**. Poetry is the highest art; aristocrats compose verse for each other in elaborate tournaments. **Hidden writings exist** — in the Archival's unlogged collections, in locked private libraries, in the memories of old servants who still remember names. Mínhuì has copied, by hand, the old stories of Avalúne when it was whole — kept in a small chest no priest would think to search, in the quarters of a quiet noblewoman who catalogues scrolls.

## 11. TECHNOLOGY AND CRAFTS

### Egyptian Technology — The Bronze-Age Peak

Egyptian technology peaked in the New Kingdom at a level that astonished later ages. **Metalworking:** copper from Sinai, bronze after 2000 BCE, iron slowly introduced in the late period. Gold was worked with extraordinary skill — filigree, granulation, cloisonné, lost-wax casting. The gold mask of Tutankhamun (c. 1323 BCE) weighs 11 kg of solid gold and represents centuries of accumulated craft. **Silver** was rarer than gold (Egypt had no domestic source) and thus more prestigious in some periods. **Stone-working** was the signal achievement — monumental sculptures in granite and quartzite, obelisks weighing hundreds of tons, pyramid blocks fitted without mortar. The workers used copper saws, wooden wedges wetted to split stone, and ramps of Nile mud.

**Textile production:** flax cultivation, retting, combing, spinning on hand-held or supported spindles, horizontal ground looms for linen. The quality of royal linen was legendary — 200 threads per inch in the finest New Kingdom specimens, barely achievable by modern industrial weaving.

**Pottery:** Egyptian potters used the slow wheel from c. 3000 BCE, the fast wheel later. Glazed **faience** — a blue-green ceramic made from crushed quartz — was an Egyptian invention and an important export. **Glass** was invented or perfected in Egypt by the 18th Dynasty; Egyptian glassworkers produced core-formed vessels, rods, amulets, and the earliest cast glass.

**Engineering:** surveyors (the *harpedonaptai*, "rope-stretchers") used knotted ropes and sighting staves to align structures to cardinal directions with astonishing accuracy. The Great Pyramid is oriented to true north within a fraction of a degree. Irrigation canals, shadufs (counterweighted well-levers), and the complex Nilometers that measured the flood level to predict harvests. Boats — from reed skiffs to massive cedar-hulled trading ships — were a central technology; Egyptian shipwrights sailed the Red Sea to Punt and the Mediterranean to Lebanon and Crete.

### The Atlantean Dimension — Orichalcum and the Wonder-Metal

Plato describes **orichalcum** as Atlantis's signature metal — reddish, bright, "flashing with the red light of fire," mined everywhere on the island and valued second only to gold. No such metal exists historically. Later fantasy has inherited it as a magical alloy (often gold + copper + an unnamed third element; sometimes identified speculatively with bronze, brass, or natural platinum alloys). Atlantis also had **ships, canals, temples of extraordinary scale, and a network of bridges** suggesting extremely advanced engineering for Plato's imagined time.

### The Steampunk Dimension — Victorian Industrial Aesthetic

Steampunk — the aesthetic that Aurum overtly wears — draws on the real technologies of the **19th-century industrial revolution** reimagined with an extra layer of ornament, complexity, and functioning-magic. Core elements: **brass (for its malleability, warm colour, and capacity for engraving); copper (for pipes and pneumatic tubes); polished wood (ebony, mahogany, teak); leather (for straps, cases, bindings); glass (for domes, portholes, gauges)**. Key technologies: **steam power** (boilers, pistons, condensers), **clockwork** (gears, escapements, mainsprings), **pneumatic tubes** (Victorian London had real pneumatic mail systems from the 1860s), **airships** (the real Zeppelin LZ 1 flew in 1900; steampunk airships are ornamented versions), **analog computational instruments** (the Antikythera mechanism from ancient Greece is a proto-example; Babbage's Difference Engine of the 1820s a real ancestor).

### Applying This to Aurum — Golden-Age Craftsmanship

Aurum's technology is the fusion of all three: **bronze-age Egyptian stone and gold craft + imagined Atlantean orichalcum and scale + Victorian steampunk brass and clockwork**, all discreetly powered by tiny drops of blood magic.

**The signature metal is "aurite"** (the Aurum name for orichalcum) — a reddish-gold alloy the priestly caste guards the formula for. It holds spells; it rings like a bell; it does not tarnish. The Veiled Advisor's sword hilt is aurite. The Archival's doors are inlaid with it. The hull of the Sovereign's flagship airship is plated with it.

**Goldsmiths and clockmakers** are the celebrated artisan classes. Aurum exports the most extraordinary precision instruments of Avalúne — pocket watches of nineteen complications, astrolabes that track every major star, music boxes that play programmable tunes, mechanical birds that sing. **Glassblowers and lamp-makers** supply the light of the city — the streetlamps of Aurum are leaded stained-glass globes that light themselves at dusk (small charm inside).

**Textile production** is advanced: silk farms in domed gardens, spider-silk looms that weave veils of supernatural lightness, automated Jacquard-style clockwork looms that can weave complex patterns from coded cards.

**Engineering:** airship manufacture is a massive industry; the sky cities are connected by regular airship routes, and every noble family owns a private skiff. **Clockwork elevators** run in the towers; **pneumatic mail** cannisters shoot through glass tubes threading every building; **steam-powered trams** on brass rails carry citizens between the districts of each ring. **Medical technology** includes clockwork prosthetics (for those who lose limbs) and surgical instruments of astonishing precision.

**The secret technology** — kept in the deepest Archival — is the **blood-magic lift engines** at the root of every city. They are huge, ancient, carved with Yuèlì's broken name. They require feeding. They are the reason everything else works. No publication describes them. The engineers who maintain them are recruited from the priestly caste and never leave the Archival's inner rooms.

## 12. WARFARE AND WEAPONS

### Egyptian Warfare — The New Kingdom Army

Under the New Kingdom pharaohs — Thutmose III, Amenhotep III, Ramesses II, Ramesses III — Egypt fielded one of the ancient world's most formidable armies. Core components: **infantry** armed with bronze-tipped spears, axes, khopesh (sickle-swords), and rectangular wooden shields; **archers** using composite bows (adopted from the Hyksos) firing bronze- or flint-tipped arrows; the revolutionary **chariot corps** — lightweight two-man chariots with a driver and an archer, used for mobile shock assaults. The chariot was the tank of the Bronze Age.

Organisation was **decimal** — units of 10, 50, 100, 250 — with officers (*standard-bearers*) at each level. The army was divided into **divisions** named after gods (the Division of Amun, of Ra, of Ptah, of Set), each about 5,000 men. The pharaoh personally led campaigns; **Ramesses II at Kadesh (1274 BCE)** against the Hittites produced one of history's first detailed battle narratives.

**Armour** was minimal — a padded linen cuirass for officers, sometimes scale armour of bronze or leather plates; rank-and-file wore a kilt and carried a shield. The climate did not encourage heavy armour. Helmets were leather or linen, occasionally bronze for the elite. **Fortifications** were major — massive mud-brick fortresses along the Nubian frontier, the **Wall of the Ruler** along the Sinai border, the Delta fortresses against Libyan incursions.

Women rarely fought as regular soldiers but occasionally commanded — **Queen Ahhotep I** rallied troops during the reunification after the Hyksos expulsion; **Hatshepsut** led military campaigns to Punt and Nubia. In most periods combat was male.

### Atlantis at War

Plato describes Atlantis as a conquering empire with a vast army and navy, extending its reach to Libya, Egypt, and western Europe before being defeated by an idealised ancient Athens. Its chariots, ships, and infantry are given fantastic numbers (1,200 ships, 10,000 chariots, countless infantry) — this is ideological, making the moral victory of Athens more impressive. The Atlantean army becomes a symbol of hubris: vast, powerful, and ultimately defeated by virtue.

### Applying This to Aurum — Warfare From the Sky

Aurum fields no army on the ground — its military is **the sky itself.** No other nation in Avalúne has airships, and the asymmetry is total.

**The Aurum Air-Legion** consists of:
- **Capital-class airships** — three of them, named after the first three sky cities. Each is the size of a small floating town, armoured with aurite plating, armed with steam-driven cannons, staffed by 400.
- **Cruiser-class airships** — a dozen or so, smaller, faster, used for reconnaissance and power projection. Each carries 80.
- **Skiffs and corsairs** — dozens, piloted by pairs of priestly-caste officers, used for rapid deployment.
- **Sky-marines** — elite soldiers trained to drop by clockwork-braked rope-lines onto enemy positions. Armoured in aurite-plated coats with reinforced linen underlayers. Armed with **clockwork crossbows** firing bolts in rapid succession, **cavalry sabres** of folded steel, and **phial-bombs** — small glass spheres of alchemical fire.
- **The Veiled Guard** — the Sovereign's personal force. Fifty warriors, each a priestly-caste child chosen for combat aptitude, trained from age six. Armour of silver-grey aurite. Sabres etched with sigils. Veils over their faces in battle.

**Tactics:** Aurum does not engage in ground wars. When disciplined, it intimidates from the sky — an airship appearing over a rebellious region is usually enough. When necessary, it strikes with phial-bombs, crossbow volleys, and brief precision raids by sky-marines. **Aurum has not lost a battle in eight centuries.**

**Women in warfare:** the priestly caste includes female officers and Veiled Guard members. Among the Air-Legion's engineers, navigators, and medics, women are proportionally represented. Among the sky-marines, women are rarer but present. **The public aristocracy** does not typically involve its daughters in combat — that is considered less refined.

**How war is declared:** through the oracle of the Radiant Ones — the Sovereign asks the gods; the priests interpret the nodding; the decision is announced. In practice, the Veiled Advisor decides first and the ritual follows. **How war is ended:** on Aurum's terms. **What war looks like:** a clean, precise, terrifying demonstration of superiority, followed by ceremony, followed by tribute. Aurum does not conquer — it dictates. Its neighbours' polite acquiescence is its preferred outcome.

**The real military weakness** — which no Aurum officer in the public record has noticed — is that **the airships only fly because of the tree.** If the paulownia ever truly dies, if the blood supply is ever truly cut, if the magic that holds the cities up ever truly fails — the entire air-legion falls from the sky. This knowledge is held only by the Veiled Advisor and his innermost circle. Mínhuì knows too. She has watched them add redundancies for centuries.

## 13. TRADE AND ECONOMY

### Egyptian Trade — The Nile as Highway

Egypt's economy was **agrarian, surplus-based, redistributive.** The Pharaoh, in theory, owned all the land; peasants worked it and surrendered a share to the temples and the royal granaries, which redistributed grain in exchange for labour (the wage was literally bread and beer). Markets existed — open-air *peheret* where goods changed hands — but the money economy was weak; **barter against a weight-standard of copper, silver, or gold (the *deben*)** was the usual practice for transactions. Coined money did not arrive until the Ptolemaic period.

**Foreign trade** was a state monopoly run by the pharaoh. Egypt traded:
- **Exports:** grain, linen, papyrus, gold (Nubian mines), faience, glass, alabaster vessels, salted fish, natron, fine stoneware.
- **Imports:** cedar from Lebanon (crucial — Egypt had no tall timber), copper from Sinai and Cyprus, silver from Anatolia, tin for bronze, horses from Anatolia, incense and myrrh from Punt (probably modern Somalia/Eritrea), ivory from Nubia, lapis lazuli from Afghanistan, obsidian from Ethiopia.

**The expedition to Punt** under Hatshepsut (c. 1475 BCE) is the most famous — five ships sailed down the Red Sea and returned laden with incense trees, ebony, ivory, gold, baboons, and exotic animals, commemorated in the reliefs at Deir el-Bahri. **Long-distance caravan routes** crossed the Sinai and the Eastern Desert, linking Egypt to Canaan, Mesopotamia, and ultimately the Silk Road systems.

### The Atlantean Model — Empire and Tribute

Atlantis in Plato is not primarily a trader but a **tribute-empire**. Its vast wealth comes from its own island's resources (orichalcum mines, fertile soil, cattle including elephants) AND from tribute from conquered territories. Its harbours were full of ships. Its wealth bred arrogance; its arrogance bred hubris; its hubris bred punishment.

### Applying This to Aurum — The Great Export

Aurum's economy is built on two legs: the **internal extractive system** (the country below the cities produces food, the harvest class produces the magic that keeps them aloft) and **external trade dominance**.

**Aurum's exports are the envy of Avalúne:**
- **Precision clockwork** — watches, astrolabes, music boxes, medical instruments, automated looms.
- **Fine glass and stained-glass panels** — used in temples, palaces, and wealthy homes from Tyramare to Maramir.
- **Aurite goods** — jewellery, small ornamental weapons, musical-instrument fittings. The formula for the alloy itself is not exported.
- **Silk and spider-silk textiles** — woven in the sky-garden farms, fine as breath, valued in every foreign court.
- **Illuminated scrolls and printed books** — the Archival runs presses that produce beautiful, expensive, ideologically filtered texts in multiple languages.
- **Pharmaceutical preparations** — Aurum's physicians compound medicines famed for their potency.
- **Luxury foods** — candied ginger, saffron, rare teas, dried apricots, preserved lotus root, and the celebrated **Aurum vintage wines** from the sky-vineyards.

**Aurum's imports:**
- **Raw materials** — bronze ore, iron, leather, wool (used by commoners despite the priestly prohibition), cotton.
- **Food staples** — wheat, rice, cattle, fish — pulled up from the country below and from Ardenmere.
- **Books from other nations** (for the Archival's comparative collections, though what comes in rarely goes out).
- **Slaves** — a quiet import from the southern kingdoms, recorded under the name "indentured household service." Most serve in the wealthy households. A few are routed, under false names, into the harvesting pipeline when their magic is detected.
- **Curiosities and luxuries** — jade from Aoli, ambergris from the coast, rare animals for the menagerie gardens.

**Currency:** Aurum has issued the **gold Solaris** and **silver Lunaris** coin for eight hundred years. Both bear the Sovereign's profile on one face and the paulownia leaf on the reverse (the public is told this represents "the tree of the Archival, symbol of learning"). The Solaris is the reserve currency of Avalúne — merchants everywhere accept it. Smaller denominations (the copper **Aes**, the brass **Obol**) circulate among common citizens.

**Markets:** every city ring has its **bazaar halls** — huge glass-domed buildings with pneumatic-tube ordering systems, merchant stalls arrayed in concentric circles, tea stalls and music and haggling. Street-food vendors in the outer ring; exclusive boutiques in the inner rings; private appointments for the Ten Houses. **The richest single merchant family** — the Velastre — owns a fleet of fifty airships and has compounds in four sky cities. The Sovereign's court has accepted their daughter into the Ten Houses by marriage, twice.

**The trade route beneath the cities** — unseen but vital — is the supply chain that brings food up, sends manufactured goods down, and moves certain sealed cargoes on specific moonless nights. Fog obscures the pattern. No citizen examines it too closely.

## 14. LANGUAGE AND WRITING

### Egyptian Writing — Three Scripts, One Language

The Egyptian language had a written life of more than three thousand years — one of the longest in human history — in three successive scripts:

- **Hieroglyphs (*medu-netjer*, "the god's words")** — the monumental pictorial script used for temple walls, tomb inscriptions, royal decrees, and sacred texts. Some 700–1,000 signs were in regular use; a scribe had to master several hundred. Hieroglyphs were **simultaneously phonetic (sign = sound), ideographic (sign = meaning), and determinative (sign = clarifier)**. A single word might combine all three. The script could be written left-to-right, right-to-left, or vertically — the direction readable from which way the human and animal figures were facing.
- **Hieratic** — a cursive simplification of hieroglyphs used by scribes for administrative documents, medical texts, literary works. Fast to write, less ornate. Used on papyrus, ostraca (limestone flakes), and leather.
- **Demotic** — a further-simplified cursive script that emerged in the Late Period, used for daily documents.

Only a small fraction of the population was **literate** — estimates range from 1% to 10%. Literacy was power. The scribe was exempt from heavy labour, taxes, and conscription. *"Be a scribe. It saves you from labour and protects you from every kind of work."* ("The Satire of the Trades," c. 1900 BCE).

### Sacred Texts and Oral Tradition

The **Pyramid Texts** (c. 2400 BCE) — the earliest known religious writings of humanity — were carved inside the burial chambers of Old Kingdom pyramids. The **Coffin Texts** (Middle Kingdom) democratised these spells, painted onto wooden coffins. The **Book of the Dead** (New Kingdom) made them available to anyone wealthy enough to commission a papyrus copy. **The Book of Two Ways**, **The Book of Amduat**, **The Book of Gates** — underworld guides with maps.

Alongside the written tradition existed a vast **oral tradition** — hymns learned by heart, magical formulas passed master to apprentice, work songs, lullabies, stories told at firesides. The **ritual utterances of the lector priest** were memorised verbatim; the power of a spell depended on exact repetition.

### Poetry, Rhetoric, the Spoken Word

Egyptian poetry is one of the world's oldest living traditions. **Love poetry** of the New Kingdom uses nature metaphors, repetition, and playful direct address ("I belong to you like this plot of ground... which I have planted"). **Hymns** — the Great Hymn to the Aten, the Hymns to Amun, the Cannibal Hymn of Unis — combine theology and ecstatic praise. **Rhetoric** was a refined art; scribal education included learning to argue in writing and in speech. **The Eloquent Peasant** is a short story that is also a masterclass in rhetoric — nine formal speeches by a wronged peasant pleading his case.

### Applying This to Aurum — Language as Control

Aurum is the literate civilisation of Avalúne. **Universal literacy** is a goal of the regime — every citizen reads — and the Archival prints cheap press-scrolls and serial novels in vast quantities. The public pride: *"We are the people of the written word."* The hidden truth: a fully literate population is a population that can be efficiently told what is true.

**The official language** — **Aurite** — is spoken across all sky cities and is a prestige second language throughout Avalúne. It uses a **phonetic alphabet** (descended long ago from a hieroglyphic ancestor that has been simplified for printing) supplemented by a large inventory of **sigil-ideograms** for religious, legal, and technical concepts. A legal document will be half alphabetic text, half sigils. Only priestly-caste scribes read all the sigils fluently.

**Three registers** exist:
- **High Aurite** — the language of the court, the law, and the higher priesthood. Archaic, formal, grammatically complex. Mastered only by aristocrats and priestly-caste scholars.
- **Common Aurite** — the everyday language of citizens and commoners. Same vocabulary, simplified grammar, everyday sigils.
- **Temple Aurite** — the sacred script, a form of the language frozen roughly at the founding of the cities sixteen generations ago. Used only in ritual text, never spoken aloud outside the naos. Written in hieroglyph-descended signs. **Reading it requires priestly initiation.**

**The Book of Ascensions** — Aurum's sacred scripture — is written in Temple Aurite. Ordinary citizens know the approved translations. The Archival holds the original. Mínhuì can read it, of course. She has notes in the margins of her own private copy that nobody else in the sky cities would recognise as annotations at all — they are in the old Yínhé script of Aoli, a language she has not spoken aloud in a thousand years.

**Oral tradition** exists — folk songs, lullabies, street-musicians' repertoires, the whispered gossip of servants in kitchens. The censors do not reach here. The fog does some of the work. Occasionally a song surfaces that contains a line nobody can trace — *"the tree remembers the children... the tree remembers..."* — and the censors quietly remove it from the taverns, and the tune is forgotten within a season. Almost. The lullabies linger longest.

## 15. EDUCATION AND KNOWLEDGE

### Egyptian Education — Scribal Schooling

Egyptian education was **guild-based and functional.** Most children learned their trade by **apprenticeship** — watching and helping their parents. Only a minority entered formal schools, and those mostly to become scribes. Scribal training began around age five and continued for up to twelve years.

**The curriculum:**
- **Reading and writing** — hieroglyphs and hieratic. A student copied out texts onto ostraca (limestone flakes) until the forms were automatic, then graduated to papyrus.
- **Mathematics** — practical arithmetic, geometry, surveying, accounting. The **Rhind Mathematical Papyrus** (c. 1550 BCE) preserves training problems: how to calculate grain rations, the slope of a pyramid, the area of a field.
- **Astronomy** — the 36 decans (star groups), the heliacal rising of Sirius, the divisions of the night, the calendar.
- **Literature** — memorisation of classic texts (The Story of Sinuhe, Ptahhotep's Instructions), learning to compose elegant letters.
- **Geography** — lists of towns, nomes, foreign countries, trade routes.
- **Religious knowledge** — hymns, ritual texts, proper forms of address to the gods.
- **Law and administration** — court procedures, property law, inheritance law, the duties of offices.

Students wrote on **ostraca first**, graduating to **more expensive papyrus** as they advanced. Schools were often attached to temples (the **House of Life**) or to royal offices (the **Kap**, the court school where princes were educated alongside the sons of nobles).

### The House of Life (Per Ankh) — The Heart

This is the single most important real-world referent for the Aurum Archival. The **House of Life** was the intellectual institution of Egyptian temple life — **library, scriptorium, school, research centre, medical college, ritual archive** all in one. Two have been identified archaeologically: one at **Amarna** (Akhenaten's capital, c. 1340 BCE), whose mud-bricks were individually stamped with *per ankh*; one at the **Ramesseum** (Ramesses II's mortuary temple, c. 1250 BCE). The Amarna House of Life was a 15-metre-square complex of small rooms that excavator John Pendlebury compared to "the rooms of a don in an Oxford or Cambridge college" — intimate, scholarly, quiet.

The House of Life held:
- **Medical texts** (like the Ebers and Edwin Smith papyri)
- **Ritual manuals** (the daily temple liturgies, festival orders)
- **Astronomical and calendrical tables**
- **Mathematical problems and solutions**
- **Literary classics**
- **Magical spells and amulets**
- **Dream interpretation guides**
- **Lists of towns, kings, gods**

Its personnel were **priestly-caste scholars** — astronomers, physicians, theologians, architects, translators, ritual specialists. They taught the elite young, produced new texts, copied old ones, and advised the temple and the pharaoh on matters requiring recorded knowledge. **Outside the temple, you could not access the House of Life.** To consult its records you needed a priestly sponsor.

### Applying This to Aurum — The Archival as Universe

The **Aurum Archival** IS the per ankh made colossal. It is the civilisation's brain, its soul, its library, its university, its medical school, its censor's office, and its most sacred temple — all in one building at the Capital's heart.

**Education in Aurum:**
- **Common citizens** — every child attends a neighbourhood school from age five to twelve, learning literacy in Common Aurite, basic arithmetic, civic virtues, the approved history, and a craft relevant to their family's trade. Aurum has the highest literacy rate in Avalúne. Teachers are called **Tutors**; they are respected but modestly paid.
- **Craft apprenticeships** — from twelve onward, children enter their trade. Goldsmiths, clockmakers, bakers, seamstresses, airshipwrights. Guilds set standards and maintain training.
- **Aristocratic children (the Ten Houses and wealthy merchant families)** — privately tutored at home until eight, then sent to the **House of First Light**, an elite school attached to the Archival, where they learn High Aurite, classical literature, rhetoric, philosophy, music, fencing, etiquette, and court dance.
- **Priestly-caste children** — from their First Threads ceremony, they enter the Archival's own training programme. This takes between fifteen and twenty-five years, depending on their specialty. They progress through levels — **Novice, Reader, Scribe, Librarian, Archivist, Veiled** — each level unlocking access to deeper parts of the Archival.

**What is taught in the Archival:**
- Every discipline of scholarship in Avalúne.
- Eight languages are required of a full Archivist (Aurite, Tyramaran, Aoli's literary tongue, Isenholt skaldic, Braedun druidic formulas, Solis trade argot, Maramir poetic, and the ancient Yínhé that only the Chief Archivist is supposed to know).
- The official cosmology and theology.
- Advanced mathematics, including Aurum's guarded knowledge of airship engineering.
- Medicine, surgery, pharmacology.
- The **Catalog** — the Archival's own taxonomy of all recorded knowledge, which itself takes years to memorise.
- At higher levels: **the partial truths.** Some knowledge is revealed only to those who take binding oaths. Some only to Archivists. Some only to the Veiled.

**Hidden pedagogy:** the innermost chamber — the Sanctum of the Pillar — is not a classroom. It is a working ritual space where the blood magic is renewed. Only three or four souls each generation ever see it. Mínhuì is always one of them — the quiet noblewoman with the key. **What she is really teaching, by tending the tree rather than destroying it, is waiting. She is teaching time. She is the only patience in the building old enough to outlast the rot.**

## 16. MEDICINE AND HEALING

### Egyptian Medicine — Physician and Priest in One

Egyptian medicine is the most sophisticated pre-Greek medical tradition in the ancient world, and the crucial point for Aurum is that **the physician and the priest were the same person.** There was no sharp distinction between rational and magical treatment — incantations, herbs, surgery, and prayer were applied together, and none seemed more "superstitious" than the others.

**The major medical texts** (fragments of a much larger lost literature):
- **The Edwin Smith Papyrus (c. 1600 BCE)** — a surgical treatise. Forty-eight case studies of injuries, each following a rigorous pattern: examination, diagnosis, treatment, prognosis ("an ailment I will treat" / "an ailment I will contend with" / "an ailment not to be treated"). Remarkably rational. It describes the brain (for the first time in any surviving text), heart, pulse, and meninges.
- **The Ebers Papyrus (c. 1550 BCE)** — over 700 magical formulas and folk remedies across 110 pages. Gynaecology, contraception, dentistry, dermatology, parasitology. Recipes combining verified pharmacological agents (willow bark = aspirin, opium = analgesic, castor oil = laxative) with ritual incantations.
- **The Kahun Gynaecological Papyrus, Hearst Papyrus, London Medical Papyrus, Berlin Medical Papyrus** — additional specialised texts.

**The priestly healers:**
- **Priests of Sekhmet** — the lion-headed goddess who brought disease and could cure it. Her priests were the most prestigious physicians, experts in plague, poisons, and surgery.
- **Wab priests** and **sau (charm-makers)** — practised laying-on-of-hands and charm-recital.
- **Swnw (the common term for physician)** — temple-trained but could practise outside the temple.

Treatment combined:
- **Herbalism** — hundreds of plant drugs, some very effective (willow bark, pomegranate, garlic, onion, mint, thyme).
- **Surgery** — bone-setting, dental extraction, tumour removal, trepanation in some cases, suturing, cauterisation. Circumcision was routine.
- **Ritual and magic** — incantations, amulets, sacred images, prayers.
- **Heka — the word made real.** Every healer was, in a sense, a "priest of Heka." The right words, properly spoken, in the right order, with the right offerings, could move the illness from the patient to another vessel.
- **Midwifery** — practised by women, assisted by Bes and Tawaret. Birthing bricks, midwives, herbal contraceptives and emmenagogues were available.

### The Place of Healing

Temple healing cults existed at shrines where the sick could **sleep overnight (incubation)** and receive dream-diagnoses from the gods. The **Temple of Hathor at Dendera** and the **Temple of Imhotep at Saqqara** were pilgrimage sites for healing. The **sanatorium at Dendera** had bathing pools where patients could soak in sacred water infused with hieroglyphic spells washed from stele above the pools.

### Applying This to Aurum — The Kindest Lie

Aurum's medicine is the most advanced in Avalúne — and it is wrapped in more lies than anything else.

**Medical infrastructure:**
- **The College of Physicians** — a wing of the Archival where surgeon-priests train. Ten-year programme. Prestigious. Graduates serve the aristocracy, the priestly caste, and, through public clinics, the citizen class.
- **Public infirmaries** — three large hospitals in the Capital, smaller ones in each sky city. Clean, staffed, with clockwork surgical instruments and trained nurses. The sick of citizen class are genuinely well-cared-for.
- **Country clinics** — in the hinterland below, staffed by junior priests on rotation. Adequate but basic.

**Practices:**
- **Surgery** at a level that would be recognisable to a Victorian surgeon — antiseptic technique (in the priestly caste they understand germ theory in a half-magical formulation, as "impurity of the air"), anaesthetic (compounds of lotus and opium), fine clockwork sutures, prosthetics.
- **Pharmacology** — an extensive pharmacopoeia printed in the Archival's medical press, drawing on herbal traditions across Avalúne plus Aurum's own laboratory syntheses.
- **Obstetrics** — almost no maternal deaths among the aristocracy; low among citizens; higher among the country population below the fog.
- **Psychological healing** — the College of Physicians includes **Listeners**, priestly-caste practitioners who take patients' confessions and treat "disturbances of the soul." This sounds gentle. It sometimes is. Sometimes it is a screening tool that identifies citizens whose magical abilities have begun to manifest — and marks them for "elevation."
- **Charm-making and ritual healing** — a low-level service in the outer rings. Minor spells for warts, headaches, nightmares. Most are placebo wrapped in ritual. The priestly caste does not mind — it keeps ordinary citizens from experimenting with real magic.

**The core lie of Aurum medicine:** the population is told that the sky cities themselves have a "benevolent aura" that protects the health of their inhabitants. The real reason Aurum citizens are healthier than other nations is simpler — better nutrition, better sanitation, better surgery. The **"aura"** is the fog, and the fog is not health.

**Mínhuì's hidden role in medicine:** she knows the true pharmacology of the paulownia — its leaves are a powerful analgesic, its blossoms a fever-breaker, its sap (when harvested without harm to the tree) a genuine healing reagent. She supplies a few doses a year, anonymously, to an old woman physician in the Lower Ring who treats the poor for nothing. The old woman never wonders where the strange silver-scented tincture comes from. Over centuries, many quiet hundreds of citizens have been saved by this. It is the one small mercy Mínhuì still permits herself.

## 17. DAILY LIFE

### Egyptian Daily Life — The Rhythm of the Nile

An ordinary Egyptian's day began before dawn. A **farmer** rose with the first light, ate flatbread and raw onions, drank a cup of thick beer, and went to the fields — ploughing behind an ox, sowing, weeding, irrigating by shaduf. He came home at midday for a meal and a rest in the worst heat (the *siesta* of the ancient Mediterranean), returned to the fields until sunset, then bathed at the river, ate an evening meal with his family, and slept on a raised sleeping platform or on the flat roof in summer.

A **craftsman in the royal workshop of Deir el-Medina** (the artisans' village whose records are our richest source of ordinary life) kept a **ten-day week** — eight days of work, two days off. He left his house at dawn, walked to the Valley of the Kings to work on tombs, slept overnight in bunkhouses, returned home for the weekend. His wife kept the household, ground grain, baked bread, brewed beer, wove linen, raised children, tended the household shrines. Children played with dolls, balls, spinning tops, model animals; boys wrestled, girls danced.

A **noblewoman's day** began with an elaborate toilette — bathing in the household pool, being massaged with perfumed oils, having her wig dressed and cosmetics applied by a handmaid, then dressing in pleated linen. She might supervise the estate's kitchens and weaving rooms, attend to correspondence, receive guests, pray at the household shrine of Bes and Tawaret, oversee her children's nursery, visit the temple for a festival, attend a banquet in the evening with music and dancers.

A **priest's day** began in the middle of the night if he was to perform the dawn ritual — he would bathe in the sacred lake, shave, don fresh linen, process into the temple, perform the awakening, then return to his duties in the House of Life (copying, teaching, consulting) until the next ritual hour. Priests rotated service in **phyles** (watches) — four weeks on, four weeks off, returning to their private households during off-duty weeks.

### Hygiene and Personal Care

Egyptians were **obsessive about cleanliness.** Daily bathing was standard; priests bathed two to four times a day. Teeth were cleaned with fibre-brushes and salt or natron. Hair was oiled, combed, shaved and wigged. Perfumed cones of fat were worn on the head at feasts. Cosmetics — kohl, red ochre, malachite — were applied daily. Natron (a mineral salt) was used for everything: bathing, tooth-cleaning, food preservation, mummification, textile treatment.

### Sleep and Homes

Egyptians slept on **raised beds** with woven-rope or leather webbing, headrests of wood or stone instead of pillows (cool in heat, preserves hairstyle). The wealthy had mattresses stuffed with wool or straw. Oil lamps provided evening light — small clay dishes with a wick of plant fibre floating in olive oil or castor oil. Roofs served as second living rooms in the cool of evening.

### Applying This to Aurum — Well-Oiled Days

An ordinary Aurum citizen's day is, on the surface, idyllic.

**A middle-class clerk of the third ring** wakes at dawn to the chime of the city's clock-tower. He washes in a brass-fitted bathroom with hot water piped from a city cistern. He dresses in a neat pleated linen shirt, trousers, and a light wool coat (wool is now permitted for commoners). Breakfast: wheat porridge with honey, tea, a boiled egg. He walks or takes the clockwork tram to the Archival's Bureau of Records, where he spends the day copying and filing documents. Lunch: a pastry from a bakery window, eaten in a public garden. The afternoon's work. A walk home through a sunlit promenade lined with flowering trees. An evening meal with his wife and two children — vegetable stew, bread, a glass of wine. An hour of reading or of the serial novels. Bed by ten.

**A priestly-caste Archivist's day** begins at three in the morning with the cold bath and the shaving. White linen. The walk through the Archival's hush to the dawn ritual — the recitation, the opening of the inner door, the waking of the Radiant Ones (a carefully choreographed ceremony that is, all unknowing to the young officiant, a distraction from the real ritual happening in the Sanctum of the Pillar). Then breakfast in the refectory — plain porridge, tea. The day's work: cataloguing, transcribing, consulting foreign texts, teaching novices. Evening meal. Bed by eight.

**A country farmer's day, below the fog:** wake at dawn to the rooster, flatbread and weak beer (the country below still drinks beer; it is a tradition), work in the fields, midday rest, afternoon fields, evening meal of stew and bread, bed by dark. He looks up occasionally at the shadows overhead where the cities hang. He does not think too closely about what he is seeing. He remembers that his neighbour's daughter was "elevated" to the Capital three years ago — an honour. The family mentions her sometimes. He thinks he remembers her laugh. He cannot remember her name. The fog in the morning smells of dew and something metallic; he has stopped noticing.

**Mínhuì's day:** she rises before dawn. She walks the inner Archival in the dark, feeds the tree's soil a measure of water and finely ground silver dust, lights a single stick of incense at its base. She speaks to Fèngzhé's fragment in the old tongue. She returns to her quarters. She catalogues scrolls all day with quiet efficiency. She takes tea at four. She reads. She attends an occasional evening lecture or banquet as the eccentric scholarly cousin of an old Aurum family (a cover identity she has refreshed three times over sixteen generations). At night she returns to her books. She does not sleep much. She never has. **A thousand years is a long day.**

## 18. MYTHOLOGY AND LEGEND

### Egyptian Mythology Beyond Formal Religion

Beyond the temple cult existed a dense world of **stories, legends, and domestic religion.** Popular gods like **Bes** (a dwarf with a lion's mane, protector of households, women in childbirth, and sleeping children) and **Tawaret** (a hippo-crocodile goddess of pregnancy) had no great temples but were the dearest friends of ordinary families. Household shrines held figurines of them.

**Foundational mythic stories:**
- **The Osiris myth** — murdered by his brother Set, dismembered and scattered, gathered and revived by his sister-wife Isis, the first mummy, the judge of the dead. The core Egyptian story of death, loyalty, return.
- **The contendings of Horus and Set** — the son of Osiris claiming the throne against his uncle. A long, often comic, sometimes obscene story told in many versions.
- **The Book of the Heavenly Cow** — how humanity nearly provoked Ra into destroying us all, and how Sekhmet was sent to slaughter humanity but was tricked by beer dyed red and drunk into unconsciousness. Hathor was reborn from her.
- **The Destruction of the Enemies of Ra** — Apep (Apophis), the great serpent of chaos, every night attacks the sun-barque of Ra; every night Set (here a hero) defeats him; every morning the sun rises.
- **The Tale of Setne Khamwas and the Book of Thoth** — the prince who stole a god's book and suffered for it.

**Monsters and creatures:**
- **Ammit, the Devourer** — the hybrid lion-hippo-crocodile who eats the hearts of those who fail the judgment.
- **Apep/Apophis** — the chaos serpent.
- **Demons** — shedu, utukku, visitors in dreams, all warded by amulets and spells.
- **Souls (ka, ba, akh)** — the Egyptian person was composite; each part had its own destiny after death.

### Atlantis in the Cultural Imagination

Plato's Atlantis did not stay in Plato. It became, over two and a half millennia, one of the most powerful myths of Western culture:
- **Francis Bacon's *New Atlantis* (1626)** — a hidden civilisation called Bensalem ruled by "Salomon's House," a secret society of scientist-sages. Bensalem preserves its existence as a secret to protect itself; only approved delegates leave, disguised among the nations.
- **Ignatius Donnelly's *Atlantis: The Antediluvian World* (1882)** — the founding pseudoarchaeological text. Donnelly argued Atlantis was historically real, that all world civilisations descended from it, and that it was destroyed in a great flood. Wildly influential; scientifically baseless.
- **H.P. Blavatsky's Theosophy (1888)** — Blavatsky built on Donnelly to posit "root races," including an **Atlantean root race** of advanced psychic humans who preceded us. Theosophy mainlined the "advanced hidden ancestor civilisation" trope into 20th-century occultism, early science fiction, and New Age movements.
- **Disney's *Atlantis: The Lost Empire* (2001), *Stargate Atlantis* (2004–2009), numerous novels and films** — Atlantis as the prototype of "lost hyperadvanced precursor civilisation" in all modern fantasy and sci-fi.

### Applying This to Aurum — The Founding Myth and the Shadow Stories

Aurum's **official founding myth** is recited at every Ascension festival: *In the first age the world was chaos. The Radiant Ones descended from the heavens and gathered the worthy onto golden disks, and the unworthy were left below. The Sovereign was anointed as the Radiant Ones' voice on earth. The Archival was raised to preserve the sacred knowledge. The sky cities rose, and ma'at was restored. Since then we have held the heavens stable. Praise the Radiant Ones.* Every citizen learns this in school. It is painted on temple murals. It is embroidered on formal banners. It is, of course, a lie.

**The real story:** sixteen generations ago, a group of priests discovered how to use the paulownia tree's living connection to the hidden god Mínhuì to generate magical lift. They lifted their first city as a "gift to the worthy." To keep it aloft they needed an ongoing supply — and they invented the harvesting system, and they invented the fog, and they invented the Radiant Ones. Mínhuì, who had been hiding in plain sight as a quiet caretaker of her brother's tree, watched it happen. She did not act. She has not acted for sixteen generations. (Why? She is the goddess of memory and waiting. She is waiting for the right child to arrive.)

**Popular mythology** — the stories ordinary Aurum citizens actually tell each other:
- **"The Gold-Smith of Solthys"** — a parable about a clockmaker who tried to build a heart for his dead daughter and was punished by the gods for overreaching. Teaches the limits of human craft.
- **"The Veiled One's Mirror"** — a ghost story about a servant who looked into a mirror in the Archival and saw her own face disappearing. Used to frighten children away from asking too much.
- **"The Fog-Maiden"** — an urban legend about a woman in the morning fog who speaks the names of the forgotten. Dismissed as drunken superstition. Some believe her. Some old servants leave offerings at street-corner shrines.
- **"The Silver Library"** — the whispered legend that somewhere in the Archival is a library that shows you the history you have forgotten. Scholars scoff. Children dream.

**Monsters and forbidden creatures:**
- **The Gutter-Fog creatures** — said to walk in the mist at dawn, tall and pale, looking for their children. Civic authority denies their existence. Guards patrol the lower ring at dawn anyway.
- **The Black Page** — in the Archival, a scroll that reads differently each time; forbidden even to Archivists. (It is one of Mínhuì's, and it is real.)
- **The Hollow Mother** — a folk figure, an older woman with no face who walks the Long Night and touches children who were to be harvested the next day, leaving them sick. The sick children cannot be "elevated" — illness disqualifies them. The priests hate her. Citizens love her. **She is Mínhuì, on the one night a year she permits herself to act directly.**

The myths of Aurum are the cracks in the façade. Every culture has stories that refuse the official version. Aurum's are slowly, patiently, gathering.

## 19. TABOOS, LAWS, AND ETHICS

### Egyptian Ma'at — The Moral Order

Egyptian ethics organised themselves around a single concept: **ma'at.** It meant simultaneously **truth, justice, cosmic order, balance, and right action.** It was personified as a goddess (the daughter of Ra, wearing a single ostrich feather) and embodied in every legal, religious, and personal ethical decision. The pharaoh's core duty was to **uphold ma'at** against **isfet (chaos, falsehood, disorder).**

The **Negative Confession** from the Book of the Dead preserves the ethical code explicitly. When the deceased stood before the forty-two divine judges in the Hall of Two Truths, they had to declare 42 sins they had NOT committed. Each is a window into Egyptian ethics:
- "I have not stolen."
- "I have not killed men."
- "I have not caused the shedding of tears."
- "I have not taken milk from the mouths of children."
- "I have not stopped flowing water."
- "I have not committed deceit."
- "I have not polluted myself."
- "I have not cursed a god."
- "I have not stolen bread."
- "I have not taken the property of orphans."

The heart was then weighed against the feather of ma'at. If the heart was heavier (burdened with guilt), Ammit devoured it. If it balanced, the deceased joined Osiris.

### Hospitality, Oaths, and the Sacred Word

Hospitality was a moral imperative. To feed a traveller was sacred. Oaths were often sworn **"by the life of Pharaoh"** or **"by the life of the gods"**; breaking them brought divine retribution. The spoken word had power — the right words in the right order could heal, wound, bind, free. This was **heka** — magic as an aspect of language itself. False oaths, slander, and lies were ethical abominations because they corrupted the word.

### Taboos

- **Ritual impurity** from corpses (embalmers were paradoxically revered and avoided), menstruation (women sometimes withdrew to "menstrual houses"), eating forbidden foods (fish, onions during priestly service), sexual activity before certain rites.
- **Speaking ill of the pharaoh** — treason as well as sacrilege.
- **Damaging the temple, the tomb, the sacred image.** Tomb-robbing was punished by impalement; the ka of the deceased would take revenge.
- **Disrupting the Nile.** Polluting water sources, damming flow, killing fish out of season — all variously discouraged or forbidden.

### Applying This to Aurum — Refinement as Ethic

Aurum's **official ethical code** is called the **Codex of Solumen** (the Codex of the Light). It is taught in every school. Its central virtues:

1. **Decorum** — the first virtue. Self-possession, grace, restraint. The body is mastered; the voice is measured; the temper is sheathed.
2. **Learning** — knowledge honours the Radiant Ones. An educated citizen is a pious citizen.
3. **Service** — to one's family, one's caste, one's city. The good citizen fulfils their place.
4. **Loyalty** — to the Sovereign, the Ten Houses, and the priestly caste, in that order.
5. **Honour of the dead** — the tombs are tended, the names are remembered (the official names).
6. **Charity** — to the needy of one's own community. Wealthy families fund infirmaries, schools, gardens.
7. **Silence** — about what is not yours to discuss. Scandal, gossip, rumour are vulgar. Certain topics are simply not raised in polite conversation.

The seventh virtue is the trick. **"Silence about what is not yours to discuss"** is the ethical formulation of the fog. Well-bred Aurum citizens simply do not talk about the harvesting, the country below, the true purpose of the Archival, the disappearances. Curiosity about these things is the opposite of refinement — it is gauche, low-born, impolite. **A cultural taboo that polices the civilisation's central crime.**

**Oaths and sacred promises:** oaths in Aurum are formal, witnessed, written. A priestly-caste oath of service is irrevocable — **to leave the priesthood is treason against the Radiant Ones.** The **Oath of the Archival** is the deepest vow any citizen can take; it binds one to the Archival for life, and is made over a ceremonial cut drawn by the Veiled Advisor's own hand. The blood goes into a small vial. The vial goes into a vault. Nobody breaks an Oath of the Archival because the vial reacts to perjury. (A half-magical half-bureaucratic leash.)

**The deep cultural wrongs:**
- **Practising magic** — the highest taboo. Any citizen demonstrating magical ability is considered unclean, dangerous, in need of rescue by the priesthood. Families who hide a magical child commit a crime punishable by exile of the entire family to the country below.
- **Speaking the forbidden names** — the true names of certain gods, the old name of the continent (Avalúne), the name Mínhuì — are not officially forbidden but are simply *not known.* The Archival has scrubbed them from every printed text over sixteen generations. Very old servants sometimes mutter them. Children sometimes hear them in dreams and forget.
- **Returning from the country below without papers** — a severe legal offence. The fog is the wall.
- **Assault on a priest of the Archival** — punishable by death. Assault on the Veiled Advisor is unthinkable.
- **Damaging a printed book** — a serious misdemeanour. Aurum sanctifies the written word.

**Honour and shame:** shame in Aurum is primarily social — to be seen as lacking decorum, to be ridiculed in aristocratic circles, to lose one's place in the subtle ranking of the Ten Houses. Honour is public recognition of refinement, learning, and service. **A deeply shame-conscious culture, where a citizen's face among their peers is their most valuable asset.** This is why the ultimate threat the regime holds over any dissenter is not imprisonment but **exposure** — to be publicly declared a "contaminated soul" is the worst fate in the sky.

## 20. THE FEELING OF THE CULTURE

### The First Impression

A traveller arriving at Aurum by airship in the early morning — let us say from Tyramare, bringing news and trade — crosses over the open sea, and then suddenly the Capital is ahead and **it is impossible not to gasp.** Five concentric rings of golden stone and brass and glass, domed with emerald and sapphire inlays, haloed in a perpetual light haze of gilded mist, floating in defiance of every natural law. Smaller sky cities hang in the middle distance like moons. Skyways thread between them. Airships of every size move in disciplined lanes along marked corridors. The **scale** is the first thing. The **beauty** is the second. The **silence** is the third: from a mile away there is no city-noise, only the creak of ropes and the murmur of wind in the gilded spires.

### Smells

- **Incense** — always, everywhere. Cinnamon, saffron, myrrh, sandalwood, lotus. Temples burn it, private homes burn it, shops burn it at their doorways.
- **Perfume** — heavy, floral, elaborate. Citizens of the upper rings bathe in it. Jasmine, rose, orchid, rare animal essences.
- **Brass polish** — a warm metallic tang from the daily care of every fitting.
- **Clean linen** — the smell of well-bleached fresh cloth, drying on racks in every aristocratic laundry.
- **Cooking** — bakery steam, saffron rice, tea, honey, citrus peel, fresh bread, roasted meat. Aurum eats well and you can smell it.
- **Oil and steam** — from clockwork elevators and the tram networks.
- **Something faintly metallic under it all** — the fog. Citizens have stopped noticing. Visitors from other lands sometimes feel a slight headache after a few days and do not know why.

### Sounds

- **The clocks.** Every public square has a clock-tower. Every hour rings out, chimes cascading across the rings in a wave.
- **Wind through the skyways** — a constant background hum that changes pitch as the cables flex.
- **Airship engines** — distant muted thunder during the day, a low purr at night.
- **Pneumatic tube cannisters** — the soft *whoosh-thunk* of messages arriving.
- **Fountains and ornamental water** — the priestly caste's temple precincts are full of gentle running water.
- **Music** — public musicians in every garden, violinists, harpists, small clockwork orchestras. The city sings.
- **The occasional cry of a caged paradise bird** in an aristocratic conservatory.
- **Conversation in the refined cadences of Aurite** — quiet, precise, musical.
- **The silence of certain streets at certain hours** — particularly around the Archival, where a hush falls naturally.

### Sights

- **Gold and silver and brass** — everywhere. The colour palette is warm metal, soft stone, deep blue, emerald green, ivory white, dove grey. Very little red. Very little black.
- **Stained glass** — every window, every lamp, every dome. The whole Capital glows in coloured light at sunrise and sunset.
- **Clockwork in motion** — everywhere you look, something is ticking, rotating, calibrating.
- **Elaborate wigs and veiled faces** — the aristocrats on their morning walks, cosmetic perfection.
- **Robes in carefully coded colours** — you can read a person's caste at a glance, if you are Auran.
- **The paulownia leaves** — gold in autumn, silver-green in spring — printed on every official banner, stitched into every state uniform. Nobody thinks about where it is.
- **The fog at dawn** — a faint golden shimmer in the air, burning off by mid-morning. Beautiful. Nobody comments on why it tastes slightly metallic.

### The Ambient Mood

**Aurum feels like a civilisation holding its breath.** There is pride — vast, quiet, absolute — the pride of a people who believe they are objectively better than everyone else. There is refinement — the culture spends enormous energy on etiquette, beauty, precision. There is calm — the streets are clean, the citizens are polite, the trams run on time, the temples glow. There is also, under all of it, a tension nobody names — a sensation you get around the innermost ring, near the Archival, at certain hours, when the light slants a particular way and you feel, for half a breath, that something is waiting inside.

**What outsiders know Aurum for:**
- The most exquisite clockwork in the world.
- Airships.
- The best medicine.
- The finest silks.
- The most refined manners.
- The Archival — the greatest library in Avalúne, if you can get access.
- An attitude that makes even kings feel like pupils.
- The peculiar fact that **nobody ever immigrates away from Aurum.** Citizens visit other lands, trade with them, admire them at arm's length — and always return. Once you have lived in the sky, the ground is hard to want.

**What outsiders do NOT know:**
- That the cities float on blood.
- That the paulownia tree in the Archival is older than the civilisation itself.
- That a quiet noblewoman with a catalogue has been watching for a thousand years, waiting for the right arrival.

**Aurum feels like a beautiful dream one is not sure one is dreaming.** It feels like a library hushing you. It feels like being admired by a stranger in the most expensive room you have ever entered. It feels like cold clean metal and warm golden light and an incense that is almost, but not quite, covering the scent of something older underneath. **The feeling of Aurum is the feeling of a civilisation that has been asked a question and has spent sixteen generations not answering it.** Somewhere — someday — a sunshine healer with the old blood will walk into the Archival, and the tree will lean toward him, and Mínhuì will look up from her catalogue, and the held breath will finally be let out.
