## 1. THE LAND AND GEOGRAPHY

**Key fact for worldbuilding: Byzantium and Venice were both built on the edge between land and sea — and they both turned that edge into power.** Constantinople sat on a triangle of land between the Golden Horn, the Sea of Marmara, and the Bosporus, guarding the only passage between the Mediterranean and the Black Sea. Venice sat on 118 small islands in a saltwater lagoon, connected to the mainland only by causeway and ship. Both cities lived by the water and because of it. Both ate from the sea, warred on it, got rich on it, and feared it (plague came by ship, invaders came by ship, but so did spice and silk and silver).

### The Byzantine landscape
- **Constantinople (Byzantium, the City)** — built on seven hills in imitation of Rome, surrounded on three sides by water. The **Theodosian Walls** (built by Emperor Theodosius II, early 5th century) ringed the land side with a triple defence: moat, outer wall, massive inner wall. They held for over a thousand years until gunpowder broke them in 1453. The sea walls along the Golden Horn and the Marmara were famous for being walkable — the water itself was the second defence.
- **The Bosporus** — the narrow salt strait that separates Europe from Asia. Fast currents, cold water, fish runs (mackerel and tuna in season, the catch that fed the city). Every ship crossing between the Mediterranean and the Black Sea passed through it, and every ship paid a toll.
- **The imperial hinterland** — Anatolia (modern Turkey): highlands, olive groves, vineyards, wheat plains. Thrace: rolling farmland and forest. The Aegean islands: fishing, salt, cedar.
- **Sacred geography** — the Hagia Sophia at the centre, the imperial palace complex beside it, the forum of Constantine with its great porphyry column, monasteries scattered across the hills, and far beyond the city: **Mount Athos**, the "Garden of the Virgin" — a mountainous peninsula in northern Greece reserved for monks, where no woman has set foot for over a thousand years.

### The Venetian landscape
- **The Lagoon** — a shallow enclosed body of brackish water, 550 square kilometres, separated from the Adriatic by thin barrier islands (the *lidi*). The lagoon was a living thing — tides washed through it twice a day, currents shifted sandbars, and fever rose from it in summer.
- **The 118 islands** — some solid ground, most reclaimed from mud by driving wooden pilings (alder, oak, larch) deep into the clay. Venice is a city literally built on a forest stood upside down in the water. Those piles are still there under every palace.
- **The Grand Canal** — a reversed S-shaped waterway that carves the city in half. Four bridges span it, most famously the **Rialto** (stone bridge completed 1591 by Antonio da Ponte, replacing a series of collapsing wooden predecessors going back to 1181).
- **Florence's landscape** — utterly different. Inland, Tuscan, built on the Arno river. Rolling hills of olive and vine. The Apennines behind, the sea hours away. Florence grew by banking and wool, not by sea trade — a reminder that "Italian city-state" was never one thing.
- **Sacred geography** — every island had its church. San Marco on the main island was the political heart, rebuilt and bejewelled after the Fourth Crusade. Torcello was the oldest settlement, its basilica older than Venice itself. Murano was glass. Burano was lace. San Michele became the cemetery island.

### Climate and weather
- **Constantinople** — mild, maritime, with hot dry summers and wet mild winters. Fog rolled off the Bosporus. Snow fell in winter but rarely lingered.
- **Venice** — humid and unstable. Spring *acqua alta* tides flooded the Piazza San Marco, sometimes for days. Summer heat bred mosquitoes, malaria, plague. Winter fog thick enough to hide a gondola ten feet from its prow. The famous **scirocco** — a hot dry wind from the Sahara — would arrive and hang over the city for days, sapping the will.
- The sea gave everything and could take it back. Both cities understood this in their bones.

### What this means for Tyramare
Tyramare is a **city of islands and canals**, built where river meets sea, surrounded by water on every side. It has a landward wall (inherited, half-forgotten, never breached in living memory) and sea walls that are walkable and partially ceremonial. It has a Grand Canal. It has a lagoon where the water is brackish and the light is pearl-coloured. It has districts that are whole islands. It has bridges — stone, wood, rope, spite. It has tides that flood the lowest plazas twice a year and nobody questions it; they just raise the furniture. The marble under the foundations is green with water-stain. The wooden piles have been there so long nobody remembers who drove them. Fog is a character. Salt is a character. The sea gives, the sea takes, the sea watches.

## 2. SOCIAL STRUCTURE

**Key fact for worldbuilding: both Byzantium and Venice built societies where birth mattered desperately but was never quite enough.** You could be emperor with a peasant father (Justin I, a swineherd). You could be empress with an actress mother (Theodora, who rose from the hippodrome). You could be a doge with a sewn-down family name. But you could ALSO be born into the purple and die in a monastery, erased by a rival faction. The top was reachable and the floor could open beneath you. Below the ceremonial class were ranks of middle people — craftspeople, merchants, scribes, eunuchs, lawyers, guildsmen — who actually made the city run.

### The Byzantine hierarchy (c. 9th–12th century)
1. **The Emperor and the imperial family** — the *basileus* (ruler) was God's viceroy on earth. The title "Porphyrogennētos" ("born in the purple") was reserved for children born in the **Porphyra Chamber** of the imperial palace during their father's reign, their purest possible claim. Anna Komnene, the historian of the *Alexiad*, used the title all her life.
2. **The Great Officers and the court** — the *prōtosebastos*, the *sebastokrator*, the *caesar*, the *logothete tou dromou* (foreign minister), the *megas doux* (grand admiral). An elaborate ladder of 60+ ranks, each with precise colours, insignia, and access to the emperor.
3. **The Eunuchs** — a parallel hierarchy running beside the secular one. Castrated boys, often from the provinces, rose to extraordinary power as chamberlains (*koubikoularioi*), treasurers (*sakellarios*), and patriarchs. By the 10th century Byzantine writers compared the eunuchs around the throne to the choir of angels around God — beings outside ordinary time and sex. The eunuch Narses commanded armies. The eunuch Joseph Bringas ran the empire.
4. **The Patriarch, metropolitans, and high clergy** — the Patriarch of Constantinople stood beside the emperor. Bishops ran cities. Monks ran consciences.
5. **The senatorial aristocracy** — landowning families (the Phokas, the Doukas, the Komnenos, the Palaiologos). Their estates fed Constantinople and their sons commanded its armies.
6. **The middle class** — merchants, silk weavers, bakers, scribes, lawyers, tax collectors, provincial officials. Organised into guilds (*systemata*) under the Prefect of the City.
7. **The common people (the *demos*)** — artisans, fishermen, dock workers, sailors, hippodrome fans. Organised politically as the Blue and Green factions, able to riot a city to ashes if they chose (the Nika riot of 532).
8. **Slaves** — household slaves, agricultural slaves, domestic labour. Byzantine slavery was real but fluid; slaves could buy freedom, and manumission was considered a pious act. By the later empire, slave agriculture had mostly given way to the *paroikoi* (tied peasants).

### The Venetian hierarchy (after the Serrata)
The defining moment: **the *Serrata* of 28 February 1297**. The Great Council was formally "locked" — membership restricted to families already enrolled. In 1506 and 1526 two great registers were formalised: the **Libro d'Oro** ("Golden Book") of noble births and marriages, and the **Libro d'Argento** ("Silver Book") of legitimate-citizen births. If your name was in the Gold, you were patrician and could sit on the councils. If your name was in the Silver, you were a *cittadino originario* — full citizen, eligible for high administrative office, but not for political power. Everyone else was *popolo* — the populace.

1. **The Patriciate (*nobili*)** — about 1,200 adult men at the height, drawn from roughly 150 families. Every patrician man was automatically a member of the Great Council at 25.
2. **The Cittadini Originari** — a hereditary middle class of lawyers, notaries, ducal secretaries, the Grand Chancellor. A bureaucratic caste with real influence and wealth but no vote.
3. **The Guild members (*arti*)** — divided (in Florence more rigidly than Venice) into *arti maggiori* (bankers, wool, silk, judges, physicians, furriers) and *arti minori* (smiths, bakers, tanners, shoemakers).
4. **Workers and labourers** — the *popolo minuto*. The Arsenalotti (shipwrights of the Arsenale) were a category of their own — feared, organised, the doge's personal guard on ceremonial occasions.
5. **Courtesans** — a distinct social class with its own stratifications (see §7 and §17). The *cortigiane oneste* were educated, literary, moved at the highest levels. The *meretrici* were streetwalkers.
6. **Foreigners** — the *Fondaco dei Tedeschi* housed German merchants, the *Fondaco dei Turchi* the Turks; ghettos for Jews were formalised in 1516. Venice was cosmopolitan by necessity and xenophobic by habit.

### What this means for Tyramare
A **two-book aristocracy** (Serina's Marble Quarter matriarchy can sit inside this — the Golden Book where women's names anchor the inheritance). An **empty throne** at the top (the Bannric) echoes the vacuum that an absent emperor leaves; in both Byzantium after 1204 and Venice always, real power sat with councils and guilds rather than a crown. **Eunuchs** as a parallel power structure are too good to lose — Tyramare could have a priestly-bureaucratic order of altered servants who manage everything and belong to no family. **Courtesans as an educated class with political influence** is a lived historical truth, not a fantasy flourish. **The Shadow Cult (Umbrathi) and Verenthae** can live easily in a stratified guild city because every Byzantine and Italian republic had secret societies beneath the official order.

## 3. GOVERNMENT AND POLITICS

**Key fact for worldbuilding: Byzantium was an autocracy with a divine emperor, and Venice was a republic without a king.** Both looked like the other on the surface — the Doge wore imperial robes, the emperor sat on councils — but the bones were opposite. Byzantium's power ran top-down through God. Venice's power ran sideways through committees designed so that no single family could ever seize the city. Between them lies a beautiful tension: the *performance* of monarchy with the *substance* of oligarchy. That is Tyramare.

### Byzantine government — the emperor as God's reflection

The **basileus** was God's viceroy. Court ritual turned every audience into a theology lesson. Ambassadors were lifted bodily on mechanical gold lions, automatons that roared; gilded birds in a jewelled tree sang as the emperor's throne rose to the ceiling on hidden gears (the **Throne of Solomon**, Liutprand of Cremona saw it in 949 CE). Nobles waited for days in the antechambers of Empress Theodora's apartments, standing on tiptoe to catch a eunuch's eye, before being admitted to kiss her golden sandals.

Key offices:
- **The Senate** — advisory, honorific, ornamental by the middle period.
- **The Prefect of the City (*eparchos*)** — ran Constantinople itself. Markets, weights, measures, guild inspections.
- **The Logothetes** — the "accountants" who ran the bureaucracy. *Logothetes tou dromou* handled foreign policy and the postal/spy service. *Logothetes tou genikou* was treasurer.
- **The Quaestor** — chief legal officer. Tribonian, under Justinian, was the architect of the *Corpus Juris Civilis*.
- **The *Themes*** — military provinces. Each theme had its own *strategos* (general-governor) with combined civil and military authority.

**Law: the *Corpus Juris Civilis* (528–534 CE).** Justinian's commission under Tribonian produced the **Codex** (imperial edicts), the **Digest** (jurists' writings, three million words boiled to one million), the **Institutes** (a textbook for students), and the **Novels** (Justinian's own new legislation, often in Greek). This became the basis of Byzantine law for 900 years and the root of the civil-law tradition across modern Europe. Later codifications — the **Ecloga** (740, Leo III), the **Basilika** (late 9th century, Leo the Wise) — translated and simplified it into Greek.

**Succession was chaos.** There was no strict primogeniture. Emperors were made by acclamation, proclamation, army coup, palace conspiracy, marriage, co-emperorship, or usurpation. Eyes were put out. Noses were slit (Justinian II returned to the throne with a golden nose). Monastic tonsure was a common soft exile — you couldn't rule if you had taken vows.

### Venetian government — the republic of no-one

The genius of Venice was machinery designed to prevent tyranny. Every office was short. Every appointment was elected. Every major decision was checked by a committee that watched another committee.

- **The Doge (*Dòxe*)** — the ceremonial head, elected for life. Wore the golden *corno ducale* and *bucintoro* robes. Forbidden by his *promissione* (oath of office) to open his own correspondence without witnesses, to hold private audience with foreign ambassadors, to own land outside Venice, or to let his sons marry without Council approval. He was the republic wearing a face.
- **The Doge's Election** — ten stages of lot and vote, designed to be uncorruptible. A boy was snatched from the piazza of San Marco (the *ballotino*) to draw the lots. Thirty members of the Great Council chosen by lot, reduced to nine, then those nine chose forty, reduced to twelve, who chose twenty-five, reduced to nine, who chose forty-five, reduced to eleven, who chose forty-one, who elected the doge — requiring at least 25 votes of 41. The process took days and no faction could game it all the way through.
- **The Great Council (*Maggior Consiglio*)** — the assembly of all patrician men over 25 (after the Serrata). Up to 2,500 members.
- **The Senate (*Pregadi*, "invited ones")** — 120-300 members drawn from the Great Council. Foreign policy, trade, finance.
- **The Signoria** — the inner cabinet: the Doge plus six *consiglieri ducali* (one from each sestiere) plus three chiefs of the *Quarantia*. This group actually ran the republic day-to-day.
- **The Council of Ten (*Consiglio dei Dieci*)** — created 1310 after the Tiepolo conspiracy, made permanent in 1455. Ten elected patricians plus the Doge and his Signoria sitting *ex officio*. Its jurisdiction: state security, treason, conspiracy. Its tool: the Venetian intelligence service — ambassadors who were spies by design, an archive of cipher codes, informants in every port, the bocche di leone (lion's-mouth letterboxes) scattered across the city where citizens could slip in signed denunciations. The State Inquisitors (three, drawn from the Ten) were the state's secret police.

**Florence's parallel:** the city was run by guild representation — the *Signoria* was elected from the guilds via the *tratta* (names drawn from leather bags, *borse*). The Medici under Cosimo the Elder (ruled 1434–1464) and Lorenzo the Magnificent (1469–1492) mastered this system without ever formally seizing it — they influenced who went into the bags.

### What this means for Tyramare
The **empty Bannric throne** is the perfect fusion: Byzantine in appearance (a divine monarchy whose crown still matters), Venetian in practice (the council runs the city and treats the throne as furniture). The **seven guild districts** map beautifully onto both the *sestieri* of Venice and the *themes* of Byzantium. A **Council of Ten-equivalent** running Tyramare's intelligence service is doctrinal — Serina's Umbrathi/Verenthae can flourish in its shadow. **Lion's mouth letterboxes** are an unbeatable piece of texture: a carved mouth in a wall where anyone can post a denunciation against any official, and somewhere across the city, a committee reads every slip. The **complex election machinery** of the doge gives Tyramare a ritual, a superstition (the child drawing lots), and a way for fate to play a hand.

## 4. RELIGION AND SPIRITUALITY

**Key fact for worldbuilding: in Byzantium the emperor and the Patriarch co-ruled, and the line between church and state was deliberately indistinct.** Emperor Justinian declared himself the defender of the faith; the Patriarch of Constantinople crowned the emperor in the Hagia Sophia; church councils were called by imperial summons; and every political crisis was also a theological one. Italian Catholic culture, by contrast, lived under an imported Pope far away in Rome — and therefore spent huge energy negotiating local religious authority (confraternities, patron saints, guild churches, family chapels). Both produced spectacular sacred beauty. Both had the same underlying question: who speaks for God in this city, and who answers for what He permits?

### Orthodox Christianity — the Byzantine core
- **The Patriarch of Constantinople** — one of five original patriarchs (Rome, Constantinople, Alexandria, Antioch, Jerusalem). After the Great Schism of 1054, effectively the senior voice of Eastern Christendom. The Patriarch crowned the emperor and could occasionally excommunicate him.
- **The Metropolitans** — archbishops of major cities. Below them, bishops; below them, priests; below them, deacons. Priests could marry before ordination; bishops had to be celibate monks.
- **Monks and the monastic life** — monasteries were the backbone of Byzantine spiritual life. **Mount Athos** (consecrated 972 CE as a monastic republic) housed thousands of monks across a dozen great monasteries and sketes; no woman has set foot on the peninsula for over a thousand years. Monks were the fiercest defenders of icons during the Iconoclast Controversy.
- **The theology of the icon** — Orthodox belief: an icon is not an idol but a **window**. The image of a saint is where the saint's presence can be reached. Prayer passes through the icon to the person it represents. This was radical enough to start a civil war.

### The Iconoclast Controversy (726–843 CE)
Emperor Leo III banned the veneration of icons in 726, possibly influenced by Islamic iconoclasm from the eastern border. For over a century the empire tore itself apart: **Iconoclasts** (the "icon-breakers") ripped down paintings and smashed mosaics; **Iconodules** (the "icon-servants") hid images in basements and paid with their lives. Three great empresses — **Irene** (regent 780–790, ruled alone 797–802, as *basileus* not *basilissa*), **Theodora the wife of Theophilos**, and the later restorers — reversed the policy. The **Triumph of Orthodoxy** in 843 CE finally restored icons forever and is still celebrated annually as a feast day.

### The Great Schism (1054 CE)
Patriarch Michael Cerularius of Constantinople and Cardinal Humbert, legate of Pope Leo IX, excommunicated each other over technical issues: the *filioque* clause in the Creed, the use of unleavened bread in the eucharist, clerical celibacy, and papal primacy. The excommunications were mutual and the split never healed. Every mosaic in every Byzantine church afterwards carried this wound: you were the true church; Rome was the heretic.

### Italian Catholicism — saints, confraternities, patronage
- **Patron saints** — every city, every guild, every confraternity had a saint. Venice was St Mark (stolen from Alexandria in 828, his body smuggled in a barrel of pork so the Muslim customs officials wouldn't inspect it). Florence was St John the Baptist. The bones of saints drove pilgrim economies.
- **Confraternities (*scuole*, *compagnie*)** — lay religious associations that functioned as mutual aid societies, funeral clubs, charity distributors, and political patronage networks. In Venice the *Scuole Grandi* were enormous, owning whole palaces, commissioning Tintorettos.
- **The plague and the saints** — St Sebastian and St Roch were the plague saints. Venice built churches to them — the **Redentore** (1577) and the **Salute** (1687) — after plague years, by solemn vow of the Senate, still processed to every year.
- **Relics and the economy of the holy** — relics were power. The bronze horses on San Marco were looted from Constantinople's Hippodrome in 1204; so was the **Pala d'Oro**, so was every major relic in St Mark's Treasury. Venice rebuilt itself as "the second Constantinople" on stolen sanctity.

### Magic, heterodoxy, and the shadow
- **Folk religion** ran parallel to orthodoxy everywhere. Venetian fishermen carved protective marks on their boats. Florentine women tied amulets to their newborns. Spells for love, ill luck, and recovery were sold in the markets despite periodic purges.
- **Heretical sects** — the Bogomils (dualist Christians, who taught that the material world was the devil's creation) spread through the Balkans from the 10th century and crossed into Italy as the Cathars/Patarines. Rome called a crusade against them. Constantinople burned their leaders.
- **Court astrology and alchemy** — entirely accepted at the highest level. Michael Scot and his successors worked for Frederick II; the Medici employed their own astrologers; Byzantine emperors consulted them before campaigns.

### The Solenthera framework for Tyramare
Serina's **Solenthera faith** can sit perfectly inside this research: an official church that looks Byzantine (patriarchy, icons, incense, hymns), a parallel network of lay confraternities (the *scuole*, perhaps under Marble Quarter matriarchs), and below them, heretical currents (the **Umbrathi** as Tyramare's Bogomils — dualists who say the bright world is the lie) and a quiet resistance (the **Verenthae**, who may be the true heirs of an older faith). The **iconoclast wound** — the memory of a time when the images were smashed — is a gift to a world where Yueli's corruption is being fought. Some in Tyramare still refuse to worship before a painted face, and nobody remembers entirely why.

## 5. FAMILY AND KINSHIP

**Key fact for worldbuilding: the dowry held the Byzantine and Italian world together — and women who controlled dowries controlled more than anyone admitted.** Under Byzantine family law, a wife could not be separated from her dowry; her husband managed it but could not sell it, and if the marriage ended the dowry went with her. Under Venetian law, daughters inherited equally with brothers when no will specified otherwise. In Florence, the *Monte delle doti* (founded 1425) was a public dowry fund — you paid in at a girl's birth and withdrew at her marriage, because every respectable father needed to provide. The women who ran the money ran the marriage market, and through it, ran the city.

### The household
- **Byzantine households** — hierarchical, patriarchal in form, but women held specific spheres of power. The *oikonomia* (household management) was explicitly the wife's responsibility. Wealthy houses had a *gynaikonitis* — a women's section, sometimes a separate floor or courtyard — where unrelated men were not admitted. This was not always a prison; it was also a salon, a workshop, a school.
- **Italian households** — Venetian palaces had a *piano nobile* (noble floor) for the family and a ground floor for business and storage. Florentine *palazzi* were built around a central courtyard, with kitchens and servants below, master's rooms above. Households included extended kin, servants, wet nurses, tutors, clerks, and often unmarried siblings of the head of the house.

### Marriage
- **Byzantine marriage** — arranged within your class. A minimum age: 12 for girls, 14 for boys (rarely observed — betrothals happened much younger, formal marriage waited). A priest blessed the rite; crowns were placed on the bride and groom's heads (the *stephana*, crowns of martyrdom — because every marriage is a kind of self-sacrifice). Divorce was possible on specific grounds (abuse, infidelity, attempted murder, apostasy). Widows had strong inheritance rights, including a quarter-share of a husband's estate if she held no dowry of her own.
- **Italian marriage** — dowry-driven, political, transactional at the top and affectionate at the bottom. Venetian patricians often married late (men in their thirties, women in their late teens); only one son per generation was usually allowed to marry, to keep the patrimony intact. Unmarried younger sons lived in the family palace or went into trade, the church, or the condottieri trade; unmarried daughters went into convents (often with dowries nearly as large as marriage dowries).
- **The cicisbeo / *cavalier servente*** — by the 17th-18th centuries especially, a married noblewoman had an official male escort: her cicisbeo. With her husband's knowledge and consent, he accompanied her to church, theatre, carnival, and private dinners. He whispered at her shoulder. The practice was partly a containment strategy (one approved lover rather than scandal) and partly a consequence of age-gap marriages and primogeniture — there were thousands of noble younger sons with time and charm and no estate. **This is an older European phenomenon in its mature form, but can be backdated in fantasy.**

### Children, wet nurses, and fosterage
- **Wet nurses** — near-universal among the Byzantine and Italian elite. A wealthy mother rarely nursed her own baby; a professional wet nurse (*balia*) was hired. She often lived in the household and was considered a kind of kin.
- **Education of boys** — from tutors at home (Byzantine tradition) or in small guild-run schools, then into apprenticeship, the bureaucracy, or the church.
- **Education of girls** — literacy was not universal but was more common among patrician women than modern myth suggests. Anna Komnene read philosophy and wrote history. Venetian noblewomen were taught reading, music, and household accounts; their courtesan counterparts were often better educated still, because they had to be.
- **Orphans and foundlings** — Florence built the **Ospedale degli Innocenti** (1419, designed by Brunelleschi) for abandoned infants, with a revolving wheel outside the wall where a mother could leave a child anonymously and ring a bell. Every major city had one. These institutions were charity and scandal equally.

### Elders and ancestors
- Orthodox Byzantine families venerated the dead with memorial services (*trisagion*) on the third, ninth, and fortieth days after death, and annual remembrances. Kollyba (boiled wheat with honey and pomegranate) was brought to church and blessed.
- Italian families kept altars and portraits; the dead lived on in chapels within family churches, in frescoes that included donor portraits of ancestors, in coats of arms above every doorway.

### Eunuchs as kin
In Byzantium, eunuchs were often given to the court as children by ambitious parents — a one-way adoption into the imperial household that raised the whole family. Eunuchs were unable to have children but could be adoptive fathers, heirs, or godparents. They made natural bureaucrats partly because they produced no dynasty.

### What this means for Tyramare
The **Marble Quarter matriarchy** can anchor directly in the historical reality that Byzantine and Venetian women controlled dowries and thereby controlled lineages. Serina's "first lady has no daughter heir, and the spine is about to tremble" is exactly the kind of succession crisis that broke Byzantine dynasties (the childless Theodora Porphyrogenita was the last Macedonian; Venetian noble houses died out when the patriline failed). The **cicisbeo** translates beautifully as a social institution Tyramare could have — a second-son order of escorts attached to noble wives. **Wet nurses and foundling wheels** add texture. **Eunuchs as a chosen family** fit perfectly beside the guild districts.

## 6. LIFE CYCLE RITUALS

### Birth and naming
- **Byzantine** — a newborn was swaddled, washed, and anointed with oil. A cross was traced over the heart. On the eighth day the child was named (usually for a saint, often a grandparent). On the fortieth day the mother was readmitted to church (the *churching*) and the child was presented. Infant baptism by triple immersion was standard from a few weeks to a few months old — it was regarded as spiritual necessity because infant mortality was real and baptism was believed to secure salvation.
- **Italian** — the bell at the church of San Giovanni in Florence rang for every birth, boys' and girls' counts kept as beans in separate urns. Godparents (*compare*, *comare*) were chosen from political allies as much as kin — a godparenthood was a social alliance.
- **Lucky and unlucky days** — astrology was consulted. A child born under a bad sign could be given an extra baptismal name to confuse the fate.

### Coming of age
- **Byzantine boys** — there was no single ritual equivalent to a Western knighting. Formal education began around seven; a boy's first beard was a minor milestone; he was considered a man when he entered state service, marriage, or monastic life.
- **Byzantine girls** — veiling at puberty was expected, though Constantinople's women were notorious among foreign visitors for going bareheaded. The first menses were treated privately, the girl coached by her mother and any elder women of the household.
- **Italian boys** — apprenticeship at 10-14 was its own rite of passage. A Venetian patrician boy joined the Great Council at 25, but each year between 20 and 25 a *ballotta d'oro* lottery gave a fraction of underage nobles early admission — a coveted honour.
- **Italian girls** — a girl's first public appearance (her *uscita*, "going out") at around 15-16 was a dressed-up moment, family-coordinated, with the goal of attracting a marriage offer. Convent entrance at 14 was the alternative path.

### Marriage ceremonies
- **Byzantine rite** — the *akolouthia tou stefanomatos* (office of crowning). The priest blessed rings, placed crowns on the couple's heads, held a common cup, and led them in a ceremonial dance around the altar (the "dance of Isaiah"). The crowns remained on for eight days; the couple wore them home and returned them to the church.
- **Venetian rite** — public, elaborate. The bride was escorted by gondola along the Grand Canal under a silk canopy. Banquets lasted days. Sumptuary laws tried and failed to limit the spending. A traditional ritual: the **Festa delle Marie** (until 1379) — twelve beautiful poor girls were dowered and displayed by the patrician families each year in a ceremonial marriage procession.
- **Florentine rite** — three stages: the *impalmamento* (handclasp betrothal before a notary), the *sponsali* (gift exchange and ring ceremony), the *nozze* (the bride's arrival at her husband's house with her *cassone* — a painted marriage chest — borne in procession).

### Death and mourning
- **Byzantine funerals** — the body was washed, wrapped in a shroud, and waked in the home. A procession with candles, incense, and chanted hymns bore the body to the church for the *exodos* (funeral rite). Burial (not cremation) in consecrated ground. **Forty-day** and **anniversary** memorials mattered as much as the funeral itself. Emperors were entombed in sarcophagi of porphyry — the imperial stone.
- **Italian funerals** — by the late medieval period confraternities organised funerals for their members; black-robed processions with candles and chanted Miserere moved through the streets. The rich endowed altars and requiem masses in perpetuity. Elaborate tombs in family chapels displayed effigies of the deceased.
- **Plague burials** — mass pits. Boccaccio describes in the *Decameron* how "bodies were heaped one upon another" and how coffins were replaced with sheets or boards. Plague dead received little ritual; the shortage of priests and the terror of infection stripped the rite down to almost nothing.

### Festivals that marked the year
- **Byzantine feasts** — the twelve great feasts of the Orthodox calendar (Annunciation, Nativity, Epiphany, Meeting of the Lord, Entry into Jerusalem, Pascha/Easter, Ascension, Pentecost, Transfiguration, Dormition, Nativity of the Theotokos, Exaltation of the Cross). Pascha was the greatest, marked by midnight liturgy, candle processions, red eggs, and lamb.
- **The Triumph of Orthodoxy** (first Sunday of Lent) — the anniversary of the restoration of icons in 843. Processions carrying icons through the streets.
- **Venetian festivals** — **Carnevale**, from the Feast of St Stephen (26 December) to Shrove Tuesday. Masks, music, pranks, commedia, duels, public dinners, anonymous affairs. **La Sensa** — the *Sposalizio del Mare*, the marriage of the sea, every Ascension Day, when the Doge sailed in the *Bucintoro* barge to the edge of the lagoon and dropped a gold ring into the water: *"Desponsamus te, mare, in signum veri perpetuique dominii"* (We wed thee, O sea, in sign of true and perpetual dominion). **Festa del Redentore** (third Sunday of July) — thanksgiving for the end of the 1576 plague; a pontoon bridge of boats from the Zattere to the Giudecca.
- **Florentine festivals** — **Calendimaggio** (May Day), **San Giovanni** (24 June, the city's patron), the **Palio** (horse race), the **Scoppio del Carro** (explosion of the cart on Easter morning — a mechanical dove ignites a fireworks-filled wagon in front of the Duomo).

### What this means for Tyramare
The wedding of the sea is **too good not to steal** — a Tyramare doge/council leader sails to the lagoon's edge and casts a ring into the water in a ritual that predates memory, wedding the city to the sea. **Carnevale** gives Tyramare's Veil district its annual season of masks and anonymity — a month when the Umbrathi can move freely and everyone is someone else. **Forty-day memorials** add ritual depth to death in Tyramare. **The lottery of beautiful poor girls** (the Marie) is a usable beat: a coastal city that dowers twelve girls a year in a public ceremony, each family vying to host.

## 7. CLOTHING AND ADORNMENT

**Key fact for worldbuilding: in both Byzantium and Italy, clothing WAS legal status.** What you could wear was written into law. Only the emperor and his immediate family could wear pure purple — the penalty for wearing imperial purple without authorisation was death. In Venice, the *Magistrato alle Pompe* (the Magistracy of Luxury, founded 1512) enforced sumptuary laws against cloth-of-gold, certain silks, cut sleeves, and above all pearls; courtesans were specifically banned from wearing pearls in 1562. A dressed body in Tyramare-world would be read like a passport, a pedigree, and a tax return at a single glance.

### Tyrian purple — the colour of power
**Tyrian purple** was extracted from the hypobranchial gland of *Murex brandaris* and *Hexaplex trunculus* sea snails along the Phoenician coast (around Tyre and Sidon, hence the name). Tens of thousands of snails yielded a tiny quantity of dye; the smell of the workshops was so vile that dye-houses were zoned to the city outskirts. One pound of wool dyed a true Tyrian purple cost the equivalent of several gold coins by the Roman period, and the Byzantines kept a state monopoly. The imperial robes were "born in the purple" and children born while the father ruled were **Porphyrogennētos** — born in the *Porphyra*, the literal purple-stone chamber of the palace.

### Byzantine imperial dress
- **The chlamys** — a semicircular cloak pinned at the right shoulder with an elaborate **fibula** (brooch), worn over a long tunic. The emperor's chlamys carried a **tablion** — a rectangular panel of gold-embroidered silk on the chest and back. Only the imperial family's tablions were purple.
- **The loros** — a long, heavily jewelled strip up to several metres long, wrapped around the body and looped over the left arm. Originally the Roman triumphal *trabea*; by Byzantium it was the emperor's most sacred garment, worn on high feast days. Encrusted with pearls, emeralds, and enamel plaques.
- **The *kamelaukion* / *stemma* (imperial crown)** — a wide jewelled diadem with a gem-cross over the forehead and long pearl strings (*prependoulia*) hanging down either side of the face, swaying with every movement.
- **The skaramangion** — a long riding caftan, worn by the emperor and military officers. Frequently silk, sometimes patterned with roundels of lions or eagles or hunting scenes.
- **Women's dress** — long tunics in layers, silk over linen; a heavy overrobe (*maphorion*); a jewelled belt; sleeves that could be cut long to the wrist or split to reveal the under-sleeve. Empresses wore their own crowns and loros. Hair was dressed and hidden under a veil or a jewelled fillet. Theodora in the San Vitale mosaic wears pendant pearl earrings that fall to her shoulders.

### Byzantine commoners and craftworkers
A dalmatic-style tunic to the knee or ankle, a leather or cloth belt, leather shoes or boots. Men working outdoors wore shorter tunics and trousers (*bracae*). Women wore longer tunics with a cloth headcover. Wool for working people; linen for undergarments and summer; silk for anyone who could afford even a trim.

### Jewellery and adornment (Byzantine)
- **Pearls** above all — the Byzantine imperial aesthetic was pearl-first, stone-second.
- **Enamel plaques** (*cloisonné*) — tiny gold cells filled with coloured glass, sewn into garments or set into jewellery.
- **Crosses** — worn at the neck, on the chest, embroidered on every surface. A pectoral reliquary (*encolpion*) containing a saint's relic was a common prestige item.
- **Cameos, intaglios, and carved gems** — often inherited from Roman forebears, reset into Byzantine mounts.

### Italian Renaissance dress — the civic wardrobe
- **Male patricians** — the **toga** (Venetian *toga patrizia*), a long black robe reaching to the ankle, with hanging sleeves, worn over a black or dark-coloured doublet. Black was the serious colour of public life in Venice. A senator in scarlet was a senator on a feast day.
- **The *corno ducale*** — the doge's distinctive horn-shaped hat, white linen over a stiff frame, worn over a brocade under-cap. A visual trademark.
- **Female patrician dress** — a full gown (*gamurra*, *vestura*) over a chemise, with a long-sleeved overgown (*zimarra*) with bell or slit sleeves. Fabric of heavy silk, often velvet, often in a pattern of *melograno* (pomegranate) or *cammino* roundel motifs. Bodices laced or pinned, necklines cut square or rounded. Hair dressed in elaborate plaits over cushions, dyed blonde by sun-bleaching on the *altana* (roof-terrace).
- **Shoes** — the **chopine**, a wood-and-cork platform shoe that raised the wearer ten to twenty inches off the ground. Patrician women and courtesans both wore them, supported by servants on either arm.
- **Working clothes** — wool tunic and hose for men, a simple kirtle and apron for women, leather shoes, a cloak against the lagoon damp.

### Sumptuary laws and their workarounds
- **Venice, 1512:** the Magistrato alle Pompe legislated against cloth-of-gold, cloth-of-silver, rare silks, cut trimmings, and excessive jewellery. In 1562, a specific law forbade **courtesans** to wear silk, pearls, or to live in palaces on certain streets.
- **Venice, 1562:** two *bocche* (mouths — letterboxes) were constructed outside the Magistrato's office in the Doge's Palace to collect secret denunciations of sumptuary violations. A neighbour could report you for wearing forbidden pearls.
- **Florence, 14th-15th century:** laws constantly legislating on dress by class, with constant lists of exceptions. The wealthy simply paid the fines — some accounts suggest the sumptuary fine was effectively a luxury tax.
- **Courtesans circumventing:** Veronica Franco and her peers were photographed in costume-books wearing pearls, breeches under skirts, and exposing the breast — their defiance of the laws was part of their mystique. *The cortigiana onesta kept up her status by showing she could dress like the wives forbid to dress like her.*

### Courtesan dress
Veronica Franco (1546–1591) and the *cortigiane oneste* were known for pearls (forbidden), intricately cut sleeves (forbidden), and the *zibellino* — a sable or marten pelt with a jewelled gold head, carried or worn at the waist, believed to collect fleas and parasites. They wore their hair dressed high, often bleached gold. They held gloves and fans. They carried perfumed pomanders. They carried a stylus and a book — the education was part of the costume.

### What this means for Tyramare
Tyramare's jeweled surface is **literal**: pearls, cloth-of-gold, cut sleeves, platform shoes, embroidered panels, enamel plaques sewn to robes. The **Marble Quarter matriarchs** wear heavy-silk zimarre in the family colours. The **Veil courtesans** wear forbidden pearls defiantly. The **Iron Quarter** wears black like Venetian senators, with lacquered steel under the robe. The **Solenthera clergy** wear Byzantine-inflected vestments with tablions of gold embroidery. The **Bannric crown** sits in a glass case in the council chamber, a silk cord of tiny pearls still hanging from its side — nobody has crowned anyone in a hundred years. And somewhere a sumptuary officer with a carved-marble letterbox collects denunciations of anyone wearing above their station.

## 8. FOOD AND DRINK

**Key fact for worldbuilding: the Byzantine-Italian table was the first truly cosmopolitan cuisine of the medieval world.** Constantinople was eating cinnamon from Ceylon, pepper from India, sugar from Egypt, caviar from the Black Sea, and antelopes from Asia Minor while Europe was still boiling turnips. Venice then inherited the trade and turned the spice rack into an economy. Both cultures ate olive oil where the north ate butter, salted fish where the north ate game, and wine where the north ate ale. Food in Tyramare should smell of salt, oil, honey, and a dozen incoming ships.

### The Byzantine table
- **Bread** — the foundation. Wheat bread for the rich; barley and mixed-grain for the poor. Constantinople's bread supply was a state affair, with Egyptian grain ships arriving annually and bakeries under strict price controls. **Paximadi** — twice-baked barley rusks, a staple for soldiers, sailors, and monks, still eaten in Greece today.
- **Olive oil** — cooking, lighting, anointing, medicine, ritual. Every kitchen had it.
- **Fish and shellfish** — Constantinople feasted on Bosporus tuna, mackerel, mullet, anchovy, sardine. Salted and pickled fish from the Black Sea. Caviar (*khaviari*) from the sturgeon of the Sea of Azov.
- **Meat** — lamb most prestigious, mutton more common, goat, beef (rare, expensive). Pork was eaten widely despite the influence of neighbouring Islam. Game: partridge, pheasant, hare, boar.
- **Garos (garum)** — the great Byzantine condiment, direct inheritance from Roman cooking. Fermented fish sauce, made from anchovies, sardines, or mackerel entrails and blood, salted and left in the sun for two to three months. Used on everything. The umami of the empire.
- **Murri** — a fermented barley sauce, the savoury partner to garos.
- **Cheese** — fresh white cheese (*myzithra*, *anthotyro*), hard yellow cheese (*kasseri*-type). Feta-like brined cheese.
- **Vegetables** — leeks, garlic, onions, cabbage, chickpea, fava bean, lentil, pumpkin, aubergine (a new Eastern import, spreading from the 9th century), spinach. Fresh herbs: mint, dill, parsley, savory, oregano, rosemary.
- **Fruit** — figs, grapes (raisins, wine, fresh), pomegranate, apple, pear, quince, apricot, peach, mulberry. Citrons from the 3rd century; lemons spread in the later period; oranges from the 10th century.
- **Sweets** — honey was the universal sweetener. Sugar arrived through Egypt and Crusader trade and was used medicinally at first. **Baklava's ancestors** appeared in Byzantine kitchens — layered pastries with honey and nuts. Quince paste. Candied almonds.
- **Spices** — pepper, cinnamon, cloves, ginger, nutmeg, mastic (the resin of the Chios lentisk, a Byzantine monopoly).
- **Wine** — resinated whites (*retsina*'s ancestor) and unresinated reds. Wine of Thasos was the most prized. Monasteries produced the best wine. It was watered at table (mixing was a ritual in itself).

### The Italian table (Venetian and Florentine)
- **Polenta's ancestor** — before maize, polenta was made of millet, chickpea, or barley. Thick porridges for the poor.
- **Pasta** — present in Italian kitchens from the 12th century onwards; documented in Genoese wills and Venetian households by the 13th. Fresh ravioli and macaroni were luxury; dried pasta (from Sicily and Genoa) was a sailor's provisions.
- **Risotto's ancestor** — rice arrived via Arab Spain and Sicily, cultivated in the Po Valley from the 15th century. A Venetian invention.
- **Polenta nera** — made of farro, barley, or chestnut flour in the mountains.
- **Bread** — the Tuscan *pane sciocco* (unsalted bread) was already a regional signature in the late medieval period, some say because of a salt tax imposed by Pisa. Venetian bread was salted, denser, more pan-loaf.
- **Venetian specialities** — *sarde in saor* (fried sardines marinated in onions, vinegar, pine nuts, raisins — sweet-and-sour, Arab-influenced), *risi e bisi* (rice and peas, served to the doge on St Mark's Day), *bigoli in salsa* (thick dark pasta in anchovy-onion sauce, a Lenten dish), *fegato alla veneziana* (liver with onions), *baccalà mantecato* (whipped salt cod), **cicchetti** (small bar snacks, the ancestor of tapas, served at *bacari* with a glass of wine).
- **Florentine specialities** — *ribollita* (twice-boiled bread-and-vegetable soup), *pappa al pomodoro* (bread-and-tomato stew, after tomatoes arrive), *bistecca alla fiorentina* (thick T-bone over chestnut-wood fire), *cantuccini* with *vin santo*.
- **The spice trade gave the table its sparkle** — saffron in risotto, cinnamon in meat, cloves in stewed game, black pepper on everything. Venetian merchants sold spices to all of Europe and kept the best for themselves.
- **Wine** — Tuscan reds (Chianti in its medieval form), Venetian whites (Soave, Verdicchio), dessert wines (Vin Santo in Tuscany, Malvasia shipped from Greece to Venice and on to northern Europe).
- **Coffee** arrived late (the first Venetian *caffetteria*, Il Caffè, opened at San Marco in 1683) but Venice was the European gateway.

### Feasting and hospitality
- Byzantine feasts at the imperial palace ran to seventeen courses in the **Triklinos of Nineteen Couches**; guests reclined Roman-style into the early middle period and sat on benches afterward. Automatons entertained between courses.
- Italian feasts were theatre. The *credenza* was a tiered sideboard of plate, designed to display wealth. The *scalco* (steward) choreographed service. Carving was a performance art; Vincenzo Cervio's *Il Trinciante* (1581) was an actual treatise on it.
- **Hospitality as political instrument** — in both cultures, every feast was about who sat where, who was served first, who was handed the bread, who was offered the best cut.

### Fasts and food taboos
- Orthodox fasting is **strict** — no meat, dairy, fish, wine, or oil on full fast days; roughly **180 days a year** of some level of fast. Lent, Advent, Apostles' Fast, Dormition Fast, plus Wednesdays and Fridays most weeks. This shaped cuisine: olive, pulse, grain, and lagoon-mollusc (which Orthodoxy permits even in strict fasts) became the workhorse of Byzantine cooking.
- Catholic fasting was looser, but Lent, Fridays, and vigils of major feasts demanded fish. Venice's *bigoli in salsa* and *baccalà mantecato* are Lenten dishes.
- **Heretics** — Bogomils abstained from meat, wine, and sex; their "perfect ones" ate only bread and water.

### What this means for Tyramare
Tyramare smells of salted fish, olive oil, garos (call it *garro* or *sauros* in-world), honey, cinnamon, and the wine ships unloading at Spice Harbour. Its staples are bread, oil, fish, and pulse. Its luxuries are saffron, pepper, silk-wrapped sugar cakes, and a jewelled pomegranate from Maramir. Every inn in Stillhaven has wine on draft, cicchetti-style plates on the counter, and one fish stew pot bubbling. Every Solenthera fast day drives the whole city onto pulse and lagoon. At a noble feast, automaton songbirds in a gilded tree announce each course.

## 9. ARCHITECTURE AND SETTLEMENT

**Key fact for worldbuilding: Byzantine architecture is about the enclosed sacred interior. Italian architecture is about the civic facade and the private palace.** The Hagia Sophia's outside is austere; its inside explodes into gold. The Palazzo Ducale's outside is patterned and playful; its inside (where the councils met) was secretive and functional. Tyramare can hold both impulses: the secret rich interiors of the Marble Quarter houses (step through an unmarked door and find an atrium of porphyry and water), and the public jewelled facades of the Solace temples.

### The Hagia Sophia — the mother of the East
- **Commissioned by Justinian, 532 CE**, after the Nika Riot destroyed the previous church. Completed in **five years, ten months, four days** — December 537. Architects: **Anthemius of Tralles** (a mathematician) and **Isidore of Miletus** (a geometer-engineer).
- **The dome** — 31 metres in diameter, rising on four **pendentives** (curved triangular supports that transition a square plan into a circular dome). The first fully pendentive dome of this scale ever built. Procopius said it seemed to hang "as if suspended from heaven by a golden chain."
- **The interior** — marble of every colour (*verde antico*, porphyry, giallo antico, *pavonazzetto*), mosaics of gold ground so vast they seemed to turn the walls to sunlight, icons of the Theotokos and Christ Pantocrator (the "ruler of all") staring down from the apse and dome.
- Justinian, entering the finished church, is said to have exclaimed *"Solomon, I have surpassed thee."*

### Byzantine church architecture
- **The Greek cross plan** — four equal arms under a central dome, four subsidiary domes over the corners. Developed from the 9th century.
- **The iconostasis** — the screen between nave and sanctuary, progressively higher and more icon-covered from the 14th century.
- **Mosaic and fresco** — the interior surface above marble dados was ALL image. Gold ground made the church an approximation of heaven.
- **Monasteries** — walled complexes with a central *katholikon* (main church), refectory, library, *skete* cells, a guesthouse. Mount Athos, Hosios Loukas, Daphni, and the Meteora monasteries perched on pillars of rock.

### The Theodosian Walls
Built 408–413 under Emperor Theodosius II. A triple defence: a 20-metre-wide **moat**, an **outer wall** about 8 metres tall, then a massive **inner wall** 12 metres high and 5 metres thick with 96 towers. They held off Avars, Arabs, Bulgars, Rus, and (mostly) Crusaders for over a thousand years until the Ottoman cannons of 1453.

### Venetian architecture
- **The building technique** — timber piles driven into the mud, topped with a platform of oak planks, then Istrian limestone (water-resistant), then brick. Every stone palace sits on a buried forest.
- **Byzantine-Venetian hybrid style (11th-13th century)** — St Mark's Basilica (begun 1063). Five domes in the Byzantine-Greek-cross manner, covered with 8,000 square metres of gold mosaic, faced outside with stolen Byzantine marbles and the **four bronze horses** looted in 1204. The *Pala d'Oro* (golden altarpiece) of cloisonné and gems is the quintessential Byzantine masterpiece surviving in Venice.
- **Venetian Gothic (13th-15th century)** — pointed arches, quatrefoils, tracery. The **Doge's Palace** (Palazzo Ducale, rebuilt 1340 onwards) with its ground-level arcade, upper loggia, and pink-and-cream marble diamond-patterned facade. **Ca' d'Oro** (1425-1440, the House of Gold) once had gilded surfaces across its facade.
- **Venetian Renaissance (late 15th-16th century)** — Mauro Codussi, Jacopo Sansovino, Andrea Palladio. Classical orders, symmetry, restraint. Palladio's churches (San Giorgio Maggiore, Il Redentore) across the basin from San Marco reshaped European church architecture.
- **The palazzo plan** — water entrance (*porta d'acqua*) at ground level for goods; land entrance (*porta di terra*) opposite on the *calle*; a central ground-level hall (*portego*) for reception; warehouses (*fondaco*) off the hall; family rooms on the *piano nobile* upstairs; servants' quarters above; a *cortile* (courtyard) with a wellhead (*vera da pozzo*) at the centre; an *altana* (rooftop wooden terrace) for laundry and for the women to sun their hair blonde.

### The Arsenale — the state factory
The **Arsenale di Venezia** (first established 1104, enormously expanded 1320 as the Arsenale Nuovo) was the largest industrial complex in Europe before the Industrial Revolution. At its peak it employed 16,000 **Arsenalotti** (shipwrights, ropemakers, sailmakers). It could build a complete great galley in a day using a moving production line — ships floated through flooded channels past successive workstations, each team outfitting a specific component. Henry Ford specifically cited the Arsenale as his model.

### Florentine architecture
- **Palazzi** — rusticated stone, heavy symmetrical facades, massive cornices (Palazzo Medici-Riccardi by Michelozzo; Palazzo Strozzi by Benedetto da Maiano).
- **Brunelleschi's dome** — Santa Maria del Fiore (completed 1436), the largest brick dome in the world, raised without centering on a double-shell design copied from nobody.
- **The piazza** — the civic heart, flanked by the *palazzo comunale* (town hall), the *duomo* (cathedral), and the *baptistery*.

### Housing for common people
- **Byzantine Constantinople** — multi-storey wooden tenements, crowded, flammable, frequently destroyed by fire. Shopfronts at street level, living rooms above. Tiled roofs. Narrow alleys.
- **Venice** — the *popolo* lived above their shops in tall narrow houses crammed along the *calli*. The **casa a schiera** (row house) pattern. Flat roofs with *altane*. Communal wells in small *campi* (squares), because every drop of fresh water had to be caught from rain — the wells were actually cisterns fed by drainage through a sand filter.

### Interior furnishings
- Byzantine houses — couches (inherited from Rome), chests, tables, wall hangings (tapestries, icon-curtains), oil lamps, braziers. Floors of tile, mosaic, or wood; walls frescoed.
- Italian houses — *cassoni* (painted marriage chests — major art objects), *credenze* (sideboards), *lettucci* (daybeds), canopied beds, inlaid *intarsia* cabinets. The finest houses had frescoed ceilings and walls hung with tapestries from Flanders.

### What this means for Tyramare
Tyramare's **Solace** temple should be a direct Hagia Sophia — a pendentive dome, gold mosaic inside, a Christ Pantocrator-equivalent (a Solenthera face in gold?) staring down from the apex. The **Iron Quarter** should echo the Arsenale — a walled inland basin with flooded channels, a moving line, and 10,000 armourers. The **Aurumvale** palaces should be the Venetian palazzo model, with water doors, piani nobili, altane, internal courtyards with wellheads. The **Marble Quarter** houses have rusticated stone facades like Florentine banking palaces — heavy, symmetrical, implying centuries of money.

## 10. ART, MUSIC, AND STORYTELLING

**Key fact for worldbuilding: in Byzantium, art was theology. In Italy, art was politics AND theology AND patronage AND self-promotion, all at once.** Byzantine mosaics were meditations — every gem-cube (*tessera*) tilted slightly to catch the candle and make the saint's face seem to move. Italian frescoes were arguments — who got painted, who got the best placement, whose patron saint shared the chapel with whose rival's patron saint, and whose donor portrait stood in the crowd at the Adoration of the Magi.

### Byzantine mosaic — the art of glass and gold
- **Technique** — small cubes of glass, stone, or gold-leaf-sandwiched glass (*smalti con oro*) pressed at slight angles into a setting bed of wet lime plaster. Each cube reflects candlelight differently. The effect is that the image shimmers and breathes as you move through the church.
- **Gold ground** — above the dado of marble, the whole wall was gold-ground. The figures float against heaven. This is not a setting; it is a state of being.
- **Christ Pantocrator** — "ruler of all" — the iconic Byzantine image. A mature, bearded Christ with one hand raised in blessing and the other holding a jewelled gospel book, looking down from the central dome. Example: the Deësis mosaic in the south gallery of the Hagia Sophia (13th century); the Pantocrator of Daphni Monastery near Athens.
- **The Theotokos** — the "God-bearer," the Virgin Mary, usually in the apse, often enthroned with the Christ-child on her lap. Stern, majestic, crowned with a gold halo.
- **The Ravenna mosaics (6th century)** — San Vitale in Ravenna (consecrated 547) preserves the finest surviving Byzantine imperial mosaics: Justinian and his court on one side of the apse, Theodora and her court on the other, both processing toward the altar carrying liturgical gifts. Sant'Apollinare in Classe, Sant'Apollinare Nuovo, the Mausoleum of Galla Placidia — Ravenna is a Byzantine echo chamber in Italy, the site where East and West still meet under a dome.

### Byzantine icon painting
- Egg-tempera on wooden panel, sometimes over linen over wood, sometimes silver-leafed or gold-leafed.
- Strict iconographic rules. The saint's pose, clothing, hand-gestures, and accompanying symbols were fixed by tradition; a painter was a scribe, not a creator. What varied was the quality of line, the intensity of expression.
- **Schools** — Constantinopolitan (the most refined), Cretan (after 1204, the heir), Macedonian, Russian.
- The icon was kissed, prayed before, carried in procession, placed in every home.

### Italian painting — from tempera to fresco to oil
- **Tempera panels** — the 13th-14th-century manner: egg yolk binding pigments on gesso-prepared wood, gold-leaf haloes tooled with patterns. Duccio, Giotto, Simone Martini.
- **Fresco** — pigment on wet plaster (the *giornata*, a single day's painting area). Giotto at the Arena Chapel (Padua, 1305). Fra Angelico at San Marco (Florence, 1440s). Piero della Francesca at Arezzo. Michelangelo at the Sistine (1508-12).
- **Oil painting** — the Venetian revolution. Antonello da Messina brought Northern oil technique to Venice around 1475; Giovanni Bellini, Giorgione, Titian made it the new normal. Oil allowed glazes, luminosity, and a whole new approach to flesh and fabric.
- **The Venetian palette** — Titian's reds, lapis blues, umber shadows. Tintoretto's torchlit darknesses. Veronese's silver-gold daylights.
- **Sculpture** — Donatello's bronze David, Michelangelo's marble David, Verrocchio's equestrian Colleoni in Venice. Lost-wax bronze, carved Carrara marble, polychrome wood for altarpieces.

### Patronage
- **Byzantine** — imperial, ecclesiastical, monastic. An emperor founded a monastery and was painted into its founder-icon. A Patriarch commissioned a gospel book in gold. Private patronage was limited but real — wealthy families paid for mosaic portraits of themselves to be included in their parish churches.
- **Italian** — PUBLIC. The guilds of Florence competed to commission the most spectacular work: Or San Michele had niches for each guild's patron saint, each guild outdoing the last (Ghiberti's St Matthew for the bankers, Donatello's St George for the armourers). The Medici bought artistic immortality. Venetian patricians bought altarpieces for family chapels with portraits of themselves kneeling at the Virgin's feet.

### Music
- **Byzantine chant** — monophonic (single melodic line), modal (the **octoechos**, eight modes), with no harmony. **St Romanos the Melodist** (early 6th century) composed the *kontakia* — elaborate hymns of 20-30 stanzas (*oikoi*, "houses") that were sung in the liturgy like sermons set to music. Notation (*ekphonetic* and later *Middle Byzantine neumes*) was developed by the 10th century. Mount Athos preserved and developed the tradition.
- **Instruments** — the *lyra* (a pear-shaped bowed string instrument), the *psaltery* (plucked), the flute, hand-drums, finger-cymbals. Instruments were for secular life; liturgy was voice alone.
- **Italian sacred music** — Gregorian chant (Western), then organum (2 voices), then polyphony. The great Venetian polyphonic tradition of the San Marco choir school — Adrian Willaert (music director 1527-62), Andrea Gabrieli, Giovanni Gabrieli. Antiphonal choirs singing from opposite lofts in San Marco invented *cori spezzati* ("broken choirs"), forerunner of baroque concerted music.
- **Secular music** — the *frottola*, the madrigal, the lute song. Every noble house had a lute and a harpsichord. Courtesans sang and played for paying audiences.

### Literature and storytelling
- **Byzantine** — historical works (Procopius' *Secret History*, which exposed Justinian and Theodora; Anna Komnene's *Alexiad*, the first history written by a woman in Europe), saints' lives (*vitae*), epic poems (*Digenes Akritas*, the border-warrior epic), theological treatises.
- **Italian** — Dante's *Divine Comedy* (completed 1320), Petrarch's sonnets, Boccaccio's *Decameron* (1349-53, the hundred stories told by ten young people fleeing the Black Death). The birth of modern Italian as a literary language. Also the courtesan literature — Veronica Franco's *Lettere familiari* and *Terze rime*.

### Dance and performance
- The Hippodrome of Constantinople was entertainment and politics combined — chariot races, mimes, bear-baiting, public executions.
- Venetian and Florentine carnival featured masked balls, commedia dell'arte (the stock characters Harlequin, Columbine, Pantalone, the Doctor), acrobats on the piazza.

### What this means for Tyramare
**The Solace** is mosaic-and-icon Byzantium. **The Aurumvale** palaces have Venetian-Renaissance painted ceilings — Tintoretto-dark allegories of the founding of the city. **Every guild** has its patron in a niche on the exterior of a public hall. **Chant** in the temples, **polyphonic choirs** at state festivals, **lute-song** in the taverns, **lyra** and **hand-drum** on the canals at night. **Veronica Franco's equivalent** is a courtesan-poet of the Veil writing letters that the Council of Ten reads line by line. **A mosaic of the founding council** faces a mosaic of an empty throne on opposite walls of the assembly hall. The gold ground behind is real.

## 11. TECHNOLOGY AND CRAFTS

**Key fact for worldbuilding: Tyramare's craft inheritance is glass, silk, gold, ink, and ships.** The Byzantine world was the last direct heir of Roman engineering; the Italian city-states industrialised before industry and perfected craft specialisation. Murano glass, Venetian silk, Florentine wool, Byzantine icon-tempera, the Arsenale's shipbuilding, Constantinople's automata, paper and ink and cartography — Tyramare should be a city where every alley leads to a workshop and every workshop has a secret.

### Glass — the Murano monopoly
- **Murano glass** — from 1291 by decree, all Venetian glass-making was moved to the island of Murano. The stated reason was fire safety (furnaces in a wooden city were catastrophic). The real reason was secrecy. Glassmakers were forbidden to leave Venetian territory on pain of death; their daughters could marry into patrician families (an honour otherwise impossible) as incentive to stay.
- **Cristallo** — the almost-colourless transparent glass invented by Angelo Barovier in the mid-15th century. Revolutionary: previously "clear" glass had been greenish or smoky.
- **Lattimo** (milk-white glass), *calcedonio* (marbled), *filigrana* (thread-embedded), *millefiori* (thousand-flower, cross-sections of canes). The Venetians invented the whole vocabulary.
- **Mirrors** — Venice invented the flat tin-mercury mirror, a state secret until the 17th century. A large Venetian mirror was worth more than a painting by Raphael.
- **Beads** — seed beads (*conterie*) and lampwork beads (*perle a lume*) traded across the entire world. The *impiraresse*, female bead-stringers, were a whole guild of their own.

### Textiles
- **Byzantine silk** — Justinian's agents (two monks, according to Procopius) smuggled silkworm eggs out of China inside hollow bamboo canes around 552 CE. Imperial silk workshops in Constantinople and Thebes became state monopolies. Foreign sale of the highest-grade **purple silks** was forbidden; lesser Byzantine silks were exported to Italy via Venice and Amalfi.
- **Byzantine brocades** — patterned silks with roundels of lions, eagles, griffins, hunters, or sacred subjects. The Bamberg Gunthertuch — a surviving Byzantine imperial silk depicting a mounted emperor — is one of the treasures of medieval textile art.
- **Italian silk** — Lucca, then Florence, then Venice. Velvet (Genoese *velluto*, Venetian *alto e basso* patterned velvet), damask, cut-and-uncut velvet, cloth-of-gold (*teletta*).
- **Florentine wool** — the **Arte della Lana** guild was the richest in Europe. English raw wool shipped into Tuscany, washed, carded, spun, dyed, woven, finished, sheared, and sold back to Europe.
- **Dyes** — indigo and woad (blue), madder and brazilwood (red), weld (yellow), kermes (the crimson insect-dye from Mediterranean oaks), **Tyrian purple** (murex snail), saffron (yellow). The colours of wealth.

### Metalworking and jewellery
- **Byzantine** — cloisonné enamel (gold wire cells filled with coloured glass, fired), reliquary crosses, gold-embroidered silks with enamel plaques, chalices and patens of silver-gilt.
- **Italian** — filigree, niello (black sulphide alloy inlaid into silver), granulation. Venice and Genoa were centres for pearl-setting and coral. Florence's goldsmiths became painters (Ghiberti, Verrocchio, Benvenuto Cellini).
- **Iron and steel** — Milanese armour was the gold standard of Europe. The Missaglia family of Milan supplied plate armour to half the continent.

### Paper, ink, and printing
- **Papermaking** arrived in Italy via Fabriano in the 13th century (from the Arab world, from the Chinese); Venice adopted and industrialised.
- **Printing** — Johannes of Speyer opened the first Venetian press in 1469. **Aldus Manutius** founded the Aldine Press in 1494; he invented the italic typeface, the semicolon, and the portable octavo book. By 1500 Venice was the printing capital of Europe — perhaps 150 presses.
- **Byzantine writing** — parchment (sheepskin, goatskin, calfskin), papyrus (declining after the Arab conquest of Egypt), then rag paper. Ink: iron gall and carbon-based.

### Glass, lenses, and optics
- Reading glasses were invented in Italy (probably Pisa or Venice) in the late 13th century. "Roidi da ogli" — "roundels for eyes" — appear in Venetian inventories from the 1280s.
- Murano's glass expertise spun off into lens-grinding — precursors of the telescope (Galileo's 1609 telescope used Venetian glass).

### Shipbuilding and navigation
- **The Byzantine dromon** — the fast warship of the middle empire. Two-banked oars, a lateen (triangular) sail, a siphon mounted at the prow for Greek fire. Faster and more manoeuvrable than its Western counterparts.
- **The Venetian galley** — long, slim, low, oared, with two or three masts for lateen sails. The Arsenale built galleys in a moving line for centuries.
- **The round ship (*nave*)** — the fat-bellied sailing cargo ship. The Genoese *cocca* by the 14th century, the *carrack* by the 15th.
- **Navigation** — the magnetic compass arrived in Italy from China (via the Arabs) in the 12th century. The first known portolan chart is the Carta Pisana (c. 1290). **Portolani** — practical sailing charts of coastlines with bearings, distances, and hazards — were Genoese and Venetian specialities, carefully guarded.

### Automata and imperial machines
Constantinople's imperial palace had **mechanical toys**: gilded lions that roared (air bellows, mechanical tongues), gold birds that sang in a gilded tree above the throne (flute mechanisms with compressed air), a throne that rose to the ceiling on hidden gears. Liutprand of Cremona, a Lombard ambassador, described them in 949 CE. This tradition of automata was a Hellenistic inheritance (from Alexandria) and influenced later Islamic and eventually European clockwork.

### Agricultural tools
- Both cultures used the iron plough, the scythe, the sickle, the threshing flail.
- **Olive press** — screw-press technology of Roman origin, still in use.
- **Wine press** — basket press, beam press.
- **Water mill** — Venetian mainland and Tuscan waterways were lined with them.

### What this means for Tyramare
Tyramare's **Glass Quarter** on one of the outer islands is a Murano-equivalent, secretive, wealthy, its masters forbidden to leave the city. **Silk workshops** in Spice Harbour. **Cloisonné enamel** and **filigree goldwork** in the Marble Quarter. **Iron Quarter** as the Arsenale-plus-Milanese-armoury (armour, swords, glaives, the mass-production of galleys). **Automata** in the Solace temple — a mechanical dove that descends on a hidden chain at high liturgy, a silver hand that lifts the chalice at the right moment. **Printing presses** in the back-streets of Stillhaven, churning out pamphlets the Council of Ten has not quite managed to ban. **Portolan maps** are the Travel-Quarter's quiet wealth.

## 12. WARFARE AND WEAPONS

**Key fact for worldbuilding: both Byzantium and the Italian city-states fought by specialty, not mass.** Byzantine warfare was professional, defensive, strategic — the *tagmata* and the themes, the dromon navy, the diplomacy that preferred a bribe to a battle. Italian warfare was privatised — condottieri captains under contract, crossbowmen by the hundred, the sea-galley as the weapon of choice. A Tyramare soldier should be paid, trained, and cynical — and a Tyramare admiral should be a noble elected for a year.

### The Byzantine military

- **The tagmata** — the professional standing army of the capital. Four core regiments: the **Scholae** (household guard, infantry), the **Excubitors** (imperial guard, originally spear-carriers), the **Arithmos/Vigla** (the Watch — night guard, protected the emperor on campaign), and the **Hikanatoi** (the "competents," formed 810). Each tagma was commanded by a *Domestikos* reporting directly to the emperor.
- **The themes (*themata*)** — provincial armies. Each region was a theme; the strategos governed it and commanded its soldiers. Themes provided the bulk of field armies — farmer-soldiers who held land in return for military service. The theme system was instituted in the 7th century and worked for 400 years.
- **The tagma of the Varangians** — the emperor's personal guard, initially Norsemen from the Rus, later Anglo-Saxons who fled to Constantinople after 1066. Loyal to the emperor personally rather than to any faction. Paid well; fought with broad axes; feared. "The wine-bibbers of the emperor" one Greek text calls them, dismissing their drinking and praising their courage in the same breath.

### Weapons and equipment

- **Infantry weapon**: the *spathion* (a long, straight Roman-descended sword), the *paramerion* (a curved sabre, from the 10th century, an influence from the Turkic steppe), spears (the *kontarion*, up to 4 metres for the front line), javelins, bows.
- **Armour**: *klivanion* (lamellar armour — small rectangles of iron laced together, very effective for mounted archers), *lorikion* (mail), *epilorikion* (padded gambeson). The **kataphrakt** — the super-heavy armoured cavalryman, horse and rider both armoured head to hoof, shock weapon of the 10th-century army under Nikephoros Phokas.
- **Bows**: the composite recurve bow, taken from Persian and Hunnic traditions.
- **Siege engines**: inherited Roman onagers, later the trebuchet (14th century).

### Greek fire — the state secret

Invented (or perfected) around **672 CE** by Kallinikos of Heliopolis, Greek fire was the Byzantine Empire's wonder weapon. A liquid incendiary based on petroleum, resin, sulphur, and possibly quicklime, **heated and pressurised inside a bronze vessel** and projected through a **siphōn** (a tube, sometimes bronze and ornamented as a lion or serpent head) mounted at the prow of a dromon. The flames burned on water. Water could not put them out. It clung to whatever it touched. Only vinegar, urine, and sand could smother it.

The formula was **compartmentalised**. Different guilds made different parts; only a tiny circle knew the whole recipe. The knowledge was kept within a few families in Constantinople, and when Constantinople fell, the formula was lost. Scholars still argue about the exact composition. The Byzantines used Greek fire to destroy two massive Arab fleets at the sieges of Constantinople in 678 and 718 — saving the empire twice.

### Italian warfare — the contract armies

- **Condottieri** — "contractors." From the late 13th century onwards, Italian city-states hired professional mercenary captains under *condotta* (contract). Names: **Muzio Attendolo Sforza**, whose son Francesco seized Milan in 1450; **Braccio da Montone**; **Erasmo da Narni "Gattamelata"** (honoured with Donatello's bronze equestrian in Padua); **Bartolomeo Colleoni** (Verrocchio's equestrian in Venice). Ruthless, venal, often brilliant. Captain and company went where the money was — Florence this year, Milan next.
- **The ransom economy** — condottieri warfare was rarely lethal. Nobles and men-at-arms were worth more captured than killed. Battles could be long, manoeuvring, tactical, and surprisingly bloodless; the **Battle of Zagonara (1424)** famously had three deaths, "all by drowning" — Machiavelli's snide exaggeration but close to the truth in spirit.
- **Crossbowmen** — the great Italian specialty. Genoese crossbowmen fought as elite mercenaries across Europe (including the disastrous French side at Crécy, 1346). The crossbow remained dominant in Italy long after the longbow and firearm took over elsewhere.
- **Infantry pikes and halberds** — standard formations of pike and polearm-infantry by the 15th century.

### Venetian naval warfare

- **The war galley** — Venice's weapon. Low, slim, oared, three-masted with lateen sails, 170 oarsmen, a ram at the prow, marines and crossbowmen on the deck. Grappling, boarding, ramming were the tactics.
- **The great galley (*galea grossa*)** — a larger trading-cum-fighting galley developed in the 14th century, which could cross the Mediterranean carrying high-value cargo and a strong contingent of marines.
- **The **capitano generale da mar** — the Venetian fleet admiral, elected from the patriciate for a specific campaign.
- **The Battle of Lepanto (1571)** — the last great galley battle, the Holy League (Venice, Spain, the Papal States) under Don Juan of Austria destroyed the Ottoman fleet. Over 400 galleys engaged. Cervantes lost the use of his left hand there.

### Women in warfare

- **Byzantine** — empresses regent could command armies through generals; Irene's mother Maria Theophano was known for her political command. Theodora the wife of Justinian saved the throne during the Nika riot with the line "**Purple makes a fine winding-sheet**" — refusing to flee when her husband was about to run.
- **Italian** — the *donna guerriera* was rare but existed. **Caterina Sforza** (1463-1509) defended Forlì and Imola; when her children were taken hostage and she was threatened, she famously bared herself from the battlements and shouted *"Fatelo, se volete: impiccateli pure, qui ho lo stampo per farne altri"* — "do it, if you wish: hang them; here I have the mould to make more." Probably apocryphal but much quoted. Onorata Rodiani, legendary Cremonese woman who disguised herself as a man and fought as a condottiero under Braccio da Montone.

### Declaring war, making peace

- Byzantine diplomacy preferred **money to blood**. The *logothetes tou dromou* managed a web of client kingdoms, hostage-princes educated at Constantinople, subsidies to barbarian tribes to fight each other. The famous saying "Byzantium fights with diplomacy and only uses the sword as a last resort."
- Italian city-states signed treaties constantly; broke them constantly; formed and broke leagues. Florence vs Milan, Venice vs Genoa, Genoa vs Pisa — the pattern of Italian warfare from 1100 to 1500 was endless intracity conflict occasionally interrupted by a foreign invasion everyone had to unite against (1494, Charles VIII of France).

### What this means for Tyramare
The **Iron Quarter** is the condottieri guild — a professional body of captains under contract to the Council. They fight the wars the merchants want fought and collect the ransoms. The **Tyramare fleet** is galleys in the Venetian manner, built at the Iron-Quarter Arsenale, with a mixture of marine-men and crossbowmen. **A Greek-fire equivalent** is a closely-guarded state secret held by two or three families of alchemist-artisans — the recipe is fragmentary and compartmentalised. **A Varangian-equivalent guard** for the empty throne is a unit of foreign mercenaries, perhaps Isenholt beast-bonded, sworn to the council rather than to any Tyramare family. **Death-combat** (Serina's Iron-Quarter note) could be a ritualised trial-by-combat tradition surviving from an older military caste.

## 13. TRADE AND ECONOMY

**Key fact for worldbuilding: Byzantium was the hinge of the eastern Mediterranean, and Venice made itself the hinge of the whole Mediterranean.** Constantinople sat astride the only sea passage to the Black Sea and the overland termini of the Silk Road. Its markets sold everything: Bulgarian linen, Arabian perfume, Chinese silk, Indian pepper, Ethiopian ivory, Russian furs, Egyptian linen. Every merchant in that world passed through. Venice then positioned itself as the Western gateway — buying from Constantinople, selling to Germany, carrying spices to Flanders, wool to Egypt. The whole Byzantine-Italian economy ran on the movement of goods through a few choke-points — and the governments that controlled those choke-points grew obscenely rich.

### The Byzantine economy

- **The *solidus* / *bezant* / *nomisma*** — the gold coin of Byzantium, first struck by Constantine in 312 and continuing essentially unchanged for 700 years. The *solidus* was the **reserve currency of the medieval world**, used from Scandinavia to Samarkand. Called the *bezant* in the Latin West, it held its gold content (about 4.5 g, 24 carat) almost perfectly until the 11th-century debasement. The stability of the nomisma was the empire's economic brand.
- **The *silver miliaresion* and *copper follis*** — silver and copper coinage for smaller transactions.
- **State monopolies** — purple silk production, Greek fire, luxury silk exports, grain distribution in Constantinople. The state controlled key commodities and licensed the rest.
- **The Prefect's Book (*Eparchikon Biblion*, c. 900)** — Leo VI's manual of urban regulation. Every guild in Constantinople had its rules written down: how bakers could price bread, where silk dealers could set up, how cattle dealers could weigh meat, what moneychangers could charge. Inspectors enforced it. Fines and exile punished violations.
- **Guilds (*systemata*)** — bakers, butchers, silk merchants (*metaxopratai*), silk spinners, silk weavers, silk dyers, goldsmiths, bankers (*argyropratai*), perfumers, candle-makers, grocers, fishmongers. Each guild had its street or district.

### The Italian commercial revolution

Between about 1000 and 1300 CE, the Italian city-states invented the machinery of modern commerce.

- **Double-entry bookkeeping** — in use by Italian merchants by the 13th century, codified by Luca Pacioli's *Summa de arithmetica* (1494).
- **Bills of exchange** — a merchant in Venice could pay a Florentine agent in Flanders via a signed paper letter, avoiding the physical transport of gold through bandit country.
- **Marine insurance** — the first known insurance contract is Genoese, 1347.
- **Banking** — the **Medici Bank** (1397-1494) had branches in Florence, Venice, Rome, Milan, Geneva, Lyon, Bruges, London, Avignon. Lent to popes, kings, cardinals, and the Papacy especially. The family's wealth funded the Florentine Renaissance.
- **Joint-stock ventures (*colleganza*)** — Venetian partnership contract. A stay-at-home investor (*socius stans*) put up capital; a travelling merchant (*socius tractans*) ran the voyage. Profits were split 3:1 or 2:1 in favour of the capital. These contracts built the Venetian fortune.
- **The ducat** — Venice's gold coin, introduced 1284 by Doge Giovanni Dandolo, weighing 3.545 g of almost pure gold. Became the standard Mediterranean coin for the next five centuries; replaced the debased bezant.

### The Florentine florin

The *fiorino d'oro* — introduced 1252 — also 3.5 g of pure gold, stamped with the city's lily (*fiore*, "flower"). Together with the ducat, the two coins were the medieval equivalents of the dollar and the euro.

### The guild system in Florence

- **Arti maggiori** (major guilds) — Judges and Notaries, Cloth Merchants (Calimala), Wool Merchants (Lana), Bankers (Cambio), Silk Merchants (Seta), Physicians and Apothecaries, Furriers and Skinners. These were the political elite.
- **Arti minori** (minor guilds) — Smiths, Butchers, Shoemakers, Masons and Woodworkers, Linen Merchants, Wine Dealers, Innkeepers, and so on. Fourteen in total.
- **The *Signoria*** (the city government) was composed of representatives drawn from the arti — a system manipulated and perfected but never formally abolished by the Medici.

### Long-distance trade

- **The Silk Road termini** — Constantinople, Trebizond, Tana (on the Sea of Azov), and Acre (until its fall in 1291) were the Mediterranean's connections to the overland routes to China. After 1291, Alexandria became the critical link as the Mamluks controlled the spice trade from Aden through Egypt.
- **The "Great Venetian Convoys" (*mude*)** — seasonal state-organised fleets sailing to Alexandria, Beirut, Constantinople, Trebizond, Flanders, London, Aigues-Mortes. Departure dates were set by the Senate. Profit-sharing was mandated. The mude made Venice the hinge.
- **Spices** — pepper (#1 by volume), cinnamon, cloves, nutmeg, mace, ginger, saffron. Venice bought from Alexandria and Beirut (and historically from Constantinople), sold to all of Europe at multiples of the purchase price.
- **Luxury textiles** — silk, velvet, cloth-of-gold from Italy and Byzantium eastward and northward; English and Flemish wool southward.
- **Slaves** — the uncomfortable truth. Both Venice and Genoa ran substantial slave trades, especially in the 14th-15th centuries, buying Slavic and Circassian people from the Black Sea slave markets and selling into Italian households and Egyptian service.
- **Russian furs, Baltic amber, German silver** — moved through Constantinople and Venice to Egypt; **Egyptian linen, Indian spice, Chinese silk** — moved the other way.

### Markets and fairs

- **The Rialto market** of Venice — the beating heart of Mediterranean commerce. The Rialto Bridge spanned the canal here; the Fondaco dei Tedeschi (German traders' warehouse) and Fondaco dei Turchi (Turkish traders' warehouse) flanked the area. Every currency in the world was changed there.
- **The Champagne fairs** — the seasonal fairs of northeastern France were the great 12th-13th century meeting point between Italian and Northern merchants. Italian bankers and textile dealers met Flemish wool merchants.
- **Byzantine markets** — the *Mese*, Constantinople's main avenue, ran from the Forum of Constantine west through the city, lined with colonnaded shop-arcades, goods arranged by guild.

### What this means for Tyramare
**Spice Harbour** is the Rialto-equivalent — the commercial heart where every currency is changed, every ship unloads, every bank has a branch. The **Tyramare ducat** (maybe called the *solar* or the *mar'aan*) is the stable gold coin of Caerndrath, minted under Council authority. Tyramare runs **seasonal trading convoys** (the *marée*?) that the Council scheduled year by year. The **guild structure** of the seven districts maps directly onto the arti of Florence. **Bills of exchange, colleganza-style joint ventures, double-entry books** are all things Tyramare has that other provinces don't — part of its competitive advantage. The **Fondaco** is a Foreign Quarter inside Spice Harbour: Isenholt warehouses, Maramiri caravan-factors, Aoli silk-brokers, each with their own floor.

## 14. LANGUAGE AND WRITING

### Languages

- **Greek** — the language of Byzantium. Latin was the official language under the early empire (through Justinian's reign), but Greek was the vernacular and became the administrative language from the 7th century onwards under Heraclius. **Medieval Greek** (also called *Koine* in its late-antique form, *Byzantine Greek* in the middle period) differed from classical Attic but was continuous with it — educated Byzantines could read Homer and Plato. A diglossia persisted: a high literary register (*Atticising* Greek), and an everyday vernacular (*demotic*). Imperial documents were in the high register; folk ballads like *Digenes Akritas* in the low.
- **Latin** — retained by the Western Church, by Italian scholarly communities, by Byzantine legal texts (the *Corpus Juris Civilis*, translated into Greek over centuries as the *Basilika*).
- **Italian vernaculars** — the birth of modern Italian is usually dated to **Dante's *Divine Comedy*** (finished 1320), written in Tuscan vernacular. Florentine Tuscan became the literary standard. But each city had its own dialect — Venetian (*Veneziano*), Genoese (*Zeneize*), Milanese, Neapolitan. These were (and are) mutually partly-intelligible Romance languages.
- **Trading pidgins** — the *Lingua Franca* (also called *Sabir*) was a mixed Romance-Arabic-Greek-Turkish pidgin used in Mediterranean ports for merchant communication. Every sailor knew a version of it.

### Writing systems and literacy

- Byzantine literacy was higher than anywhere else in medieval Europe (with the possible exception of the Arab world). Aristocratic men and women typically read. The imperial bureaucracy required a literate class of clerks (**taboullarios**, the notarial profession). Monks read; nuns read.
- Italian urban literacy was extraordinary. Florence at the peak of the Renaissance had perhaps 30-40% adult male literacy — catastrophically higher than the European norm. The *abbaco* schools taught commercial mathematics and vernacular literacy to merchants' sons and daughters. **Women's literacy** was real but class-dependent: Venetian patrician wives managed accounts, courtesans wrote poetry, nuns copied manuscripts.
- **The chancery hand** — the clear round legal hand of Italian notaries, a direct ancestor of modern printed type.
- **Manuscript culture** (up to the 15th century) — monastic and commercial scriptoria produced books to order. Illuminated manuscripts with gold leaf and ground lapis were prestige items.
- **Printing** — Aldus Manutius, Venice, 1494 onwards, as discussed in §11.

### Poetry and rhetoric

- **Byzantine rhetoric** — the art of speech was central to education. The *progymnasmata* — standard rhetorical exercises — remained the core of upper-class schooling from antiquity through the fall of Constantinople.
- **Byzantine poetry** — epigrams (short polished verses), hymns (*kontakion*, *kanon*), epics (*Digenes Akritas*), panegyric orations for emperors, *ekphraseis* (elaborate word-paintings of buildings, artworks, and spectacles).
- **Italian poetry** — Dante's terza rima, Petrarch's sonnet sequence (the **Canzoniere**, some 366 poems addressed to Laura), Boccaccio's prose tales. The **petrarchan sonnet** became the European lyric standard — fourteen lines, ABBAABBACDECDE or similar. Veronica Franco wrote *terze rime* (terza-rima verses) as literary responses to male poets who had invited or insulted her, and her letters (*Lettere familiari a diversi*) are a masterclass in courtesan-intellectual prose.

### The courtesan as letter-writer

**Veronica Franco (1546-1591)** is Serina's direct reference for the literate courtesan. She maintained correspondence with poets, with Henry III of France (who visited her), with cardinals. She published *Terze Rime* in 1575 and *Lettere familiari a diversi* in 1580. Her letters advised, scolded, negotiated, flirted, and defended her reputation. When she was denounced to the Inquisition for witchcraft in 1580, she defended herself at her own trial and was acquitted.

### Oaths, correspondence, and dispatches

- The **written word was bound by ritual**. A merchant's contract signed before notary and witnesses was binding before the law. An oath sworn on relics could not be broken without divine retribution.
- The **ambassador's dispatch** — Venetian ambassadors sent fortnightly reports (*dispacci*) to the Senate, and on return submitted a *relazione*: a comprehensive report on the foreign state they had served in. These *relazioni* are now one of the richest sources for European political history.
- The **cipher** — the Venetian chancery developed sophisticated numerical ciphers for diplomatic correspondence; the *cifra veneziana* was internationally famous.
- **Denunciation** — the *bocche di leone* letterboxes carved into walls of the Doge's Palace and other key buildings. A citizen slipped a signed letter into the lion's mouth; the Council of Ten read it. Anonymous letters were officially disregarded but in practice pursued. Unsigned denunciations went into a secret archive that historians are still working through.

### The archive

Venice built Europe's first continuous state archive, the **Archivio di Stato**, which preserves over a thousand years of Venetian records. A city that wrote everything down and kept it. A city where you could be summoned to explain a contract your grandfather had signed, and they would have the paper to hand.

### Byzantine archives

Similar impulse, less surviving material (fires, the 1204 sack, the 1453 conquest destroyed most of it). But the imperial administration ran on paper (papyrus, parchment) and every act of government was recorded.

### What this means for Tyramare
Tyramare speaks a **high language** and a **common tongue** (a diglossia — high ceremonial Tyramaric in the council and temple, everyday Tyramari in the market). It has **letter-writing as both weapon and art form** — the Veil courtesans' letters are read and collected; the Marble Quarter matrons' account-letters are read and decoded by rivals; the Umbrathi ciphers are broken and remade. It has **bocche di leone-equivalents** in every district. It has **archives** — stacks and stacks of records in the Council buildings, with scribes who can find a deed from a century ago in an hour. It has **a state printing press** and perhaps an **unauthorised one** in a back alley. A literate city — but selectively literate. Common people read the price boards in the market; patricians read Greek and Tyramaric; courtesans read everything.

## 15. EDUCATION AND KNOWLEDGE

**Key fact for worldbuilding: Byzantine and Italian urban cultures both believed that knowledge could be MADE, not merely inherited.** The Roman encyclopaedic impulse survived in Byzantium through scholars like Photius, Suda, and the Patriarch Michael Keroularios; it revived in 14th-century Italy through Petrarch's letter-hunting and exploded in 15th-century Florence through Medici-funded recovery of Greek classics. In both worlds, a scholar who could read Greek, Latin, and Arabic could rise on the strength of brain alone — and a wealthy patron with a library could turn his palace into a university.

### Byzantine education

- **The *paideia*** — the classical ideal of the "rounded" education, inherited from ancient Greece. A child learned letters (reading, writing, arithmetic) at 7-8, grammar (syntax, poetic analysis) at 10-12, rhetoric (composition, speech) at 13-15, philosophy and/or law beyond that.
- **Schools** — primary schools in every town, run by private teachers. Higher education concentrated in Constantinople.
- **The Magnaura** — the imperial "university," re-founded under Bardas and Constantine VII (mid-9th century) with a chair each in philosophy, geometry, astronomy, and grammar. Students paid no fees; some received stipends. Leo the Mathematician headed it.
- **The Patriarchal School** — theological and philosophical training under the Patriarch's auspices.
- **Monastic libraries** — Mount Athos, the Stoudios monastery in Constantinople, the monasteries of Cappadocia. Mount Athos' libraries alone preserve tens of thousands of manuscripts, including many classical texts surviving nowhere else.
- **Copying** — monks copied manuscripts as prayer. The late-9th-century revival of classical learning under the Patriarch Photius (whose *Myriobiblos* or *Bibliotheca* catalogued 280 books he had read, many now lost except in his summary) was a pivotal moment in world cultural history.

### What women were taught

Byzantine aristocratic women were often formally educated. **Anna Komnene** (1083-1153) studied philosophy, grammar, rhetoric, astronomy, and medicine. Her *Alexiad*, a fifteen-book history of her father's reign, is the only major European historical work of the middle ages written by a woman. She ran a literary salon as an old woman, rediscovering Aristotle's commentators and fostering the circle that eventually produced the scholarly recovery of Aristotle in the West.

### Italian education

- **The abbaco schools** — commercial schools teaching vernacular reading, writing, and practical mathematics (including the Indian-Arabic numeral system, introduced via Leonardo Fibonacci's *Liber Abaci* of 1202). Merchants' sons attended from age 8 or so. These schools taught the arithmetic that ran the banks.
- **Latin grammar schools** — for those destined for law, medicine, the clergy, or the humanities. Church-affiliated or lay. Teachers (*magistri*) licensed by the city.
- **Universities** — **Bologna** (traditional founding 1088, the oldest continuous university in Europe, famous for law), **Padua** (1222, famous for medicine and philosophy — Galileo lectured there 1592-1610), **Florence** (1321), **Pavia**, **Naples** (1224, founded by Frederick II). Students organised themselves into *nationes* by region; professors were often elected by students who paid them directly.
- **Humanism** — the Italian Renaissance movement that sought to recover the learning of classical antiquity. **Petrarch** (1304-1374) discovered Cicero's letters in a library at Verona (1345) and opened the floodgates. **Leonardo Bruni** wrote humanist histories. **Lorenzo Valla** used textual criticism to expose the forged *Donation of Constantine* (1440). **Poggio Bracciolini** recovered Lucretius' *De Rerum Natura* from a German monastery (1417).
- **The Medici libraries** — Cosimo the Elder funded the recovery of Greek manuscripts from Constantinople and founded the **Biblioteca Medicea Laurenziana** (public library, building by Michelangelo, 1571). Lorenzo the Magnificent added to it.
- **The Platonic Academy of Florence** — an informal circle around **Marsilio Ficino** (1433-1499), funded by Cosimo de' Medici. Ficino translated the complete works of Plato, Plotinus, and the Hermetic Corpus into Latin. The academy shaped Renaissance philosophy and through it the entire Western philosophical tradition.
- **Women's education in Italy** — patrician girls often learned letters and music. Some learned Latin and Greek (**Isotta Nogarola**, **Cassandra Fedele**). Most were educated at home by tutors. Convent schools educated girls destined for the religious life.

### What knowledge was valued

- **Byzantine** — theology first, law second, medicine third, mathematics-and-astronomy fourth, natural philosophy fifth. Classical literature as foundation for rhetoric.
- **Italian** — increasingly: the classics themselves (Latin, then Greek), commercial and legal skills, medicine, then philosophy. Engineering and military science emerged as respected disciplines in the 15th century (Leonardo's notebooks; Filarete's architectural treatise).

### The hidden curriculum: apprenticeship

Most practical knowledge moved through apprenticeship. A boy joined a master at 10-14, lived in the workshop, learned the craft over 5-10 years, and might produce a *masterpiece* (a "master piece" — a work demonstrating his qualifications) to become a master himself. Women apprenticed to glove-makers, lace-makers, bead-stringers, midwives, and (in a few cases) painters' workshops as daughters or wives.

### Alchemy, astrology, and magic

- Every serious scholar in both worlds knew astrology. Emperors and doges alike consulted horoscopes.
- Alchemy was taken seriously by philosophers and princes. Cosimo de' Medici's astrologer-alchemist circles included Ficino and Pico della Mirandola; their Christian Hermeticism was part of the Medici intellectual project.
- The **Cabala** (Jewish mystical interpretation of scripture) entered Italian Christian humanism via **Pico della Mirandola's 900 theses (1486)** — an attempt to show that all truth was one. Rome condemned him; the tradition survived.

### What this means for Tyramare
The **Council archive** doubles as a library of state and a university. The **Marble Quarter** funds a salon-and-library equivalent of the Medici Academy — the matriarchs fund scholars, fund translations, fund the recovery of lost cosmological texts (this could be where the memory of Yueli's true name lives). **Abbaco schools** for merchants' children in Spice Harbour. **An elite academy** in Aurumvale where noble boys and girls (the girls fewer but present) study Tyramaric, Old-Imperial, and the commentaries of the ancient philosophers. **The Solenthera temple** runs its own school for priests. The **Veil courtesans** are the best-educated women in the city — Franco-equivalents — and their salons are where ideas happen.

## 16. MEDICINE AND HEALING

**Key fact for worldbuilding: Byzantine medicine preserved Greek and Roman learning when the West forgot it, and the Italian city-states invented the public hospital.** A Byzantine physician in the 10th century was still reading Galen, still practising cataract surgery with bronze needles, still compounding elaborate herbal preparations. A Venetian physician in the 15th century was inheriting all of that plus Arabic medicine (via Constantinople) plus the new anatomical work of Padua. When plague struck, both cultures responded with the best they had — and both invented public-health institutions that echo into the present.

### Byzantine medicine

- **The xenon** — the Byzantine hospital. Originally a hospice (*xenon* means "guest-house"), these evolved into specialised medical centres (*iatreion*, *nosokomeion*). The **Pantokrator xenon** in Constantinople (attached to the Pantokrator Monastery, founded by John II Komnenos in 1136) is the most famous: a charter (*Typikon*) survives describing 50 beds, separate wards for male and female patients, surgery, ophthalmology, and medicine specialists, physicians who rotated monthly, a pharmacy, private beds, trained nurses, and a duty to wash hands after each patient. This hospital system was **centuries ahead of anywhere else in Europe**.
- **Physicians** — trained in the empire's universities and apprenticed under senior doctors. Paid by the state in the xenon system. Women physicians (*iatrinai*) existed, especially for gynaecology and midwifery.
- **Pharmacology** — hundreds of herbal compounds inherited from Dioscorides' *De Materia Medica* (1st century CE), continuously recopied and supplemented. Theriac — the all-purpose antidote, over 70 ingredients including viper flesh and opium — was compounded by specialist pharmacists.
- **Major medical writers:**
  - **Paul of Aegina** (c. 625-690) wrote the *Medical Compendium in Seven Books*, which systematised Byzantine medicine. Book Six on surgery lists over 40 procedures and 15 instruments. His neurosurgical descriptions — trepanation, spinal decompression, hydrocephalus drainage — were extraordinary for the period.
  - **Aetios of Amida** (6th century) — sixteen-book medical encyclopaedia.
  - **Oribasius** (4th century) — Julian's personal physician, whose *Synagogae Medicae* (Medical Collections) preserved Galen.
  - **Alexander of Tralles** (6th century) — pragmatic clinician, used amulets alongside drugs.
- **Surgery** — cataract couching (pushing the lens aside with a needle), lithotomy (bladder stone removal), amputations, cauterisation, hernia repair. Operations were performed without effective anaesthesia beyond wine, opium, mandrake, and henbane.

### Italian medicine and the Schola Medica Salernitana

- **Salerno** — from the 10th century, a medical school drawing students from all of Christendom. Christian, Jewish, and Arabic medical traditions mingled. **Trotula of Salerno** (probably 12th century) was a woman physician whose name is attached to an influential trilogy of medical texts on women's health — the *Trotula* (*De passionibus mulierum*, *De ornatu mulierum*, *De curis mulierum*). Whether Trotula was one woman, a pseudonym, or several people debated, but the scholarship is real and was read across Europe.
- **Bologna** and **Padua** — after Salerno's decline, the northern universities became Europe's medical centres. The first public dissections of human cadavers took place at Bologna in 1315 (Mondino de Liuzzi). Padua in the 16th century produced Vesalius' *De humani corporis fabrica* (1543).
- **Translation movement** — from the 12th century onwards, Greek and Arabic medical works were systematically translated into Latin in Italy and Iberia, giving Latin Christendom access to a vast corpus.

### The plague doctor

During repeated plague epidemics (1348 onwards), a specialised *medico della peste* emerged, hired by cities to treat infected patients. The costume — long waxed-leather coat, wide-brimmed hat, **beak-mask filled with aromatic herbs** (thought to filter miasma), glass eyepieces, a cane to examine patients from arm's length — became iconic. The actual costume was developed by Charles de Lorme in 1619, somewhat later than Serina's main reference period, but usable.

### Quarantine

- **Venice invented quarantine** — the word itself comes from the Venetian *quarantena*, "forty days," the period of isolation imposed on arriving ships during plague outbreaks. The first official quarantine was in 1348 during the Black Death.
- **Lazaretto Vecchio** (founded 1423) and **Lazaretto Nuovo** (1468) — islands in the Venetian lagoon where arriving plague-suspect ships and crews were held. Lazaretto Vecchio was also a hospital for plague victims; archaeological work there has uncovered thousands of mass-burial skeletons.
- **Dubrovnik** (Ragusa) had a 30-day *trentino* from 1377 — possibly the first formal maritime quarantine in history.

### Folk and domestic medicine

- **Herbs** — grown in every household garden and every monastery. Mallow, mint, rosemary, sage, thyme, savory, fennel, borage, comfrey, plantain, chamomile, vervain. A Byzantine or Italian housewife knew what each one did.
- **Amulets and charms** — officially frowned on, in practice universal. A leather pouch of herbs worn around the neck for childbirth; a cross-marked stone in the bed for a sick child; a prayer to Cosmas and Damian (the Byzantine physician-saints) or Saints Roch and Sebastian (the Italian plague saints).
- **Midwives (*maia*, *comare*)** — women who trained on the job. Delivered children, set bones, administered folk remedies, laid out the dead. Sometimes allied with priests or physicians; sometimes targeted as witches.
- **Bloodletting** — routine for rich and poor alike. Barber-surgeons handled the cheap end.

### Spiritual healing

- **Pilgrimage to relic-shrines** — a lame pilgrim might crawl to the tomb of St Demetrios at Thessaloniki, or to St Mark at Venice, or to Cosmas and Damian at Rome, and be healed. Ex-votos — wax body parts, silver hands, painted panels — hung around every major shrine.
- **Incubation** — sleeping in a shrine and receiving a dream-cure, a practice inherited from the Hellenic Asclepios cult and absorbed into Christian healing-saint shrines. Cosmas and Damian's shrines were famous incubation sites.

### What this means for Tyramare
Tyramare has **the Solace xenon** — a public hospital attached to the great temple, with specialist wards, bathing pools, and a garden of medicinal herbs (connect to Aoli's Yán Shǒu for a cross-cultural medicine-trade note). It has **a quarantine island** in the lagoon, with a grim history of plague burials. It has a **plague-doctor's guild** within the Solace — beaked masks and all. It has **women physicians in the Marble Quarter** (Trotula-equivalents) and midwives in every parish. It has **relic-shrines for healing** (the thumb-bone of a Solenthera saint that sweats oil when the city is in danger). It has **poison-gardens in the Veil** that double as medicine-gardens — a toxicologist and a healer are the same person.

## 17. DAILY LIFE

**Key fact for worldbuilding: a Byzantine or Italian city was LOUD, CROWDED, and deeply rhythmic.** Church bells and hours, market opening and closing, ship-arrivals and departures, public liturgies and processions, the daily walk to the well and the daily haul from the fish-quay. A day in Constantinople or Venice had a PATTERN — set by the bells of the nearest church or the cannon of the Arsenale. Tyramare should feel like a city whose every hour is marked by something.

### The day of a Byzantine commoner (Constantinople, c. 1100)

- **Before dawn** — the church bells (*semantron*, a wooden board struck rhythmically in Orthodox tradition, often replacing the Western bronze bell) call for matins. A baker is already at the oven; the fish-quay at the Golden Horn is already crowded with the night catch.
- **Dawn** — shops open in the colonnaded Mese. Bread bought hot. Water carried from public fountains (the great **cistern of Basilica**, the cistern of Philoxenos, and hundreds of smaller wells). Public baths (the **Zeuxippos** bath, the **Arcadian** bath) open.
- **Morning** — work. Artisans at workshops, merchants at stalls, officials at the Praitorion (government office). The emperor holds his daily audience.
- **Midday** — break for lunch (bread, cheese, olives, a cup of watered wine). The forum is crowded with idlers, vendors, ascetic preachers, acrobats.
- **Afternoon** — more work. Litigation in the law-courts. Women at the weaving.
- **Evening** — the baths again for those who can afford them. Dinner with family — meat for the rich, pulse for the poor, wine for everyone. Vespers at church.
- **Night** — the streets empty. The *Vigla* (the imperial guard) patrols; the Prefect's watchmen check for fire. The hippodrome stands dark. Lamp-oil sellers close their shutters.

### The day of a Venetian commoner (c. 1400)

- **Before dawn** — the Marangona bell of the Campanile tolls to call shipyard workers to the Arsenale. The fish market at Rialto opens.
- **Dawn** — the Pistoria (baking) begins. Women haul fresh water from the neighbourhood *pozzo* (well — actually a cistern).
- **Morning** — work. Gondolas ferry merchants between palaces and warehouses. Traders gather on the Rialto bridge (the old wooden one) to transact. The *Camera degli Imprestidi* (the public loan office) opens.
- **Midday** — lunch. A bowl of *risi e bisi*, a slice of polenta, a piece of salt cod, wine from Crete.
- **Afternoon** — more trade, more ferrying. A senate session might be sitting.
- **Evening** — the taverns (*bacari*) open. Cicchetti — small snacks on the counter — and *ombre* of wine (named for the shadow of the Campanile, historically sought by wine-sellers).
- **Night** — the Marangona bell signals the end of legal business and the start of night. Only authorised persons (couriers, doctors, midwives, nightwatch) are supposed to be out. The *sbirri* (night-guards) patrol. The gondolas are moored to their *pali* (striped mooring posts).

### Hygiene and personal care

- **Baths** — public baths were common in Byzantium; both monks and nuns were expected to bathe twice a week. Venice had fewer public baths but most households had copper tubs heated at the kitchen fire. Steam rooms and communal bath culture existed but were less central than in Byzantium.
- **Teeth** — cleaned with linen rubs, ash, ground pumice. Miswak-style twigs from the Arab world were known. Tooth powder recipes in medical texts.
- **Hair** — washed with egg, ash-lye (*cenere*), vinegar. Bleached blonde with lemon juice, saffron water, or sulphur baths on the altana.
- **Cosmetics** — white-lead face powder (*cerussa*) — known to be toxic but used anyway. Red cinnabar or carmine on the lips and cheeks. Kohl on the eyes (Byzantine and Italian both). Belladonna drops to dilate the pupils. Patches (*mouches*) to hide smallpox scars.
- **Perfume** — essential oils, pomanders (a perforated metal ball of aromatic paste), rose-water, orange-flower water, musk, civet, ambergris, violet.

### Sleep and domestic rhythms

- Most people slept in small rooms, often several per room. Straw pallets for the poor, wool mattresses for the middling, feather beds for the rich. Canopied beds in palaces.
- Children slept with nurses, siblings, or parents. The **cradle** (*cuna*) was close to the mother's bed.
- The city kept a rhythm of the **watches** — the calls of nightwatchmen at set hours, the clang of the Marangona, the chant of the muezzin (in Muslim quarters — Venice had Turkish merchants in residence).

### Work rhythms and the holiday calendar

- **Working week** — six days. Sunday was Sabbath; attendance at liturgy was expected.
- **Holidays** — the Orthodox and Catholic liturgical calendars were *packed*. A Venetian could count on perhaps 100 feast days a year when work was limited or forbidden. Saints' days, civic festivals, processions, carnival weeks, Lent, Holy Week, Easter Octave, Ascension, Pentecost, the doge's election anniversary, the deliverance from plague.
- **Carnival** — the great season of licence, from St Stephen's Day (26 December) to Shrove Tuesday. Masks worn publicly. Identities dissolved. Debts sometimes forgotten.

### Public spaces and leisure

- **The piazza** — civic heart. Markets by day; performances, executions, announcements, processions.
- **The hippodrome** (Byzantine) — chariot racing, political spectacle, occasional crisis.
- **Taverns and bacari** (Italian) — wine, cicchetti, gossip, gambling.
- **Gambling** — dice, cards (cards arrived in Europe via Venice from the Mamluk world in the 14th century), ball-games (*giuoco del pallone*, *giuoco del calcio* in Florence — a rough forerunner of football).
- **Theatre** — commedia dell'arte troupes performed in piazzas. Venice had permanent public theatres from the 1630s onwards (the first was the Teatro San Cassiano in 1637).
- **Gondola rides at dusk** — lovers, courtesans, assignations. A closed *felze* (wooden cabin) on the gondola gave privacy.

### Domestic life

- A Venetian patrician's day — late breakfast, correspondence, a visit to the Rialto or the Doge's Palace, midday dinner, siesta, the afternoon promenade to San Marco, supper, a visit to a tavern or a theatre, home late.
- A Byzantine matron's day — prayer at the domestic icon-corner, breakfast, household management (accounts, supervising servants), weaving, midday meal, visits or receiving visitors, afternoon chores, evening liturgy, supper with the family, retire.

### What this means for Tyramare
Tyramare keeps its hours by **three bells** (one in the Solace, one at the Iron-Quarter Arsenale, one at the Council in Aurumvale). The **morning market** at Spice Harbour is the busiest hour in the city. The **public baths** of the Marble Quarter are matriarchal institutions — where the money is talked about. The **bacari** (call them *baccatti* or *sparsi*) of Stillhaven serve cicchetti-equivalents and wine on draft. The **gondolas** are called *meralini* and carry passengers, cargo, assignations. The **carnival** of Tyramare lasts from one solstice feast to the spring moon. Every hour of the day has a ritual; every ritual is also a performance.

## 18. MYTHOLOGY AND LEGEND (beyond formal religion)

**Key fact for worldbuilding: both Byzantium and the Italian city-states told themselves origin stories that were partly history, partly myth, and entirely political.** Constantinople imagined itself as the New Rome, the new Jerusalem, and the last city before the Antichrist came. Venice imagined itself as the city that God founded in the sea, protected by St Mark, and destined to be the second Byzantium. These founding myths gave the cities their identity and justified their prerogatives. Tyramare needs its own equivalent — and its own secret, darker set of stories told below the official version.

### Byzantine legends

- **The foundation of Constantinople (330 CE)** — Constantine the Great, led by a vision, refounded the old Greek colony of Byzantion as the New Rome. Imperial tradition said that an eagle carried a plan from Constantine's hand and dropped it on the spot. The **seven hills** of Constantinople were a direct imitation of Rome's seven.
- **The Column of Constantine (the Hooped Column, *Çemberlitaş*)** — a porphyry column set up in Constantine's forum, supposedly containing nails from the True Cross, the alabaster jar that broke on Christ's feet, and other relics. The city's fortunes were tied to it. An old prophecy: if the column falls, the city falls.
- **The *Apocalypse of Pseudo-Methodius*** — a 7th-century apocalyptic text, hugely popular in Byzantium. The last Roman emperor would hand his crown to Christ at Jerusalem; then the Antichrist would come. Byzantines in every crisis read this text and asked whether their emperor was the last.
- **The Legend of the Pillar** — the Emperor Constantine's mother St Helena discovered the True Cross at Jerusalem. Fragments were distributed across the Christian world; the greatest chunk stayed at Constantinople.
- **Saint George** — the dragon-slayer saint. Originally Cappadocian, massively popular across Byzantium; his cult exported to Venice (the church of San Giorgio Maggiore) and to every Italian guild of soldiers.
- **The Statue of Athena** at the Augusteion, reputedly pointing west toward the enemies of Rome. Removed around 1204 by an iconoclast mob that thought her pointing gesture summoned Western invaders.

### The *Digenes Akritas* epic

The great Byzantine vernacular epic, composed c. 12th century. **Digenes** ("two-blooded") — son of an Arab emir who converted and a Greek princess. **Akritas** — "the borderer." He grows up on the eastern frontier, tames horses, kills a dragon, defeats Amazons, loves his wife Eudokia. A Byzantine Beowulf. His death scene, embracing his wife as he dies, is one of the great moments of medieval literature.

### Venetian origin-myths

- **The flight from Attila** — the traditional account says the cities of Roman Venetia (Aquileia, Altino, Padua) fled to the lagoon islands to escape Attila the Hun in 452 CE, founding Venice. The date usually given is **25 March 421** — the feast of the Annunciation — at noon, at the church of San Giacomo di Rialto. A date chosen to make the city simultaneously founded on the day Christ was conceived and before Constantinople's refounding.
- **The body of St Mark** — stolen from Alexandria in 828 CE by two Venetian merchants (Buono of Malamocco and Rustico of Torcello) who smuggled it past Muslim customs in a barrel of pork. The body became Venice's supreme relic; the gospel-lion its symbol; every doge a guardian of Mark's tomb.
- **The ring of the sea** — Pope Alexander III, grateful for Venetian mediation with Barbarossa, supposedly gave the doge a gold ring and said: *"Cast this into the sea, that you and your successors may be lords of it."* The Sposalizio del Mare (Wedding of the Sea) ritual was born.
- **The Pala d'Oro's hidden history** — many of the Pala's enamel plaques were looted from the Pantocrator monastery of Constantinople in 1204. Venetians knew this and were proud of it.

### Monsters and beings

- **The basilisk** — the cockatrice-serpent whose stare kills. Mentioned in Byzantine and Italian folklore, stored in bestiaries.
- **The sirens** — classical inheritance, adapted Christianly. Half-bird half-woman sea-temptresses on Italian maps and mosaics. Venetian sailors sometimes spoke of hearing them in the lagoon fog.
- **The water spirits of the lagoon** — Venetian folklore has the *bissa* (lagoon-snake), and stories of drowned Venetians returning.
- **The dragon under the city** — Byzantine legend (Constantinople) and Italian (the dragon of San Marco at Venice or the city-dragon of Bologna) had the city founded over a bound dragon who might wake.
- **The *Anima del Purgatorio*** — the souls of the dead who could walk at night seeking prayers.
- **The *incubus* and *succubus*** — the night-demons who came to lovers in sleep, a fear well-documented in plague literature and witch-trials.

### Prophecy and divination

- Byzantine court astrology — every emperor consulted his astrologers before a campaign.
- Oracles of the sibyl (Cumaean, Erythraean) were preserved and extrapolated into Christian prophecy. The **Tiburtine Sibyl** was said to have prophesied Christ to Emperor Augustus — a story still painted on Italian church walls in the Renaissance.
- The **Last Emperor prophecy** (Pseudo-Methodius) — the Christian emperor would resign his crown at Jerusalem at the end of the world.
- **Nostradamus** (1503-1566) — French but read widely in Italy. The same genre.

### The empty throne

A persistent motif in late Byzantine art and literature: the *Hetoimasia*, the "prepared throne" — an empty throne with the instruments of the Passion on it (crown, lance, sponge) awaiting Christ's Second Coming. Depicted in countless mosaics and icons. **A perfect visual precedent for Tyramare's empty Bannric throne** — the throne that waits for a king who will not come back, or who will return at the end of the story.

### What this means for Tyramare
Tyramare has **a founding myth** (the guilds fled a flooded inland city and built anew in the lagoon at the moment of a celestial sign — an eclipse, a comet, a meteor at noon). It has **a stolen relic** that gave the city its patron — something taken from Aoli's Shi Sect and reburied in the Solace, the shame and glory of the city in equal measure. It has **a ring-to-the-sea** ritual. It has **a Hetoimasia**: the empty Bannric throne echoes the Prepared Throne, awaiting a return. It has **monsters**: a creature in the lagoon, a dragon under the islands, sirens in the fog. It has **a prophecy of the Last Council** that everyone knows a version of. And below it all: a quiet, subversive second mythology — the **Umbrathi** say the city was built on a corpse, the **Verenthae** say it was built to HIDE a corpse, and everyone agrees the water underneath remembers something.

## 19. TABOOS, LAWS, AND ETHICS

**Key fact for worldbuilding: both Byzantium and the Italian city-states wrote their ethics into law with obsessive specificity — and then routinely broke them. The gap between the law on the scroll and the life in the street was enormous.** The same city that executed a noble for wearing forbidden cloth-of-gold let its senators take foreign bribes. The same temple that preached charity ran workshops of bonded labour. This gap was the *opera buffa* of civic life — and it is exactly where Tyramare lives.

### The great wrongs

- **Treason (*lèse-majesté*, *attentato allo stato*)** — against emperor or doge or council. In Byzantium, the traditional punishment was blinding (the punishment that disqualified from the throne without killing), or slitting of the nose, or monastic exile. In Venice, the Council of Ten could condemn to secret execution (drowning in the lagoon, garrotting in the Prison, throat-cutting in a sealed cell — the *camera sordina*). The body was disposed of without public funeral.
- **Heresy** — investigated by ecclesiastical courts. In Byzantium, burning was rare; tonsure and exile more common. In Italy, the Roman Inquisition (from 1542) and earlier diocesan tribunals could burn, though most sentences were lighter.
- **Witchcraft** — prosecuted less frenetically in Italy than in Northern Europe. Denunciations were common; most cases ended in recantation. Veronica Franco's 1580 trial is representative — accused, defended herself, acquitted.
- **Counterfeiting coinage** — in both cultures, a capital crime. Coin-clipping and die-forging broke the empire's finances and the Council's credit.
- **Parricide** — unforgivable.
- **Breaking sacred oaths** — the great invisible crime. An oath on relics could not be broken; divine punishment would follow.

### Honour and shame

- **Byzantine honour (*timē*)** — deeply tied to family name, to public reputation, to deference shown at court. A slight at a reception could last generations. The court was a machine for distributing *timē*; a fallen favourite was publicly humiliated (*theatralizesthai*, "being made a theatre of").
- **Italian honour** — a matter of public face. A gentleman did not bear an insult. **Duelling** was technically illegal but universally practised among the nobility; a *duello d'onore* was the recognised response to an insult that could not otherwise be wiped out. Duelling manuals (Fabris, Fioravanti) described both the technique and the etiquette.
- **Women's honour** — centred on chastity for married and unmarried women, on public modesty of dress and speech. Loss of reputation was catastrophic — a ruined daughter could be sent to a convent as alternative to marriage. Courtesans lived in the interesting space outside this system: their "honour" was their word, their wit, and their contracts.

### Hospitality

- Both cultures inherited from the Greek and Roman worlds a powerful ethic of hospitality. Denying a traveller shelter was a deep wrong; offering poisoned food was worse.
- The *fondaci* — foreign-merchant compounds — were specifically about orderly hospitality. A Turkish trader lived in the Fondaco dei Turchi under Venetian protection; violation of the compound was treason.
- A Byzantine matron who fed a stranger might be entertaining an angel — a deep theme of saints' lives.

### Oaths and vows

- Oaths on saints' relics were binding. Breaking one incurred automatic excommunication, hellfire, and — in the temporal law — confiscation of goods.
- Every Venetian senator at election swore on the gospel to uphold the constitution. Every guild member swore on induction.
- Monastic vows were held absolute. Abandoning the habit was punishable by civil as well as religious penalty.
- **The *promissione ducale*** — the oath each doge swore at election, progressively more restrictive over centuries. By the 18th century it had reached hundreds of clauses.

### Corruption, bribery, and the reality

- Bribery was endemic. Byzantine ambassadors routinely distributed gold to officials at foreign courts; everyone understood this as normal diplomacy.
- Venetian offices were theoretically uncorruptible through short terms and multiple checks; practically, patricians found ways to channel contracts to their kin. The Council of Ten existed partly to police this and to fail regularly at it.
- A famous Florentine saying attributed variously: *"Tutto si compra, tutto si vende, tutto si paga"* — "Everything is bought, everything is sold, everything is paid for."

### Specific Venetian laws

- **Sumptuary laws** (1299 onwards, repeatedly reissued) — limits on wedding banquets, dowry size, clothing, jewellery, numbers of guests, gifts.
- **The courtesan legislation of 1562** — banned silk, pearls, public carriages, and residency on certain streets for courtesans.
- **Laws against the mask** — at various times, masks were restricted. Nobles were forbidden to wear masks when voting in the Great Council. Anyone wearing a mask to a brothel, a monastery, or a gambling house was punished. But during Carnival, all these laws were suspended.
- **The *parchi*** — laws that governed patrician behaviour in the Broglio (the piazza in front of the Doge's Palace), the traditional space for political lobbying.

### Specific Byzantine laws

- **The Codex Justinianus** contained extensive laws on Christian moral behaviour: laws on blasphemy, on heresy, on homosexual acts (penalised brutally by Justinian, who attributed earthquakes to them), on Jews, on pagans, on prostitution.
- **Laws against women's veiling** — at various moments, empresses tried to require more modest dress; the laws were widely ignored.
- **Laws on court precedence** — a staggering catalogue of who bowed to whom, who spoke first, who sat where. The Book of Ceremonies (*De Ceremoniis*) of Constantine VII is 800 pages of this.

### Deep cultural wrongs

- **Violating a guest** — the original sin in Greek and Byzantine culture, going back to the Iliad.
- **Failing to mourn a parent** — an Orthodox scandal.
- **Selling a fellow citizen into slavery** — the great forbidden in both cultures. Selling foreigners was acceptable.
- **Betraying the city to a foreign power** — treason, inevitably executed.
- **Accepting a bribe to decide a trial** — theoretically a capital crime; practically common.

### What this means for Tyramare
Tyramare's official law is **obsessive** (a sumptuary code, a guild code, a council code, a temple code — all written down in detail, all constantly revised). Tyramare's honour system is **public face**, with duelling in the Iron Quarter and courtesan-honour in the Veil (their given word is binding; their silence is a service). **Sacred oaths** are real — break one and the Solenthera curses follow. **Bribery** is oil in the machine. The **Council of Ten-equivalent** prosecutes corruption selectively, and the Umbrathi use that to turn cases against enemies. The gap between law and practice IS the story — every character in Tyramare walks it.

## 20. THE FEELING OF THE CULTURE

**Key fact for worldbuilding: both Constantinople and Venice were cities that OVERWHELMED the senses.** Foreign visitors kept travel-journals full of the same note: they had never seen so much wealth, so many people, so much beauty, so much corruption, all in one place. Liutprand of Cremona on Constantinople: "The Greek emperor sat upon a throne of gold covered with silk, beside a golden tree in which mechanical birds of gold sang; four gold lions roared at the foot." Philippe de Commynes on Venice in 1495: "It is the most triumphant city that I have ever seen... and the best governed."

### What a traveller would notice first

Arriving by sea — the **skyline of domes and campanili**, gilded, catching the sunrise across the water. The smell of **salt and silk and fish and incense** before you'd even landed. Boats in every direction, every colour, every size. A crowd at the quay in twenty languages.

Arriving by land — the **walls first** (a tawny length of patched limestone and brick, older than any living memory), then the city spreading down to the water, roofs red-tiled and sun-bleached, a Solace dome reflecting light.

### Smells

- **Salt** — always. Lagoon salt, brackish and iodine-heavy.
- **Fish** — at the harbour, the market, the fish-quay at dawn, the gutters at midday when the stalls have closed. Constantinople and Venice both had this olfactory signature.
- **Incense** — rose out of every church at the appointed hours. Frankincense, myrrh, labdanum, cedar-resin.
- **Spice** — pepper, cinnamon, clove, nutmeg, saffron, mastic — wafting from the warehouses near the docks.
- **Perfume** — rose-water, orange-flower, musk, ambergris — from the ladies' windows and the courtesans' rooms.
- **Sewage and mud** — the less romantic but inescapable reality. Venice's canals in summer smelled; Constantinople's sewers sometimes failed.
- **Smoke** — cooking fires, glass furnaces, cloth-dyers, braziers. Venice's foggy evenings held the smoke of ten thousand fires over the water.
- **Fresh bread** — at every street corner in the morning.

### Sounds

- **Bells and semantra** — the marking of hours.
- **The Marangona** — Venice's shipyard-and-city bell.
- **Voices** — criers hawking wares, porters shouting cargo, gondoliers calling *"Oé, pope!"* ("Coming through!"), priests chanting the liturgy, children yelling in alleys.
- **Water** — lapping at hulls, dripping from oars, gurgling under bridges, running from fountains.
- **Music** — lute songs from tavern windows, lute lessons from palace interiors, chanted liturgy from Solace domes, courtesan songs from Veil windows at evening.
- **Languages** — in the harbour, a confusion of tongues: Tyramaric, Imperial, Isenholt, Maramiri, Aoli traders' pidgin. In the courtesan salons of the Veil, educated poetry in two languages at once. In the Solace, the high ceremonial speech nobody quite uses elsewhere.

### Sights

- **Domes** — the rounded profiles, gilded or tiled blue, multiple in formation.
- **Mosaics** — flashing from every public wall, catching the sunset, shimmering as shadows move across them.
- **The Grand Canal (or Tyramare equivalent)** — a street of water, flanked by palazzi whose painted facades and patterned marble change with the hour.
- **Masked figures** — during Carnival, and more quietly at other times, the anonymous black bauta-mask and tricorn hat, the colourful Columbina half-mask, the long-beaked plague-doctor.
- **Crowds** — piazzas full, markets full, bridges full. No empty city centre.
- **Gondolas** — sleek black, or occasionally (before sumptuary law forbade it in 1562) ornate and gilded. All the Venetian gondolas were painted black by decree to prevent noble display.
- **Green mould** on the lower stones of every building where water-level meets stone.
- **Doves** — thousands, around San Marco and every piazza. A living part of the city.

### Textures

- **Marble** under the hands — cold, veined, polished flat by centuries of touch.
- **Silk** and **velvet** — in every shop, on every noble, heavy with gold thread.
- **Damp linen** in the summer air.
- **Wet rope** on the dock-stones.
- **Brass** — door-knockers, bell-pulls, lamp-fittings — polished daily by servants.
- **Wax** and **paper** — the seal on every letter, the book in every library.

### Ambient mood — the feeling

Tyramare at its best: **jewelled, alive, musical, cosmopolitan**. A city where every hour of every day something extraordinary is happening somewhere — a procession, a concert in a palace, a courtesan's salon arguing philosophy, a Solace liturgy lifting the hair on the back of your neck, a galley of spices unloading at Spice Harbour.

Tyramare at its truest: **beautiful and rotten in the same breath**. A mosaic with a crack. A palace with a drowned servant in its canal. A courtesan's letter that is also a Council denunciation. A feast where three-quarters of the guests have bribed the same senator this month. A saint's bone that has been secretly switched for a rival saint's bone, and nobody knows which way.

Tyramare at night: **quieter, smaller, darker, more uncertain**. The Marangona has tolled. The watches are out. Gondolas slip past with *felze* closed. A knot of masked figures crosses a bridge and vanishes. Someone hums a song two streets away. The lagoon breathes in and out.

### What outsiders know Tyramare for

**"Everything costs more in Tyramare and everything is worth more in Tyramare."** That's the saying. You go to Tyramare for silk and glass and information. You go to Tyramare to be seen, to be schooled, to be poisoned. You go to Tyramare because the sea carries you there, and once arrived, you cannot quite bring yourself to leave.

The **Marble Quarter matriarchs** are spoken of with both respect and fear across the continents. The **Veil courtesans** are the most famous women in the world after Aoli's Wen Sect. The **Iron Quarter** swords are the best steel in Caerndrath. The **Solace** is the most beautiful temple any traveller will see in this life. The **empty Bannric throne** is a standing joke and a standing mystery.

And everyone — foreigner and local, merchant and priest, noble and courtesan — knows that underneath the marble there is a rot; and that the rot is what makes the marble glow the way it does. **The jeweled surface and the rot beneath, the ritual and the coin, the faith that speaks in lullabies, the guild politics that run deeper than any king, and the women who hold the money in one hand and the knife in the other.** That is Tyramare.
